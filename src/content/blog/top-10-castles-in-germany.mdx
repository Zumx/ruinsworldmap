Germany has, by most counts, over 20,000 castles and castle ruins — the
densest concentration in Europe and arguably the world. The vocabulary itself
splits two ways: *Burg* for the medieval defensive fortress, *Schloss* for the
later palace-castle. These ten cover both, and the full geographical and
stylistic range of the country.

## 1. Neuschwanstein, Bavaria

Ludwig II's cliff-top Romantic fantasy above Hohenschwangau, finished after
his death and never lived in as planned. The world's most photographed castle
and the template for every theme-park imitation since.

## 2. Burg Eltz, Rhineland-Palatinate

A small valley castle that has belonged to the same family for over 850 years,
never destroyed, and uniquely intact in its furnished interiors. The hike in
through the forest is half the experience.

## 3. Hohenzollern, Baden-Württemberg

The ancestral seat of the Prussian royal house, rebuilt in the 1850s as a
neo-Gothic vision on a lone Swabian hilltop. The mist that pools beneath it on
autumn mornings is the photograph.

## 4. Heidelberg Schloss, Baden-Württemberg

The great red-sandstone Renaissance ruin above the Neckar, half-blown-up by
French troops and never fully rebuilt — the picturesque ruin that defined
German Romanticism.

## 5. Wartburg, Thuringia

A UNESCO-listed hilltop castle where Luther translated the New Testament into
German in 1521 — as foundational to the country's identity as any building it
owns.

## 6. Marksburg, Rhineland-Palatinate

The only Rhine castle never destroyed, perched above Braubach. The textbook
medieval *Burg* — kitchens, armoury, gatehouse all intact and properly
interpreted.

## 7. Schloss Schwerin, Mecklenburg-Vorpommern

An island palace on a lake in the north, neo-Renaissance turrets stacked on
medieval foundations, still in use as the state parliament. Germany's answer
to Chambord.

## 8. Burg Hohenwerfen, near Salzburg border

A 900-year-old fortress on a rock above the Salzach, with one of the best
falconry displays in Europe and a cable car that saves the climb.

## 9. Schloss Sanssouci, Brandenburg

Frederick the Great's Rococo summer palace at Potsdam — terraced vineyards,
intimate scale, and the king's grave on the upper terrace where he asked to be
buried beside his dogs.

## 10. Burg Cochem, Rhineland-Palatinate

The classic Mosel postcard: a castle on a vine-covered cone above a hairpin
bend in the river. Heavily restored in the 19th century, but the silhouette is
unbeatable.

## How German castle visiting works

Most *Burgen* and *Schlösser* run April-to-October interiors with restricted
or closed winter access; the grounds are usually open year-round. Many
state-owned sites (Bavaria, Baden-Württemberg, Prussia) sell combined
multi-castle tickets — worth checking if you are planning more than two.
Tours are often German-only at smaller sites; print or download an English
guide ahead.

## Cluster by region, not by country

Germany is too wide to tour as one trip. The Middle Rhine between Koblenz and
Bingen packs a dozen castles into 65 km of river — best done by KD boat or
the rail line on either bank. Bavaria's Romantic Road links Neuschwanstein,
Hohenschwangau, and the Würzburg Residenz. The Mosel loops Cochem, Eltz, and
Beilstein in a weekend. Pick one valley.

## Trains, boats, and cable cars

German rail puts most major castles within walking distance of a station, and
the Rhine and Mosel are best seen from the water. Hilltop sites often have
shuttle buses or funiculars from the town below — Hohenzollern, Hohensalzburg,
Hohenwerfen — so the climb is optional even for serious fortresses.

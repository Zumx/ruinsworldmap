## Masters of the Sea

The Phoenicians occupied a narrow strip of coastline along the eastern Mediterranean — the cities of Tyre, Sidon, Byblos, Arwad, and Beirut, in what is now Lebanon and the adjacent coast of Syria — from roughly the 10th century BC onward. They were not a unified political entity but a collection of city-states sharing a Semitic language, a common material culture, and an outward-looking orientation toward the sea. Their greatest legacy is not a ruined empire but an idea: the alphabetic writing system they developed from earlier Semitic scripts and disseminated through trade across the Mediterranean world, giving rise ultimately to the Greek, Latin, and Arabic alphabets and thus to most of the world's writing systems in use today.

Phoenician seafarers were the dominant long-distance traders of the Mediterranean between roughly 900 and 600 BC. Their cedar-planked merchant ships, and warships built in the distinctive bireme style, carried timber from the mountains of Lebanon, glass from Sidon, purple dye (extracted from the murex sea-snail and enormously valuable in antiquity, giving Phoenicia its Greek name, from the word for purple-red), fine metalwork, ivory carvings, and textiles across routes stretching from the Levantine coast to Spain and possibly beyond. Ancient sources, including the Hebrew Bible, record the Phoenicians as the great seafarers of their age, and the archaeological evidence from shipwrecks, harbours, and colonial settlements confirms this reputation.

## The Phoenician Homeland: Byblos, Sidon, and Tyre

Byblos (modern Jbeil, north of Beirut) is the oldest of the Phoenician cities and one of the oldest continuously inhabited settlements in the world, with occupation going back to the Chalcolithic period. The site preserves remains from multiple eras including a Crusader castle built from ancient stone, Bronze Age temples, Egyptian-influenced stelae, and Phoenician royal tombs. It was through Byblos that Egyptian papyrus was exported to the Aegean world — so consistently that the Greeks named papyrus byblos and eventually extended that word to books and scripture (hence the English word 'Bible').

Sidon (modern Saida) was renowned in antiquity for its glassware, purple dye, and bronze objects. Its ancient harbour and urban layers lie largely beneath the modern city, but excavations over the decades have revealed Iron Age and Persian-period remains. The Sidon excavations by the British Museum uncovered sarcophagi of extraordinary quality, including the Sarcophagus of the Satrap (now in Istanbul's Archaeological Museum), demonstrating the wealth of Phoenician elite culture under Persian rule in the 5th and 4th centuries BC.

Tyre (modern Sur, in southern Lebanon) occupied a rocky island linked to the mainland by a causeway built by Alexander the Great when he besieged it in 332 BC. That siege, which lasted seven months, is one of the great set pieces of ancient military history; Alexander's engineers constructed a mole nearly a kilometre long by dumping rubble into the sea, transforming Tyre from island to peninsula. The modern city covers most of the ancient site, but excavations and surveys have revealed Phoenician, Hellenistic, and Roman layers including a well-preserved Roman hippodrome that is a UNESCO World Heritage Site.

## The Alphabet's Journey

The Phoenician alphabet, developed in the Levant from earlier consonantal scripts around 1050 BC, was a radical simplification compared with Akkadian cuneiform or Egyptian hieroglyphics. It used around 22 signs, each representing a consonant, with no vowels — readers supplied vowels from context. This simplicity made it learnable by merchants and officials without years of scribal training, and it spread rapidly through trade contacts.

The Greeks adopted the Phoenician alphabet, probably in the 9th or 8th century BC, adding vowel signs to create the first fully phonemic alphabet. From Greek it passed to the Etruscans and then to Rome, producing the Latin alphabet used for English and most European languages today. Hebrew and Aramaic scripts derived from Phoenician directly, and through Aramaic, the Nabataean and Arabic scripts. The Phoenician alphabet is thus the common ancestor of an extraordinary proportion of human literacy.

## Carthage: The Greatest Colony

The most famous Phoenician colony was Carthage, founded according to tradition in 814 BC on a promontory overlooking the Bay of Tunis in what is now northern Tunisia. Whether the traditional date is precise or not, Carthage grew to become the largest and wealthiest city in the western Mediterranean, controlling a maritime empire that included extensive territories in North Africa, Sicily, Sardinia, and Spain. At its height in the 4th and 3rd centuries BC, it was arguably the most powerful state in the Mediterranean world west of Greece.

Carthage was destroyed by Rome at the end of the Third Punic War in 146 BC, after which the Romans are said to have salted the earth — a legendary detail that is probably not literally true but reflects the completeness of the destruction. The Romans later rebuilt a major city on the same site, and it is largely Roman Carthage that is visible today: the Antonine Baths, one of the largest bath complexes in the Roman Empire; the Tophet, a sacred precinct that has fuelled debate about Carthaginian child sacrifice; the Punic harbours, where the circular military harbour is visible from the air; and the Byrsa hill, where excavations have revealed sections of Punic-era domestic architecture beneath the Roman layers. Carthage is a UNESCO World Heritage Site.

## Colonies Across the Mediterranean

Beyond Carthage, the Phoenicians planted trading posts and colonies across the Mediterranean. Kition (modern Larnaca) on Cyprus was a Phoenician settlement from the 9th century BC. In Sardinia, Phoenician settlements at Sulcis, Tharros, and Nora left substantial remains including temples, harbour facilities, and cemeteries. In Sicily, Motya (now the island of San Pantaleo in western Sicily) was a Phoenician city occupied from the 8th century BC until its destruction by Dionysius I of Syracuse in 397 BC; its ruins include the Tophet, stretches of city wall, and a causeway to the mainland, and the island holds one of the finest collections of Punic archaeology in the world.

In Spain, Gadir (modern Cadiz) was one of the oldest Phoenician foundations in the far west, traditionally dated to around 1100 BC, though the archaeological evidence currently points to around the 9th or 8th century BC. The Phoenicians of Gadir and other Iberian settlements exploited the silver and copper resources of the Rio Tinto region and traded with local Iberian peoples, establishing patterns of exchange and cultural contact that shaped Iberian prehistory.

## What Survives in the Archaeological Record

The Phoenicians left fewer monumental ruins than their contemporaries in Egypt, Mesopotamia, or Greece, partly because their cities were built of perishable materials and partly because they were so consistently reoccupied and rebuilt. Tyre, Sidon, and Byblos are all living cities where archaeological access is constrained. In the colonial world — Carthage, Motya, Tharros, Kition — more extensive excavation has been possible, yielding rich assemblages of pottery, glass, metalwork, ivory, and inscriptions.

Punic inscriptions are found across the western Mediterranean and form an important body of evidence for the Phoenician language and its colonial daughter tongue, Punic. The Punic language survived the fall of Carthage and continued to be spoken in North Africa into the early centuries AD; Saint Augustine, writing in the 4th and 5th centuries AD, mentions it as still alive in his native region of Numidia.

The Phoenicians' most durable legacy remains invisible in stone but permanently present in every alphabet-based writing system: the 22 consonants they arranged and named gave the world a tool for recording language that has proven more adaptable and more widely adopted than any other.

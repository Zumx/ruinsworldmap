Yemen occupies the southwestern corner of the Arabian Peninsula, and for much of antiquity it was one of the wealthiest and most urbanised parts of the Arabian world. The reason was control of the incense trade: frankincense and myrrh, both native to the Hadramawt and Dhofar regions, were indispensable in ancient Mediterranean religious practice, and the overland caravan routes that carried them northward through Arabia to Egypt, the Levant, and Mesopotamia made the South Arabian kingdoms fabulously rich. The kingdoms of Saba (biblical Sheba), Qataban, Hadramawt, and Himyar built monumental temples, dam systems, and cities in the highland wadis and in the fringes of the great sand sea of the Rub al-Khali. Yemen is also home to one of the most complete medieval Islamic townscapes in the world — Old Sana'a and the tower-house city of Shibam — and to the extraordinary natural and cultural landscape of Socotra. The ongoing civil war that began in 2015 has placed the entire archaeological and historical heritage of Yemen under severe threat from airstrikes, looting, military use of heritage sites, and the collapse of site management and conservation infrastructure.

## 1. Marib — Great Dam and Sabaean Temples

Marib was the capital of the ancient Sabaean kingdom, the most powerful of the South Arabian states, and its ruins preserve some of the most significant monuments of Arabian antiquity. The Great Dam of Marib, originally built in the 8th century BC and substantially rebuilt in the 6th century AD, was one of the largest irrigation dams in the ancient world, controlling a reservoir that enabled agriculture across thousands of hectares of otherwise arid valley. Its two surviving sluice towers — the North and South sluices — are still standing, built in massive cut stone. The temples of Awam (sometimes called the Temple of the Moon God Almaqah or the Mahram Bilqis, associated by tradition with the Queen of Sheba) and Barran are partially excavated monumental sanctuaries whose carved columns and inscribed altars represent the visual language of Sabaean civilisation at its height. Marib has been in a conflict zone since 2015 and access is severely restricted.

## 2. Old Sana'a

Old Sana'a is one of the oldest continuously inhabited cities in the world and one of the most architecturally distinctive. Its tower houses — some rising six and seven stories in layers of dark basalt stone, fired brick, and alabaster-windowed gypsum-plaster upper floors — create a skyline unlike any other city on earth. The houses are densely packed within the walls of the old city, interspersed with mosques, suqs, public gardens (maqshamas), and hammams. The Great Mosque of Sana'a, founded in the 7th century AD and among the oldest in the Islamic world, stands near the centre of the old city. A UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1986, Old Sana'a sustained significant damage in airstrikes in 2015, particularly in the historic souk and residential quarters. The city has been under Houthi control since 2015 and is not accessible to most visitors.

## 3. Shibam, Hadramawt

Shibam in the Wadi Hadramawt is a 16th-century tower-house city whose mud-brick skyscrapers — some eight and nine stories tall — earned it the sobriquet 'Manhattan of the Desert'. The city is enclosed within a defensive wall and its 500-odd mudbrick towers were built on the same principle as modern high-rise construction: individual plots maximising height rather than area. The technology of mudbrick construction at height requires continuous maintenance — the walls must be replastered every few years — and the city has maintained this practice continuously for centuries. Shibam is a UNESCO World Heritage Site. Access has been severely restricted since 2015 due to the conflict in Yemen, though Hadramawt has been under different military control and conditions there have fluctuated.

## 4. Baraqish (Yathil), Jawf Province

Baraqish is the most completely preserved pre-Islamic walled city in Yemen, the ancient Minaean city of Yathil whose massive stone circuit walls still stand to a height of several metres around the entire perimeter of the old town. Inside the walls, a large temple of Nakrah and traces of domestic architecture survive. The Minaean kingdom controlled parts of the northern incense route in the 4th–2nd centuries BC. Baraqish has been subject to shelling and military use during the civil war, with significant structural damage reported since 2015. Pre-conflict access was from Sana'a.

## 5. Zafar, Ibb Governorate

Zafar was the capital of the Himyarite kingdom, the last and in some ways most complex of the South Arabian states, which unified most of Yemen under its control in the 3rd–6th centuries AD. The Himyarites adopted Judaism as a state religion in the late 4th century AD — one of the most unusual episodes in Arabian history — before a brief period of Christian Aksumite Ethiopian rule and then the Islamic conquest. The ruins at Zafar, in the highland Ibb region, include monumental carved buildings, inscriptions in the ancient South Arabian script, and large cisterns. Pre-conflict, the site was accessible from Ibb; current conditions are uncertain.

## 6. Sumhuram (Khor Rori), Near Salalah, Dhofar (Oman/Yemen border zone)

While Sumhuram lies strictly in modern Oman near Salalah, it was the principal port of the South Arabian frankincense trade and is directly connected to the Yemeni kingdoms of antiquity. Founded in roughly the 4th century BC as a trading post associated with the Hadhramawtic or Qatabanian spheres, it sits above a sheltered lagoon (Khor Rori) from which dhows loaded frankincense for Arabia, India, and the Mediterranean. The site has a well-preserved town plan of stone-built houses, a temple, and a harbour gate, excavated by American and Italian teams. It is a UNESCO World Heritage Site (part of the Land of Frankincense listing) and is accessible from Salalah.

## 7. Qana, Hadramawt Coast

Qana was the main seaport of the Hadramawtic kingdom, the harbour through which frankincense from the Hadramawt mountains was loaded onto ships for the Red Sea, Persian Gulf, and India. Greek geographers knew it well; the Periplus Maris Erythraei of the 1st century AD describes it as a prosperous emporium. The ruins on a bluff above the coast include a substantial town, cisterns, warehouses, and rock-cut tombs. Limited excavation has confirmed its period of peak activity from roughly the 1st century BC to the 4th century AD. The Hadramawt coastal zone has been somewhat less affected by the civil war than the northwestern highlands, but access remains logistically difficult.

## 8. Shabwa, Hadramawt

Shabwa was the capital of the Hadramawtic kingdom, a large city in the desert edge zone southeast of Marib. Excavated by French and Yemeni archaeologists before the conflict, the site preserves a palace complex, a temple of Sin the moon god, and extensive residential quarters. A remarkable series of South Arabian inscriptions from Shabwa documents the commercial and administrative life of the Hadramawtic state in the 2nd and 3rd centuries AD. The site is in territory that has seen fighting, and pre-conflict surveys showed signs of looting.

## 9. Al-Qahira Citadel, Taiz

The citadel of Al-Qahira above the city of Taiz is a medieval Islamic fortification commanding the mountain pass above one of Yemen's largest cities. The existing stone towers and walls date primarily to the Rasulid period (13th–15th centuries AD), when Taiz was the principal capital of the most culturally sophisticated Yemeni dynasty of the medieval period. The Rasulids patronised architecture, poetry, science, and crafts at a level that made their court a significant centre of Islamic civilisation. The citadel sustained significant damage during sustained fighting in Taiz between 2015 and 2022, when the city was effectively besieged. Al-Qahira remains the most prominent historical monument of the city.

## 10. Socotra Archaeological Zone

The island of Socotra, in the Arabian Sea roughly 240 kilometres east of the Horn of Africa, has a human occupation stretching back at least 2,000 years and possibly longer. Its unique flora — the dragon blood tree, the bottle tree — featured in ancient trade in aromatics and medicines. Rock art, Bronze Age burials, and remains of ancient incense collection and trade infrastructure have been documented across the island. A corpus of ancient inscriptions in South Arabian, Palmyrene, Indian Brahmi, and Greek scripts left by sailors and pilgrims in Hoq Cave provides extraordinary evidence for the island's role in the maritime trade network of the 1st millennium AD. Socotra is a UNESCO Natural World Heritage Site; its cultural heritage remains incompletely documented but of great significance.

## Planning Your Visit

As of the mid-2020s, Yemen remains largely inaccessible to foreign visitors due to the ongoing civil war. The areas around Sana'a, Marib, Baraqish, Zafar, and Taiz have all been affected by active conflict. Hadramawt, including Shibam, Shabwa, and the coastal sites, has been somewhat more stable under separate administration but access is still extremely difficult. Socotra is accessible with a permit and has received some visitors through the UAE-administered period. The Omani site of Sumhuram is fully accessible from Salalah and offers the most direct encounter with the South Arabian trade network outside Yemen itself. When conditions eventually allow safe travel, Yemen's archaeological and architectural heritage will be one of the most compelling destinations in the Arab world.

## Why Walls Were Built

The decision to enclose a settlement with a wall was never purely military. Walls defined the city as a political and religious entity distinct from the territory beyond its gates. In Mesopotamia, the earliest defensive enclosures appear at sites like Tell es-Sawwan and Ain Ghazal in the late 8th and 7th millennia BC — low mud-brick perimeters that may have served as much to control access and signal permanence as to repel attackers. By the 3rd millennium BC, the walls of Uruk in southern Iraq stretched for roughly nine kilometres, enclosing an area of around six square kilometres and incorporating roughly nine hundred semicircular towers according to later tradition. The city's patron goddess Inanna was said to have brought the walls into being, and the Epic of Gilgamesh opens with an invitation to walk Uruk's walls and admire their craftsmanship in kiln-fired brick.

This conjunction of defence, identity, and divine sanction runs through nearly every walled city in antiquity. The Greek polis was defined in part by its walls — a city without walls was a paradox, almost a contradiction in terms. Roman colonies received walls as a matter of right and civic pride even when no military threat was imminent. Walls marked the threshold between civilisation and wilderness, between the governed and the ungoverned.

## Materials and Construction

The materials available in a given landscape shaped fortification design more than almost any other factor. In the alluvial plains of Mesopotamia and the Nile delta, where stone was scarce, sun-dried mud brick was the universal material, occasionally supplemented by fired brick for facing. At Babylon under Nebuchadnezzar II in the 6th century BC, the inner city wall — the Imgur-Enlil — was constructed of fired brick and measured roughly six metres thick at the base, with an outer defensive wall of even greater mass beyond it. The ancient visitor approaching Babylon would have seen glazed-brick decoration on the Ishtar Gate set into this system: deep blue lapis lazuli blue ground with bulls and dragons in yellow and white.

Where stone was plentiful, it dominated. The Cyclopean masonry of Mycenaean citadels — enormous limestone blocks fitted without mortar — gave sites like Mycenae and Tiryns (both in the Argolid, Greece, roughly 14th–13th centuries BC) walls up to eight metres thick. The Greeks of the Classical and Hellenistic periods preferred carefully cut ashlar blocks, sometimes bonded with metal clamps rather than mortar. The walls of Messene in the Peloponnese, built around 369 BC after the city's liberation from Spartan control, are among the best preserved in the Greek world: they run for roughly nine kilometres with towers at regular intervals and a magnificent arcaded gateway at Arcadia.

Roman military engineering combined cut stone facings with a concrete and rubble core — the same opus incertum and opus reticulatum visible in city walls from Aosta in the Alps to Caesarea Maritima on the Levantine coast. The Aurelian Wall at Rome, begun in 271 AD in response to growing external pressure on the empire, enclosed the entire city within a circuit of roughly nineteen kilometres, incorporating existing structures including an aqueduct arch as one of its towers.

## Gate Design and the Architecture of Entry

Gates were simultaneously the weakest point in any defensive circuit and the most architecturally elaborated. Controlling passage through the gate was the primary military challenge; marking it as an assertion of power was the primary political one. The Lion Gate at Mycenae, roughly dated to around 1250 BC, is the earliest surviving monumental sculpture in Europe: two lions flanking a column carved in relief above the lintel. It was not merely decorative — the heraldic display announced the authority of the ruling dynasty to anyone approaching the citadel.

The casemate gate, developed in the Levant and widely used in the Iron Age, placed a series of chambers on either side of the entrance corridor, allowing defenders to trap and isolate attackers who had forced entry. The six-chambered gates at Hazor, Megiddo, and Gezer in Israel — all attributed by some scholars to a single Solomonic building programme in the 10th century BC, though the dating is contested — are the most cited examples. Later, Hellenistic military architects developed the courtyard tower gate with overlapping fields of fire from flanking towers, a design visible at sites like Perge in southern Turkey.

## Frontier Defences: Beyond the City Wall

Not all fortifications were urban. Linear frontier systems — walls, ditches, palisades, and chains of signal towers stretching across landscapes — represent a different scale of ambition and a different theory of defence. Hadrian's Wall in northern Britain, begun around 122 AD under the emperor Hadrian, ran for roughly 80 Roman miles (approximately 117 kilometres) from Wallsend on the Tyne to Bowness-on-Solway, incorporating a ditch on the north, a turf or stone wall, milecastles at every mile, and turrets between them. It was not primarily a barrier against mass invasion — no wall that thin could have stopped a determined army — but a controlled frontier with regulated crossing points, a customs system, and a chain of communication along its length.

The Limes Germanicus, the Roman frontier system along the Rhine and Danube, extended for roughly 550 kilometres in its Upper German and Raetian sections, combining a wooden palisade, a ditch, and forts linked by roads. The Great Wall of China represents the most extreme expression of this impulse: its various phases, from the Warring States period onwards and reaching their fullest elaboration in the Ming dynasty (14th–17th centuries AD), created a system of walls, towers, and garrisons extending thousands of kilometres across northern China and Inner Mongolia. The Ming Great Wall is not a single continuous structure but a complex of parallel and overlapping walls built in different campaigns, largely of rammed earth in the west and fired brick in the east.

## Siege Warfare and the Evolution of Fortification Design

Fortification design never evolved in isolation from offensive military technology; it was always in dialogue with siege warfare. The Assyrian army of the 9th–7th centuries BC was the ancient world's most formidable siege force, deploying battering rams, siege towers, and systematic earthworks to reduce walled cities. In response, defenders raised walls higher, thickened their bases, multiplied towers, and dug water-filled moats. The principle of the projecting tower — which allowed defenders to rake the wall face with flanking fire against attackers approaching with ladders or rams — became standard across the Near East and eventually the Mediterranean world.

The introduction of artillery — torsion catapults capable of hurling stones of fifty kilograms or more — in the 4th century BC transformed the calculus again. Walls needed to absorb shock without cracking; towers needed to be lower and thicker to resist direct hits rather than tall for observation. The Hellenistic military architect Philo of Byzantium, writing around 200 BC, recommended a wall of roughly 4.5 metres thick filled with earth and rubble rather than the earlier solid stone construction, precisely because of this concern. The round tower replaced the square tower progressively across the Hellenistic and Roman world because a curved face deflected projectile impacts more effectively.

## What Survives and How to Visit

Fortification walls are among the best-preserved ancient structures because their mass resisted spoliation. Villagers building houses in the Middle Ages could more easily carry off the thin slabs of a temple roof than the enormous blocks of a city wall's facing. As a result, the great surviving circuits are genuinely impressive: the walls of Dubrovnik (though medieval rather than ancient), the Theodosian Walls of Constantinople built in the early 5th century AD and still standing to their full height in stretches, the Hellenistic walls of Side and Perge in Turkey, the Roman walls of Lugo in Spain (a UNESCO World Heritage Site, preserved almost completely around a circuit of roughly two kilometres), and the fortress walls of Buhen in Nubia, studied before being inundated by the Aswan Dam.

For those interested in frontier systems, the best experience of Hadrian's Wall is the central section between Housesteads and Steel Rigg — a UNESCO World Heritage Site — where the wall follows the crags of the Whin Sill and the remains of milecastles, turrets, and the vallum ditch are visible in the landscape.

Morocco sits at the western edge of the classical world — a land the Romans called Mauretania Tingitana after the Berber Mauretanian kingdom that preceded their provincial administration. Its archaeological record stretches from Neolithic megalith builders through Phoenician traders who established coastal stations, Berber kingdoms of the pre-Roman period, a remarkably productive Roman provincial presence, and the medieval Marinid dynasty whose ruined medrasa and mosques left behind some of North Africa's finest decorative stonework. The Roman site of Volubilis, preserved in the wheat-growing plain at the foot of the Middle Atlas, is the most complete Roman city in North Africa outside Tunisia; alongside it, sites like Chellah and Lixus layer Roman, Phoenician, and medieval Moroccan history in ways that reflect the country's deep position at the crossroads of Mediterranean and Atlantic worlds.

## 1. Volubilis, Meknes Province

Volubilis was the administrative capital of the Roman province of Mauretania Tingitana, reaching its height in the second and third centuries CE. The city is unusually well preserved for two reasons: it was abandoned relatively abruptly after the Roman withdrawal in the late third century, and the region has been sparsely populated since, reducing the recycling of its stone. A triumphal arch built in 217 CE in honour of the emperor Caracalla still stands to most of its original height. The Capitoline temple platform, a large basilica-forum complex, the public baths, and several substantial private houses with mosaic floors in situ — hunting scenes, mythological subjects, the famous Labours of Hercules — survive across a site of around 42 hectares. A UNESCO World Heritage Site. Located 30 kilometres north of Meknes, easily reached by grand taxi or bus.

## 2. Chellah, Rabat

Chellah occupies a hilltop above the Bou Regreg river estuary at Rabat that was sacred — and settled — from Phoenician times through the Roman period and into the medieval Marinid dynasty. The Roman city of Sala Colonia is present as foundations and excavated structures; what strikes the visitor is the Marinid necropolis and sanctuary built over the ruins in the fourteenth century. The Marinid walls, a monumental gate, the ruined mosque and minaret covered in storks' nests, and the tomb enclosures of Marinid sultans create a romantic layering of Roman and medieval Islamic ruins that is unique in Morocco. Entry through the Marinid gateway is theatrical. The site is within Rabat and easily combined with the city's Hassan Tower (an unfinished almohad minaret nearby).

## 3. Lixus, Larache Province

Lixus was one of the earliest Phoenician settlements in Morocco, founded perhaps as early as the twelfth century BCE on a hilltop above the Loukkos river estuary near modern Larache, though the earliest confirmed material dates to around the eighth century BCE. It was later a Punic and then Roman city; a large fish-salting industry (garum — the Roman fish sauce traded across the empire) is well evidenced in the lower town. On the hilltop, a mosaic of Neptune in a bath complex and a large amphitheatre are among the visible Roman remains. The Phoenician and Punic levels are less visible but archaeologically significant. The site is accessible from Larache and considerably quieter than Volubilis.

## 4. Sijilmasa, Tafilalt Oasis

Sijilmasa was the great medieval trans-Saharan trading city, founded in the mid-eighth century CE as the northern terminus of the gold and salt routes crossing the Sahara from sub-Saharan West Africa. At its height between the tenth and fourteenth centuries, Sijilmasa was among the most important commercial centres in the Muslim world, a city of merchants, scholars, and traders handling commodities that enriched the dynasties of Morocco. The Alaouite sultans eventually destroyed the city in the seventeenth century to eliminate a rival power base. Today the ruins — mostly mud-brick walls, collapsed towers, and excavated palace structures — lie in the Tafilalt oasis near Rissani. Excavations by a Moroccan-American team have mapped the city's extent. The site is off the main tourist circuit but connects directly to the trans-Saharan heritage of southern Morocco.

## 5. Walili (Oualili), Meknes Region

Walili is the Berber name for Volubilis, and 'Walili' is also used sometimes to refer specifically to the pre-Roman Berber and Mauretanian settlement layers beneath and around the more prominent Roman remains. Juba II — the Berber king installed by Augustus who had been educated in Rome and married Cleopatra Selene (daughter of Cleopatra VII) — ruled from Volubilis/Walili before the area became a full Roman province. The Mauretanian-period buildings beneath the Roman city represent one of the more significant Berber urban contexts in Northwest Africa, though they are less immediately visible than the Roman overlay.

## 6. Sala (Roman Sala Colonia), Rabat Region

Beyond the Chellah site, the Roman city of Sala Colonia extended across a wider area of what is now Rabat. Excavations have revealed a forum, thermae (baths), and residential areas. The Roman city was active from the first to the third centuries CE, contemporary with Volubilis. Most of its remains are subsumed within the modern urban fabric of Rabat, but the Chellah excavations and the Rabat Archaeological Museum hold significant material from the site.

## 7. Tingis (Ancient Tangier), Tangier

Tingis — modern Tangier — was the capital of Mauretania Tingitana and a significant Roman city. As with many continuously occupied ancient cities, the Roman layers are largely inaccessible beneath the modern city, but the Tangier Archaeological Museum displays mosaics, sculptures, and objects from the Roman period, including material from Volubilis and smaller regional sites. The promontory of Cape Spartel to the west of Tangier has been associated in ancient geography with the Pillars of Hercules and the 'Grotto of Hercules' tourist attraction, though the caves themselves are a natural phenomenon rather than an archaeological site.

## 8. Tamuda (Tetouan Region), Northern Morocco

Tamuda was a Mauretanian Berber city near modern Tetouan, active from at least the third century BCE. It was destroyed by the Romans in the first century CE and then replaced by a Roman castellum (military fort) on the same site. Excavations have identified the city's street plan, walls, and a sanctuary, as well as the overlying Roman fort with its defensive ditches. The site represents the transition from pre-Roman Berber urbanism to Roman military colonisation in northern Morocco and is studied by Spanish and Moroccan archaeological teams.

## 9. Msoura Stone Circle, Asilah Region

Msoura (or Mzora) is one of the largest prehistoric stone circles in North Africa, located in the farmland south of Asilah. An oval arrangement of standing stones — originally over 170 in number, around 90 still upright — surrounds a large tumulus. The monument is probably from the Neolithic or Bronze Age, broadly contemporary with Stonehenge, though precise dating is difficult. Local tradition associated it with the tomb of the mythological giant Antaeus. The site is in a field on private land and is accessible but not signposted or managed; visitors typically arrive from Asilah with a local guide.

## 10. Cotta and the Garum Factories, Tangier Peninsula

On the Atlantic coast of the Tangier peninsula, the Roman fish-processing site of Cotta preserves large rectangular stone tanks used for producing garum and preserved fish — a major industry of the Mauretanian Atlantic coast from the first through fourth centuries CE. The tanks, some still retaining their waterproofing plaster, sit directly on the cliffs above the sea. Similar factory complexes have been identified at Lixus and at several points along the Moroccan and Spanish coasts, documenting the scale of the Roman fish-sauce trade. Cotta is accessible from the road between Tangier and Cape Spartel and is among the best-preserved garum production sites in the western Mediterranean.

---

Morocco's major archaeological sites are accessible year-round, though spring (March to May) and October offer the most comfortable temperatures. Volubilis is the clear priority for any visitor interested in Roman North Africa; a rental car gives the most flexibility to combine it with Chellah in Rabat and Lixus near Larache. The Archaeological Museum in Rabat and the Tangier Archaeological Museum hold the best regional collections.

## A World Unmade

Around 1200 BC, the eastern Mediterranean was home to an interconnected system of palace economies, long-distance trade networks, and diplomatic correspondences that bound together civilisations spanning from Egypt to Mesopotamia, from the Aegean to the Levantine coast. Within a period of roughly fifty years, almost all of it was gone. The great Mycenaean palace centres of Greece were burned and abandoned. The Hittite Empire collapsed entirely, its capital Hattusa torched. The prosperous cities of the Levantine coast — Ugarit, Megiddo, Hazor, Lachish — were destroyed, some never reoccupied. Egypt survived, but barely, and permanently reduced. The palatial Bronze Age world of the eastern Mediterranean underwent a systems collapse of a severity not seen again in the region until the fall of the Western Roman Empire.

This event, known to archaeologists as the Bronze Age Collapse or the Late Bronze Age Collapse, is one of the most debated episodes in ancient history. The causes remain genuinely contested; the evidence is fragmentary; and the collapse itself was not instantaneous or uniform. But its consequences were so far-reaching that they effectively reset the trajectory of Western civilisation.

## The Evidence from the Ruins

The archaeological signature of the collapse is stark. At the Mycenaean palace of Pylos on the southwestern Peloponnese, the Linear B tablets found in its archive — records of palace administration in the last days before destruction — include requisitions for rowers to be sent to the coast and instructions for border watchers, suggesting that the palace authorities were aware of some threat and attempting to respond. Pylos was burned around 1180 BC and never rebuilt. No Linear B tablets survive from after the collapse.

At Tiryns and Mycenae, there is evidence of destruction and subsequent occupation at a greatly reduced level. The great walls — the 'Cyclopean' limestone fortifications so massive that later Greeks assumed they must have been built by giants — continued to stand, but the palaces were abandoned.

The Hittite capital Hattusa in central Anatolia (near modern Bogazkoy, Turkey) was systematically emptied and burned around 1200–1180 BC. Unusually, there is little evidence of a violent final battle; the city appears to have been deliberately vacated before the burning, suggesting an organised withdrawal. The Hittite Empire disappeared from the historical record entirely. Its cuneiform archives, preserved in the ruins, contain letters referring to famine, ships that were delayed, grain that was needed urgently.

At Ugarit on the Syrian coast (modern Ras Shamra), an archive of clay tablets includes one of the last pieces of correspondence written there: a letter to the King of Alashiya (Cyprus) from the king of Ugarit describing his ships as absent, his infantry and chariotry deployed in Hittite territory, and his coastline attacked by ships at sea. 'Enemies come from ships,' the letter says. It was never sent; the tablets were found still in the kiln when the city was excavated.

## The Sea Peoples

Egyptian records, particularly from the reign of Ramesses III (roughly 1186–1155 BC), describe a confederation of migrating peoples who attacked Egypt from the north and from the sea. The reliefs and inscriptions at Medinet Habu, Ramesses III's mortuary temple on the west bank at Luxor, show naval and land battles against these groups, identified by names such as Peleset, Tjeker, Shekelesh, Denyen and Weshesh. Ramesses claimed to have defeated them decisively in a battle around 1175 BC.

The Sea Peoples have been the dominant explanatory framework for the Bronze Age Collapse in popular history for over a century, but modern scholarship has substantially revised their role. They were real — groups of migrants and raiders whose ethnic and geographic origins remain disputed but who included peoples possibly from the Aegean, Anatolia and Cyprus — but they are increasingly seen as a symptom and partial cause of a wider disruption rather than its primary driver. Some of those displaced groups eventually settled: the Peleset are widely identified with the Philistines of the Levantine coast.

## Multiple Causes, One Outcome

The current scholarly consensus, developed most thoroughly by Eric Cline of George Washington University in his influential 2014 study '1177 BC: The Year Civilization Collapsed', holds that the Bronze Age Collapse was the product of multiple simultaneous stresses acting on a system that had become highly interconnected and therefore highly vulnerable to cascading failure.

The candidates include: prolonged drought, evidenced by paleoclimatological data from oxygen isotopes in sediment cores and pollen records from the Eastern Mediterranean showing a drier period around 1200 BC; earthquakes, which struck multiple sites in the region around this time including Mycenae, Tiryns and Ugarit; social upheaval within palace economies that had concentrated wealth at the top and may have created internal instability; disruption to the long-distance trade networks that supplied bronze-producing civilisations with the tin they could not source locally (tin for bronze came largely from Afghanistan or Anatolia; without it, weapons and tools could not be manufactured); and the movements of peoples, whether as raiders, migrants, or both.

No single factor is sufficient; all plausibly contributed. The collapse was a perfect storm in an over-integrated system that had no redundancy.

## What Survived and What Did Not

Egypt survived, but the New Kingdom never fully recovered its earlier power. The period following the collapse saw a fragmented polity, weakening royal authority, and eventual foreign dynasties. The empire in Nubia and the Levant was largely relinquished. The great temples of Luxor and Karnak remained, but new construction slowed.

The Assyrians in northern Mesopotamia recovered relatively quickly and built the Neo-Assyrian Empire that would dominate the Near East by the early first millennium BC. The Phoenician cities — Tyre, Sidon, Byblos — emerged from the collapse as maritime trading powers that would spread their alphabet and their culture across the Mediterranean.

In Greece, the collapse produced what archaeologists call the Greek Dark Ages, a period of roughly three centuries from around 1100 to 800 BC during which population declined sharply, long-distance trade nearly ceased, literacy disappeared (Linear B died with the palaces, and the Greek alphabet would not be adapted from Phoenician until around the 8th century BC), and monumental architecture was abandoned. When Greece re-emerged into history, it did so as a world of small city-states rather than palace kingdoms.

## The Ruins That Remain

The most accessible physical remains of the Bronze Age world before the collapse are in Greece, Turkey and Egypt. Mycenae, with its Lion Gate and Treasury of Atreus (a tholos tomb of the 14th century BC), is open to visitors in the Argolid; UNESCO inscribed the Mycenaean sites as part of the Archaeological Sites of Mycenae and Tiryns in 1999. Hattusa near Bogazkoy in Turkey, with its gate lions, sphinx gate and extensive mudbrick fortifications, is inscribed by UNESCO and reachable from Ankara. The letters and tablets from Ugarit are distributed among the Louvre, the National Museum in Aleppo (itself damaged in the Syrian conflict), and other collections.

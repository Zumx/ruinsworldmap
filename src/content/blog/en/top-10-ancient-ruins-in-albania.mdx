Albania occupies the western part of the ancient region known as Illyricum, colonised by Greeks from the 7th century BC and subsequently absorbed into the Roman and Byzantine worlds. The country's archaeological landscape is dense and largely under-explored: decades of communist-era isolation meant that many sites received little systematic international attention, and significant excavation work has accelerated only in recent decades. Greek colonies on the coast, Illyrian hillforts in the mountains, Roman towns in the interior, and Early Christian basilicas scattered across the landscape make Albania one of the most rewarding and least crowded destinations for archaeological tourism in the Mediterranean.

## 1. Butrint, Saranda District

Butrint is Albania's most famous archaeological site and its only UNESCO World Heritage listing. Occupied continuously from the Bronze Age through the medieval period, it served as a Greek colony, an Illyrian settlement, a Roman town, a Byzantine ecclesiastical centre, and a Venetian fort. Compressed into a small peninsula at the southern tip of Albania, the site preserves an extraordinary stratigraphic record: a Greek theatre from the 3rd century BC, Roman baths with mosaic floors, a baptistery with some of the finest late antique floor mosaics in the Balkans, a basilica, Venetian tower, and medieval castle. Open year-round; entry fee applies.

## 2. Apollonia, Fier Region

Apollonia was founded by Greek colonists from Corfu and Corinth around 588 BC and grew into a major city with a reputation for its philosophical school — Julius Caesar sent his nephew Octavian (the future Augustus) to study there. The surviving ruins spread across a hillside and include the colonnaded Bouleuterion (council house), a nymphaeum, the remains of the city's walls, a triumphal arch, and the monastery of St Mary built in the 13th century on the ancient acropolis. The associated museum holds finds from the excavations including sculpture and inscriptions.

## 3. Byllis, Fier Region

Byllis was an Illyrian city that became a major urban centre in the Hellenistic period, covering an area of roughly 30 hectares on a plateau above the Vjosa River. Its well-preserved Hellenistic circuit wall, studded with towers and punctuated by monumental gates, is among the most impressive examples of ancient military architecture in Albania. The site also preserves remains of an agora, a theatre, an odeon, and several Early Christian basilicas with mosaic floors. Byllis is relatively unvisited but easily reached from Fier.

## 4. Antigonea, Gjirokastër Region

Antigonea was founded in the late 3rd century BC by the Epirote king Pyrrhus's son Ptolemy, or possibly by the Epirote king himself. Set on a dramatic plateau above the Drino Valley, the site preserves stretches of Hellenistic fortification walls, the grid-plan layout of the town's streets, the foundations of private houses and public buildings, and evidence for commercial activity. The acropolis commands sweeping views over the surrounding mountains. The site is freely accessible and signposted from Gjirokastra.

## 5. Amantia, Vlorë Region

Amantia was an Illyrian city that flourished in the Hellenistic period, located on a hilltop above the modern village of Ploça. Its most impressive surviving feature is a substantial stadium, one of the few Hellenistic stadia preserved in the western Balkans, with seats cut from the bedrock of the hillside. The circuit walls, acropolis buildings, and traces of the urban plan are also visible. The site is accessible by minor road from Vlorë.

## 6. Dimale (Dimal), Berat Region

Dimale was an important Illyrian fortified town, mentioned in connection with Macedonian and Roman campaigns in Illyria. The site, on a steep hill above the Seman River, preserves Hellenistic and Roman walls, towers, and traces of internal structures. It was the scene of Roman military operations during the First Macedonian War in the late 3rd century BC. The site is off the main tourist circuit but accessible with local knowledge.

## 7. Onchesmos (Saranda), Saranda District

The modern coastal town of Saranda sits above the ancient settlement of Onchesmos, a port serving as the gateway to Butrint and the interior. Remains of Late Antique and Byzantine structures, including a large 6th-century synagogue with mosaic floors discovered beneath the modern town, attest to a prosperous and cosmopolitan community. The synagogue mosaics, with their geometric patterns and Jewish symbols, are among the most significant of their type in the region.

## 8. Durres (Dyrrachium), Durrës Region

Durres is a major modern port city, but its ancient predecessor, Dyrrachium — one of the most important harbours on the Adriatic, marking the western end of the Via Egnatia — left substantial remains. A large Roman amphitheatre from the 2nd century AD, capable of seating around 15,000 spectators, is partially excavated beneath the city centre. Early Christian catacombs with painted images, city wall sections, and an archaeological museum with finds from regional sites round out the offering.

## 9. Phoinike (Finiq), Sarandë District

Phoinike was a major city of the ancient region of Epirus Vetus, serving as the main administrative centre of the Chaonians and later a Roman colonia. The city's Hellenistic walls and towers are well preserved, and ongoing Italian-Albanian excavations have revealed an agora complex, houses with mosaic floors, temples, and a substantial Late Antique and early Byzantine occupation layer. The site is actively being excavated and has an associated museum in the nearby village.

## 10. Hadrianopolis, Gjirokastër Region

Hadrianopolis, founded in the 2nd century AD and named for the emperor Hadrian, flourished as a Roman town in the Drino Valley. Excavations have uncovered a theatre, baths, a triumphal arch, and mosaic floors. The site is being developed for tourism and offers an accessible introduction to Roman provincial urbanism in the Albanian interior.

Albania's ancient sites are generally accessible, well signposted, and remarkably uncrowded by Mediterranean standards. Most are open daily; Butrint requires a ticket. The best time to visit is spring or early autumn, when temperatures are moderate and the landscapes lush.

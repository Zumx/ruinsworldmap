Romania's ancient past runs deeper than the Roman conquest that gave the country its name and its language. Before Rome crossed the Danube, the Dacians — a Thracian people — had built a sophisticated civilisation in the Carpathian mountains, complete with massive hilltop sanctuaries, urban centres, and an art tradition of striking originality. Roman Dacia, established after Trajan's campaigns of 101–102 and 105–106 AD, was one of the empire's most mineral-rich provinces, yielding gold and silver that funded monuments across the empire. The Black Sea coast, known to the Greeks as Pontus Euxinus, hosted Greek colonies from the 7th century BC that lasted in some form for over a millennium. Between these two poles — the Carpathian highlands and the Pontic coast — Romania holds ancient ruins that reward determined exploration.

## 1. Sarmizegetusa Regia, Hunedoara County

In the Orăștie mountains of western Transylvania, Sarmizegetusa Regia was the sacred and political capital of the Dacian kingdom under kings Burebista and Decebalus. At its height, around the 1st century BC through the early 2nd century AD, the site comprised terraced sanctuaries with circular and rectangular timber temples, a large limestone-paved sacred precinct, and workshops producing sophisticated metalwork and pottery. The Romans destroyed it comprehensively after the Second Dacian War in 106 AD. What survives today includes the cobbled sacred road, the outlines of the major sanctuaries, and the monumental stone andesite cylinders that once supported the temples. The site is protected as a UNESCO World Heritage component ('Dacian Fortresses of the Orăștie Mountains') and is reached by forest track from the village of Grădiștea de Munte.

## 2. Histria, Constanța County

On a now-landlocked peninsula of the Black Sea coast, Histria was founded by Greek colonists from Miletus around 657 BC, making it the oldest known Greek city on Romanian territory. It functioned continuously as a Greek, then Hellenistic, then Roman city until roughly the 7th century AD, when it was finally abandoned following Avar and Slavic incursions. The excavated remains include a Greek sacred precinct with temples, Roman baths, the late-antique basilica, city walls with towers, and a bishop's palace. The on-site museum holds fine Greek and Roman ceramics, sculpture, and inscriptions. Histria lies about 75 kilometres north of Constanța on the edge of the Razelm lagoon complex; a dirt road connects it to the main highway.

## 3. Adamclisi — Tropaeum Traiani, Constanța County

At Adamclisi on the Dobrogean plateau, a massive Roman monument commemorates Trajan's victory over the Dacians and their Sarmatian allies. The original Tropaeum Traiani, built around 109 AD, was a drum-shaped concrete monument roughly 40 metres in diameter, decorated with fifty-four carved metope panels depicting Roman soldiers and barbarian captives. Replicas of the metopes now clad the reconstructed drum; the originals are in the adjacent museum. Nearby, the Roman town of Tropaeum Traiani (distinct from the trophy monument) preserves a circuit of walls, a Christian basilica, and street blocks. The monument is among the most important Roman memorials outside Italy and is accessible from the modern village of Adamclisi.

## 4. Ulpia Traiana Sarmizegetusa, Hunedoara County

Not to be confused with the Dacian Sarmizegetusa Regia, this Roman city in the Hațeg depression was the capital of Roman Dacia, founded by Trajan shortly after the conquest. At its peak in the 2nd and early 3rd centuries AD, it was the largest city in the province, with an estimated population of around 25,000. The excavated forum complex, including the forum itself, the capitolium, and the amphitheatre — the latter capable of seating several thousand — remains impressive in scale. The site museum holds inscriptions, sculpture, and domestic objects. The city declined after Aurelian withdrew Roman forces from Dacia around 271 AD and was eventually stripped for building material, but the amphitheatre outline and forum precinct are clearly legible on the ground.

## 5. Cetatea Sucidava, Olt County

At modern Corabia on the Danube, the Roman and late-antique fort of Sucidava guarded a crossing point between Dacia and Moesia Inferior. The site is notable for a remarkable secret fountain built within the fort walls — a stone-vaulted underground passage descending to a spring, constructed in late antiquity to supply the garrison during sieges. The fort itself shows occupation from the Dacian period through the Byzantine era, with a 4th-century AD phase associated with Constantine the Great's Danube campaigns. The museum at Corabia holds finds from excavations. Sucidava is one of the more accessible Danubian frontier sites in Romania.

## 6. Porolissum, Sălaj County

Near the village of Moigrad in northwestern Romania, Porolissum was a major Roman legionary fort and associated civilian settlement (vicus/municipium) on the northern frontier of Dacia from the time of Trajan through the Roman withdrawal. The site preserves the outlines of the principia (headquarters building), temples including one to Bel, a customs post, and parts of the town. An amphitheatre, recently excavated and partly consolidated, is one of only a handful in Roman Romania. Porolissum sat at the terminus of the Dacian limes — the frontier road and fortification line — and commanded the Meseș mountain pass. The scenery alone justifies a visit.

## 7. Drobeta (Turnu Severin), Mehedinți County

At the Iron Gates gorge of the Danube, the Roman fort of Drobeta (modern Drobeta-Turnu Severin) was the bridgehead for Trajan's famous bridge across the Danube, designed by the architect Apollodorus of Damascus around 105 AD. The bridge, over a kilometre long, was the longest in the ancient world when built. Only the bridge's massive stone piers, partly submerged, survive in the river. The fort and associated civilian settlement are partly excavated, with foundations visible in the municipal archaeological park alongside the Danube. The Iron Gates Museum in town contextualises the site and holds Roman sculpture and inscriptions.

## 8. Apulum (Alba Iulia), Alba County

Beneath and around the baroque fortress city of Alba Iulia lies Apulum, which was the largest Roman city in Dacia — in fact, two overlapping settlements sharing the name, one a legionary fortress and one a civilian colonia. The Legio XIII Gemina was stationed here throughout the Roman period. Finds from Apulum fill the National Museum of the Union in Alba Iulia, including remarkable Roman gold jewellery, inscriptions, and sculptural pieces. While the modern city has engulfed most of the Roman fabric, dedicated archaeological walks reveal sections of the Roman wall and the outlines of the principia beneath the later fortress. The stratified history of Alba Iulia — Roman, Dacian, medieval — is part of what makes it compelling.

## 9. Callatis (Mangalia), Constanța County

Founded by Greek colonists, probably from Heraclea Pontica, around the 4th century BC, Callatis on the Black Sea coast was one of three major Greek colonies in what is now Romania (alongside Histria and Tomis/Constanța). It survived and adapted through Hellenistic, Roman, and Byzantine periods until at least the 7th century AD. Much of ancient Callatis lies beneath the modern resort town of Mangalia, but excavations have uncovered Greek and Roman tombs with wall paintings, a Hellenistic necropolis, and sections of the ancient city wall. The municipal museum holds a significant collection of Greek pottery, funerary goods, and inscriptions from the site.

## 10. Blidaru Dacian Fortress, Hunedoara County

One of a cluster of Dacian fortifications in the Orăștie mountains that together form the UNESCO-listed Dacian Fortresses complex, Blidaru is particularly striking for its double-walled enclosure using the characteristic Dacian murus dacicus technique — a construction combining a timber framework with stone facing. Built around the 1st century BC and strengthened in the 1st century AD as Rome's threat grew, Blidaru was one of the key defensive nodes protecting the approaches to Sarmizegetusa Regia. The walls stand to several metres in height in places. Reaching it involves a forest hike of around an hour from the nearest road, which keeps visitor numbers low and the atmosphere undiluted.

## Planning Your Visit

Romania's major ancient sites are scattered across considerable distances. The Dacian mountain fortresses of the Orăștie cluster are best reached by car from Deva or Hunedoara; allow a full day for Sarmizegetusa Regia alone, and a separate day for Blidaru and nearby Costești-Cetățuie. The Black Sea sites (Histria, Adamclisi, Callatis) make a logical two-day circuit from Constanța. Alba Iulia and Drobeta-Turnu Severin are along the main west-east highway. Most state sites charge modest admission fees. The best months are May through September, though the mountain sites are particularly attractive in late spring when the beech forest is in leaf.

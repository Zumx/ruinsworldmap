Oman occupies a singular position in the ancient world's trade geography. Its coastline on the Arabian Sea made it a natural intermediary between Mesopotamia, the Indian subcontinent, and East Africa, and the frankincense trees of the Dhofar highlands supplied one of the most sought-after luxury commodities of antiquity. The country's archaeology spans from Neolithic copper-working settlements and the extraordinary Bronze Age tower-tomb complexes of the Hafit and Umm an-Nar periods to the Hellenistic port city of Qalhat, the Ibadhite fortified towns of the interior, and the ancient South Arabian frankincense-trade entrepôt of Sumhuram. Four Omani sites have received UNESCO World Heritage status, reflecting an archaeological significance that many visitors to the country's wadis and deserts are only beginning to discover.

## 1. Bat Tombs and Tower at Bat, Al Dhahirah Region

The archaeological complex at Bat in the Al Dhahirah region, inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1988, preserves the densest concentration of Hafit and Umm an-Nar period tombs anywhere in Arabia. The Hafit tombs, dating to roughly 3000–2600 BCE, are beehive-shaped stone towers built on ridgelines, each originally covering a single or small-group burial. The adjacent Umm an-Nar period settlement and necropolis (roughly 2600–2000 BCE) represents the height of Bronze Age Omani civilisation, when the country exported copper to Mesopotamia on an industrial scale. The World Heritage site encompasses Bat, Al-Khutm, and Al-Ayn, with Al-Ayn's hilltop row of beehive tombs set against the backdrop of Jebel Misht being among the most photogenic prehistoric monuments in Arabia. A modest information panel has been installed; the site is open to self-guided visits.

## 2. Bahla Fort and Oasis, Ad Dakhiliyah

Bahla Fort, inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1987, rises from a palm oasis town in the Hajar mountain foothills and was the seat of the Banu Nebhan tribe from the 12th to the end of the 15th century CE. The fort itself is a massive mud-brick and stone structure whose walls extend for kilometres around the oasis, incorporating the town within a unified defensive system. The main citadel tower is visible from considerable distance. The surrounding falaj irrigation system and the earthen town walls extend the architectural achievement considerably. Bahla underwent major UNESCO-funded restoration in the 1990s and early 2000s after damage from heavy rains. The present structure dates primarily from the medieval Islamic period, but the oasis has been continuously settled since at least the Iron Age, and the surrounding landscape contains Bronze Age tombs.

## 3. Sumhuram (Khor Rori), Dhofar Region

Sumhuram, on a natural harbour at Khor Rori near Salalah in the Dhofar region, was an ancient South Arabian trading port and frankincense entrepôt active from roughly the 3rd century BCE to the 4th century CE. It is associated with the Hadhramaut kingdom and formed part of the Frankincense Trail that carried Dhofar's prized resin overland to Yemen and by sea to the Persian Gulf and beyond. The walled town survives as substantial stone ruins on a rocky promontory above the creek, with visible walls, domestic structures, a Sabaean-inscribed stone gateway, and the remains of a temple. Italian archaeological missions from 1996 onward have excavated and documented the site extensively, recovering South Arabian inscriptions, pottery, and objects attesting to Indian Ocean trade connections. The site is inscribed as part of the UNESCO Frankincense Trail of Oman (2000).

## 4. Al Baleed Archaeological Park, Salalah

Al Baleed, on the Salalah lagoon, was one of the most important medieval Islamic ports in the Indian Ocean world, active from roughly the 11th to the 16th century CE and identified with the ancient city of Zafar (or Dhofar) described by Marco Polo and Ibn Battuta as a major horse and frankincense export centre. The archaeological park preserves the ruins of a substantial walled town with a congregational mosque whose rows of columns and double minaret survive to considerable height, residential and commercial quarters, and a palace complex. Italian and Omani excavations have revealed a layered sequence of occupation and abundant Indian Ocean trade ceramics including Chinese porcelain, Persian stonepaste wares, and East African goods. The adjacent Museum of the Frankincense Land provides excellent contextual narrative. Al Baleed is part of the UNESCO Frankincense Trail listing.

## 5. Qalhat, South al Sharqiyah

Qalhat, on the coast of the Gulf of Oman south of Sur, was the chief medieval port city of Oman from roughly the 10th to the 15th century CE, exporting horses, frankincense, and copper and receiving goods from India, China, and East Africa. Ibn Battuta and Marco Polo both visited and described it as a prosperous commercial city with substantial buildings. The site was devastated by a Portuguese raid in 1508 and never recovered. The ruins today include the remains of the city walls, residential buildings, a Portuguese-era cistern, and most strikingly the Tomb of Bibi Maryam, a 14th-century mausoleum with elaborately glazed tilework that survives in partial but remarkable condition. Qalhat was inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2018. A new visitor centre at the site provides context.

## 6. Salut, Al Dhahirah Region

Salut is a multi-period site in the Wadi Bahla area of the Al Dhahirah interior that has produced evidence of occupation from at least the late Bronze Age (around 1200–300 BCE) through the Iron Age and into the pre-Islamic period. Excavations by the University of Bologna have revealed an impressive mudbrick and stone citadel complex with multiple building phases, granaries, storage rooms, and a collection of inscriptions and ceramics reflecting the emergence of early oasis agriculture in inner Oman. The Iron Age phase (roughly 1100–300 BCE) is particularly well documented, providing evidence for early Omani falaj irrigation systems and the agricultural settlements that sustained interior populations. The site is not heavily developed for tourism but is accessible from the main road.

## 7. Hili Archaeological Park, Al Ain (UAE border context)

While Hili Archaeological Park falls within the emirate of Abu Dhabi near Al Ain, the Umm an-Nar Bronze Age culture it represents is the same civilisation documented at Bat and other Omani sites. The Hili Grand Tomb (around 2600–2000 BCE), with its carved facade panels depicting human and animal figures, is the finest single structure of the Umm an-Nar period surviving anywhere. Its inclusion here reflects the cross-border nature of ancient Omani copper-working culture; Oman-based visitors can easily combine Bat with Hili for a complete picture of the Bronze Age. Hili is part of the UNESCO Al Ain Oasis World Heritage Site (2011).

## 8. Rustaq Fort and Archaeological Area

Rustaq Fort, in the Batinah region south of Muscat, is the largest fort in Oman and has served as a seat of power since at least the 13th century CE, though the present structure dates primarily from the 17th century when Oman resisted Portuguese domination and established the Yaruba Imamate. The fort's four towers, each with a distinct architectural character, and the surrounding falaj system and historic town provide one of the richest concentrations of Islamic-era built heritage in the country. The Rustaq area also contains Bronze Age tombs and Iron Age sites in the adjacent wadi system, connecting the medieval history to the much older occupation of this fertile inland district. The spring at Ain Al Kasfa nearby has been a source of water since prehistoric times.

## 9. Nizwa Fort and Ancient Town

Nizwa, the historic capital of inner Oman, contains a 17th-century fort with an enormous round tower — one of the largest in the Arab world — built by Imam Sultan bin Saif al-Yarubi around 1650 CE. The fort dominates an ancient oasis town whose markets, mosques, and falaj-watered date palm gardens have been a centre of trade and learning since pre-Islamic times. Medieval Nizwa was famous for its scholars and silversmiths, and the traditional souk around the fort preserves craft traditions of considerable antiquity. The falaj systems irrigating the oasis date in their current form to the Islamic period but follow irrigation technologies developed in Iron Age Oman. The Nizwa area also hosts several notable pre-Islamic tombs and rock inscriptions.

## 10. Tanuf Ruins and Aflaj, Al Hamra

The ruined mud-brick village of old Tanuf in the Wadi Tanuf, partly destroyed by British bombing during the Jebel War of 1957, occupies a site with deep pre-modern occupation. The falaj channels feeding the village palms are among the finest surviving examples of the ancient Omani underground irrigation system, sections of which date back well over a thousand years. The nearby old village of Al Hamra preserves a largely intact example of multi-storey Omani domestic architecture from the 17th–18th centuries. The Aflaj of Oman were inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2006; the Falaj Daris at Nizwa and the Falaj Al-Khatmain at Al Ain are the most celebrated individual channels, but the Tanuf system provides one of the most visually accessible introductions to the technology.

Oman is one of the most visitor-friendly countries in the Arabian Peninsula, with a straightforward e-visa system, well-maintained roads, and a government strongly committed to heritage tourism. Muscat is the main gateway; most sites in the Al Dhahirah and Ad Dakhiliyah regions are within a day's drive. Dhofar (Salalah) is best reached by flight. The Khareef (monsoon) season in Dhofar from July to September brings lush greenery and is the best time to visit southern sites. For the interior and Bat, October to March is ideal.

Mongolia occupies a vast sweep of steppe, desert, and mountain landscape that has served as the stage for some of Inner Asia's most consequential civilisations. Long before the Mongol Empire reshaped the medieval world, the region was home to the Xiongnu confederacy, the Türk and Uyghur khaganates, and countless smaller pastoral polities whose material traces endure in stone inscriptions, earthen ramparts, and nomadic burial mounds. The Orkhon Valley, threading through the Khangai mountains, became the geographic heartland of successive steppe empires, and its archaeological density is unmatched anywhere on the Central Asian grasslands. From the ruined capital of the Mongol khans to Turkic memorial complexes carved with the oldest known Turkic script, Mongolia rewards travellers willing to venture beyond Ulaanbaatar.

## 1. Karakorum (Kharkhorin), Övörkhangai Province

Karakorum was the imperial capital of the Mongol Empire, established by Ögedei Khan around 1235 on the western bank of the Orkhon River. At its height in the mid-13th century it housed craftsmen, merchants, and envoys from across Eurasia, and the Flemish friar William of Rubruck left a vivid account of its palace, temples, and silver fountain. The city was abandoned after Kublai Khan shifted the capital to Khanbaliq (Beijing) in 1264. Today little of the urban fabric remains above ground — the great palace of Tumen Amugulang survives only as a platform — but ongoing Mongolian-German excavations have revealed streets, workshops, and Buddhist temples. The adjacent Erdene Zuu monastery, built partly from Karakorum's own stones in 1586, provides the most immediate architectural connection to the site.

## 2. Erdene Zuu Monastery, Kharkhorin

Though technically a 16th-century construction rather than an ancient ruin, Erdene Zuu is archaeologically inseparable from Karakorum. Built by Abtai Sain Khan following the Mongolian acceptance of Tibetan Buddhism, the complex is enclosed by a white wall studded with 108 stupas — a number sacred in Buddhist cosmology. Several of the original temples survive from the late 16th and 17th centuries, making it one of Mongolia's oldest standing religious complexes. The monastery was heavily damaged under Soviet rule in the 1930s, with most of its temples demolished, but three principal temples have been preserved and house fine collections of thangkas, sculptures, and ceremonial objects. It is a UNESCO World Heritage component as part of the Orkhon Valley Cultural Landscape, inscribed in 2004.

## 3. Orkhon Valley Cultural Landscape

The Orkhon Valley World Heritage Site encompasses roughly 121,000 hectares along the Orkhon River and represents over two millennia of continuous human settlement on the steppe. Key archaeological elements include the ruins of Karakorum, the Uyghur capital of Khar Balgas, the Türk memorial complexes at Khöshöö Tsaidam, and hundreds of burial mounds, deer stones, and petroglyphs spread across the valley floor and surrounding hills. The landscape itself — a combination of river floodplain, dry steppe, and volcanic uplands — was the ecological engine that sustained successive nomadic polities. Systematic survey work has identified dozens of discrete sites. Visiting the full valley requires a vehicle and some days, but the core area around Kharkhorin is accessible on day trips from Ulaanbaatar.

## 4. Khar Balgas (Ordu Baliq), Övörkhangai Province

Khar Balgas, meaning "black city" in Mongolian, was the capital of the Uyghur Khaganate from around 745 to 840 CE, when it was sacked by the Yenisei Kirghiz. Known in Uyghur sources as Ordu Baliq ("the palace city"), it stood on the eastern bank of the Orkhon River roughly 27 kilometres north of Karakorum. The rectangular mud-brick enclosure, measuring approximately 1,500 by 2,000 metres, is still clearly visible, its walls rising in places to several metres. A central citadel mound dominates the interior. Chinese historical sources describe it as a cosmopolitan city with Manichaean temples, markets, and an outer suburban zone. The site has never been fully excavated but has been surveyed by joint Mongolian and international teams.

## 5. Bilge Khagan and Kül Tigin Memorial Complexes, Khöshöö Tsaidam

On the eastern fringe of the Orkhon Valley stand two Türk memorial complexes dating to the early 8th century CE — among the most important monuments of early Turkic civilisation anywhere. The complex of Kül Tigin (died 731 CE), younger brother of Bilge Khagan, includes a ceremonial temple with a sculpture of a seated figure, a large turtle-stone pedestal, and the famous Orkhon inscriptions. These bilingual texts in Old Turkic runic script and Chinese are the earliest surviving Turkic writing and recount the history of the Second Türk Khaganate. The adjacent Bilge Khagan complex is structurally similar. Both were excavated and partly restored in the early 20th century by a Mongolian-Russian expedition. A small museum at the site houses replica inscriptions and finds.

## 6. Tsogtiin Tsagaan Balgas, Dundgovi Province

In the Gobi steppe of central Mongolia, the ruins of Tsogtiin Tsagaan Balgas represent a Mongolian princely palace complex from the early 17th century, built by Prince Tsogt Taij. The whitewashed mud-brick walls enclosing a rectangular compound survive to several metres in height, and the interior contained a Tibetan-style temple whose ruins still carry traces of painted plaster. Tsogt Taij was a notable poet and military figure who opposed the expansion of the Khalkha Mongolian Buddhist establishment; his complex reflects the transitional period between steppe nomadism and sedentary Buddhist culture. The site is relatively little visited but accessible from the road between Ulaanbaatar and Dalanzadgad.

## 7. Deer Stones of Uushigiin Uvur, Mörön

Deer stones are tall, narrow standing stones carved with stylised running deer, weapons, and geometric symbols, erected across the Eurasian steppe from roughly 1200 to 700 BCE by Bronze Age pastoral peoples. The most impressive concentration in Mongolia stands at Uushigiin Uvur near Mörön in Khövsgöl Province, where around 14 carved stones survive in a field alongside burial mounds (khirigsuurs). The deer depicted on these monuments are shown with elongated necks and antlers rendered as vegetal branches — a style found from Mongolia westward to the Black Sea. Their precise function remains debated; they likely marked elite burials or served as ritual focal points. The Uushigiin Uvur group is exceptionally well preserved and is among the most visited prehistoric sites in Mongolia.

## 8. Khitan Ruins at Bars Khot, Dornod Province

In the eastern Mongolian steppe, the ruins of Bars Khot preserve the remains of two successive walled cities associated with the Khitan Liao dynasty, which controlled much of Inner Asia from the late 9th to the early 12th century CE. The sites — known as Bars Khot I and II — are defined by substantial earthen ramparts enclosing areas of several square kilometres. Bars Khot I dates to the early Khitan period, while Bars Khot II, with a more regular grid plan influenced by Chinese urbanism, belongs to the mature Liao state. Survey and excavation have recovered roof tiles, ceramics, iron tools, and evidence of Chinese-style administrative buildings. The remoteness of Dornod Province means the site receives few visitors.

## 9. Khustain Nuruu Bronze Age Burials

The Khustain Nuruu National Park, best known for its reintroduced Przewalski's horses, also contains a significant concentration of Bronze Age and Early Iron Age archaeological features. Khirigsuur burial mounds — circular stone enclosures containing central burial chambers — dot the landscape alongside deer stones and occasional Bronze Age habitation scatters. One group near the park centre has been partially excavated, revealing inhumations with bronze ornaments, pottery, and animal bones. The burials are attributed to the Bronze Age pastoral cultures of roughly 1500 to 700 BCE. The park is around 100 kilometres west of Ulaanbaatar and is visited primarily for its wildlife, but the archaeology adds a compelling dimension to any trip.

## 10. Tsagaan Suvarga Geological and Archaeological Zone, Dundgovi

Tsagaan Suvarga in the Gobi Desert is primarily known for its dramatic eroded cliffs of white and ochre sedimentary rock, but the surrounding area has yielded Paleolithic and Bronze Age finds, and the cliff faces carry petroglyphs attributed to ancient pastoral peoples. The images include hunting scenes, animals, and human figures. Geologically the site represents a Paleozoic sea bed uplifted and eroded over millions of years, and finds of ancient marine fossils in the same landscape underscored the region's deep time. The petroglyphs are not formally managed or well documented compared to sites further north, but the combination of geological spectacle and prehistoric rock art makes the zone worth including on any southern Mongolia itinerary.

Mongolia's ancient sites are spread across enormous distances and mostly lack the infrastructure of well-developed tourist destinations. The best time to visit is between May and September, when roads are passable and weather is manageable; the Gobi sites can be brutal in midsummer heat. Ulaanbaatar is the gateway for all travel, and a combination of domestic flights (to Mörön, Dalanzadgad, or Choibalsan) and 4WD vehicles covers most destinations.

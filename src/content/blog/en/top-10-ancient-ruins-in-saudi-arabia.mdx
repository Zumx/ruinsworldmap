Saudi Arabia is emerging as one of the most exciting archaeological destinations in the world, and with good reason: the Arabian Peninsula was not the empty desert crossroads of popular imagination but a land of urban Nabataean and Lihyanite kingdoms, ancient incense-trade cities, Neolithic rock art, and the cradle of a major world religion. For decades the country was largely closed to international tourism, leaving its heritage sites known primarily to specialists. Since the Vision 2030 opening of the country to visitors, sites that once required a special research permit are now accessible to any traveller. Hegra, the southern twin of Petra, is the most immediately spectacular revelation, but the Al-Ula valley, the buried city of Qaryat al-Faw, and the rock art of Jubbah represent a depth of heritage that few visitors expect.

## 1. Hegra (Mada'in Salih), Al-Ula Region

Hegra, the ancient Nabataean city known in classical sources as Egra or Haegra, is Saudi Arabia's first UNESCO World Heritage Site (inscribed 2008) and contains the best-preserved and most densely concentrated Nabataean funerary architecture outside Petra. The Nabataean kingdom, centred on Petra in modern Jordan, controlled the frankincense and spice trade routes from the 4th century BCE onward, and Hegra served as its southern commercial hub. The site preserves 111 decorated rock-cut tomb facades carved from dramatic sandstone inselbergs across the valley floor, dating from roughly the 1st century BCE to the 1st century CE. The facade inscriptions in Nabataean Aramaic name the tomb owners, their families, and the penalties for unauthorised burial — creating a uniquely personal ancient archive. The surrounding landscape of sculpted sandstone massifs and dry wadi channels adds considerable visual drama.

## 2. Dadan and Lihyanite Ruins, Al-Ula

The ancient city of Dadan (also called Dedan in Hebrew scriptures) preceded Hegra in the Al-Ula valley by several centuries and was the capital of the Dadanite and subsequently Lihyanite kingdoms, active from roughly the 9th to the 4th centuries BCE. Dadan controlled the incense trade route through the Hejaz and was a significant urban centre in its own right. The archaeological remains include the outlines of mudbrick urban structures, rock-cut tombs, a Lihyanite temple, and dozens of inscriptions in the Dadanite script — a north Arabian alphabetic writing system predating Nabataean. The lion tombs, carved from a sandstone cliff face nearby, are monumental funerary sculptures of extraordinary quality. Recent Saudi-European excavations have dramatically expanded knowledge of Dadan's urban layout. The site is managed as part of the broader Al-Ula heritage zone.

## 3. Diriyah, Near Riyadh

Diriyah, in the Wadi Hanifah northwest of Riyadh, was the capital of the first Saudi state (1744–1818 CE) and the ancestral home of the Al Saud dynasty. The mudbrick At-Turaif district, inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2010, preserves the palace complexes and residential quarters of the 18th-century emirate in exceptional condition, with Najdi mudbrick architecture displaying the distinctive triangular crenellations and carved plasterwork that characterise the central Arabian building tradition. The site is currently undergoing major restoration and tourism development as one of Saudi Arabia's flagship heritage projects. While the 18th-century date falls outside the strictly ancient period, Diriyah sits within a wadi containing archaeological evidence of occupation stretching back several millennia, and the site's transformation into a heritage destination makes it the most accessible of Saudi Arabia's major historic complexes.

## 4. Qaryat al-Faw, Northern Najran Region

Qaryat al-Faw is the most extensively excavated pre-Islamic city in the interior of Arabia, located at the edge of the Rub' al-Khali desert in northern Najran. Active from roughly the 4th century BCE to the 4th century CE, the city was the capital of the Kingdom of Qana and a major node on the overland frankincense route from southern Arabia to the Persian Gulf and Mediterranean markets. Excavations by King Saud University from the 1970s onward have revealed a grid-planned city with a market building, palace, temple, and residential quarters, along with an extraordinary collection of bronze statues, alabaster vessels, and gold objects in a distinctly South Arabian artistic tradition. Thousands of inscriptions in the Musnad script have been recovered. The site is remote and was for many years accessible only on organised university tours; it is now increasingly integrated into tourism infrastructure.

## 5. Jubbah Rock Art, Ha'il Region

Jubbah, in the Ha'il region of northern Saudi Arabia, is one of the most important concentrations of prehistoric rock art in Arabia, inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2015 as part of the Hail Rock Art complex. The carvings and engravings on the sandstone outcrops at Jubbah depict humans, cattle, ibex, camels, ostriches, and hunting and herding scenes over a time range spanning from roughly 7000 BCE — when the Arabian interior was wetter and supported grassland fauna — through the Bronze Age and into the historical period. The transition from Neolithic hunter-gatherer imagery to herder and later camel-pastoral imagery provides a visual chronicle of climate change and cultural adaptation. The Jubbah rock art includes some of the largest human figures in Arabian rock art and several panels with narrative scenes of unusual complexity.

## 6. Tayma, Tabuk Region

Tayma, in the northern Hejaz, was a major oasis city from at least the 2nd millennium BCE, mentioned in Assyrian records from the 8th century BCE and famous as the place of exile chosen by the Babylonian king Nabonidus, who resided there for around ten years in the 6th century BCE while his son Belshazzar governed Babylon. The Haddaj Well, one of the oldest and largest wells in Arabia, has been used since antiquity and is still visible. Epigraphic finds at Tayma include Aramaic, North Arabian, and cuneiform inscriptions, making it one of the most linguistically diverse ancient sites in the peninsula. A carved stone stele depicting a priest was found here in 1880 and is now in the Louvre. Saudi and German archaeological teams have been excavating the site intensively since the early 2000s.

## 7. Madain Saleh North Area and Jabal Ithlib

Within the broader Hegra archaeological zone, the Jabal Ithlib sandstone outcrop at the northern margin of the Nabataean settlement area preserves a diwan (community gathering hall) carved into the rock face, along with a cultic niche with a carved Nabataean eagle and the remains of a water channel system. The Jabal Ithlib area was a religious district separate from the main funerary zone, and the diwan may have served as a place for banquets associated with mortuary cults. Several rock inscriptions in the vicinity document Nabataean religious activity and the names of deities worshipped at the site. The combination of the diwan, cultic niches, and rock inscriptions makes the Jabal Ithlib area a significant complement to the main tomb facades.

## 8. Al-Hijr Caravanserai Route Stations

The ancient routes crossing the Hejaz between Petra and the Yemen-based kingdoms left a trail of way-stations, water cisterns, and caravan rest-points that are increasingly being documented and mapped. Several of these stations, incorporating carved water management features and inscribed stones, are visible along the tracks connecting Al-Ula to Khaybar and Tayma. The caravan infrastructure represents the economic backbone of the Nabataean trading system, and the individual stations are archaeological sites in their own right, even where no substantial structures survive. Khaybar, north of Medina, itself contains extensive evidence of pre-Islamic settlement and Jewish occupation documented in both archaeological and historical records, though access to the Medina region remains restricted to non-Muslims.

## 9. Najran Old Town (Al-Ukhdud), Najran Region

The Al-Ukhdud archaeological site in Najran preserves the remains of an ancient city associated with the 'People of the Ditch' mentioned in the Quran (Sura al-Buruj), traditionally linked to the Christian martyrdom events of 523 CE under the Himyarite king Yusuf Asar Yath'ar (Dhu Nuwas). The site shows occupation from several centuries BCE and contains a palace complex, a Christian church, and dense residential structures, along with South Arabian inscriptions documenting the historical events. Excavations by the Saudi Heritage Authority have revealed a stratified sequence from the pre-Islamic to early Islamic periods. The site is adjacent to the modern city of Najran and has a small museum. Najran's broader archaeological landscape includes Neolithic sites and ancient agricultural terracing.

## 10. Hima Rock Art, Najran Region

The Hima rock art site complex near Najran, inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2021 alongside Jubbah as part of the Hail and Najran sites, contains thousands of rock engravings and inscriptions spanning roughly 7,000 years from the Neolithic through the Islamic period. The images depict cattle, ibex, ostriches, camels, horses, and human figures, and the inscriptions include ancient South Arabian, Thamudic, Minaic, and early Islamic texts, making Hima one of the most important epigraphic sites in the Arabian Peninsula. Several panels show the arrival of the dromedary camel in Arabia, which can be traced through the iconographic record to around 1200–1000 BCE. The site sits on the ancient incense route and the inscriptions record the passage of travellers over millennia.

Saudi Arabia has transformed its tourism infrastructure with remarkable speed. Visitors enter on an e-visa or a tourist visa available on arrival for many nationalities. The Al-Ula region — covering Hegra, Dadan, and the rock art — has a dedicated regional authority, luxury accommodation options, and guided tour operators. Internal flights connect Riyadh, Jeddah, and Ha'il to regional airports serving the major sites. The best visiting season is October to April, avoiding the summer heat; Al-Ula's winter temperatures are mild and the light on the sandstone tombs in the early morning is extraordinary.

The Indus Valley Civilisation — also known as the Harappan Civilisation after its type site — was the largest of the three great early Bronze Age civilisations, extending from the Himalayas to the Arabian Sea and from Balochistan east into what is now northwestern India, at its peak around 2600 to 1900 BC. Its cities were planned with a rigour that contemporary Mesopotamian and Egyptian cities did not match: fired brick construction, covered drainage systems, standard weights and measures, and street grids oriented to the cardinal directions. At its height the civilisation's population may have reached five million — a substantial fraction of the world's people. Yet for all its sophistication, the Indus script remains undeciphered, the rulers are unknown, and the causes of the civilisation's decline around 1900 BC are still debated. This archaeological eloquence and interpretive silence make the Indus Valley one of the most intellectually gripping archaeological zones in the world.

## 1. Mohenjo-daro, Sindh, Pakistan

Mohenjo-daro — 'Mound of the Dead' in Sindhi — is the most extensively excavated of all Harappan cities and the most visited archaeological site in Pakistan. Occupied primarily from around 2500 to 1900 BC, it preserves a planned urban layout across two main areas: the Citadel Mound, elevated on a mud-brick platform and housing the famous Great Bath, a granary, and an assembly hall; and the Lower Town, a residential and commercial zone laid out on a grid of main streets and lanes with fired brick houses incorporating bathrooms and connected to a covered brick drain system of remarkable sophistication. The Great Bath — 12 metres by 7 metres, lined with bitumen-sealed fired brick — may have served ritual purification functions. The National Museum in Karachi holds key finds, including the bronze 'Dancing Girl' figurine and the carved steatite 'Priest-King' bust. Mohenjo-daro is a UNESCO World Heritage Site, inscribed in 1980.

## 2. Harappa, Punjab, Pakistan

Harappa, on the Ravi river in Pakistani Punjab, gives the civilisation its alternative name. Excavated first by Alexander Cunningham in the 1870s and systematically by the Archaeological Survey of India from the 1920s onward, Harappa presents a less visually coherent ruin than Mohenjo-daro — significant damage was done in the 19th century when fired bricks were plundered for railway ballast — but its archaeological significance is fundamental. The site preserves the earlier phases of Harappan urban development and the transitional late Harappan levels, and finds from Harappa include the greatest assemblage of Indus seals anywhere, elaborate fired-clay figurines, and the earliest evidence of the Indus script. The Harappa Archaeological Museum on site displays material from excavations across many decades. The site is accessible from Sahiwal by road.

## 3. Dholavira, Kutch, India

Dholavira, on the Khadir island in the Rann of Kutch in Gujarat, is the best-preserved and perhaps the most visually dramatic of all Harappan cities on Indian territory. The site's layout is more legible than Mohenjo-daro because it was never heavily built over; the three-part division into Citadel, Middle Town, and Lower Town is clear from the ground. The city's most remarkable feature is its water management infrastructure: a series of reservoirs carved from the rock and lined with dressed stone, fed by catchment channels from the surrounding plain, that gave the city storage for the dry seasons of the Rann. A unique inscription — ten large Indus script signs carved in wood or inlaid material and displayed on the North Gate — is perhaps the largest Indus script text known, though its meaning remains unknown. UNESCO inscribed Dholavira in 2021. The site is remote but accessible by road from Bhuj.

## 4. Lothal, Gujarat, India

Lothal, in the Gulf of Khambhat region of Gujarat, is the most extensively excavated Harappan port city, occupied from around 2400 to 1900 BC. The defining feature of Lothal is a massive fired-brick structure along the eastern edge of the settlement, interpreted by the excavator S.R. Rao as a tidal dock basin for loading and unloading sea-going vessels — a still-debated interpretation, but one that draws attention to Lothal's role in the Harappan maritime trade network that reached the Persian Gulf and Mesopotamia. The bead workshop at Lothal produced carnelian, faience, and shell ornaments; a warehouse for storing trade goods stood adjacent to the dock. The Archaeological Museum at the site houses finds from the excavation including jewellery, terracotta figurines, and a gaming board. Lothal is reachable by road from Ahmedabad.

## 5. Rakhigarhi, Haryana, India

Rakhigarhi in Haryana state is the largest known Harappan site by area, covering roughly 350 hectares — larger than either Mohenjo-daro or Harappa — though it is far less excavated than either. The site has been known since the early 20th century but large-scale systematic excavation has only been conducted since the 1990s. Recent seasons have uncovered a burial ground with well-preserved skeletons; ancient DNA analysis published in 2019 on Rakhigarhi skeletal material contributed to major debates about the population history of South Asia and the relationship between Harappan and later Indo-Aryan populations. The site is largely unexcavated and unmanaged for public access; a site museum has been proposed. Rakhigarhi is about 150 kilometres north of Delhi in Hisar district.

## 6. Kalibangan, Rajasthan, India

Kalibangan on the Ghaggar river (the ancient Sarasvati of Vedic tradition) in Rajasthan is important for two distinct reasons: it preserves a pre-Harappan (Early Harappan) settlement beneath the mature Harappan city, providing stratigraphic evidence for the transition from the Kot Dijian cultural phase to the mature Harappan, and it documents the oldest known ploughed field in the world — a grid of furrows visible in the Early Harappan levels, suggesting sophisticated agricultural planning. The mature Harappan city at Kalibangan is divided into a Citadel and a Lower Town, consistent with the standard Harappan urban form. A series of raised platforms on the Citadel may represent fire altars, evidence that has informed (and complicated) debates about the religious practices of Harappan society. The site is accessible by road from Hanumangarh.

## 7. Mehrgarh, Balochistan, Pakistan

Mehrgarh, on the Kacchi plain of Balochistan at the foot of the Bolan Pass, is the most important prehistoric site in the wider Indus Valley region for understanding the origins of farming and settled life in South Asia. Occupied from around 7000 BC through roughly 2500 BC — when the site was apparently abandoned as the Harappan cities to the east were reaching their peak — Mehrgarh preserves evidence of the transition from mobile hunter-gatherer life to permanent agricultural settlement in this region. Early levels contain mudbrick rooms for grain storage, domesticated cattle and sheep, and the earliest known use of cotton in South Asia. Crucially, Mehrgarh also documents the earliest evidence of dentistry from the Neolithic levels — drilled molars with observable decay. The site is excavated by a Franco-Pakistani team; it is remote and requires organised access.

## 8. Ganweriwala, Punjab, Pakistan

Ganweriwala, on the bed of the dried Hakra river in Cholistan desert in southern Punjab, is one of the five largest known Harappan cities, comparable in scale to Mohenjo-daro and Harappa, but almost entirely unexcavated. The site was identified through survey by M.R. Mughal in the 1970s; its buried remains are detectable through surface pottery scatters and mound topography across several square kilometres. Ganweriwala sits in one of the most archaeologically dense stretches of the Harappan world — the Cholistan desert contains over 400 known Harappan sites along the ancient Hakra channel — but the remote desert environment and limited archaeological resources have left it unstudied. It represents the scale of what the Indus civilisation achieved in a landscape that is now almost entirely arid.

## 9. Banawali, Haryana, India

Banawali in Fatehabad district of Haryana is a well-stratified Harappan site with clear Early Harappan and mature Harappan levels and evidence of a planned street system comparable to other major Harappan cities. Excavations by the Archaeological Survey of India from 1974 to 1977 revealed a particularly well-organised Lower Town with a plano-convex fired brick construction that differs slightly from the standard Harappan form, suggesting regional variation in building practice. The site has produced a good assemblage of Harappan pottery, terracotta figurines, and ornaments. A reconstructed bull figurine from Banawali — representing the humped zebu, a common Harappan motif — is among the most reproduced objects in the iconography of the civilisation. Banawali is accessible by road from Fatehabad town.

## 10. Surkotada, Gujarat, India

Surkotada in the Kutch district of Gujarat is a smaller Harappan fortified settlement excavated by J.P. Joshi in the early 1970s. Despite its modest size — roughly 1.4 hectares of enclosed settlement — Surkotada has produced evidence of exceptional significance: horse bones from levels dated around 2000 BC have been argued to represent the earliest domesticated horses in South Asia, though this identification remains debated by specialists. The site also preserves a well-stratified occupation sequence of the late Harappan and post-Harappan transition period, making it important for understanding the civilisation's decline phase. The fortified wall of the site is partly traceable. Surkotada is located in the Kutch district and is reachable by road from Bhuj, near Dholavira.

The Indus Valley sites present practical challenges that vary significantly across India and Pakistan. In Pakistan, Mohenjo-daro is accessible year-round but the climate is most tolerable from October to March; the site has an on-site guest house. Harappa is similarly accessible. In India, Dholavira (Gujarat) and Lothal are the most visitor-friendly sites with site museums and interpretive infrastructure. The best collections of Harappan material are in the National Museum, New Delhi, the National Museum of Pakistan in Karachi, and the archaeological site museums at Mohenjo-daro and Harappa itself.

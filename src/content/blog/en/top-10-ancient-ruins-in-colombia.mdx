Colombia's pre-Columbian civilizations occupied an enormously varied landscape — Caribbean coast, Andean valleys, tropical highlands, and Pacific lowlands — and developed in relative isolation from the better-known Aztec and Inca empires to the north and south. The result is an archaeological heritage that surprises many visitors with its originality: a highland city of the dead carved into volcanic tufa, a mountaintop urban complex hidden by jungle for centuries, and a highland plateau covered with monumental stone statuary unlike anything found elsewhere in the Americas. Colombia holds two UNESCO World Heritage Sites for archaeology, and both reward serious study. [Open the map](/map) to explore pre-Columbian Colombia.

## 1. Ciudad Perdida (Teyuna), Sierra Nevada de Santa Marta

Ciudad Perdida ('Lost City') is a pre-Columbian urban centre built by the Tairona people on the slopes of the Sierra Nevada de Santa Marta, probably between the 9th and 11th centuries AD. Rediscovered by grave robbers in 1972 and subsequently excavated by Colombian archaeologists, the site consists of about 169 terraces cut into the steep mountain slopes and connected by a network of stone staircases and paved paths. At the top of the complex, a central plaza with circular stone platforms served as the main ceremonial area. The city may have housed several thousand people and functioned as the political and ceremonial capital of the Tairona region. Access requires a four- to six-day guided trek through jungle; no roads reach the site. The Kogi, Arhuaco, and Wiwa indigenous communities consider it sacred.

## 2. San Agustín, Huila Department

San Agustín is the largest concentration of pre-Columbian funerary monuments and statuary in the Americas. The culture responsible — known simply as the San Agustín culture — flourished in the upper Magdalena River valley from roughly 1 to 900 AD, though some activity extends to around 1500 AD. Over 500 large stone statues have been found in the area, carved in a range of styles from naturalistic human figures to fantastical beings with jaguar fangs, and placed as guardians over burial mounds. The largest figures stand up to 4 metres tall. Dozens of burial mounds have been excavated, revealing chamber tombs with painted interior walls and rich offerings of gold, ceramics, and stone objects. The site is spread across a landscape of agricultural terraces and river canyons and is best visited over two or more days. UNESCO World Heritage Site.

## 3. Tierradentro, Cauca Department

Tierradentro preserves an extraordinary series of underground burial chambers — hypogea — carved into volcanic tufa hillsides by an unnamed pre-Columbian culture that flourished here from roughly 300 to 900 AD. The chambers are reached by spiral staircases cut into the hillside and decorated with painted geometric designs in red, black, and white — columns with serpent motifs, anthropomorphic faces, and geometric bands on walls and ceilings. The paintings represent the most elaborate pre-Columbian underground funerary decoration in South America. More than a hundred painted hypogea have been documented, along with hundreds of smaller undecorated chambers. The site also includes a separate area with monolithic stone statuary related to the San Agustín tradition to the south. UNESCO World Heritage Site, jointly designated with San Agustín.

## 4. El Infiernito, Boyacá Department

El Infiernito ('Little Hell') near Villa de Leyva in the Boyacá highlands preserves the most visible remains of the Muisca people, who dominated the central Andean highlands of Colombia from around 600 to 1600 AD and were encountered by Spanish conquistadors in the 1530s. The site consists of two rows of upright stone columns, some shaped as phalluses (which gave rise to the colonial-era name), which appear to function as a solar calendar marking the solstices and equinoxes by the alignment of shadows. Radiocarbon dating suggests the columns were placed between roughly 900 and 1000 AD. The site lies at an altitude of about 2,200 metres and is open to visitors with a local guide.

## 5. Parque Arqueológico de Facatativá, Cundinamarca

The Piedras de Tunja, in the Facatativá archaeological park near Bogotá, preserve a remarkable collection of pre-Columbian rock paintings — red, black, and yellow figures of humans, animals, and geometric forms — made by cultures that occupied the Bogotá plateau from at least 3000 BC. The paintings are located on large erratic boulders in a natural landscape of dense vegetation, and the combination of landscape setting and prehistoric art gives the site an unusual atmosphere. Several hundred individual paintings have been identified. The site is easily accessible from Bogotá by public transport.

## 6. Monquirá, Boyacá Department

Monquirá, also in the Villa de Leyva area of Boyacá, preserves what is interpreted as a Muisca astronomical observatory site. The landscape here was used for ritual and calendrical purposes by Muisca priests (zipa and zaque), and the alignment of natural and carved stone elements with solar events suggests a sophisticated astronomical tradition. The broader Villa de Leyva area — a high, arid plateau at around 2,200 metres — contains multiple concentrations of pre-Columbian activity across a landscape that has changed relatively little since colonial times.

## 7. Tayrona Park Archaeological Sites, Magdalena Department

The Tayrona National Natural Park on the Caribbean coast of Colombia preserves, within its protected boundaries, numerous Tairona period sites smaller than Ciudad Perdida. Stone terraces, platforms, and drainage channels are found throughout the park's jungle-covered hills, and the paved Tairona road system connecting coastal lowlands to highland settlements is visible in several places. The context of living tropical forest, Caribbean shoreline, and archaeological remains makes Tayrona one of Colombia's most visually striking destinations for both nature and history.

## 8. Alto de los Ídolos, Huila Department

Alto de los Ídolos, near Isnos in the Huila department and within the broader San Agustín UNESCO zone, is one of the most atmospheric of the individual archaeological parks that make up the San Agustín complex. The site preserves a series of burial mounds each covered by a stone-lined burial chamber roofed with a stone slab and guarded by a monolithic statue. The funerary monuments here are grouped on a hilltop with views across river valleys, and the scale and variety of the statuary — some figures painted in red and white, others purely sculpted — give a vivid sense of the ritual investment made by this culture in its mortuary landscape.

## 9. Chorro de Quevedo, Bogotá

The historic centre of Bogotá, particularly the plaza known as Chorro de Quevedo in the La Candelaria neighbourhood, marks the traditional founding site of the Spanish colonial city in 1538 — but below the colonial layers lie the remains of a Muisca settlement that the conquistadors encountered. Archaeological investigations in central Bogotá have revealed stratified deposits from the Muisca period underneath colonial and later structures, and the Museo del Oro (Gold Museum) in central Bogotá presents the archaeological context of Colombia's extraordinary pre-Columbian goldwork traditions in exemplary fashion. The museum's collection of Muisca, Tairona, Calima, and Quimbaya goldwork is the finest in the world.

## 10. La Gaitana Waterfall Area, Huila Department

The upper Magdalena valley, which contains San Agustín and Tierradentro, preserves additional concentrations of pre-Columbian activity throughout its tributary valleys. Rock carvings, small burial mounds, and terraced hillsides indicate a densely settled agricultural landscape in the periods before and after the florescence of the San Agustín monumental tradition. Some of these sites are on private land and accessible with the permission of local farmers — a reminder that Colombia's pre-Columbian heritage extends far beyond the mapped and excavated sites.

The best time to visit Colombian archaeological sites is during the dry seasons: December to February and July to August. San Agustín and Tierradentro are in mountain areas with changeable weather; both have adequate accommodation in nearby towns. The trek to Ciudad Perdida requires good physical condition and is operated by licensed tour operators from Santa Marta.

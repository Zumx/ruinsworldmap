The high desert plateaus, canyon systems, and volcanic mesas of the American Southwest hold one of the most remarkable concentrations of pre-Columbian architecture in North America. The peoples who built here — known to archaeologists primarily as Ancestral Puebloans (formerly called Anasazi), Hohokam, Sinagua, and Mogollon — developed sophisticated building traditions over many centuries, culminating in great houses, cliff dwellings, and kiva-centred village complexes that rival the architectural achievements of any preindustrial society. The Ancestral Puebloans in particular left an indelible mark across the Colorado Plateau from roughly 700 to 1300 AD, before many of their communities shifted south and east, likely in response to prolonged drought, to become the ancestors of the Hopi, Zuni, and Rio Grande Puebloan peoples who still live in the region. Their descendants maintain living cultural connections to these abandoned sites, which makes the Southwest's ancient ruins a landscape of ongoing Indigenous heritage, not merely archaeology.

## 1. Mesa Verde, Colorado

Set on a high forested mesa in southwestern Colorado, Mesa Verde National Park preserves the most extensive collection of cliff dwellings in North America. The Ancestral Puebloans began building on the mesa top from around the 7th century AD, but the remarkable cliff palaces for which the park is best known were constructed during the late occupation period, roughly 1190 to 1270 AD, in the sheltered alcoves of the canyon walls. Cliff Palace, the largest cliff dwelling in North America, contains around 150 rooms and 23 kivas in a single sandstone alcove. Spruce Tree House, Long House, and Balcony House are also accessible on ranger-guided tours. Why the inhabitants moved from the mesa tops into the cliffs remains debated; abandonment occurred around 1300 AD. Mesa Verde has been a UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1978. Book cliff dwelling tours in advance during summer.

## 2. Chaco Canyon, New Mexico

In a remote canyon on the San Juan Basin of northwestern New Mexico, Chaco Culture National Historical Park contains the ruins of the most ambitious architecture in the pre-Columbian Southwest. Between roughly 850 and 1150 AD, the Chacoans built twelve 'great houses' — multi-storey masonry structures with hundreds of rooms, great kivas, and sophisticated astronomical alignments. Pueblo Bonito, the largest, contains over 600 rooms rising four to five storeys in a D-shaped plan; its walls were built with refined core-and-veneer masonry of considerable beauty. An extensive road system radiating from Chaco connected it to outlying communities across hundreds of kilometres, suggesting it was a regional ceremonial and economic centre rather than simply a residential settlement. Chaco is a UNESCO World Heritage Site; the unpaved access roads and minimal facilities make it one of the more demanding visits in the Southwest.

## 3. Bandelier National Monument, New Mexico

On the volcanic Pajarito Plateau southwest of Los Alamos, Bandelier preserves the ruins of Ancestral Puebloan settlements occupied from roughly 1150 to 1550 AD, when Spanish contact began transforming the region. The canyon floor holds the remains of Tyuonyi — a circular great house of around 400 ground-floor rooms arranged around a central plaza — while the soft volcanic tuff of the canyon walls is honeycombed with cavates: hand-carved rooms and storage spaces augmenting the masonry buildings below. The 'cave kivas' of the Alcove House, reached by a 140-foot climb on wooden ladders, are among the most intimate ancient spaces accessible to visitors anywhere in the Southwest. The landscape of ponderosa pine and canyon juniper adds to the experience. Managed by the National Park Service; shuttle buses from White Rock are required during summer.

## 4. Hovenweep National Monument, Utah/Colorado

Straddling the Utah-Colorado border on the mesa country between Blanding and Cortez, Hovenweep preserves six clusters of Ancestral Puebloan towers and villages built between roughly 1200 and 1300 AD. The towers — free-standing round, square, and oval structures rising from canyon rims and boulder tops — are architecturally unique in the Southwest. Their function is debated: possible uses include astronomical observation (several have window alignments to solstice and equinox sunrises), defensive watchtowers, water control structures, and ceremonial spaces. The Square Tower Group in the main unit is the most accessible and impressive. Hovenweep sees far fewer visitors than Mesa Verde despite being only a few hours away, lending the monuments a solitude appropriate to their setting. A small campground operates near the visitor centre.

## 5. Canyon de Chelly, Arizona

Within the Navajo Nation in northeastern Arizona, Canyon de Chelly (pronounced 'de Shay') is a canyon system of extraordinary beauty that has been continuously inhabited for nearly 5,000 years. The Ancestral Puebloans built numerous cliff dwellings in its alcoves, of which White House Ruin and Antelope House are the most accessible and visually striking. The canyon came under Navajo occupation after the abandonment of the Puebloan sites, and Navajo families still farm and graze sheep on the canyon floor under a unique arrangement with the National Park Service. Visitors may drive the rim roads independently but can only enter the canyon floor on foot to the White House Trail, or with an authorised Navajo guide. The experience of sites within an actively lived landscape is distinct from most Southwest archaeology parks.

## 6. Montezuma Castle, Arizona

Misnamed by early American settlers who assumed a Mexican origin, Montezuma Castle National Monument in the Verde Valley of central Arizona preserves a remarkable five-storey, 20-room cliff dwelling built by the Sinagua people between roughly 1100 and 1425 AD. Tucked into a natural alcove about 27 metres above the valley floor, the structure survives in excellent condition — its walls of limestone and adobe mud retain much of their original plaster, and the tiered rooms are clearly legible. The Sinagua were skilled farmers who built their communities in the fertile Verde Valley before abandoning them around the early 15th century, possibly migrating to join Hopi villages. The site is one of the best-preserved cliff dwellings in North America. Nearby Montezuma Well — a collapsed limestone sink with prehistoric irrigation canals — is part of the same monument.

## 7. Wupatki National Monument, Arizona

On the open desert north of Flagstaff near the San Francisco Peaks, Wupatki was built by a mixed community — probably including Ancestral Puebloans, Sinagua, and Cohonino peoples — following the eruption of Sunset Crater volcano around 1085 AD, which blanketed the area with volcanic ash that temporarily improved soil moisture for farming. The main Wupatki Pueblo, a 100-room structure of red Moenkopi sandstone, was occupied from roughly 1120 to 1225 AD and was one of the largest buildings in the Southwest at the time. Adjacent to it is an oval-shaped amphitheatre-like structure and a ball court of a type normally associated with the Hohokam to the south — evidence of the cultural mixing that defined Wupatki. A natural blowhole nearby, which breathes warm air upward, likely had ceremonial significance.

## 8. Aztec Ruins National Monument, New Mexico

Despite the name — another early American misnomer — Aztec Ruins National Monument near Farmington, New Mexico, preserves an Ancestral Puebloan great house built between roughly 1100 and 1130 AD and later reoccupied and remodelled in the 1200s. The site shows strong architectural and artefactual connections to both Chaco Canyon (the earlier phase) and Mesa Verde (the later phase), making it important for understanding the reorganisation of Puebloan society between these two periods. The Great Kiva — a large circular ceremonial chamber over 14 metres in diameter — has been reconstructed and offers the most vivid sense anywhere in the Southwest of what these subterranean communal spaces looked like when in use. A UNESCO World Heritage Site as part of the Chaco Culture designation.

## 9. Petrified Forest National Park, Arizona

While better known for its fossilised wood, Petrified Forest National Park also preserves significant archaeological remains. Puerco Pueblo, a 100-room Ancestral Puebloan village occupied around 1300 AD, stands near a large petroglyph panel where a spiral symbol marks the summer solstice sunrise with a shaft of light — one of several astronomical markers identified at the site. The park's dry high-desert environment has preserved rock art, potsherds, and architectural remains in exceptional condition across a landscape that was far more densely populated in prehistory than it appears today. The Painted Desert Inn, a historic Civilian Conservation Corps building, also interprets the broader cultural history. Rangers offer guided walks to the petroglyph sites.

## 10. Tonto National Monument, Arizona

In the Sonoran Desert above the Theodore Roosevelt Lake in central Arizona, Tonto National Monument preserves two cliff dwellings built by the Salado people between roughly 1300 and 1450 AD. The Lower Ruin, accessible on a self-guided trail, contains nineteen rooms in a natural cave; the Upper Ruin, larger and accessible only on ranger-guided tours in autumn and winter, has around forty rooms. The Salado were skilled weavers and potters: the museum at the monument holds extraordinary cotton textile fragments and polychrome pottery that survive due to the desert conditions. The lake views from the ruins add a visual drama absent from many Southwest sites. The Upper Ruin tour must be booked in advance through the monument office.

## Planning Your Visit

The American Southwest's ancient sites are spread across several states, and distances are considerable. A dedicated Southwest archaeology circuit might link Chaco Canyon, Aztec Ruins, Mesa Verde, Hovenweep, and Canyon de Chelly over eight to ten days by car. The best seasons are spring (March to May) and autumn (September to November); summer temperatures at lower-elevation sites like Montezuma Castle and Tonto can exceed 40°C. Most sites are managed by the National Park Service and covered by the America the Beautiful annual pass. Water, shade, and sturdy footwear are essential at all desert sites. Many of these places are sacred to living Indigenous communities; treat them accordingly.

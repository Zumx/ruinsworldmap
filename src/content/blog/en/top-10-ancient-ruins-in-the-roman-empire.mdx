At its greatest extent, in the early 2nd century AD, the Roman Empire stretched from the Atlantic coasts of Portugal and Scotland to the Euphrates, from the Rhine and Danube to the Sahara and the Nile's first cataract. Across this vast territory, Roman engineers and builders left a physical imprint that still shapes the landscape of three continents. Amphitheatres, aqueducts, forums, temples, walls, baths, roads, and port facilities — the range of Roman construction types is remarkable, and what survives ranges from near-complete monuments to vast fields of tumbled stone. The empire's building programme was not merely utilitarian; it was a deliberate statement of civilisational power, and the ruins read accordingly. ## 1. The Colosseum, Rome

The Flavian Amphitheatre — universally known as the Colosseum — was inaugurated by Emperor Titus in 80 AD following a decade of construction under Vespasian. It seated between 50,000 and 80,000 spectators for gladiatorial contests, animal hunts, and public executions. The elliptical structure measures 188 by 156 metres at its outer edge and rises to 48 metres across four storeys of travertine limestone, tuff, and brick-faced concrete. The hypogeum — the labyrinthine network of tunnels and chambers beneath the arena floor — has been partially opened to the public and reveals the logistics of staging spectacles at industrial scale. Medieval stripping of its marble and metal fittings left scars still visible in the outer wall. A UNESCO World Heritage Site as part of the Historic Centre of Rome. Pre-booked timed entry is essential.

## 2. Pompeii, Campania

Pompeii's particular tragedy — burial under volcanic ash and pumice during the 79 AD eruption of Vesuvius — became archaeology's greatest windfall. The city was sealed in the middle of an ordinary morning, preserving bakeries with loaves still in the ovens, walls covered in electoral graffiti, lupanar frescoes, private gardens, and the petrified outlines of the dead in the infamous plaster casts. Pompeii was a prosperous commercial town of perhaps 11,000 inhabitants, not an imperial showpiece, and its ordinariness is precisely what makes it revelatory. The forum, several temples, two theatres, the large palestra, and the House of the Faun with its Alexander Mosaic are among the principal monuments. Only about two-thirds of the site has been excavated; the remainder is deliberately preserved. UNESCO listed in 1997 together with Herculaneum and Oplontis.

## 3. Leptis Magna, Libya

Leptis Magna, on the Libyan coast east of modern Tripoli, was one of the wealthiest cities of Roman Africa and the birthplace of Emperor Septimius Severus (193–211 AD), who lavished resources on it. The Severan Forum and Basilica, with their elaborate carved friezes of Medusa heads and garlands, survive to near-full wall height in places. The Old Forum dates to the 1st century BC, and the Arch of Septimius Severus, erected around 203 AD, still stands at a major crossroads. The harbour, now silted, included elaborate warehouses and a lighthouse. The Hadrianic Baths are among the largest and most complete Roman bath complexes outside Rome itself. UNESCO listed in 1982. The site's relative remoteness has protected it from over-tourism and from some of the stripping suffered by Roman sites in more accessible locations.

## 4. Ephesus, Turkey

Ephesus, on the Aegean coast of western Turkey near modern Selçuk, was the most important city in the Roman province of Asia and at its peak in the 2nd century AD probably held 200,000 inhabitants, making it one of the largest cities in the empire. The Celsus Library, built around 117–120 AD as a tomb-monument and reading room for nearly 12,000 scrolls, is the most photographed structure: its two-storey facade with niched statues remains largely intact after 20th-century anastylosis. The Harbour Street and the Great Theatre (seating roughly 25,000) give scale to the city's commercial and public life. The Terrace Houses — two blocks of wealthy private dwellings with mosaic floors, frescoed walls, and underfloor heating — are covered by a modern shelter and require a separate ticket. UNESCO listed in 2015.

## 5. Palmyra, Syria

Palmyra, an oasis city in the Syrian desert, grew rich as a caravan trade hub between the Roman world and Parthia and later Sasanian Persia, reaching its height in the 2nd and 3rd centuries AD. Its semi-independent status under the Severan dynasty and its spectacular culture — a fusion of Roman, Parthian, and local Semitic traditions — produced a distinctive cityscape. The Great Colonnade, over a kilometre long, was lined with hundreds of columns, many bearing bracket consoles for portrait statues of wealthy donors. The Temple of Bel, consecrated in 32 AD, was one of the best-preserved Roman-era temples in the Near East before its deliberate destruction in 2015. Significant portions of the colonnade, the Tetrapylon, and the funerary towers survive or have been partially restored. UNESCO listed in 1980; currently on the List of World Heritage in Danger.

## 6. Baalbek, Lebanon

Baalbek, in the Beqaa Valley of Lebanon, contains the largest surviving Roman temple complex in the world. The Temple of Jupiter Heliopolitanus, begun under Augustus and expanded through the 2nd century AD, stood on a platform of enormous stone blocks; three of the Trilithon blocks, each weighing roughly 800 tonnes, are among the largest worked stones ever moved in the ancient world. Six columns of the Temple of Jupiter still stand, 22 metres high, defining the skyline. Alongside it, the near-complete Temple of Bacchus (actually probably dedicated to Mercury) is arguably the best-preserved large Roman temple anywhere — its interior is intact to the coffered ceiling. The smaller circular Temple of Venus adds a third monument to the ensemble. UNESCO listed in 1984.

## 7. Pont du Gard, France

The Pont du Gard, crossing the Gardon river in the Gard département of southern France, is the highest surviving segment of the aqueduct that carried water to the Roman colony of Nemausus (Nîmes), roughly 50 kilometres away, across challenging terrain. Built around the mid-1st century AD, the bridge stands 49 metres high across three tiers of arches, the largest of which span 24 metres. The upper tier carried the water channel; the middle and lower tiers provided structural support and road crossing. The construction used no mortar — the limestone blocks, some weighing up to six tonnes, were cut and fitted with exceptional precision. The aqueduct's total gradient across 50 kilometres is only 12.6 metres, demonstrating the engineering exactness of Roman surveyors. UNESCO listed in 1985. A visitor centre and museum occupy the south bank.

## 8. Hadrian's Wall, England

Hadrian's Wall, ordered by Emperor Hadrian during his visit to Britain in 122 AD, ran 73 miles (117 kilometres) from Wallsend on the Tyne estuary to Bowness-on-Solway on the Irish Sea, marking the northern limit of Roman Britain. It was not merely a wall but a complex garrisoned system: milecastles (small forts) at every Roman mile, watchtowers between them, and 17 full garrison forts including Housesteads (Vercovicium) and Chesters (Cilurnum). The ditch to the north and the vallum (a flat-bottomed ditch flanked by mounds) to the south were integral parts of the system. The best-preserved sections, at Housesteads and Steel Rigg, follow the dramatic escarpment of the Whin Sill. UNESCO listed in 1987 as part of the Frontiers of the Roman Empire, extended in 2023.

## 9. Diocletian's Palace, Split

Diocletian's Palace in the Croatian city of Split was built by Emperor Diocletian as his retirement complex between roughly 295 and 305 AD. It is not a ruin in the conventional sense — the old Roman walls and towers are still standing because the medieval and modern city grew up inside them. Around 3,000 people live and work within the palace precincts today. The Mausoleum of Diocletian was converted into Split Cathedral in the 7th century and remains in use. The Temple of Jupiter became a baptistery. The Peristyle, a colonnaded courtyard, serves as the city's central gathering space. Below street level, the subterranean halls (built to support the imperial apartments above) survive almost intact and can be toured. UNESCO listed in 1979.

## 10. Theatre of Orange, France

The Roman Theatre of Orange in the Vaucluse region of Provence retains what is arguably the best-preserved stage wall (scaenae frons) of any Roman theatre in the world. Built under Augustus in the late 1st century BC, it is 103 metres wide and 37 metres high — a vertical expanse of masonry that Louis XIV, no stranger to imposing structures, reportedly called 'the finest wall in my kingdom'. The theatre seated roughly 10,000 spectators. The central niche of the stage wall still holds a large statue of Augustus, which was reassembled from fragments. The cavea is carved partly into the hill of Saint-Eutrope. The theatre is in active use for the Chorégies d'Orange summer opera festival. UNESCO listed in 1981.

The Roman Empire was a civilisation built for permanence, and the ambition shows in what endures. Visiting these sites across southern Europe, North Africa, and the Near East reveals not only the reach of Roman power but the local textures it absorbed and transformed — Semitic religious traditions at Baalbek, Punic trading wealth at Leptis Magna, provincial affluence at Ephesus and Pompeii. Practical access varies enormously: some sites have daily flights and modern facilities nearby, while Palmyra remains effectively closed to visitors. Seasonal timing matters — summer at Pompeii or the Colosseum is intense, while spring and autumn give cooler conditions and lighter crowds.

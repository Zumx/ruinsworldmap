Armenia sits at the crossroads of the ancient Near East, the Caucasus, and the classical Mediterranean world. Its landscape is littered with monuments spanning more than five thousand years: Bronze Age megalithic monuments, an Iron Age Urartian fortress, a uniquely preserved Hellenistic temple, medieval monasteries that would be the finest ancient buildings in any other country, and a prehistoric stone circle that has been called the Armenian Stonehenge. The country is compact enough that several major sites can be visited in a single day, and tourism infrastructure, while still developing, has improved significantly in recent years.

## 1. Garni Temple, Kotayk Province

The Temple of Garni is the only surviving Greco-Roman-style columned temple in the post-Soviet world, built in the 1st century AD by the Armenian king Tiridates I, who had recently received his crown from the Roman emperor Nero in a famous ceremony in Rome. The Ionic temple, dedicated probably to the sun god Mihr, stands on a basalt promontory above the Azat River gorge, its setting dramatic even without the building. Destroyed by an earthquake in 1679, it was reconstructed between 1969 and 1975 using surviving fragments and is now the most visited non-religious ancient monument in Armenia.

## 2. Zvartnots Cathedral, Armavir Province

Zvartnots (Cathedral of the Vigilant Forces) was built in the 640s AD by the Armenian Catholicos Nerses III and was one of the largest and most architecturally ambitious buildings in early medieval Armenia. The circular rotunda, rising over three stories, collapsed in the 10th century, probably from an earthquake, but the excavated foundations and surviving sculptural fragments convey the building's extraordinary ambition. The site museum displays reconstructed capitals and reliefs; a hypothetical reconstruction drawing gives a sense of the original form. Zvartnots is a UNESCO World Heritage Site.

## 3. Erebuni Fortress, Yerevan

Erebuni was founded in 782 BC by the Urartian king Argishti I as a military fortress and administrative centre controlling the Ararat plain. The name Erebuni is considered to be the origin of the name 'Yerevan,' the Armenian capital. The site occupies a hill overlooking the city and preserves the plan of the Urartian citadel, including a columned hall (apadana), a temple, storage magazines, and sections of mudbrick wall with painted plaster. The associated museum displays weapons, pottery, bronzes, and inscriptions from the excavations.

## 4. Karahunj (Zorats Karer), Syunik Province

Karahunj, also called Zorats Karer or the 'Armenian Stonehenge,' consists of around 220 large standing stones and fallen megaliths arranged across a hilltop in southern Armenia. The site dates to roughly the 3rd–2nd millennium BC, and several of the upright stones have circular holes bored through their upper sections, which some researchers have interpreted as astronomical sighting devices, though this interpretation remains debated. The age of the holes themselves is uncertain. Regardless, the site's scale — the hilltop covers several hectares — and the drama of the basalt columns against the landscape make it one of Armenia's most atmospheric prehistoric sites.

## 5. Tatev Monastery, Syunik Province

Tatev Monastery, perched on a near-vertical basalt cliff above the Vorotan River gorge, was founded in the 9th century AD and served for centuries as the most important ecclesiastical and intellectual centre of southern Armenia. The current buildings date primarily to the 9th to 13th centuries, with significant later additions and restorations. The monastery is reached via the Wings of Tatev, the world's longest non-stop double track cable car, which replaced a treacherous mountain road. The surrounding gorge landscape is spectacular.

## 6. Khor Virap Monastery, Ararat Province

Khor Virap, at the foot of Mount Ararat near the Turkish border, is famous as the prison where Gregory the Illuminator was held for thirteen years before converting the Armenian king Tiridates III to Christianity in 301 AD, making Armenia the first nation to adopt Christianity as a state religion. The monastery buildings are largely 17th century, but the underground pit-prison of Gregory survives beneath the chapel and can be descended via a steep ladder. The panoramic view of Mount Ararat is one of the most iconic in the country.

## 7. Amberd Fortress, Aragatsotn Province

Amberd is a medieval Armenian fortress standing at around 2300 metres on the southern slope of Mount Aragats, at the confluence of two rivers. The main fortified complex, including the castle and the attached Vakhutavan Church (1026 AD), dates primarily to the 11th–13th centuries. The site's high-altitude setting — with snow-covered Aragats above and the Ararat plain visible below — is exceptional. A bathhouse complex near the church shows the sophistication of the fortress's amenities.

## 8. Dvin Archaeological Site, Ararat Province

Dvin served as the capital of Armenian kingdoms and Sassanid Persian governors from the 4th to 9th centuries AD, a major city of the Late Antique and early medieval Caucasus. The site, now an uninhabited tell southeast of Yerevan, has been partially excavated, revealing palatial structures, a cathedral, baths, and evidence for extensive craft production. The associated museum in the village of Dvin holds finds including glazed ceramics, metalwork, and architectural sculpture.

## 9. Metsamor Archaeological Site, Armavir Province

Metsamor is an ancient settlement and metallurgical centre occupied from the Chalcolithic through the medieval period, with its most important phases in the Bronze and Early Iron Ages. The site's ancient inhabitants smelted iron and bronze, and a hoard of obsidian, semi-precious stones, and metal objects from the site is displayed in the on-site museum. Nearby royal graves from the 2nd millennium BC contained rich assemblages. The site's multi-period stratigraphy makes it important for understanding the long prehistory of the Ararat plain.

## 10. Noravank Monastery, Vayots Dzor Province

Noravank, set in a narrow gorge of the Amaghu River in southern Armenia, is considered one of the finest examples of medieval Armenian architecture. The main church of Surb Astvatsatsin (Holy Mother of God), built in the 13th to 14th centuries, is a two-storey structure with an elaborate sculptural programme and an exterior staircase leading to the upper chapel — a design of unusual spatial elegance. The red-orange cliffs rising directly behind the monastery create a setting of dramatic intensity.

Armenia's main sites are accessible on good roads from Yerevan and are generally open year-round, though winter brings snow to higher-altitude sites like Amberd and Karahunj. Spring and autumn are the most comfortable seasons. The drive from Yerevan to the south (Tatev, Karahunj, Noravank) can be covered in two days with a car. Entry to most sites is inexpensive.

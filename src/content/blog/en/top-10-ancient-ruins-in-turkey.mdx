Turkey holds one of the most extraordinary concentrations of ancient ruins in the world. The Anatolian peninsula has been continuously inhabited for at least ten millennia and has formed part of nearly every major civilisation of the ancient Near East and Mediterranean: Neolithic farming cultures, Hittite empire, Phrygian, Lydian, and Urartian kingdoms, Greek colonisation along the Aegean and Black Sea coasts, Persian empire, Hellenistic successor states, Roman province of Asia, Byzantine Empire. The result is a landscape layered with remains from periods spanning an almost incomprehensible range of time — from Göbekli Tepe's monumental stone enclosures of around 9500 BC to Byzantine churches and fortifications of the 14th century AD. ## 1. Ephesus

Ephesus, on the Aegean coast near modern Selçuk in Izmir Province, was the most important city in the Roman province of Asia and at its height in the 2nd century AD probably held 200,000 people, making it one of the largest cities in the ancient world. The Celsus Library, built around 117–120 AD as a tomb for the senator Tiberius Julius Celsus Polemaeanus and a public reading room, is the defining image of the site: its two-storey marble facade, restored through anastylosis in the 1970s, remains essentially intact. The Harbour Street ran from the theatre to the ancient port (now silted), lined with columns. The Great Theatre seated roughly 25,000. The Terrace Houses — two blocks of wealthy private dwellings — preserve mosaic floors, frescoed walls, and marble revetments under a modern protective shelter; a separate entry fee applies. UNESCO listed in 2015.

## 2. Troy (Hisarlık)

Troy, identified with the mound of Hisarlık near the Dardanelles in Çanakkale Province, was excavated by Heinrich Schliemann from 1871 and subsequently shown to contain at least nine major occupation phases spanning from roughly 3000 BC to the 4th century AD. The Homeric Troy, if historical, is most plausibly associated with Troy VIh or VIIa, both dating to the Late Bronze Age (roughly 1400–1180 BC). Earlier levels contain fine Early Bronze Age remains including monumental walls and a megaron (great hall). The site is visually complex because Schliemann's aggressive early digging destroyed significant portions of the stratigraphically important middle levels. A reconstructed wooden horse and a small museum help orient visitors. UNESCO listed in 1998. The site is close to Çanakkale, making it straightforwardly accessible.

## 3. Pergamon

Pergamon (Bergama), on a dramatic acropolis above the modern town of the same name in Izmir Province, was the capital of the Attalid dynasty and one of the most important Hellenistic cities of Asia Minor, flourishing between the 3rd and 1st centuries BC. Its Library was said to rival Alexandria's, holding some 200,000 volumes. The Temple of Athena, the steeply raked Theatre (seating roughly 10,000) carved into the acropolis's western face, and the ruins of the enormous altar to Zeus — its frieze depicting the Gigantomachy removed to Berlin in the 19th century — are the principal Hellenistic monuments. Below the acropolis, the Roman city contains the Red Basilica (a large temple converted to a church) and the Asklepion healing sanctuary, where the physician Galen worked in the 2nd century AD. UNESCO listed in 2014.

## 4. Hierapolis, Pamukkale

Hierapolis was founded as a Seleucid city in the early 2nd century BC and became a Roman spa city because of the calcium-rich thermal springs that produce the famous white travertine terraces of Pamukkale directly below it. The city was a significant pilgrimage site: the Plutonium, a cave emitting carbon dioxide at fatal concentrations, was interpreted as an entrance to the underworld, and the castrated priests of Cybele who managed it were believed to be immune to its effects. The large Necropolis extending along the northern approach to the city contains hundreds of sarcophagi and tomb types from Hellenistic through Byzantine periods and is one of the best-preserved ancient cemeteries in Turkey. The theatre, built in the 2nd century AD and well restored, and the Martyrium of Philip the Apostle add to the ensemble. UNESCO listed in 1988 as a combined cultural and natural site.

## 5. Aphrodisias

Aphrodisias, in the Caria region of southwest Turkey near modern Geyre, is one of the most beautifully excavated and presented sites in the entire Mediterranean world. The city was dedicated to Aphrodite and grew to prominence during the Roman period, when its white marble statuary schools produced work exported across the empire — sculptor signatures from Aphrodisian workshops have been found from Rome to Jordan. The Tetrapylon (a monumental four-columned gateway), the Sebasteion (a two-storey portico dedicated to the Julio-Claudian emperors with extraordinary relief sculptures), the Hadrianic Baths, and the Stadium — among the largest and best-preserved in the ancient world, seating roughly 30,000 — form the main complex. The site museum contains the finest collection of classical sculpture in Turkey. UNESCO listed in 2017.

## 6. Göbekli Tepe

Göbekli Tepe, on a limestone ridge near Şanlıurfa in southeast Turkey, is the most important archaeological discovery of the 20th century for understanding early human civilisation. The site consists of multiple circular stone enclosures, each containing massive T-shaped limestone pillars up to 5.5 metres high and weighing up to 10 tonnes, carved with animals, abstract symbols, and occasional anthropomorphic features. The earliest enclosures date to around 9500 BC — before agriculture, before pottery, before cities — requiring the coordinated labour of hunter-gatherer populations to quarry, transport, and erect. The site was deliberately backfilled with debris around 8000 BC, preserving it remarkably well. Excavations have revealed at least 20 enclosures so far; geophysical surveys suggest many more remain buried. UNESCO listed in 2018. A modern canopy shelter covers the main excavation area.

## 7. Aspendos

Aspendos, near modern Serik in Antalya Province, preserves the best-intact Roman theatre anywhere in the world. Built around 155 AD by the local architect Zeno for the emperor Marcus Aurelius, it seats approximately 15,000 spectators and retains its original stage building (scaenae frons) almost complete, rising to the full height of the cavea's upper gallery — a rare survival usually the first casualty of post-antique reuse or collapse. The Seljuk Turks used the building as a caravanserai and carried out repairs, sealing in mortar that helped preserve the stage facade. Atatürk reportedly remarked that it should be used for performances rather than allowed to decay; it now hosts the Aspendos International Opera and Ballet Festival each summer. The surrounding region also contains a long Roman aqueduct crossing the Eurymedon valley on tall arched piers.

## 8. Hattusa

Hattusa, near modern Boğazkale in Çorum Province in central Anatolia, was the capital of the Hittite Empire, one of the great powers of the Late Bronze Age Near East from roughly 1650 to 1200 BC. At its height the city covered about 180 hectares, enclosed by a massive double circuit of mudbrick walls on stone foundations, with distinctive corbelled tunnel gateways and carved sphinx and warrior reliefs still in situ. The Great Temple precinct, occupying the lower city, is the best-preserved Hittite temple complex in Turkey. The nearby rock sanctuary of Yazılıkaya displays carved processions of Hittite gods in remarkable relief. The archives found at Hattusa — thousands of cuneiform tablets — included the Treaty of Kadesh (around 1259 BC), the earliest international peace treaty whose text survives. UNESCO listed in 1986.

## 9. Çatalhöyük

Çatalhöyük, on the Konya Plain in south-central Turkey, was one of the world's earliest towns, occupied from roughly 7500 to 5700 BC — contemporary with the later phases of Göbekli Tepe. The site is famous for its unusual agglomerated architecture: houses built directly against each other without streets, entered through holes in the roof. Interior walls were plastered and repeatedly repainted with geometric and figurative murals. Wall installations of cattle skulls and elaborate funerary practices, with the dead buried beneath the floors of occupied houses, give a vivid picture of Neolithic religious life. The site was excavated by James Mellaart from 1961 and has been the subject of extensive renewed excavations since 1993. UNESCO listed in 2012. A climate-controlled excavation shelter allows visitors to observe ongoing work and the remarkably preserved mud-brick structures.

## 10. Xanthos-Letoon

Xanthos, in the Lycian region of southwest Turkey near modern Kinik in Antalya Province, was the principal city of ancient Lycia and has produced some of the most distinctive funerary monuments in the ancient world: the Harpy Tomb (5th century BC) with its relief sculptures now in the British Museum, the Tomb of Payava, and dozens of Lycian pillar tombs standing on the acropolis and hillsides. The nearby sanctuary of Letoon, dedicated to the goddess Leto and her children Apollo and Artemis, includes three temples and was the religious and federal centre of the Lycian League. The Xanthos-Letoon site is also central to the decipherment of Lycian: the Letoon trilingual inscription (in Lycian, Greek, and Aramaic) provided the key to cracking the Lycian language. UNESCO listed in 1988.

Turkey's major archaeological sites are managed by the Ministry of Culture and Tourism through museum directorates. Entry fees are standardised and reasonable; the Museum Card (Müze Kart) provides unlimited access to all state-managed sites and museums for a fixed annual fee and represents excellent value for extended visits. The Aegean and Mediterranean coast sites — Ephesus, Aphrodisias, Aspendos — can be oppressively hot in July and August; spring and autumn are ideal. Inland sites like Hattusa and Göbekli Tepe are accessible year-round, though winters in central Anatolia can be harsh.

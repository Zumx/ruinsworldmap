Bulgaria occupies a territory that has been at the intersection of European and Asian civilizations for millennia. Thracian tribes dominated the region in antiquity and left behind a striking tradition of burial mounds and goldwork; the Romans conquered and transformed Thrace into a heavily urbanized province; and the First and Second Bulgarian Empires, medieval states of considerable power, left fortified capitals and rock monuments that still mark the landscape. Bulgaria holds six UNESCO World Heritage Sites, and its archaeological heritage extends from Neolithic tells to Ottoman-era monuments. For visitors focused on ancient history, the country remains underestimated relative to its immediate neighbours Greece and Turkey. [Open the map](/map) to navigate this layered landscape.

## 1. Roman Theatre of Plovdiv, Plovdiv

The ancient theatre of Philippopolis — Plovdiv's Roman-era name — was built during the reign of Trajan in the late 1st or early 2nd century AD and could seat roughly 7,000 spectators. Discovered by accident during a landslide in 1972, it has been carefully excavated and partially restored, and it is now one of the best-preserved Roman theatres in eastern Europe. The cavea is built into the slope of one of Plovdiv's hills with a commanding view south across the Thracian plain, and the stage building retains several of its Corinthian columns. Performances and concerts are still held here, making it a living monument rather than merely a museum piece. Located in the city's historic old town, freely accessible.

## 2. Nessebar, Black Sea Coast

Nessebar (ancient Mesembria) is a small peninsula on the Black Sea coast that has been continuously inhabited since the Bronze Age and preserves remarkable layered remains. Founded as a Thracian settlement, it became a prosperous Greek colonial town in the 6th century BC, was absorbed into Rome, and flourished as a Byzantine city. The peninsula is thickly covered with medieval Bulgarian and Byzantine churches — over forty have been identified, many now ruined — alongside a Hellenistic temple of Apollo, Roman fortifications, and Byzantine city walls. The combination of Black Sea setting and dense historic fabric makes Nessebar one of Bulgaria's most atmospheric towns. UNESCO World Heritage Site.

## 3. Perperikon, Rhodope Mountains

Perperikon is a rocky peak in the eastern Rhodopes that preserves what is believed to be one of the largest ancient cult sites in the Balkans. The site was occupied continuously from the Chalcolithic period (around 5000 BC) through the medieval Bulgarian period, but its most dramatic remains are the Thracian sanctuary and later town dating from the Early Iron Age through the Roman period. Carved directly into the living rock are massive chambers, staircases, altars with cup-marks, wine-processing installations, and palatial complexes. An ancient oracle here was consulted, according to Roman sources, by the forces of Alexander the Great. The summit requires a walk of about 20 minutes from the nearest road.

## 4. Pliska, Northeastern Bulgaria

Pliska was the first capital of the First Bulgarian Empire, established after Khan Asparuh's forces settled south of the Danube in 681 AD. The outer enclosure of the city covered an enormous area — roughly 23 square kilometres — making it one of the largest medieval cities of early medieval Europe. Within this, the inner city contained the royal palace complex, the Great Basilica (one of the largest churches of its era, built after Bulgaria's conversion to Christianity in 864 AD), administrative buildings, and bath structures. The site lies near the modern village of Pliska and preserves substantial standing ruins of palace walls, column fragments, and the basilica's massive foundations. A site museum displays sculptural and epigraphic finds.

## 5. Madara Rider, Northeastern Bulgaria

The Madara Rider is a rock relief carved on a nearly vertical cliff face roughly 23 metres above the ground, showing a horseman spearing a lion while a dog runs alongside. Carved in the late 7th or early 8th century AD, it is the only large-scale early medieval rock relief in Europe. Three Greek inscriptions carved beside the figure record the deeds of early Bulgarian khans — Tervel, Kormesiy, and Omurtag — in a practice borrowed from Byzantine imperial iconography. The site is near the ancient capital of Pliska and formed part of the ceremonial landscape of the First Bulgarian Empire. UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1979.

## 6. Serdica, Sofia

Beneath the streets of Bulgaria's modern capital lies the Roman and early Byzantine city of Serdica, a town that Constantine the Great is said to have considered as his imperial capital ('Serdica is my Rome'). Excavations during the construction of Sofia's metro system in the early 21st century revealed substantial remains of the Roman city: defensive walls, a street grid, a large basilica complex, and portions of an amphitheatre. Many finds are now displayed in an underground museum at the Serdika metro station, making them accessible to the thousands of daily commuters who pass through. Walking the old city of Sofia reveals fragments of Roman masonry incorporated into later buildings throughout.

## 7. Thracian Tomb of Kazanlak, Kazanlak

The Thracian tomb at Kazanlak, discovered in 1944 near the Valley of the Roses, is one of the finest examples of ancient painted architecture to survive in Bulgaria. Built around the 4th century BC, the tomb consists of a narrow corridor and a circular domed burial chamber whose walls are covered with vivid paintings depicting a funerary feast and a chariot race. The paintings are executed in a style that combines Thracian and Greek artistic conventions and are among the best-preserved examples of ancient fresco painting in the entire Balkans. The original tomb is sealed to protect the paintings; a replica tomb beside it is open for viewing. UNESCO World Heritage Site.

## 8. Abritus, Northeastern Bulgaria

Abritus, near modern Razgrad in northeastern Bulgaria, was a Roman legionary fortress and later city of the province of Lower Moesia. The site is notable partly for its archaeology — substantial fortification walls and administrative buildings survive above ground — and partly for its historical significance: the Battle of Abritus in 251 AD, fought outside the city's walls, resulted in the death of the emperor Decius and his son in battle against the Goths, the first time a reigning Roman emperor had been killed by a foreign enemy. Finds from the site are displayed in the History Museum of Razgrad.

## 9. Rock Churches of Ivanovo, Rusenski Lom

The rock-hewn churches and monasteries of the Rusenski Lom river canyon near Ivanovo were carved and painted by Bulgarian Orthodox monks beginning in the 13th century. The most important of the sites preserve 14th-century frescoes of remarkable quality, executed in the Turnovo school style that represents the high point of medieval Bulgarian painting. The frescoes at the Church of the Holy Virgin at Ivanovo are particularly fine, showing the influence of Byzantine art of the Palaeologan period. The canyon itself — a deep limestone gorge with dramatic rock formations — provides a spectacular setting. UNESCO World Heritage Site.

## 10. Tsarevets Fortress, Veliko Tarnovo

Tsarevets, occupying a rocky hill above the Yantra River in Veliko Tarnovo, was the main fortress of the Second Bulgarian Empire (1185–1393 AD) and housed the royal palace, the patriarchal church, and hundreds of dwellings within its defensive walls. The fortress fell to the Ottoman Turks in 1393 after a three-month siege, ending Bulgarian medieval independence. Excavations have revealed the foundations of the palace complex, the patriarchal cathedral, and over four hundred houses, churches, and administrative buildings within the walls. Much of the circuit wall has been reconstructed, and the hilltop is one of Bulgaria's most visited sites. Light shows illuminate the fortress on summer evenings.

Bulgaria's sites are generally open from April to October, with winter access often restricted at more remote locations. The Plovdiv theatre and Tsarevets fortress are accessible year-round. Train and bus connections link major cities; for sites like Perperikon or the Kazanlak tomb, a car or organized tour is the most practical approach.

Tunisia is one of the richest archaeological destinations in the Mediterranean world. Its layered history encompasses Berber and Numidian kingdoms, the Phoenician and Punic civilisation centred on Carthage, Roman provincial culture at its North African peak, Byzantine administration, and early Islamic settlement — all compressed into a small country of some 163,000 square kilometres. The Romans called their North African provinces the breadbasket of the empire, and the density of cities, temples, baths, amphitheatres, and farms they left behind confirms that wealth. Tunisia's inland sites are among the least visited major Roman monuments in the Mediterranean despite their extraordinary quality. The Bardo National Museum in Tunis holds one of the world's finest collections of Roman mosaics, many excavated from these sites. ## 1. Carthage

Carthage, on a promontory north of Tunis, was the capital of the Phoenician-descended Punic civilisation and for centuries Rome's most formidable rival. Founded, according to tradition, in 814 BC by settlers from Tyre, it grew into the dominant power of the central Mediterranean before Rome's victory in the Third Punic War and its total destruction in 146 BC. The Romans refounded it as a colony around 44 BC, and Roman Carthage became one of the largest cities in the western empire, eventually the capital of Roman Africa. What survives today is mainly Roman: the Antonine Baths (2nd century AD), among the largest bath complexes in the empire with six massive column stumps surviving; the Punic Tophet sanctuary where evidence of child sacrifice remains contested among scholars; Roman housing on the Byrsa Hill; and the Carthage National Museum. UNESCO listed in 1979.

## 2. Dougga

Dougga (ancient Thugga) in northwest Tunisia is frequently described as the best-preserved Roman city in North Africa, and the claim is defensible. The city occupies a hillside site at roughly 600 metres elevation and covers about 70 hectares, much of it unexcavated. Its Capitol, built around 166 AD and dedicated to the Capitoline triad (Jupiter, Juno, and Minerva), stands to near-full height with three of its original columns still upright and a pediment carrying a relief of the deified Antoninus Pius. The theatre, seating around 3,500, is carved into the hillside and still used for performances. The Baths of Caracalla, a large late Roman bath complex, and the Arch of Septimius Severus add to the ensemble. Pre-Roman Numidian structures, including a Libyco-Punic mausoleum, coexist with the Roman city. UNESCO listed in 1997.

## 3. El Djem Amphitheatre

The amphitheatre of El Djem (ancient Thysdrus) is the third largest amphitheatre in the Roman world — after the Colosseum and Capua — and among the best preserved. Built around 238 AD in a prosperous olive oil-producing town, it measures 148 by 122 metres and held perhaps 35,000 spectators. Three of its four storeys are largely intact on the north side, giving a complete picture of the arched arcade system that characterised the great amphitheatres. Unlike the Colosseum, it was never systematically stripped for building material, though a 17th-century Turkish garrison blew a hole in the outer wall with gunpowder to dislodge a local rebellion. The subterranean corridors and beast chambers beneath the arena are accessible. A small archaeological museum nearby displays mosaics from local villas. UNESCO listed in 1979.

## 4. Bulla Regia

Bulla Regia, north of Dougga in the Medjerda valley, is unlike any other Roman site in Tunisia: its elite residents built their townhouses partially underground, with reception rooms and mosaic-floored dining rooms descending below street level to stay cool during the summer. Several of these 'underground villas' have been excavated and preserved, and a few remain almost intact with their elaborate multi-coloured mosaics in situ. The city was a Numidian royal capital before becoming a Roman colony; the remains visible today date primarily to the 2nd and 3rd centuries AD, including a theatre, a forum, a temple of Apollo, and a substantial bathhouse. Bulla Regia is rarely crowded and offers an intimate encounter with provincial Roman domestic life that the larger sites cannot match.

## 5. Sbeitla (Sufetula)

Sbeitla, in the semi-arid interior of central Tunisia, preserves the best example of a Roman forum with three temples still standing: a triumphal arch of the 3rd century AD leads into the forum, which is framed by three adjacent capitoline temples to Jupiter, Juno, and Minerva, their facades still largely upright. The temples at Sbeitla are exceptional in being three separate buildings where Roman practice usually combined the triad in one structure; the reason is debated. Adjacent structures include a theatre, baths, several Byzantine churches, and an olive oil processing facility. The small modern town of Sbeitla surrounds the site. It was also the site of the Battle of Sbeitla in 647 AD, when Arab Muslim forces defeated the Byzantine Exarchate of Africa in a battle that opened the door to the subsequent Islamic conquest of North Africa.

## 6. Kerkouane

Kerkouane, on Cap Bon east of Tunis, is the only substantially intact Punic city surviving anywhere in the world — all others were either reoccupied in the Roman period (and their Punic layers buried and disturbed) or destroyed, as Carthage was in 146 BC. Kerkouane was abandoned in the mid-3rd century BC, possibly after being sacked during the First Punic War, and never reoccupied. The result is a frozen snapshot of a Phoenician-descended city: narrow streets, small attached houses with standard floor plans, terracotta bathtubs, mosaic and opus signinum floors in characteristic Punic geometric patterns, and evidence of purple dye production from murex shells. The city's small scale and technical preservation quality make it one of the most important archaeological sites in the entire Mediterranean basin. UNESCO listed in 1985.

## 7. Utica

Utica, north of Tunis near the mouth of the Medjerda River, was one of the oldest Phoenician colonies in the western Mediterranean, traditionally founded around 1100 BC though the archaeology confirms Phoenician presence by the 9th century BC at the latest. It was Carthage's nearest major rival and switched to Rome's side during the Third Punic War, becoming the capital of the new Roman province of Africa after Carthage's destruction in 146 BC. The visible remains are primarily Roman: a forum, baths, houses with mosaic floors, and the Punic necropolis. The ancient city extended to the coast, but millennia of siltation from the Medjerda have left it landlocked and partially buried. The site is less developed for visitors than the major monuments but holds considerable historical significance as one of the earliest Phoenician settlements in the West.

## 8. Chemtou (Simitthus)

Chemtou preserves the quarries of giallo antico (Numidian yellow marble), one of the most prized building stones in the Roman world: the distinctive yellow and rose-veined marble was shipped from this location in northwest Tunisia to building projects across the empire, from Rome to Cologne. The site includes the quarries themselves, a Roman city with forum, bridge, and baths, a Numidian sanctuary on a hilltop, and an exceptional museum displaying both the technical systems of quarrying and the history of the site from Numidian through Roman and Byzantine periods. A Numidian altar inscription here helped scholars understand the Libyan script. The museum and site together make Chemtou one of the most intellectually rewarding sites in Tunisia for visitors interested in Roman economic history.

## 9. Thuburbo Majus

Thuburbo Majus, south of Tunis near the town of El Fahs, rose to prominence in the 2nd and 3rd centuries AD as a prosperous market town in the agricultural hinterland of Carthage. Its Capitol, built around 168 AD, preserves four Corinthian columns still standing. The Summer Baths (Bains d'Été) are notable for their elaborate mosaic floor of a wrestling scene, still in situ. The Forum features a substantial portion of its surrounding portico columns. Several private houses with mosaic floors have been preserved. The site is not UNESCO listed and relatively lightly visited, offering a calm contrast to the larger destinations. Its proximity to Tunis (about 60 kilometres) makes it a feasible day trip.

## 10. Pupput (Hammamet)

Pupput, within the modern resort town of Hammamet on Cap Bon, is a Roman site whose existence came to widespread attention only in the 1990s through rescue excavations ahead of construction. The finds include an extensive necropolis with hundreds of sarcophagi, funerary mosaics of exceptional quality, and the remains of a town occupied continuously from the 1st through the 5th centuries AD. The mosaics, now largely in the Nabeul regional museum, include portrait pieces of remarkable technical fineness. The site remains partially visible in open-air display near the modern hotels but is better accessed through the Nabeul museum. It stands as a reminder that significant Roman sites in Tunisia continue to be discovered and that the archaeological map of the country is still incomplete.

Tunisia's major archaeological sites are managed by the Institut National du Patrimoine. Most charge modest admission fees. The country's compact size makes a week-long circuit of Roman and Punic sites very feasible: Carthage and the Bardo Museum in Tunis as a base, then Dougga, Bulla Regia, Chemtou, and Utica in the north, and El Djem, Sbeitla, and Sfax's Taparura site in the centre and south. Temperatures at inland sites like Dougga and Sbeitla are significant in July and August; spring (March–May) and autumn (September–October) are the most comfortable seasons.

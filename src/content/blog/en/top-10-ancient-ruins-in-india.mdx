India's archaeological landscape is one of the most diverse and chronologically deep in the world. It encompasses the urban centres of the Indus Valley Civilization — among the earliest cities in human history, flourishing around 2600–1900 BC — the carved rock edicts and stupas of the Mauryan Empire, the extraordinary temple complexes of medieval dynasties, and the vast ruined capitals of empires that held sway over much of the subcontinent within the last thousand years. The sheer geographic and cultural breadth of the country means that no single list can capture its range; the sites described here represent the most significant and accessible concentrations of above-ground ancient remains. ## 1. Hampi, Karnataka

Hampi preserves the ruins of Vijayanagara, the capital of the Vijayanagara Empire that dominated southern India from the 14th to the 16th centuries. At its height in the early 16th century, the city was one of the largest in the world, home to perhaps 500,000 people, its markets described by visiting Portuguese traders as exceeding Lisbon in wealth and size. The Portuguese chronicles of Domingo Paes and Fernão Nunes provide vivid first-hand accounts of the city's grandeur. The empire's collapse following defeat at the Battle of Talikota in 1565 left the city comprehensively sacked, but the stone structures — hundreds of temple complexes, royal pavilions, elephant stables, stepped tanks, and market streets — remained embedded in a surreal boulder landscape along the Tungabhadra river. The Virupaksha Temple remains an active place of worship. UNESCO World Heritage Site. The nearest rail junction is Hospet; the December-to-February season is most comfortable.

## 2. Nalanda, Bihar

Nalanda was the greatest centre of Buddhist learning in the ancient world, a monastic university that attracted students and scholars from China, Tibet, Korea, Indonesia, and Central Asia between roughly the 5th and 12th centuries AD. At its peak it housed thousands of monks and teachers studying not only Buddhist philosophy but also logic, grammar, medicine, and mathematics. The Chinese pilgrim Xuanzang spent several years at Nalanda in the 7th century and left detailed descriptions of its curriculum and buildings. The site was destroyed — burned, according to Islamic historical sources — in around 1193 AD during the campaigns of Bakhtiyar Khilji, an event with catastrophic consequences for the preservation of Buddhist textual traditions in India. The excavated ruins cover about 14 hectares and include the remains of multiple monasteries, temples, and lecture halls. A modern Nalanda University was re-established nearby in 2014. UNESCO World Heritage Site.

## 3. Sanchi, Madhya Pradesh

Sanchi is the most complete surviving Buddhist architectural complex in India, and its Great Stupa — Stupa 1 — is one of the oldest stone structures in the subcontinent, its original core built by Emperor Ashoka in the 3rd century BC. The stupa was enlarged to its current hemispherical form in the 1st century BC and surrounded by four elaborately carved stone gateways (toranas) whose bas-relief panels depict the life of the Buddha, Jataka tales, and episodes from Ashokan history with extraordinary narrative energy. The Buddha himself is never shown in human form on these early panels, represented instead by a wheel, an empty throne, or footprints. The larger complex contains three stupas of varying dates, an Ashokan pillar, and several monastic buildings. UNESCO World Heritage Site. Sanchi is about 46 kilometres northeast of Bhopal, accessible by road and rail.

## 4. Fatehpur Sikri, Uttar Pradesh

Fatehpur Sikri was the capital of the Mughal Empire under Emperor Akbar between 1571 and 1585, when it was apparently abandoned due to water supply problems. The city was built in red sandstone with extraordinary speed over roughly fourteen years and represents Akbar's syncretic architectural vision — a deliberate fusion of Islamic, Hindu, and Jain architectural elements that mirrors his theological experiments. The Diwan-i-Khas (Hall of Private Audience) centres on a single ornate column supporting a circular platform, its purpose debated for centuries. The Jami Masjid is one of the largest mosques in India; the Buland Darwaza gateway commemorating Akbar's conquest of Gujarat rises 54 metres. UNESCO World Heritage Site. Fatehpur Sikri is about 40 kilometres west of Agra, commonly visited in combination with the Taj Mahal.

## 5. Konark Sun Temple, Odisha

The Konark Sun Temple, built by King Narasimhadeva I of the Eastern Ganga dynasty around 1250 AD, was conceived as the chariot of the sun god Surya — its base carved with twelve pairs of stone wheels (each over three metres in diameter), its platform pulled by a cavalcade of sculpted horses. The temple is covered on every surface with an exuberant programme of sculpture including erotic scenes on the lower register, celestial musicians and dancers higher up, and battle and hunting scenes on the processional platforms. The original tower (shikhara) collapsed long ago — its fate is debated, but it apparently fell by the 19th century — and the interior of the main structure was filled with sand to prevent collapse of the remaining porch structure (jagamohana). UNESCO World Heritage Site. Konark is about 65 kilometres from Bhubaneswar; a light and sound show runs in the evenings.

## 6. Dholavira, Gujarat

Dholavira, on the Khadir island in the Rann of Kutch, is one of the largest and best-preserved cities of the Indus Valley Civilization, occupied from roughly 3000 to 1500 BC. It is unique among Indus sites for the sophistication of its water management system — a series of reservoirs and channels designed to capture and store the monsoon runoff in an arid environment — and for a large inscription in the Indus script (still undeciphered) displayed above the northern gateway, the longest such inscription known. The city's three-part division — citadel, middle town, and lower town — follows the general Indus urban pattern, but Dholavira's layout is unusually regular and its monumental stone construction distinctive. UNESCO World Heritage Site since 2021. Accessible from Bhuj in Gujarat; the surrounding landscape of the white salt desert is dramatically beautiful.

## 7. Ellora Caves, Maharashtra

The Ellora cave complex contains 34 monasteries and temples cut into a basalt escarpment between roughly the 6th and 11th centuries AD, representing Buddhist, Hindu, and Jain sacred architecture in remarkable physical proximity. The Kailasa Temple (Cave 16), commissioned by the Rashtrakuta king Dantidurga around 760 AD, is carved from the top down as a monolithic freestanding temple — an entire mountain of rock removed to reveal a temple complex covering roughly twice the area of the Parthenon. The Buddhist caves (Caves 1–12) include the three-storey Tin Tal monastery; the Jain caves (Caves 30–34) preserve exquisite carved ceilings. UNESCO World Heritage Site. Ellora is about 30 kilometres from Aurangabad in Maharashtra.

## 8. Ajanta Caves, Maharashtra

The Ajanta caves, cut into a horseshoe-shaped gorge above the Waghora river, contain 30 Buddhist cave monuments with some of the finest surviving examples of ancient Indian painting. The caves were carved in two phases: the earliest group (roughly 2nd century BC to 1st century AD) and a larger group commissioned during the Vakataka dynasty (roughly 460–480 AD). The later caves' wall paintings — depicting the life of the Buddha and Jataka tales in rich mineral pigments — are considered masterpieces of world art. The caves were abandoned after the Vakataka dynasty's collapse and their paintings were unknown to the outside world until 1819, when a British hunting party stumbled upon them. UNESCO World Heritage Site. About 100 kilometres northeast of Aurangabad.

## 9. Vijayanagara Beyond Hampi — Lepakshi, Andhra Pradesh

The Virupaksha Swamy Temple at Lepakshi, built by the Vijayanagara commander Virupanna Nayaka in the mid-16th century, is among the finest examples of Vijayanagara mural painting. The ceiling of its mukhamantapa (outer hall) is covered with a continuous narrative of mythological scenes executed in rich, well-preserved pigments. The Nandi monolith at Lepakshi — a seated bull around 4.5 metres high — is one of the largest in India. The site is about 130 kilometres north of Bengaluru.

## 10. Champaner-Pavagadh, Gujarat

Champaner-Pavagadh is an archaeological landscape combining Chalcolithic remains on the Pavagadh hill with a medieval Hindu temple complex and the most complete surviving example of an early Sultanate-period city. The Jami Masjid of Champaner (built in the early 16th century under the Gujarat Sultanate) is widely considered the finest example of pre-Mughal mosque architecture in India, its two minarets, elaborate carved stone screens, and interior forest of columns combining local Hindu and Jain craft traditions with Islamic spatial organization. UNESCO World Heritage Site. About 45 kilometres east of Vadodara.

India's ancient sites are connected by an extensive rail network, and the Archaeological Survey of India manages hundreds of ticketed protected monuments across the country. The October-to-March season avoids the worst heat at lowland sites; hill sites and the far south are manageable year-round. Entry fees are nominal for most sites, with higher charges for foreigners at the most visited UNESCO monuments. Local Archaeological Survey offices can advise on less-visited sites in each region.

## Who Were the Celts?

The term 'Celts' has been applied, stretched, contested and rehabilitated by scholars over two centuries of debate. In the strictest linguistic sense it denotes speakers of the Celtic branch of the Indo-European language family — a group that includes Gaulish, Goidelic (the ancestor of Irish, Scottish Gaelic and Manx) and Brittonic (ancestral to Welsh, Cornish and Breton). In the broader archaeological sense it has been used, more controversially, to describe the material culture of the European Iron Age, particularly the Hallstatt and La Tène cultural complexes that flourished from roughly 800 to 50 BC across central and western Europe.

The ancient Greek and Roman sources that give us descriptions of the Celts — their appearance, their battle practices, their ritual habits — were written by outsiders who were often enemies, always condescending, and frequently treating the Celts as a literary archetype of the barbarian. The reality, as the archaeology increasingly demonstrates, was more complex: a diverse range of Iron Age societies sharing certain material traditions, languages and religious practices across a vast territory from Anatolia (where the Galatians settled in the 3rd century BC) to Ireland, but lacking the political unity that the classical sources sometimes projected onto them.

## The Hallstatt Culture

The Hallstatt culture, named for a lakeside site in the Austrian Alps where a large Iron Age cemetery was excavated from the 1840s onward, spans roughly 800 to 450 BC and is conventionally divided into an earlier Bronze Age phase and a later Iron Age phase. The Hallstatt site itself was remarkable: several thousand graves spanning several centuries, excavated with varying degrees of care by early archaeologists and more rigorously by later ones, revealing rich burials of salt mine workers and their elite patrons alongside spectacular metalwork, imported goods from the Mediterranean and central Europe, and the earliest preserved organic textiles from the region.

The salt mines at Hallstatt were the economic foundation of this culture's prosperity. Salt was as valuable as any metal in the ancient world — essential for food preservation — and the Hallstatt mines were producing it in industrial quantities. Miners who died in accidents deep underground have been found naturally preserved by the salt; their leather clothing, wooden tools and the physical traces of their work survive in extraordinary condition. UNESCO inscribed Hallstatt–Dachstein / Salzkammergut as a Cultural Landscape in 1997.

Elite burials of the early Iron Age in western Europe — the so-called 'princely' burials of Burgundy, the Rhine valley and southern Germany — were furnished with imported Greek and Etruscan luxury goods: Attic black-figure pottery, bronze wine-mixing vessels (krateres), and elaborate goldwork. The Vix crater, found in a burial mound at Mont Lassois in Burgundy and dating to around 500 BC, is a Greek bronze vessel standing 1.64 metres high and weighing over 200 kilograms — the largest surviving ancient bronze vessel. It was presumably traded north along a route that brought wine and prestige goods from the Mediterranean in exchange for raw materials, slaves and amber.

## La Tène and the Flowering of Celtic Art

Around 450 BC, the centre of gravity in Celtic culture shifted westward and northward, and a new artistic style emerged from the fusion of Hallstatt traditions with Mediterranean and Scythian influences. The La Tène style, named for a site on Lake Neuchâtel in Switzerland where a large deposit of metalwork and weapons was found in the 19th century, is characterised by curvilinear abstraction, interlocking spirals, trumpet curves and stylised animal forms that anticipate later insular Celtic art of Ireland and Scotland by centuries.

La Tène metalwork appears across a vast geographical range: swords with decorated scabbards, torcs (twisted metal neck rings), shield bosses, chariot fittings, mirrors and brooches. The craftsmanship demonstrates a high level of technical sophistication — the use of coral and glass inlay, the casting of complex three-dimensional forms in bronze — that contradicts any notion of Iron Age Celtic societies as technologically backward.

## Hillforts: Architecture of Power

The most visible and geographically widespread legacy of Iron Age Celtic Europe is the hillfort — a defended hilltop enclosure demarcated by one or more circuits of earthwork banks and ditches, sometimes reinforced with timber or stone. Several thousand hillforts survive in various states of preservation across Britain, Ireland and continental Europe; estimates for Britain alone range from around 3,000 to over 4,000.

Hillforts were not purely military installations. The largest of them, the oppida of the late Iron Age (roughly 150–50 BC), were substantial urban settlements with internal street systems, metalworking workshops, coin minting, long-distance trade connections and dense occupation. But even the smaller earlier hillforts of the 8th to 5th centuries BC show evidence of permanent habitation, grain storage, animal keeping and craft production.

Maiden Castle in Dorset, England, is among the most impressive in the British Isles. Built progressively from around 600 BC onward on a chalk ridge east of modern Dorchester, it eventually enclosed roughly 47 acres (19 hectares) within a complex series of multiple rampart circuits and sophisticated inturned entrances — architectural features designed to funnel attackers into killing grounds. At its height it may have housed a population of several thousand. The excavations led by Mortimer Wheeler between 1934 and 1937 found a war cemetery at the eastern entrance dated to the Roman conquest of around AD 43, with skeletal remains showing sword wounds and, in at least one case, a Roman iron bolt embedded in a spine.

Danebury in Hampshire, excavated intensively by Barry Cunliffe between 1969 and 1988 in one of the most systematic Iron Age excavations ever conducted in Britain, revealed the internal organisation of a hillfort in extraordinary detail: streets of rectangular post-built houses, large granary buildings, a central area apparently reserved for ritual use with pits containing specially deposited animal bones and complete pots, and evidence for craft production including weaving, iron smelting and leather working. Danebury was occupied from roughly 550 to 100 BC.

## Continental Oppida

In continental Europe, the late Iron Age oppida represent a qualitative shift in the scale of Celtic urbanism. Caesar's Gallic Wars of 58–51 BC are fought largely around oppida: Bibracte (Mont Beuvray in Burgundy), the capital of the Aedui tribe, covered roughly 200 hectares on a hilltop and has been partially excavated since the 19th century, yielding workshops, aristocratic residences and evidence of coin production. Manching in Bavaria, built on a low plateau near modern Ingolstadt, enclosed roughly 380 hectares with a timber-laced stone wall (murus gallicus) several kilometres long and was one of the most urbanised centres north of the Alps in the 2nd and 1st centuries BC.

The murus gallicus, described by Caesar and confirmed archaeologically across a wide area, used iron nails to bind timber beams into the stone wall core — a structural technique that produced a robust facing resistant to battering. Tens of thousands of iron nails have been found at Bibracte alone.

## Ireland and the Insular Tradition

In Ireland, which lay outside the Roman conquest zone and preserved its Iron Age traditions later than continental Europe or Britain, hillforts and related monument types continued in use and in cultural significance through the early medieval period. The Hill of Tara in County Meath, a complex of interconnected earthworks including the Rath na Ríogh (Fort of the Kings), the Mound of the Hostages (a Neolithic passage tomb reused in the Bronze Age), and numerous barrows and ring-ditches, was the symbolic seat of the High Kings of Ireland. Its landscape has never been systematically excavated; a large iron Age burial recently identified by geophysical survey suggests that the complex is far larger than surface features imply.

Navan Fort (Emain Macha) in County Armagh, the ancient capital of Ulster celebrated in the Ulster Cycle tales, was excavated from the late 1950s onward. The site includes a large circular enclosure containing a massive circular timber structure built around 95 BC and then deliberately filled with limestone blocks and burned — a sequence that suggests a monumental ritual act of destruction rather than a military assault.

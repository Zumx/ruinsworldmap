Syria sits at one of the most consequential crossroads in the ancient world. The fertile corridor between the Mediterranean coast and the Syrian steppe, watered by the Orontes, the Euphrates, and their tributaries, has been inhabited almost continuously for more than ten thousand years. The first cities anywhere on earth appeared in this region; the oldest known alphabet was invented here; empires from the Akkadian through the Assyrian, Achaemenid, Macedonian, Seleucid, Roman, and Byzantine have left their marks on the landscape. Later, the Arab Islamic conquest of the 7th century AD added another transformative layer. Syria's archaeological richness is extraordinary — and in recent years it has suffered grievously. The conflicts since 2011 have damaged or destroyed significant portions of Palmyra, Aleppo's old city, and other key sites. What follows describes these places as they were known from archaeology, acknowledging that the current state of access and preservation requires checking before any visit.

## 1. Palmyra (Tadmor), Homs Governorate

An oasis city in the Syrian desert, Palmyra was the capital of the Palmyrene Kingdom and a crucial caravan hub between Rome and Parthia. At its height in the 2nd and 3rd centuries AD, it was a magnificent Romanised city with distinctively Semitic artistic traditions. The colonnaded street — the cardo maximus — stretched nearly one kilometre, flanked by Corinthian columns. The Temple of Bel, built around 32 AD and incorporating an inner cella of striking originality, was one of the best-preserved Roman temples in the Near East until 2015, when it was largely destroyed by ISIS. The Valley of the Tombs, with its tower tombs rising from the desert, and the theatre, the tetrapylon, and the agora remain partly standing. Palmyra is a UNESCO World Heritage Site; portions have been stabilised since 2017 but much remains fragile or destroyed.

## 2. Apamea, Hama Governorate

On the west bank of the Orontes near modern Qal'at al-Madiq, Apamea was one of the four great cities of the Seleucid Empire, founded by Seleucus I around 300 BC and named for his Persian wife. Under Rome it became a prosperous metropolis and a key base for the legions guarding the eastern frontier. The colonnaded main street — at roughly 1,850 metres one of the longest known in the Roman world — was adorned with twisted fluted columns, an unusual decorative feature. The mosaic museum built at the site contained exceptional floor mosaics, though many were looted during the conflict after 2011. The theatre and the surrounding residential and commercial quarters were partially excavated by Belgian teams over decades. Apamea's sheer scale rewards careful exploration despite the damage.

## 3. Bosra, Daraa Governorate

In the Hauran region near the Jordanian border, Bosra was the capital of the Roman province of Arabia Petraea from 106 AD and a major caravan centre on the route between Arabia and the Mediterranean. The Roman theatre here is one of the best-preserved in the world: built in the 2nd century AD and encased within a medieval Arab citadel that protected it through the centuries, the theatre retains its full cavea (seating), stage wall, and colonnaded portico. The surrounding city preserves a colonnaded street, triumphal arches, baths, a cathedral, and a Nabataean arch. Bosra was a place of significance in early Islamic history — the monk Bahira traditionally recognised the future prophet Muhammad here during a caravan journey. UNESCO listed it in 1980. It has been relatively less damaged by the recent conflict than sites further north.

## 4. Krak des Chevaliers, Homs Governorate

On a strategic ridge above the Homs Gap, Krak des Chevaliers is the most impressive surviving Crusader castle in the world, held by the Knights Hospitaller from 1142 to 1271. The concentric fortification — inner and outer rings with towers, a great hall, chapel, stables, and water reservoir — was never taken by assault; it surrendered through diplomacy when its garrison was finally reduced to a small number. The architecture blends French Romanesque and Gothic military styles with Islamic influences picked up in the Holy Land. The castle is built on the site of an earlier Kurdish garrison. UNESCO listed it jointly with Qal'at Salah El-Din in 2006. The site sustained some damage during the 2011–13 fighting but remains substantially intact and was accessible to visitors before the conflict.

## 5. Ugarit (Ras Shamra), Latakia Governorate

On a tel near the port city of Latakia, Ugarit was one of the major city-states of the Late Bronze Age, flourishing between roughly 1450 and 1200 BC before being destroyed — apparently suddenly, by the Sea Peoples and associated disruptions of the Bronze Age Collapse. Ugarit is famous as the source of the Ugaritic alphabet, one of the earliest alphabetic scripts known, preserved on clay tablets that revolutionised understanding of ancient Near Eastern religion, mythology, and diplomacy when excavated by French archaeologists from 1929 onward. The site preserves the palace, royal tombs, temples to Baal and Dagon, and residential quarters. The tablets are now divided between the National Museum in Damascus and the Louvre. The excavated site itself has a quiet, agricultural-field quality typical of Bronze Age tels.

## 6. Dura-Europos, Deir ez-Zor Governorate

On a plateau above the Euphrates near the Iraqi border, Dura-Europos was a Macedonian, Parthian, and finally Roman garrison city that flourished from around 300 BC until its dramatic destruction by Sasanian Persian forces around 256 AD. The city's sudden end preserved an extraordinary cross-section of late antique religious life: a Christian house church with the earliest known Christian figural paintings (now in the Yale University Art Gallery), a Jewish synagogue with the most extensive cycle of Jewish narrative paintings in the ancient world, Mithraic temples, and Greco-Roman and Parthian shrines. The city walls, with their Roman siege tunnels and Sasanian countermines, constitute the best-documented ancient siege complex known. The site has been heavily looted since 2011.

## 7. Ebla (Tell Mardikh), Idlib Governorate

In the low hills of the Idlib plain, Tell Mardikh is the site of Ebla, one of the earliest Syrian city-states, which reached its greatest power around 2300 BC. Italian excavations from 1964 onward uncovered a royal palace with a remarkable cuneiform archive of over 17,000 tablets — one of the largest Bronze Age archives ever found — detailing the economic, administrative, and diplomatic life of a sophisticated pre-Akkadian city. The palace layout, the royal tombs of the Middle Bronze Age, and the temples visible on the tel surface indicate continuous urban development across the 3rd and 2nd millennia BC. The Idlib region has been particularly badly affected by the Syrian conflict; access to the site has been severely restricted.

## 8. Ain Dara, Afrin Region

In the Afrin region of northwestern Syria, Ain Dara is an Iron Age temple site occupied from roughly 1300 to 740 BC. The temple itself, built on a massive stone platform, bears striking resemblances in plan and scale to the description of Solomon's Temple as recorded in the Hebrew Bible — though the comparison is scholarly rather than historical. The approach to the temple's cella was guarded by colossal basalt lion sculptures, and a series of giant footprints carved into the threshold stones imply the passage of a deity. The temple was damaged by a Turkish air strike in 2018. Prior to the conflict it was one of the more accessible and well-interpreted archaeological sites in northern Syria.

## 9. Mari (Tell Hariri), Deir ez-Zor Governorate

On the middle Euphrates near Abu Kamal at the Iraqi border, Mari was one of the major city-states of the Early and Middle Bronze Age, occupied from around 2900 to 1760 BC when the Babylonian king Hammurabi destroyed it. French excavations from 1933 onward exposed a remarkable royal palace of the 2nd millennium BC with hundreds of rooms, wall paintings depicting royal ritual, and over 20,000 cuneiform tablets detailing the administrative and political life of an Amorite kingdom. The tablets revolutionised knowledge of the period when deciphered. The palace is among the best-known examples of Bronze Age palatial architecture in the Near East. The site has been looted and damaged during the recent conflict.

## 10. The Dead Cities of the Limestone Massif, Idlib and Aleppo Governorates

The 'Dead Cities' (Ancient Villages of Northern Syria) are a group of around 700 abandoned late antique settlements on the limestone hills between Aleppo and Idlib, occupied from roughly the 1st through 7th centuries AD and then progressively abandoned between the 8th and 10th centuries, probably due to shifts in the olive oil trade that had sustained them. They preserve an unusually complete picture of rural life in the Byzantine Near East: houses, churches, bath buildings, and olive presses built in well-cut limestone that has weathered the centuries. The site of Qalb Lozeh contains one of the earliest known large basilica churches in the region. The entire group is a UNESCO World Heritage Site (2011). Many individual sites in the region have been caught in the conflict; some are relatively intact, others damaged.

## Planning Your Visit

As of the mid-2020s, travel to Syria remains restricted and potentially dangerous; check the latest advice from your government's foreign affairs ministry before planning any trip. The most accessible sites for those who can visit are generally in the south near Bosra and the Hauran, and portions of coastal Latakia province. Palmyra has been partially reopened for carefully managed heritage visits since its recapture but requires specific permissions. Working with a specialist tour operator who has up-to-date on-the-ground knowledge is strongly recommended. Syria's sites represent a heritage of global importance, and interest in their eventual recovery is shared by archaeologists and travellers worldwide.

## The Vocabulary of Roman Architecture

Roman ruins appear on every continent where the empire extended, from the Antonine Wall in Scotland to the coast of North Africa, from Portugal's Atlantic edge to the cities of the Levant. Their physical vocabulary — the arched concrete vaulting, the brick-faced walls, the ashlar colonnades, the precisely engineered drainage systems — is distinctive enough that a visitor with some basic knowledge can read a Roman site almost anywhere in the world and begin to understand what they are seeing.

The Romans were systematic urban planners. A new Roman town typically began with two main streets — the cardo, running roughly north-south, and the decumanus, running east-west — crossing at a central point where the forum was sited. Around this skeleton, insulae (city blocks) were laid out on a grid. Military camps (castra) followed an even stricter template: a rectangular earthwork with four gates, internal roads leading from gate to gate, and standardised building positions for the commander's headquarters (principia), granaries (horrea) and barracks. Many Roman towns grew from such camps, which is why so many English place names ending in -chester or -caster derive from the Latin castra.

## The Forum: Heart of the City

The forum was the urban core — part marketplace, part civic assembly ground, part religious precinct. A typical forum consisted of an open rectangular piazza surrounded on three or more sides by colonnaded porticoes, with a basilica (a large hall used for law courts and commerce) along one side and a temple, usually dedicated to the Capitoline triad of Jupiter, Juno and Minerva, at the opposite end.

The Roman Forum in Rome is the most famous example, though centuries of spoliation and later construction have made it harder to read than many provincial equivalents. The forum at Pompeii, preserved under volcanic deposits from the 79 AD eruption, gives a clearer sense of the type: the open space, the colonnade fragments, the Temple of Jupiter dominating the north end, the covered halls of the macellum (market building) and the basilica flanking the sides. The forum at Lepcis Magna in Libya, part of one of the best-preserved Roman cities in the world, preserves its columns, arches and paving in remarkable condition, its sandstone warm and detailed under the Libyan sun.

## Baths: Public Architecture at Scale

The thermae — public baths — were among the largest and most architecturally ambitious structures the Romans built. They were not merely facilities for washing. They were the social centres of Roman urban life: places for exercise, conversation, business, performance and socialising, accessible to almost all free residents at low or no cost. The great imperial thermae of Rome — the Baths of Caracalla (completed around 217 AD) and the Baths of Diocletian (around 306 AD) — covered enormous areas and could accommodate thousands of bathers simultaneously.

The bathing sequence moved through a series of rooms at graduated temperatures: the frigidarium (cold room), tepidarium (warm room) and caldarium (hot room), with the final room — the laconicum — sometimes an intense dry-heat space similar to a modern sauna. This sequence required an elaborate heating system: the hypocaust, a raised floor supported on brick pillars (pilae) through which hot air from a furnace (praefurnium) circulated, heating the floor and, through hollow box-tiles in the walls, the walls themselves. The hypocaust is one of the most recognisable features of Roman ruins in Britain and northwest Europe; the small square brick piers are unmistakable once seen.

The Roman Baths at Bath in England — Aquae Sulis to the Romans, built around a natural hot spring from the 1st century AD onward — preserve the Great Bath, a lead-lined pool still filled by the spring water. It is one of the most visited Roman sites in northern Europe, and the museum around it displays an exceptional collection of votive objects thrown into the sacred spring.

## Amphitheatres and Theatres

The amphitheatre — the oval or elliptical arena for gladiatorial combat, animal hunts (venationes) and public executions — is among the most recognisable Roman building types. The Colosseum in Rome, completed under Titus in 80 AD, seated roughly 50,000 spectators and featured a complex underground service level (the hypogeum) for animal cages, stage machinery and access corridors. Its structural system — concrete barrel vaults carried on radial walls, creating the curved seating tiers — established the template for sports stadia that persists to the present day.

Provincial amphitheatres ranged from the massive — the amphitheatre at Capua in Campania, second in size only to the Colosseum, and the Arena of Nîmes in France, still used for bullfights and concerts — to modest earthwork structures in smaller towns. In Britain, the amphitheatres at Caerleon in Wales (attached to the legionary fortress) and at Chester survive partly as grassed earthworks.

The Roman theatre, distinct from the amphitheatre, was a semicircular structure for dramatic performance. The cavea (seating) was built into a hillside wherever terrain permitted or constructed on artificial vaulting where it did not. The stage wall (scaenae frons) was an elaborate multi-storey architectural facade, sometimes three storeys high, dressed with columns, niches and statuary. The theatre at Orange in Provence preserves its stage wall complete to the roofline — a unique survival — while the theatre at Aspendos in Turkey retains similar completeness.

## Aqueducts and Water Engineering

Roman water supply was an engineering achievement of the first order. The city of Rome at its imperial peak was served by eleven major aqueducts delivering an estimated one million cubic metres of water daily. Provincial cities were equally well served by proportion; access to running water for fountains (nymphaea), public baths, private houses and industrial premises was a defining feature of Roman urbanism.

Aqueducts carried water from springs or rivers in the uplands along a precisely graded channel (specus) to the city. The gradient needed to be sufficient to maintain flow but not so steep as to cause erosion — typically around 1 in 1000 to 1 in 3000 over long distances. Where the route crossed a valley, the channel was carried on an arcade of arches. The Pont du Gard near Nîmes in southern France is the finest surviving example: three tiers of arches, the topmost carrying the water channel, crossing the Gardon river at a height of nearly 49 metres. Built in the 1st century AD, it is the highest surviving Roman aqueduct bridge, a UNESCO World Heritage Site, and still structurally sound after two millennia.

At the receiving end, water was distributed through lead and terracotta pipes from a castellum divisorium (distribution tank) to fountains, baths and private connections. The pressure system — water delivered to a high tank and distributed under gravity — was sophisticated enough to supply upper-storey pipes in some towns.

## Temples and Religious Architecture

Roman religious architecture adapted Greek temple forms — the rectangular cella on a high podium, the columned porch — but gave them a distinctly Roman character. The Maison Carrée at Nîmes, built around 16 BC and dedicated to Augustus's adopted sons Gaius and Lucius Caesar, is the best-preserved Roman temple in the world: its full colonnade, entablature and pediment intact, its proportions expressing the Corinthian order at its most refined. It is a working reminder that most Roman ruins once looked like this.

Provincial temples ranged from small shrines to substantial colonnaded structures. Britain's best-preserved Romano-Celtic temples, the rectangular Romano-Celtic form with an ambulatory surrounding a central tower, appear at sites like Uley in Gloucestershire and Maiden Castle in Dorset. Temples to eastern mystery cults — Mithras, Isis, Serapis — were typically smaller, more secretive structures; the Temple of Mithras excavated in London (and now displayed at the Bloomberg headquarters) is among the most important Roman discoveries in the city.

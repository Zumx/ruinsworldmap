## Art from the Deep Past

The painted caves of western Europe represent the oldest large body of figurative art in the world. For roughly twenty thousand years — from around 40,000 to 10,000 years ago, spanning the period archaeologists call the Upper Palaeolithic — anatomically modern humans living as hunter-gatherers in the landscapes of glacial Europe made images on the walls of caves and rock shelters: painted animals, geometric signs, handprints, and occasional human figures. These images survive because they were made in sheltered locations, because cave environments maintain stable temperature and humidity, and because the people who made them generally did not live in the caves they decorated — the images were not worn away by daily domestic activity.

The discovery of this art tradition was profoundly disorienting to 19th-century European thought. When Marcelino Sanz de Sautuola first brought his daughter Maria to Altamira in Cantabria, northern Spain, in 1879 and she looked up at the polychrome paintings of bison on the cave ceiling, neither he nor the academic establishment of the time could accept that prehistoric people were capable of such naturalistic representation. The art was dismissed as a forgery for roughly two decades before the discovery of additional decorated caves — La Mouthe in 1895, Font-de-Gaume in 1901, Combarelles in 1901 — made denial impossible. By the time Henri Breuil, the French priest and archaeologist who documented more than a hundred decorated sites across his career, was working systematically in the early 20th century, it was clear that the cave art tradition was genuine, ancient, and widespread.

## Altamira and the Polychrome Masterpieces

Altamira remains the defining monument of Palaeolithic cave art. The main decorated chamber, known as the Polychrome Hall, measures roughly 18 by 9 metres and its ceiling is covered with a composition of approximately 25 animals — bison, horses, deer, and a possible wild boar — painted and engraved in red, black, and brown using ochre, charcoal, and haematite. The painters exploited the natural undulations of the limestone ceiling to give the bison a three-dimensional quality: each animal is positioned so that a natural bulge in the rock forms the animal's flank or shoulder. Radiocarbon dating of organic material within the paints has placed the main composition at around 14,000–13,000 BC, with some earlier images in the cave.

Altamira was designated a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1985 (extended in 2008 to include seventeen other Cantabrian sites), and the original cave is now closed to all but a handful of researchers due to concerns about CO2, humidity, and microbiological damage from visitor breath. An excellent replica — the Neocueva — adjacent to the site museum allows visitors to experience the scale and quality of the composition without threatening the original.

## Lascaux

Lascaux in the Dordogne valley of southwestern France is the most famous decorated cave in the world, known since its accidental discovery by four teenagers in September 1940. The cave is exceptional for the density and variety of its painted programme: the Great Hall of the Bulls contains aurochs (wild cattle) up to five metres long, the largest Palaeolithic paintings known, together with horses and a stag. The Axial Diverticule — a narrow passage painted on both walls and ceiling — includes the famous 'Chinese Horse', a small yellow horse painted with extraordinary delicacy, and a frieze of swimming deer. The Shaft of the Dead Man contains one of the very rare human figures in Palaeolithic art: a stick figure falling before a disembowelled bison, next to a rhinoceros. The date of the Lascaux paintings centres around 17,000 years ago.

Lascaux was opened to visitors after the war but closed again in 1963 when it became apparent that the CO2 and heat from twenty thousand annual visitors was destroying the paintings with green algae and white calcite growth. The 'green illness' and 'white illness' affected significant areas of the painted surfaces before the closure. A partial replica was constructed nearby, and the much more accurate Lascaux IV International Cave Art Centre, opened in 2016, uses photogrammetric scanning to reproduce the cave at full scale with exceptional fidelity.

## Chauvet: The Oldest

The Chauvet-Pont d'Arc cave in the Ardèche, discovered in December 1994 by Jean-Marie Chauvet and two colleagues, pushed the origins of European cave art back dramatically. Radiocarbon dates on charcoal from the cave floor and pigment samples from the paintings cluster around 36,000 years ago, making Chauvet the oldest substantially dated decorated cave in the world. The style of the paintings — not the primitive scratchings one might expect from the earliest art — is sophisticated and varied: lions, rhinos, mammoths, bears, hyenas, and horses in dynamic compositions that exploit panel edges and natural rock features. The famous 'Panel of Lions' shows a group of cave lions apparently charging, depicted with a sense of overlapping and movement that anticipates artistic conventions usually attributed to much later periods. Chauvet is closed to visitors and is preserved entirely as a research site; a full-scale replica, the Caverne du Pont d'Arc, opened in 2015 nearby.

## Beyond Europe: Global Cave Art

The focus on Franco-Cantabrian cave art in the mainstream literature reflects the concentration of research in that region rather than the actual distribution of Palaeolithic image-making. Rock art — paintings and engravings on cave walls and open rock surfaces — is a global phenomenon. In Sulawesi, Indonesia, a cave at Leang Tedongnge contains a painting of a pig dated by uranium-series methods to at least 45,500 years ago — currently the oldest dated figurative painting in the world — demonstrating that representational art was being made in Southeast Asia at the same time as in Europe, likely reflecting a shared human cognitive capacity rather than a European invention.

In southern Africa, the rock shelters of the Drakensberg in South Africa and Lesotho contain tens of thousands of San rock paintings, the most recent dating to the 19th century AD but with the tradition extending back to at least 26,000 years ago. The paintings are interpreted through the ethnographic record of San ritual and belief as depictions of trance experiences and shamanic practice — an interpretive framework that has influenced broader debates about the meaning of Palaeolithic European art. In the Americas, the cave art of Cueva de las Manos in Patagonia, Argentina — famous for its stencilled hands and hunting scenes — has been dated to between roughly 9,000 and 13,000 years ago.

## The Question of Meaning

Why was cave art made? Scholarly interpretations have changed substantially over the past century. Breuil's 'hunting magic' hypothesis — that images of animals were made to ensure hunting success — dominated for decades but could not explain the selective range of species depicted (predominantly horses and bison, not the animals most commonly eaten) or the absence of images of humans killing or eating animals. André Leroi-Gourhan's structuralist analysis in the 1960s proposed that cave art reflected a system of gender-based symbolic oppositions encoded in the recurring associations of species and positions. More recent interpretations, informed by the neuropsychological model, argue that the caves were spaces for altered states of consciousness during ritual practice, and that the images represent visions experienced in trance.

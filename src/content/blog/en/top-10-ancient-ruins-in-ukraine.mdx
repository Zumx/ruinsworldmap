Ukraine's territory covers one of the most archaeologically significant landscapes in Eurasia: the Pontic steppe, the great grassland corridor through which successive waves of pastoralists and nomads moved between Central Asia and Europe for millennia, and the northern shore of the Black Sea, where Greek colonies took root from the 7th century BC and grew into substantial cities trading with the Scythian, Sarmatian, and later Gothic peoples of the interior. Long before the Greeks arrived, Ukraine was home to some of the largest pre-urban settlements known anywhere in the world — the Trypillia culture mega-sites of the 4th millennium BC, some enclosing several hundred hectares. After the Greek colonial period came the Scythian royal burial mounds — kurgans — that punctuate the steppe in their thousands, and the medieval Rus city of Kyiv, one of the great medieval European capitals. The Russian invasion that began in 2022 has placed much of this heritage under grave and immediate threat; sites in occupied territories and in combat zones are subject to damage, looting, and destruction.

## 1. Chersonesus Taurica, Sevastopol (Crimea)

Chersonesus was founded around 422 BC by Greek colonists from Heraclea Pontica on the rocky Crimean peninsula and flourished for over a millennium as a polis, a Roman ally, and eventually a Byzantine city. It is the most extensively excavated Greek colonial site on the northern Black Sea coast, with a large grid-plan town, a theatre, temples, bathhouses, and necropoleis covering hundreds of hectares. The Byzantine basilicas and towers of the medieval period overlay the Hellenistic city, creating a complex multi-period palimpsest. A famous oath inscription of the late 4th century BC — citizens swearing to defend the city's democratic constitution — is one of the best-preserved epigraphic documents from the Greek colonial world. Chersonesus is a UNESCO World Heritage Site. Access has been severely restricted since the Russian annexation of Crimea in 2014.

## 2. Olbia, Mykolaiv Oblast

Olbia was one of the most important Greek colonies on the northern Black Sea, founded around 647 BC at the mouth of the Southern Bug (Hypanis) River. It was a prosperous commercial city trading grain, fish, and slaves for Greek manufactured goods, with a population at its peak of perhaps 15,000–20,000. Excavations have revealed an upper city with agora, temenos, and public buildings, and a lower city of residential and commercial quarters descending to the river. The site produced remarkable bronze coins cast in the shape of dolphins and arrowheads — among the earliest monetary forms on the Pontic steppe. Olbia was destroyed by the Getai around 48 BC and later revived as a Roman client city. The site is a national reserve near the village of Parutyne; access conditions have varied significantly since 2022.

## 3. Kamianets-Podilskyi Fortress, Khmelnytskyi Oblast

The fortress of Kamianets-Podilskyi stands on a rocky promontory surrounded on three sides by the Smotrych River gorge, a natural defensive position occupied since at least the early medieval period. The stone Old Fortress that survives today is primarily a 14th–17th century structure, built and modified by Lithuanian, Polish, Ottoman, and Cossack rulers in succession, but it incorporates earlier foundations and has been inhabited since at least the Thracian period. The complex of towers — the Russian Tower, Polish Tower, Water Tower — and connecting curtain walls, together with the medieval city preserved in the canyon below, make Kamianets-Podilskyi one of the best-preserved fortified medieval towns in Eastern Europe. The site is in western Ukraine and has been accessible to visitors throughout recent years.

## 4. Tustan, Lviv Oblast

Tustan is a medieval rock-cut fortress in the Carpathian mountains of western Ukraine, where timber structures were built directly onto and into the sandstone outcrops of a rocky ridge. From roughly the 9th to the 15th century, Tustan served as a customs point on the Przemysl-Kyiv trade route and as a refuge fortification. The rock surfaces bear thousands of carved niches, grooves, and post-holes that reveal the outlines of multi-storey timber buildings — gates, towers, living quarters — constructed around the rock without mortar. The rocks also carry medieval inscriptions and graffiti of considerable historical interest. Tustan is a national historical and architectural reserve, accessible from the Skole district of Lviv Oblast. It is far from the current conflict zones.

## 5. Bilhorod-Dnistrovskyi (Tyras/Akkerman), Odesa Oblast

Bilhorod-Dnistrovskyi on the Dnister estuary is the site of ancient Tyras, a Greek colony founded in the 6th century BC, later a Roman outpost, and then successive medieval fortifications built by Byzantine, Genoese, and Ottoman rulers. The visible castle — Akkerman Fortress, whose name derives from the Turkic for 'white stone' — is substantially a 13th–15th-century structure whose towers and curtain walls enclose a large courtyard. The Genoese built it on Byzantine foundations, and the Ottomans modified it after 1484. The walls and towers survive in good condition and are extensively studied. Earlier Tyras lies beneath the modern city; occasional excavations have recovered Greek and Roman material. The fortress is accessible from Bilhorod-Dnistrovskyi, roughly 80 kilometres from Odesa.

## 6. Scythian Kurgans (Chortomlyk, Solokha, Tovsta Mohyla), Dnipropetrovsk Oblast

The steppe of south-central Ukraine is studded with thousands of Scythian burial mounds — kurgans — of which the most lavishly furnished belong to the Scythian royal elite of the 5th through 3rd centuries BC. The Chortomlyk kurgan, excavated in the 19th century, yielded gold and silver vessels of astonishing craftsmanship including the famous Chortomlyk amphora showing Scythians taming horses. The Tovsta Mohyla kurgan, excavated in 1971, produced the Pectoral — a gold collar of extraordinary intricacy depicting Scythian daily life and mythological scenes — now in the Kyiv museum of Ukrainian history. Solokha yielded a golden comb with a sculptural battle scene on its spine. The kurgans themselves are mounds in the open steppe and are not individually managed, but the finds in Kyiv's museums are among the great treasures of world archaeology. Several major museums housing Scythian gold are in Kyiv and Dnipro.

## 7. Trypillia Mega-Sites, Central Ukraine

The Trypillia (or Cucuteni-Tripolye) culture of the 5th–3rd millennia BC built some of the largest settlements known in Neolithic and Chalcolithic Europe — sites such as Talianky, Maydanets, and Dobrovody in the Cherkasy and Vinnytsia regions covering up to 340 hectares, with hundreds of houses arranged in concentric rings or planned neighbourhoods. These were not cities in the administrative sense but sustained large populations — estimates suggest 5,000–10,000 people or more at the largest sites — for periods of 50–100 years before the entire settlement was deliberately burned and abandoned. The reasons for the burning remain debated: ritual purification, pest control, or ceremonial abandonment are all proposed. The sites are low mounds in agricultural land; a dedicated Trypillia museum operates near the village of Trypillia south of Kyiv.

## 8. Panticapaeum (Kerch, Crimea)

Panticapaeum was the capital of the Bosporan Kingdom, a Greek-origin state that controlled the Crimean peninsula and adjacent Black Sea territory from the 5th century BC to the 4th century AD. Founded by Milesian colonists around 600 BC on the site of present-day Kerch, it grew into a major city and a key exporter of Pontic grain to mainland Greece. The royal kurgans of the Bosporan kings — Melek-Chesmen, Royal Kurgan, Mela-Gora — on the outskirts of Kerch contain monumental corbelled stone burial chambers with long dromos passages, dating to the 4th century BC. They are among the finest examples of Hellenistic funerary architecture in the world. Access is severely restricted under Russian occupation of Crimea since 2014.

## 9. Khortytsia, Zaporizhzhia

The large island of Khortytsia in the Dnipro River at Zaporizhzhia was the historical centre of the Zaporozhian Cossack Host — the fortified camp known as the Sich — from the 16th through the 18th century. The island also contains a much older archaeological sequence: Bronze Age burials, Scythian-period occupation, and early Slavic materials. A national historical and cultural reserve on the island preserves both the Cossack heritage and the prehistoric landscape. The island fell within the combat zone during the 2022–2024 war; conditions at the reserve have been affected by the proximity of the front line near Zaporizhzhia.

## 10. Velyki Vymiachky and Scythian Settlements, Southern Ukraine

The fortified Scythian settlement at Kamenskoye (now identified near modern Velyka Znamianka in Zaporizhzhia Oblast) was one of the largest known Scythian urban sites — a walled town of roughly 12 square kilometres occupied from the 5th to the 3rd century BC, interpreted as a centre for iron-working that supplied the Scythian military aristocracy. The site produced slag, tuyeres, and iron objects in large quantities. While the earthworks of the town are still visible, the site lies in a zone affected by the current conflict and systematic archaeological work has been interrupted.

## Planning Your Visit

As of the mid-2020s, access to Ukrainian archaeological sites requires careful consideration of the security situation, which changes by region. Western Ukrainian sites — Kamianets-Podilskyi, Tustan — have generally been accessible and are in areas not directly affected by active combat. Odesa-region sites including Bilhorod-Dnistrovskyi have been accessible with monitoring. Kyiv's museums, including the collections of Scythian gold, have been largely protected and some remain open. The major steppe and Crimean sites are in occupied or conflict-affected territories and cannot currently be visited safely.

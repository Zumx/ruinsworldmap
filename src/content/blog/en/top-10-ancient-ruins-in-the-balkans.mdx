The Balkans form one of the most archaeologically layered regions in Europe, a peninsula where successive civilisations overlapped and collided across three millennia of recorded history. Greek colonists established cities on the Adriatic and Aegean coasts from the 8th century BC; Philip II of Macedon and his son Alexander built an empire whose cultural legacy reshaped every subsequent civilisation from Egypt to Bactria; Rome absorbed the Balkan provinces one by one from the 2nd century BC onward and invested heavily in roads, cities, and frontier fortifications. The region also sits at the threshold of Europe's deepest prehistory: Neolithic cultures in the Danube basin produced figurative art and settlement patterns that rival anything in the Near East, and the Iron Age Thracians and Dacians left spectacular gold hoards and monumental tombs across the Carpathian arc. After Rome, Byzantium held and developed the same cities for another thousand years. The result is a density of ruins that rewards serious archaeological travel above almost anywhere else in Europe.

## 1. Butrint, Albania

Butrint, on a promontory surrounded by a lake and the Vivari Channel in southwestern Albania, was occupied from the Bronze Age through the late Byzantine period — a span of roughly three thousand years. The earliest substantial visible remains are Hellenistic, including a theatre of the 3rd century BC whose cavea is cut into the hillside and still seats audiences at summer events. Roman additions include a forum, baths, the Triconch Palace, a baptistery with a spectacular mosaic floor, and a late antique circuit wall with the Lion Gate. Venice later added a series of towers and walls. The UNESCO World Heritage inscription dates to 1992; the site is managed as a national park and archaeological reserve. Butrint is reached by road from the port town of Saranda; the ferry crossing from Corfu is a popular approach.

## 2. Gamzigrad (Felix Romuliana), Serbia

Felix Romuliana, near Zaječar in eastern Serbia, is the late Roman palace complex built by the emperor Galerius around 298–311 AD as his personal estate and posthumous deification site. The walled enclosure of roughly 300 by 240 metres preserves two temples, a bath complex, audience chambers, and floor mosaics in extraordinary quality. The name Romuliana honours Galerius's mother Romula; an inscription confirms the identification. The nearby hilltop of Magura contains two consecration tumuli for the burials of Galerius and Romula, a ritual act of imperial apotheosis unique in its archaeological completeness. UNESCO inscribed the site in 2007. It stands among the best-preserved late Roman palatial complexes in the Balkans and is managed by the Zaječar Regional Museum.

## 3. Stobi, North Macedonia

Stobi, at the confluence of the Crna and Vardar rivers in central North Macedonia, was the capital of the Roman province of Macedonia Salutaris and one of the most intensively excavated urban sites in the western Balkans. The city was occupied from the Hellenistic period through the 6th century AD, when an earthquake and subsequent decline ended continuous habitation. The ruins — excavated by North Macedonian and international teams over decades — reveal a theatre, basilicas, a synagogue (one of the earliest excavated synagogues in the Balkans), private townhouses with polychrome floor mosaics, baths, and an episcopal complex. The House of Peristeria's mosaics are particularly fine. The archaeological park is open to visitors; on-site display covers finds from major excavation areas.

## 4. Heraclea Lyncestis, North Macedonia

Heraclea Lyncestis, on the outskirts of Bitola in southwestern North Macedonia, was founded by Philip II of Macedon in the mid-4th century BC. The site preserves Hellenistic and Roman urban layers, with the most visually impressive remains being two large late antique basilicas whose nave floors retain extraordinary polychrome mosaics — one depicting an inhabited vine scroll with birds and animals, among the finest late antique mosaic work in the Balkans. A small theatre and a Roman portico are also partially preserved. The site operates as an open archaeological park within Bitola's suburban fringe; a museum at the entrance displays smaller finds. Bitola is connected by road to Ohrid and to the North Macedonian interior; the site is manageable as a half-day visit.

## 5. Diocletian's Palace, Split, Croatia

Diocletian, the emperor who ended the Crisis of the Third Century and reorganised the Roman Empire, built his retirement palace at Split on the Dalmatian coast between roughly 295 and 305 AD. The complex — measuring approximately 215 by 180 metres — was the most elaborate imperial retirement residence ever constructed, combining the layout of a legionary fortress with the amenities of a luxury villa. After Diocletian's death the palace was maintained and later, during the late antique period, adapted to house a garrison and refugees from the sacked city of Salona. Over the centuries the palace's interior was subdivided into a town that still exists today: modern Split lives inside Diocletian's walls, its streets following Roman lanes and its cellars resting on Diocletian's vaulted substructures. UNESCO inscribed the historic city in 1979. Accessible year-round by air, rail, and ferry.

## 6. Sarmizegetusa Regia, Romania

Sarmizegetusa Regia, in the Orăștie Mountains of Transylvania, was the political, military, and religious capital of the Dacian kingdom at its height under kings Burebista and Decebalus, from roughly the 1st century BC to 106 AD when Trajan destroyed it in his Second Dacian War. The site occupies a plateau at around 1,200 metres altitude, surrounded by a system of outer hill forts. The inner sanctuaries include circular and rectangular temples built of andesite blocks — the sanctuaries' murus dacicus wall construction, with timber-laced stone coursing, is a form unique to Dacian architecture. The 'Dacian Stonehenge', a circular sanctuary of andesite drum columns, is the most photographed element. Not to be confused with the Roman colonial foundation of Colonia Ulpia Traiana Augusta Dacica Sarmizegetusa, which lies 40 kilometres to the southwest. The site requires a steep mountain approach.

## 7. Apollonia, Albania

Apollonia, on a ridge above the Seman river plain in central Albania, was one of the great colonial cities of the Adriatic coast, founded by Corinthian colonists in the 7th century BC and developed into a prosperous urban centre under Hellenistic and Roman rule. Julius Caesar used it as his base in the Civil War. Augustus sent his great-nephew Octavian to study there; Octavian was at Apollonia when news of Caesar's assassination arrived. The ruins cover a substantial area and include the well-preserved Bouleuterion (council chamber), a monumental propylon, a nymphaeum, and a large Roman odeion. An on-site museum occupies a medieval monastery at the centre of the ruins and displays finds from the site. Apollonia is accessible by road from Fier; the setting on the ridge with views across the Albanian plains is exceptional.

## 8. Perperikon, Bulgaria

Perperikon, on a rhyolite hilltop in the Rhodope mountains of southern Bulgaria, is a megalithic sanctuary and later settlement whose history spans from the Late Chalcolithic through the medieval period, a sequence of roughly 5,000 years. The site was one of the most sacred places in Thrace; the oracle here is tentatively identified with the Oracle of Dionysus described by ancient sources as more prestigious than Delphi. The visible ruins are primarily a medieval city with churches, cisterns, and palace structures, but the bedrock around and beneath them bears cup marks, channels, and altar cuttings of prehistoric date. Excavations by Nikolai Ovcharov from 2000 onward have produced substantial finds of Bronze Age and Thracian material. The site is accessible by road from Kardzhali; the hilltop setting is dramatic.

## 9. Nicopolis ad Istrum, Bulgaria

Nicopolis ad Istrum, near Veliko Tarnovo in northern Bulgaria, was founded by the emperor Trajan around 102 AD as a city celebrating his Dacian victories, and functioned as the main city of the Roman province of Moesia Inferior's interior. The ruins cover a substantial grid-planned urban area with a forum complex, colonnaded streets, bath buildings, and a large civic basilica. The site was active through the late 4th century and produced a significant body of inscriptions, architectural sculpture, and mosaic floors. The Gothi Bishop Wulfila, who translated the Bible into the Gothic language and in doing so created the first written Germanic language, is thought to have been active in this region. The site is partially excavated; the most significant structural remains visible are the forum and the monumental arch.

## 10. Ohrid and Plaošnik, North Macedonia

Ohrid, on the shore of Lake Ohrid in western North Macedonia, was one of the most important cities of the late antique and early Byzantine Balkans. The site of Plaošnik, on the highest point of the old city, preserves the remains of a 5th-century early Christian basilica with remarkable mosaic floors — uncovered beneath the foundations of a 9th-century church built by Saints Clement and Naum. Clement of Ohrid established the first Slavic university here in the 880s, using Glagolitic and then Cyrillic script. The basilica mosaics, the church of Saints Clement and Panteleimon partially restored above the earlier structure, and the surrounding archaeological park make Plaošnik the most significant early Christian site in North Macedonia. UNESCO inscribed Ohrid's natural and cultural heritage in 1979 (later extended). The town is accessible by air from Skopje.

The Balkans reward the traveller who moves slowly and attentively. Diocletian's Palace in Split is the single most accessible site and can be visited without forward planning at any time of year. More remote sites — Gamzigrad, Sarmizegetusa Regia — require a car and planning, particularly for the mountain approaches. The peak season runs from June to September; the shoulder months of April to May and October offer cooler temperatures and far fewer visitors at most sites. Several countries in the region — Albania, North Macedonia, Bulgaria — charge modest entrance fees.

Anatolia — the great peninsula that forms the bulk of modern Turkey — has been one of the most continuously inhabited regions on earth. Sitting at the crossroads of Europe and Asia, it witnessed the rise of the world's earliest settled communities, hosted the Hittite empire, was colonized by Ionian Greeks, absorbed into the Persian and then Roman worlds, and eventually became the heartland of the Byzantine and Ottoman empires. This layered history has left a landscape of extraordinary archaeological richness, from Neolithic mounds that predate writing by thousands of years to marble-clad Roman cities whose colonnaded streets are still visible today. ## 1. Ephesus, Aegean Coast

Ephesus was one of the greatest cities of the Roman world, home to perhaps 250,000 people at its height in the 2nd century AD. Founded by Ionian Greeks around the 10th century BC, the city's surviving remains are overwhelmingly Roman: the Library of Celsus with its restored two-storey facade, the Great Theatre capable of seating 25,000, the marble-paved Curetes Street, and the well-preserved latrines and brothel that never fail to engage visitors. Ephesus is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and among the best-preserved Roman cities anywhere. Open year-round; most accessible from Selçuk.

## 2. Troy, Troad Region

Troy (Hisarlık) occupies a strategic mound at the entrance to the Dardanelles and preserves nine distinct settlement layers spanning roughly 3000 BC to 500 AD. The city of the Trojan War legend corresponds roughly to Troy VI or VIIa, dating to the Late Bronze Age around 1300–1180 BC. What survives includes defensive walls of impressive limestone masonry, the ramp leading to the main gate, foundations of megaron-type buildings, and the remains of the later Hellenistic and Roman city of Ilion, which was refounded here after Alexander the Great's visit. UNESCO World Heritage Site. Best visited from Çanakkale.

## 3. Göbekli Tepe, Southeastern Anatolia

Göbekli Tepe, near Şanlıurfa in southeastern Turkey, has fundamentally revised our understanding of prehistoric society since excavations began in the 1990s. The site consists of circular enclosures ringed by T-shaped limestone pillars — some over 5 metres tall and weighing up to 10 tonnes — carved with vivid reliefs of animals including foxes, snakes, boars, and vultures. The structure dates to around 9600–8000 BC, predating pottery, metalworking, and organized agriculture. The implication that hunter-gatherer groups could organize large-scale construction projects has reshaped the debate about what drives the development of complex society. UNESCO World Heritage Site since 2018.

## 4. Çatalhöyük, Central Anatolia

Çatalhöyük, located on the Konya Plain in central Anatolia, is among the largest and best-preserved Neolithic settlements ever excavated. Inhabited from roughly 7500 to 5700 BC, it housed a community of several thousand people in densely packed mudbrick dwellings entered through roof openings rather than ground-level doors. The interior walls were repeatedly plastered and painted with geometric patterns and figurative scenes of hunting and vultures. The site has yielded remarkable evidence for early symbolic behavior, including burying the dead beneath the floors of houses. UNESCO World Heritage Site; open to visitors with a site museum.

## 5. Pergamon, Western Anatolia

Pergamon, on a dramatic hilltop above modern Bergama, was the capital of the Attalid dynasty from the early 3rd century BC and became one of the foremost cultural centres of the Hellenistic world. The site's most famous monument — the Great Altar of Zeus, dating to around 180–160 BC — was removed to Berlin in the 19th century, but the hilltop retains spectacular remains: a steeply raked theatre cut into the hillside, the foundation platform of the altar, a well-preserved temple of Trajan from the Roman period, and the ruins of the royal library that supposedly prompted the development of parchment when Egypt cut off papyrus supplies. The lower city's Asclepion healing sanctuary is separately accessible. UNESCO World Heritage Site.

## 6. Hattusa, North-Central Anatolia

Hattusa, near modern Boğazkale, served as the capital of the Hittite empire from around 1650 to 1200 BC. At its height the city covered roughly 1.8 square kilometres and was enclosed by massive stone-and-mudbrick defensive walls with postern gates built using corbelled arches. The site preserves the foundations of royal palaces, temples, administrative buildings, and the famous Sphinx Gate, which guarded the southern entrance. The Hittite royal archive of some 30,000 clay tablets, found here in 1906, has provided invaluable records of Bronze Age diplomacy including the earliest known peace treaty, signed between Hattusili III and Ramesses II around 1259 BC. UNESCO World Heritage Site.

## 7. Aphrodisias, Western Anatolia

Aphrodisias, in the upper Meander valley of western Turkey, was a prosperous city of the Roman imperial period whose prosperity rested partly on its famous sanctuary of Aphrodite and partly on its workshops producing high-quality marble sculpture. The local quarries provided a white and grey-blue marble of exceptional quality, and Aphrodisian sculptors exported their work across the empire. What survives today is remarkable in its completeness: a standing tetrapylon gateway, a well-preserved stadium seating 30,000 spectators (one of the best preserved in the ancient world), the Sebasteion relief panels (now in the site museum), the Bouleuterion council chamber, and a large theatre. UNESCO World Heritage Site.

## 8. Xanthos, Lycian Coast

Xanthos was the principal city of ancient Lycia and preserves one of the finest concentrations of Lycian funerary monuments anywhere. The site includes the Nereid Monument — a temple-tomb whose sculptural programme is now in the British Museum — Lycian pillar tombs, the Roman theatre, Byzantine churches, and the Harpy Tomb, whose name derives from the winged figures carrying souls of the dead carved on its marble panels. The nearby sanctuary of Letoon, dedicated to the Lycian triad of Leto, Artemis, and Apollo, was the federal religious centre of Lycia and preserves three temples and a mosaic inscription. Both sites together form a UNESCO World Heritage Site.

## 9. Perge, Southern Anatolia

Perge, near modern Antalya, was a Hellenistic and Roman city that reached its greatest prosperity during the 2nd century AD. The site is unusually well preserved: the main colonnaded street ran for nearly a kilometre and was flanked by a water channel fed by a nymphaeum at the northern end. The Antalya Archaeological Museum houses an outstanding collection of sculptural finds from Perge, including a remarkable series of statues from the theatre. Visible on site are the southern gate complex with its horseshoe-shaped court, the Hellenistic tower gate, a well-preserved bath complex, and extensive remains of the Roman city grid.

## 10. Ani, Eastern Anatolia

Ani, near the Armenian border in eastern Turkey, was the capital of the medieval Bagratid Armenian kingdom at its height in the 10th–11th centuries AD and at one point may have housed 100,000 inhabitants. The walled city preserves churches, mosques, a cathedral, palaces, and extensive fortifications in a state of atmospheric decay on the edge of a dramatic gorge. The Cathedral of Ani, built between 989 and 1010 AD, demonstrates the sophisticated stone-cutting techniques of Armenian medieval architecture. Following the Mongol invasions and a series of earthquakes, the city was progressively abandoned from the 13th century onward. UNESCO World Heritage Site since 2016. Accessible from Kars.

Anatolia's sites range from the deeply remote — Göbekli Tepe sits amid farmland far from major hotels — to the highly developed. Ephesus and Pergamon have well-established visitor infrastructure and are accessible on organized tours from major coastal resorts. Spring (April–May) and autumn (September–October) offer the most comfortable conditions for site visits. Many sites charge separate admission fees, and photography is generally permitted.

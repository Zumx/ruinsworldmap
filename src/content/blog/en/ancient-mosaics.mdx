## Colour That Survived

Of all the art forms of the ancient world, mosaic is in many respects the most durable. Fresco fades, sculpture loses its original paint, bronze statues melt down into weapons, but a mosaic laid in the floor of a 2nd-century Roman villa can survive two millennia of burial and emerge, when excavated, still carrying its colours — the deep blues of lapis lazuli or cobalt glass, the warm reds of terracotta tesserae, the yellow of antimonate glass, the luminous white of marble. The durability of fired ceramics and cut stone against the slow chemistry of burial has given us an art historical record that painted surfaces could never provide.

Ancient mosaics range from the purely functional — coarse black-and-white floor patterns in Roman service corridors — to compositions of extraordinary technical sophistication, approaching painting in the fineness of their tesserae and the complexity of their colour modelling. Understanding the range means understanding both what survives and what the different levels of the ancient social hierarchy considered worth commissioning.

## Origins: Pebble Mosaics of the Greek World

The earliest true mosaics used natural pebbles rather than cut tesserae. At Pella, the capital of Macedon and the birthplace of Alexander the Great, a series of pebble mosaics dating to around 300 BC depicts mythological hunting scenes with a vividness and technical confidence that belongs firmly in the mainstream of Greek artistic achievement. The mosaic of the lion hunt, probably a portrait of Alexander and his general Krateros, uses dark pebbles on a light ground to create figures that are modelled in light and shade with something close to pictorial illusionism. The lead strips inserted to define contours and fine details anticipate the later use of tesserae.

The transition from pebble to cut tessera mosaic occurred gradually during the Hellenistic period, and by the 3rd century BC craftsmen in the Greek east were cutting cubes of stone and glass to create polychrome compositions of unprecedented precision.

## The Alexander Mosaic and Hellenistic Pictorial Art

The most famous mosaic in the ancient world is the Alexander Mosaic from the House of the Faun at Pompeii, now in the Museo Nazionale Archeologico in Naples. Measuring roughly 5.9 by 3.1 metres, it depicts a battle between Alexander the Great and Darius III of Persia — almost certainly the Battle of Issus in 333 BC. The composition is built from roughly one and a half million tesserae, including exceptionally fine cutting in the faces and armour. The mosaic is believed to copy a large-scale Hellenistic painting from around 320–310 BC, attributed by ancient sources to Philoxenos of Eretria or to Helen of Egypt.

The Alexander Mosaic demonstrates what the finest Hellenistic mosaic could achieve: foreshortening, cast shadows, individual psychological expression, and a complex overlapping of figures in violent motion. It is at the top of a quality hierarchy that extends downward through competent mythological compositions to simple geometric patterns.

## Roman Floor Mosaics: Pompeii, Ostia, and the Provinces

Pompeii, preserved by the eruption of Vesuvius in 79 AD, provides the most extensive surviving collection of Roman domestic mosaics from any single city. Floors range from the coarse opus signinum (crushed pottery with sparse white tesserae) of simple rooms to the elaborate emblemata — central pictorial panels, sometimes pre-fabricated in workshops and set into simpler geometric surrounds — of wealthy reception rooms. The House of the Faun, which contained the Alexander Mosaic, had multiple mosaic floors throughout; the threshold mosaic showing theatrical masks and garlands is itself a sophisticated composition.

Ostia, the port of Rome, preserves an extraordinary collection of black-and-white figural mosaics from the 2nd and 3rd centuries AD. The corporatist headquarters (collegia) and commercial establishments around the Piazzale delle Corporazioni each had a mosaic identifying the trade or origin of the firm: elephants for African traders, a lighthouse for the Ostian harbour pilots, ships under sail. These are not high art but functional signage executed in mosaic — and they survive where painted signs would not.

In the Roman provinces, mosaic workshops produced distinctive regional styles. The mosaics of North Africa, particularly from modern Tunisia and Libya (ancient Proconsularis and Tripolitania), are among the most inventive and energetic surviving examples. The Bardo National Museum in Tunis holds the world's largest collection of Roman mosaics, including the vast mosaic from the villa at Dougga, depicting the myth of Ulysses and the Sirens, and the extraordinary Virgil mosaic showing the poet writing the Aeneid, flanked by the Muses Clio and Melpomene.

## The Great Palace Mosaic of Constantinople

In Constantinople, the 5th- or 6th-century mosaic pavement from the Great Palace of the Byzantine emperors occupies a special place in the history of late antique art. Excavated beneath the Arasta Bazaar near the Blue Mosque, the surviving section covers around 1,870 square metres and depicts hunting scenes, mythological episodes, rural landscapes, and animal combats with an unfussy naturalism that differs markedly from the hieratic style developing in contemporaneous church mosaics. Children ride a camel, a hunter spears a lion, an eagle fights a serpent. The tesserae are small and the modelling careful. The pavement is now displayed in situ in the Great Palace Mosaic Museum.

## Byzantine Apse Mosaics and Sacred Space

The Byzantine tradition transformed mosaic from a floor medium into a vehicle for theological statement. In the 4th and 5th centuries, apse mosaics began to replace painted decoration in churches, their gold grounds creating a luminous, de-materialised space different in kind from anything the classical world had attempted. Gold glass tesserae — a layer of gold leaf sandwiched between two layers of glass — gave the apse a surface that reflected candlelight from multiple angles, making the sacred image seem to float in its own light.

Santa Costanza in Rome (4th century), Santa Maria Maggiore (5th century), and the mausoleum of Galla Placidia in Ravenna (mid-5th century) mark the development of this tradition. Ravenna is the pre-eminent city for Byzantine mosaic: San Vitale, the Baptisteries of the Orthodox and Arians, and Sant'Apollinare in Classe and Sant'Apollinare Nuovo together constitute a concentration of 5th- and 6th-century mosaic unmatched anywhere in the world. The portrait panels of Justinian and Theodora in San Vitale, each surrounded by their courts, show the full maturity of the Byzantine court style: frontal, hieratic, the colour handled in flat planes, every element weighted with political and theological meaning.

## Technical Production and the Workshop System

Mosaics were made in workshops (officinae), and the distinction between floor and wall work, between geometric and figural panels, and between different quality levels reflects both the skill hierarchy within the workshop and the budget of the patron. Emblemata — the central pictorial panels — were sometimes made in a specialist workshop on a portable clay tray and only set into the floor at the installation stage. Evidence for this practice comes from finds of emblemata still on their trays.

The tesserae themselves were produced from a range of materials: limestone, marble, basalt, and terracotta for opaque colours; glass for vivid blues, greens, and yellows; gold glass and silver glass for sacred contexts. The size of the tesserae was adjusted to the detail required: fine work in faces and drapery used tesserae just a few millimetres across, while background areas used much larger cubes.

## A Landscape of Defended Heights

From the Atlantic coasts of Ireland and Portugal to the plains of Bohemia and the foothills of the Carpathians, Iron Age Europe is dotted with the earthwork remains of hillforts — enclosed settlements built on prominent ridges and summits, defined by one or more circuits of ramparts and ditches. They are among the most visible ancient monuments in northern and western Europe: the white chalk cuts of Maiden Castle in Dorset, the heather-covered contours of Traprain Law in East Lothian, the pine-forested ridges of the Hradiště at Závist near Prague. Most visitors walking across their interiors today see only grass and earthwork humps, but beneath that surface are the compressed remains of dense occupation: postholes of rectangular buildings, pits packed with grain and refuse, hearths, iron slag from smithing, bronze fragments, coins, and sometimes the traumatic evidence of violent destruction.

The hillfort tradition spans roughly the 1st millennium BC, from the early Iron Age of around 800 BC through to the 1st century BC and the Roman conquest of much of western Europe. In some regions, hillforts appear as early as the Bronze Age; in others — particularly the Atlantic zone — they remained in use into the early medieval period. They are not a single phenomenon but a family of related settlement types sharing the common features of topographic prominence and artificial enclosure, varying enormously in size, function, and the elaboration of their defences.

## Rampart Types and the Murus Gallicus

The classification of Iron Age rampart construction has occupied generations of archaeologists, and with good reason: the design of a hillfort's defences reflects available materials, local tradition, the likely threat, and the social organisation capable of constructing and maintaining them. The simplest form is a univallate earthwork: a single bank of dumped soil and rubble with an external ditch, sometimes with a timber palisade on the crest. More complex is the box rampart or timber-laced rampart, in which a framework of horizontal timbers laid in a grid pattern is packed with rubble and stone, creating a stable front face. The characteristic failure mode of this construction — when the front face collapsed and the timbers caught fire, either accidentally or through enemy action — produces a 'vitrified fort': a rampart fused into solid masses of glassy slag by the intense heat, found most dramatically in Scotland.

The murus gallicus — the 'Gallic wall' described by Julius Caesar in his account of the siege of Alesia in 52 BC and confirmed by excavation at multiple sites — is a specific and sophisticated form: a stone facing filled with rubble, with long horizontal beams running back from the face into the fill and spiked with large iron nails up to 30 centimetres long where they pass through the stone courses. The nails prevent the timbers from being withdrawn and the face from being collapsed by attackers using levers. Caesar noted that this construction was fire-resistant (it cannot be burned like a purely timber wall) and shock-resistant (unlike a purely stone wall, it does not shatter when struck by a battering ram). The murus gallicus appears at major oppida sites across Gaul, the Rhineland, and Bohemia.

## The Great Hillforts of Britain and Ireland

The hillforts of southern Britain are among the most intensively studied in Europe. Maiden Castle in Dorset, which covers roughly 18 hectares within its innermost rampart, is the most visually dramatic: four concentric lines of ramparts and ditches enclose a ridge, with elaborately interlocked entrances on the east and west. First enclosed in the early Iron Age around the 6th century BC, it was repeatedly elaborated over subsequent centuries to produce a defensive system of extraordinary complexity. The eastern entrance in particular — a maze of overlapping rampart ends and inturned passages — would have channelled any attacker through a series of killing grounds. Excavations by Mortimer Wheeler in the 1930s, and more recently by English Heritage, have produced evidence of dense settlement within: streets of round houses, storage pits, craft production, and a late Iron Age temple.

Danebury in Hampshire, excavated exhaustively by Barry Cunliffe over twenty-five years from 1969 to 1994, has provided the most complete picture of a southern British hillfort's internal organisation. Planned excavation of roughly half the interior revealed an ordered settlement with round houses arranged along a road system, a zone of storage pits concentrated near the centre, granaries on four-post structures, and a shrine with votive deposits near the central road. The pits — many reused as grain stores across multiple seasons — show the agricultural surplus that sustained a sizeable population. Abandonment of Danebury appears to have been sudden, around 100 BC, with no clear evidence of violent destruction but some evidence of a shift to the valley-bottom oppida of the period.

## Oppida: Urban Predecessors

The late Iron Age sees the development of a different kind of site: the oppidum (plural oppida), a Latin term used by Caesar for the major enclosed settlements of Gaul and which archaeologists have extended to cognate sites across Celtic Europe. Oppida are typically large — often hundreds of hectares — situated not always on prominent hilltops but frequently on lower plateaus or river confluences, enclosed by the distinctive murus gallicus or related rampart types. They show evidence of specialised production, long-distance trade, the use of coinage, and proto-urban organisation.

Manching in Bavaria (Germany), one of the most thoroughly excavated, covers roughly 380 hectares within its walls and shows evidence of iron and bronze working, glass production, and coin minting. Imports found there — wine amphorae from Italy, fine pottery from the Mediterranean — demonstrate connections to the wider world of the 2nd–1st centuries BC. Bibracte (Mont Beuvray, Burgundy), the capital of the Aedui tribe and the site where Caesar met with the Gallic nobility in 52 BC, covers roughly 200 hectares. Its artisan quarters, with their smithing, bronzeworking, and enamelling workshops, and its large aristocratic residences with imported Mediterranean goods, give the most complete picture of an oppidum in use.

Závist near Prague, with its multiple phases of construction and its evidence of control over the silver mines of Bohemia, shows how oppida functioned as political and economic centres in the landscape. In Britain, Colchester (Camulodunum) — the tribal capital of the Trinovantes and later the Catuvellauni — represents the British equivalent: a large area defined by multiple linear dyke systems rather than a single encircling rampart, covering several square kilometres.

## The End of the Hillforts

The story of Iron Age hillforts does not end uniformly across Europe. In areas under Roman conquest, the process was often abrupt. Caesar's assault on Alesia in Burgundy in 52 BC — surrounding it with Roman siege works encompassing roughly 15 kilometres of circumvallation — is the most famous example of a hillfort taken by Roman military engineering. Many oppida were abandoned as Roman colonies and coloniae attracted population to valley sites with better road access. Some hillforts were deliberately slighted. Others became sanctuaries — sacred hills maintained as religious rather than residential sites.

In regions outside Roman reach — Ireland, Scotland, Atlantic Spain and Portugal — the hillfort tradition continued well beyond the end of the Iron Age as conventionally defined. In Ireland, the hillforts at Navan Fort (Emain Macha), County Armagh, and Knocknarea in Sligo, associated with legendary figures in medieval Irish literature, remained culturally significant for centuries. In Scotland, brochs — the drystone towers of the Atlantic Iron Age — represent a parallel tradition of defended settlement distinct from but related to the hillfort.

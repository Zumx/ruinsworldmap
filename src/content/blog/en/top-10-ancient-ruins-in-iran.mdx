Iran sits at the crossroads of ancient civilizations. The Iranian plateau was the heartland of the Achaemenid Persian Empire — the largest empire the ancient world had yet seen, stretching from Greece and Egypt to India — and before that, it was home to Elamite civilization in the southwest and to proto-urban cultures stretching back to at least 5000 BC. After Alexander the Great destroyed the Achaemenid capital, the region saw Seleucid, Parthian, and Sasanian empires rise and fall, each leaving monuments in stone, brick, and stucco. The breadth and depth of Iran's archaeological heritage is difficult to overstate: its UNESCO World Heritage Sites alone span 5,000 years of human achievement. ## 1. Persepolis, Fars Province

Persepolis was the ceremonial capital of the Achaemenid Empire, begun by Darius I around 518 BC and expanded by his successors Xerxes I and Artaxerxes I until its destruction by Alexander the Great in 330 BC. The site is built on a vast stone platform — roughly 450 by 300 metres — cut into the foothills of the Zagros mountains, and it preserves the most extensive surviving example of Achaemenid palatial architecture. The Apadana, a hypostyle audience hall once supported by 36 columns each around 20 metres high, retains several standing columns and its extraordinary staircase reliefs — rows of delegations from the empire's subject peoples bearing tribute, depicted with a formality and sculptural precision that place them among the masterpieces of ancient art. The Gate of All Nations, the Treasury, the Palace of Darius, and the Throne Hall (the Hundred-Column Hall) complete the principal structures. The tombs of Darius II, Artaxerxes II, Artaxerxes III, and Darius III are cut into the cliff face directly above the platform. UNESCO World Heritage Site. Located 60 kilometres northeast of Shiraz.

## 2. Pasargadae, Fars Province

Pasargadae was the first capital of the Achaemenid Empire, built by Cyrus the Great following his defeat of the Median and Lydian kingdoms in the 550s BC. It is a more open and atmospheric site than Persepolis: the Tomb of Cyrus, a stepped plinth topped by a gabled chamber, stands alone in a wide plain and retains a simple grandeur that has moved visitors for two and a half millennia. Alexander the Great is reported to have honoured the tomb on his arrival and ordered its restoration after it was found desecrated by his troops. Nearby, the remains of two palatial reception halls preserve columns and doorframes bearing the first-person inscription 'I, Cyrus, the King, an Achaemenian.' The sacred precinct of Zendan-e Soleyman and a partially reconstructed garden layout — possibly the world's earliest known formal garden — complete the site. UNESCO World Heritage Site.

## 3. Bishapur, Fars Province

Bishapur was a Sasanian royal city founded by the great Sasanian king Shapur I in the 3rd century AD, following his victories over three Roman emperors — including the capture of Emperor Valerian in 260 AD, the first Roman emperor to be taken prisoner by a foreign enemy. The site preserves the foundations of the city's urban layout, a large fire temple, and a palace whose floor is decorated with Roman-style mosaics — almost certainly created by Roman prisoners of war brought back from the campaign. Most dramatically, a series of large rock-relief panels carved into a gorge above the site depict Shapur's victories over the Romans with a sculptural scale and rhetorical ambition that rival the triumphal arches of Rome itself. The reliefs are accessible by a short walk up the gorge. Bishapur lies on the road between Shiraz and Kazerun.

## 4. Susa (Shush), Khuzestan Province

Susa was one of the oldest continuously occupied cities in human history, with settlement beginning around 4200 BC in the lowlands of Khuzestan. It served at different periods as the capital of the Elamite civilization, as a key Achaemenid royal city (the site of the Palace of Darius and the story of Esther in the Hebrew Bible), as a Seleucid and then Parthian city, and as an early Christian and Islamic centre. The archaeological mound is massive — around 200 hectares — and its excavation by French archaeologists from 1897 onward produced extraordinary finds now largely in the Louvre, including the stele of the Hammurabi law code (carried there as war booty by an Elamite king around 1200 BC) and the Statue of Darius. The on-site museum provides context; the nearby Chogha Zanbil ziggurat (see below) is a short drive away. UNESCO World Heritage Site.

## 5. Chogha Zanbil, Khuzestan Province

Chogha Zanbil is the best-preserved ancient ziggurat outside Mesopotamia, built by the Elamite king Untash-Napirisha around 1250 BC as the centre of a new sacred city. The ziggurat originally rose in five storeys to a height of perhaps 52 metres; three storeys survive, still rising to around 25 metres, built of millions of mud bricks stamped with cuneiform inscriptions. The complex was surrounded by three concentric enclosure walls containing temples dedicated to numerous Elamite gods; excavations have revealed elaborate drainage and water systems and a royal funerary vault. The site was evidently never completed and may have been abandoned not long after construction, preserving it in a relatively pristine state. UNESCO World Heritage Site — the first Iranian site to be so designated, in 1979. Located about 30 kilometres from Susa.

## 6. Bam Citadel (Arg-e Bam), Kerman Province

The Arg-e Bam is the world's largest adobe (mud-brick) structure, a fortified town that grew continuously from roughly the Achaemenid period (around 500 BC) through the Safavid and Qajar periods, reaching its greatest extent by the 18th century. For centuries it served as the centre of a prosperous desert oasis city controlling trade routes through the Dasht-e Lut. The citadel was catastrophically damaged by the earthquake of December 2003, which killed approximately 26,000 people in the city of Bam; the ruins were inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2004, in part as a commitment to international restoration assistance. Restoration has been ongoing since, and substantial portions of the citadel are accessible. The combination of adobe construction technology and desert landscape makes Bam unlike any other site in Iran.

## 7. Naqsh-e Rostam, Fars Province

Naqsh-e Rostam is a rock face carved with four Achaemenid royal tombs and six Sasanian rock-relief panels, set a few kilometres from Persepolis. The tombs, cut high into the cliff in the form of crosses, belong to Darius I, Xerxes I, Artaxerxes I, and Darius II; their carved facades show the king supported by representatives of subject peoples before the fire altar. At ground level, the Sasanian reliefs — among the most accomplished in the Sasanian corpus — depict the investiture of Ardashir I, the victory of Shapur I over Valerian, and other royal scenes. A free-standing cube-shaped structure called the Ka'ba-ye Zartosht (Cube of Zoroaster), perhaps a fire temple or funerary tower, stands before the cliff. The site can be visited in combination with Persepolis.

## 8. Takht-e Soleyman, West Azerbaijan Province

Takht-e Soleyman (the Throne of Solomon) is a Sasanian-era royal fire sanctuary built around a naturally occurring crater lake fed by a warm mineral spring, set in a high volcanic landscape in northwestern Iran. The sanctuary was one of the three great fire temples of the Zoroastrian faith and a place of royal pilgrimage for Sasanian kings. Excavations have revealed audience halls, a royal palace, and the fire temple itself arranged around the lake. The Mongol Ilkhanid dynasty also built on the site in the 13th century, adding a palace that incorporated older Sasanian columns and carved stucco. UNESCO World Heritage Site. The remote mountain setting gives the site a particular atmospheric quality; accessible from Takab in West Azerbaijan.

## 9. Ecbatana (Hamadan), Hamadan Province

Hamadan was the ancient Ecbatana, the Median capital and a city of legendary wealth described by Herodotus with seven concentric walls of differently coloured materials. The extravagant description is almost certainly embellished, but Ecbatana was undoubtedly one of the great cities of the ancient Near East, serving as the summer capital of the Achaemenid, Seleucid, and Parthian empires. The modern city sits directly atop the ancient one, limiting excavation, but the Hegmataneh Hill at the centre yields ongoing discoveries of Median and Achaemenid material. The tomb attributed to the biblical figures Esther and Mordecai is a pilgrimage site in the old city. A carved stone lion near the city centre may date to the Seleucid period.

## 10. Bisotun, Kermanshah Province

Bisotun (Behistun) is an archaeological complex on the ancient road between Babylon and Ecbatana, dominated by a massive cliff bearing the Behistun Inscription — a trilingual cuneiform text in Old Persian, Babylonian, and Elamite commissioned by Darius I around 520 BC to record his defeat of rebel claimants to the Persian throne. The inscription, carved at a height of approximately 100 metres above the road, was a key document in the decipherment of cuneiform in the 19th century by Henry Rawlinson, who dangled from ropes to make paper squeezes of the text. The relief panel above the inscription shows Darius with his foot on the rebel Gaumata, surrounded by bound prisoners. Nearby, a Parthian bronze statue and a Median rock carving add further layers to the site's historical depth. UNESCO World Heritage Site.

Iran's major archaeological sites are concentrated in two main clusters: the Fars region around Shiraz (Persepolis, Pasargadae, Bishapur, Naqsh-e Rostam, and Bishapur) and the Khuzestan lowlands near Ahvaz (Susa and Chogha Zanbil). Both clusters are accessible by domestic flight and road. The spring (March to May) and autumn (September to November) seasons offer the most comfortable conditions for outdoor visiting. Archaeological sites in Iran are managed by the Iranian Cultural Heritage Organization and charge modest entry fees. Travel insurance and current visa requirements should be checked before planning; conditions for independent foreign visitors have varied considerably in recent years.

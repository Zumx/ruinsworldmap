Kazakhstan is the world's ninth-largest country, a vast territory of steppe, desert, and mountain that served for thousands of years as the corridor through which peoples, animals, goods, and ideas moved between East Asia, Central Asia, and Europe. The archaeology of the steppe is distinctive: the nomadic and semi-nomadic cultures that dominated the region left burial mounds (kurgans) rather than cities, petroglyphs rather than inscriptions, and mobile material culture in bronze, gold, and bone rather than monumental architecture. Yet the southern fringe of Kazakhstan, along the Syr Darya river and its tributaries, hosted significant settled urban cultures connected to the Silk Road, and it is here that the most impressive visible ruins survive. Kazakhstan also contains one of the world's greatest concentrations of prehistoric rock art. The country's archaeological wealth is matched by the challenges of access — distances are large, infrastructure outside major cities is limited, and many sites require specialist permits or guided tours.

## 1. Otrar (Farab), South Kazakhstan

Otrar, or Farab, near modern Shaulder in South Kazakhstan, was one of the most important cities on the Syr Darya section of the Silk Road. It was the birthplace of the Islamic philosopher al-Farabi (c. 872–950 AD), the 'Second Teacher' after Aristotle in the Islamic philosophical tradition. The city's catastrophic role in history came in 1218 when its governor, Inalchuq, killed Genghis Khan's trade mission, providing the Mongol ruler with the casus belli for the annihilation of the Khorezmian empire. Otrar Tobe, the main mound, contains layers of occupation from the 1st century AD to the 15th century. The Otrar Oasis State Historical-Cultural Reserve protects the site, and a small museum at the site presents finds including ceramics, coins, and architectural elements from the excavated city.

## 2. Sauran, South Kazakhstan

Sauran, west of Turkestan along the Syr Darya, is the best-preserved ruined medieval city in Kazakhstan, its mudbrick walls and towers still standing to considerable height on the flat steppe. The city flourished as a major commercial centre from around the 9th to 17th centuries AD, serving as a waypoint on the Silk Road and at times as the capital of the White Horde. The perimeter walls with their projecting towers enclose an area of roughly 30 hectares, and the silhouette of the ruins against the open steppe is striking. Excavations have revealed a large mosque, bazaar areas, and residential quarters. Sauran is accessible from Turkestan and is one of the most atmospheric ruined cities in the region.

## 3. Tamgaly Petroglyphs, Almaty Region

The Tamgaly gorge in the Chu-Ili mountains northwest of Almaty contains one of the densest concentrations of petroglyphs in Central Asia, with around 5,000 images pecked into the rock surfaces over a period spanning from the Bronze Age to recent centuries. The earliest petroglyphs date to roughly the 14th–12th centuries BC and include solar-headed deity figures, scenes of ritual, and zoomorphic images that are diagnostic of the Bronze Age steppe cultures. Later images show horsemen, deer, camels, and scenes that reflect the nomadic lifestyles of the Iron Age through Turkic periods. Tamgaly is a UNESCO World Heritage Site. The gorge is accessible by road from Almaty (about 170 kilometres) and is best visited with a guide who can identify the major groupings of images.

## 4. Turkestan and the Mausoleum of Khoja Ahmed Yasawi

The city of Turkestan in South Kazakhstan is the most important pilgrimage destination in Kazakhstan, centred on the magnificent mausoleum of the Sufi mystic Khoja Ahmed Yasawi (1093–1166 AD), one of the most influential figures in the Turkic Islamic world. The mausoleum was commissioned by Timur (Tamerlane) in 1389 and though never completed, it remains one of the largest and best-preserved Timurid monuments outside Uzbekistan, with its turquoise-tiled portal and vast ribbed dome. The archaeological area around the mausoleum encompasses an older city with remains extending back to the 1st millennium BC. The mausoleum is a UNESCO World Heritage Site. Turkestan is well connected by rail and road from Almaty and Shymkent and has invested significantly in heritage tourism infrastructure.

## 5. Sarayshyk, West Kazakhstan

Sarayshyk on the Ural River in western Kazakhstan was the principal city of the Golden Horde from the 13th to 16th centuries and later a Kazakh khanate urban centre before its destruction by the Zaporozhian Cossacks in 1580. The site preserves extensive surface remains including mudbrick mausolea and the outlines of urban structures visible from the ground and from the air. Several of the mausolea have been excavated and partially restored. Sarayshyk is also the burial place of several khans of the Golden Horde and the Kazakh Khanate, giving the site a particular significance in Kazakh national history. The site lies near the modern city of Atyrau and is accessible by road.

## 6. Berel Kurgan Cemetery, East Kazakhstan

The Berel kurgan cemetery near the village of Berel in the Bukhtarma valley of East Kazakhstan is one of the most significant Scythian burial sites in Central Asia. The large burial mounds contain frozen tombs of the Pazyryk culture (roughly 5th–3rd centuries BC), preserved by permafrost that kept organic materials — horse harnesses, felt hangings, wooden objects, and clothing — in a state of remarkable preservation. The Berel excavations of the 1990s and 2000s recovered a burial of elite status containing two humans and 13 horses, all elaborately equipped. The finds from Berel are displayed in the National Museum of Kazakhstan in Nur-Sultan (Astana). The site itself in the Altai foothills is accessible in summer.

## 7. Botai Site, North Kazakhstan

Botai in the Kokshetau region of northern Kazakhstan is among the most important prehistoric sites in the world: the place where the earliest evidence for horse domestication has been identified, dating to around 3500 BC. The Botai culture lived in semi-subterranean pit houses and subsisted almost entirely on horse meat and mare's milk — chemical residue analysis of ceramic vessels has confirmed the processing of mare's milk at the site, suggesting horses were being milked and likely ridden at this very early date. The site itself is a large scatter of pit house depressions on the steppe, not visually dramatic, but its significance for understanding the transformation of human–horse relations — arguably the most consequential human–animal relationship in history — is profound.

## 8. Bozok Medieval Settlement, Central Kazakhstan

Bozok, near Astana in the centre of the Kazakh steppe, is a rare example of a planned medieval settlement in the heart of the steppe zone, identified and excavated from the early 2000s. The site consists of a central palace complex surrounded by a moat and an organised settlement, dating to roughly the 9th–13th centuries AD. It appears to have functioned as a winter capital for steppe confederations. The discovery of Bozok challenged the conventional assumption that steppe peoples lived entirely without urban structures, demonstrating instead that some confederations built and occupied seasonal or semi-permanent centres. The site is located within reach of Astana.

## 9. Merke and Akyr-Tas, Zhambyl Region

The Zhambyl Region of southern Kazakhstan contains several significant medieval sites that illustrate the transition from steppe nomadism to settled culture during the Turkic period. Akyr-Tas, near Taraz, is the partially excavated remains of a large rectangular enclosure of the 8th–9th centuries, possibly a palace complex or caravanserai associated with the early Islamic period. The site is notable for its scale and for carved stone elements scattered across the surface. The ancient city of Taraz itself has deep historical layers, with occupation documented from the 1st millennium BC. The Taraz regional museum holds finds from these sites and the surrounding archaeological landscape.

## 10. Valley of the Kings (Besshatyr Burial Mounds), Almaty Region

The Besshatyr kurgan complex on the right bank of the Ili River in the Almaty Region consists of eighteen large burial mounds dating to the Saka (Scythian) period, roughly the 5th–4th centuries BC. The largest kurgan is over 100 metres in diameter and 17 metres high — among the most imposing burial monuments in Central Asia. Excavations of the kurgans have revealed timber burial chambers of elaborate construction, and the associated material culture reflects connections with the broader Eurasian steppe world of the Iron Age. The site lies within the Altyn-Emel National Park, which also protects the famous 'singing dunes' and several other archaeological monuments, making it a rewarding destination for visitors to the Almaty region.

Kazakhstan requires careful planning. International flights arrive at Almaty and Astana; internal flights or long road journeys connect the regions. Many sites in the south are accessible from Shymkent and Turkestan. The National Museum of Kazakhstan in Astana and the Central State Museum in Almaty hold the major collections. Summer (June–September) is the main season for steppe sites, but southern sites near Turkestan can be visited year-round.

## Colour in the Ancient World

The ancient world was not white marble and bare stone. It was painted. Temples, public buildings, private houses, tombs, and palace walls were covered in vivid pigments: red ochre, Egyptian blue (a synthetic copper-calcium silicate that was the world's first manufactured pigment, produced from roughly 3000 BC onwards), yellow ochre, green frit, charcoal black, and costly cinnabar red made from mercury sulphide. We live in an unusually decoloured version of antiquity because the paint has mostly vanished, leaving us with the substrate. What survives — at Pompeii and Herculaneum under the ash of Vesuvius, at Akrotiri under Theran pumice, in Egyptian rock-cut tombs sealed for millennia — gives a vivid and often startling impression of what was lost.

The techniques used to apply colour to walls varied across cultures and periods, but the most durable method — the one responsible for most of what survives — is true fresco, or buon fresco: pigment applied in water to freshly laid, still-wet lime plaster. As the plaster carbonates and hardens, the pigment is locked into the surface rather than sitting on top of it. The chemical bond is permanent; it is why Minoan frescoes painted around 1600 BC at Akrotiri on Santorini still glow with their original intensity after being buried under volcanic debris for more than three thousand years.

## Minoan and Aegean Painting

The Bronze Age Aegean produced some of the earliest and most visually sophisticated wall painting in Europe. At Akrotiri, excavated from 1967 onwards by the Greek archaeologist Spyridon Marinatos and subsequent teams, a wealthy Cycladic settlement buried by the Theran eruption (now dated to roughly 1628–1600 BC by radiocarbon and ice-core evidence, though the debate continues) has preserved frescoes in multiple rooms of multi-storey buildings. The 'Spring Fresco' from Building Delta shows swallows flying among red lilies and black rock formations — one of the earliest known landscape paintings in the world, remarkable for its naturalism and movement. The 'Boxing Children' fresco, with its profile figures of young males wearing single boxing gloves, and the 'Flotilla Fresco' depicting a fleet of ships processing between harbour towns, give a vivid image of Cycladic life.

The related Minoan painting tradition at Knossos on Crete, known primarily from Sir Arthur Evans's reconstructions (which must be treated with caution, as his team restored many compositions from small fragments), shows similar subjects — the 'Dolphin Fresco', the 'Bull-Leaping Fresco', the 'Ladies in Blue' — alongside processions, ritual scenes, and architectural backgrounds. The Mycenaean palace tradition, as seen at Tiryns and Mycenae in the Argolid and at Pylos in the western Peloponnese, adopted Minoan pictorial conventions and applied them to hunting scenes, battle scenes, and religious processions.

## Egyptian Tomb Painting

The single most extensive surviving body of ancient wall painting comes from the rock-cut tombs of Egypt, where the dry climate, sealed chambers, and the deliberate effort to create an eternal image rather than a perishable decoration combined to preserve painting traditions across more than three thousand years. The painted programmes of New Kingdom private tombs at Thebes — on the west bank at Luxor, in the area known as the Tombs of the Nobles — are extraordinary in their variety and detail. Tomb TT52, belonging to the official Nakht around the 14th century BC, contains banquet and hunting scenes whose figures retain their paint almost perfectly. TT69 (Menna) shows agricultural scenes of surveying, harvesting, and threshing. These are not generic images but specific representations of the tomb-owner's life and the sustaining activities of the afterlife.

The pigments used in Egyptian tomb painting are well documented through analysis. Blue and green were provided by Egyptian blue and green frit, both synthetics; red and yellow by ochres and by the mineral realgar; black by charcoal and by manganese dioxide. White was provided by calcite or gypsum. Lead white, widely used in Greco-Roman painting, appears only rarely in purely Egyptian contexts. The binding medium in many Egyptian paintings appears to have been water alone — effectively a fresco secco (dry fresco) technique — though animal glue and gum arabic have been identified in some analyses.

## The Four Styles of Pompeii

The buried cities of Vesuvius — Pompeii and Herculaneum, destroyed by the eruption of 79 AD — are the primary source for our understanding of Roman domestic wall painting. The German art historian August Mau's classification of Pompeian painting into four chronological styles, published in 1882, remains the standard framework. The First Style (roughly 2nd century BC) imitates marble revetment in painted plaster — plain rectangular panels of colour suggesting expensive stone. The Second Style (roughly 1st century BC through to the late Republic) breaks the surface into architectural illusions — colonnades, porticoes, landscapes seen through invented windows — creating the sensation of spaces extending beyond the wall. The Villa of the Mysteries at Pompeii, with its extraordinary frieze of life-size figures engaged in what appears to be a Dionysiac initiation ritual, is the masterwork of this period.

The Third Style (roughly 1st century BC to mid-1st century AD) flattens the architectural illusionism and replaces it with slender ornamental columns and delicate central panel paintings — small mythological scenes in the manner of framed pictures hung on a coloured ground. The Fourth Style, dominant in the final decades before the eruption, combines architectural fantasy with mythological and still-life panels in an elaborate, sometimes busy synthesis. The House of the Vettii in Pompeii, with its Fourth Style rooms around the peristyle garden, gives the most complete impression of how a wealthy townhouse looked at the height of the Roman Empire.

## Survival and Loss

The survival of ancient painting is almost entirely accidental. The Vesuvius cities owe their preservation to a catastrophe. Akrotiri owes it to another. The extraordinary Parthian-period paintings at Dura-Europos in Syria — a Roman garrison city on the Euphrates excavated largely by Yale University and the French Academy in the 1920s and 1930s — survived because the city was destroyed and buried in 256 AD during a Sasanian Persian attack, which preserved not only the paintings in the synagogue, the baptistery of the earliest known Christian church, and the Mithraeum, but even organic materials including wooden shields and a roll of parchment with military records.

In China, the painted tombs of the Han and Tang dynasties (roughly 2nd century BC to 10th century AD) survive in sealed burial chambers excavated from loess soil. The murals at the Mogao Caves near Dunhuang in Gansu province — a complex of Buddhist cave temples carved and decorated from roughly the 4th century AD onwards — represent over 45,000 square metres of painted surface, some of the best preserved ancient painting in the world. The Ajanta Caves in Maharashtra, India, contain Buddhist paintings from roughly the 2nd century BC to the 5th–6th centuries AD that survive because the caves were abandoned, sealed by vegetation, and only rediscovered by British officers in 1819.

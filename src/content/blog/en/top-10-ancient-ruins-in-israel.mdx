Few stretches of land on earth carry a denser archaeological record than Israel. From Chalcolithic settlements that predate writing to Hellenistic city grids, Roman engineering projects, and Byzantine basilicas, successive civilisations have layered themselves over one another here for ten thousand years. The landscape itself is an archive: a coastal plain that attracted Phoenician traders and Roman engineers, a Jordan Valley where freshwater drew the earliest farmers, and a Negev desert where Nabataean merchants carved cities from rock and managed rainfall in ways that still astonish hydrologists. What follows are ten sites that best illustrate that depth — ranging from a fortress that became the symbol of a nation to a fishing village where wooden synagogue columns have survived almost two millennia.

## 1. Masada, Judean Desert

Herod the Great built Masada as a palace-fortress on an isolated mesa above the Dead Sea between roughly 37 and 31 BCE. What he left behind is among the most complete Roman-period palatial complexes anywhere: a three-tiered hanging palace clinging to the northern cliff, a large bathhouse with hypocaust heating, plastered cisterns that collected millions of litres of rainwater, and storerooms still holding grain jars. The site became famous as the last holdout of Jewish Zealots during the First Jewish–Roman War, falling to the Roman Tenth Legion around 73 CE. Today it is a UNESCO World Heritage Site with a cable car for most visitors and a demanding footpath — the Snake Path — for those who prefer to arrive on foot at dawn.

## 2. Caesarea Maritima, Mediterranean Coast

Herod the Great again, this time turning a modest Hellenistic anchorage into one of the Roman world's great harbours. Built between around 22 and 10 BCE and named in honour of Augustus, Caesarea had a sunken breakwater of hydraulic concrete — Roman pozzolana mixed with seawater — whose scale was not matched anywhere in the Mediterranean. The city grid, theatre, hippodrome, and a temple platform survive alongside the harbour infrastructure. Later it became the seat of Roman governance in Judaea and an important Byzantine and Crusader centre. The Crusader walls and moat are dramatically visible from the boardwalk. An ongoing underwater excavation continues to reveal the harbour's engineering. Entry is through an Israeli national park.

## 3. Beit She'an, Northern Valley

Ancient Beit She'an sits at the junction of the Jezreel and Jordan valleys, a position that made it almost continuously occupied from the fifth millennium BCE onward. The Egyptians held a garrison here during the New Kingdom period; Philistine and Israelite layers follow. But the most spectacular remains are Roman: a colonnaded main street, a large theatre carved into the hillside, a nymphaeum, bathhouses, and a Byzantine shopping street whose mosaic pavements are partially preserved. An earthquake in 749 CE toppled much of the Roman city in a single event, leaving columns and carved stones lying exactly where they fell, which makes Beit She'an one of the most legible Roman urban plans in the Levant. The tel above the Roman city holds eighteen layers of occupation visible in cross-section.

## 4. Megiddo (Tel Megiddo), Jezreel Valley

Megiddo guarded the main pass between the coastal plain and the Jezreel Valley for roughly five thousand years and was one of the most fought-over positions in the ancient Near East. The mound today holds more than twenty distinct settlement layers. Among the most visible features are a water system from the Israelite period — a vertical shaft and horizontal tunnel that gave safe access to the spring outside the walls — Solomon-era gate structures (though the dating remains debated among scholars), and Canaanite temples from the third millennium BCE. The views from the top across the valley that gave Armageddon its name are extraordinary. Megiddo is part of a UNESCO World Heritage Site shared with Hazor and Beer-sheba.

## 5. Avdat (Oboda), Negev Desert

Avdat was a Nabataean caravan city on the Incense Route, founded around the third century BCE as a stopping point between Petra and the Mediterranean ports. The Nabataeans managed an intricate system of runoff agriculture — channels, cisterns, and terraced farms — that made this desert productive. After Nabataea became a Roman province in 106 CE the city was rebuilt in Roman style; Byzantine Christians added churches, a baptistery, and wine presses in the fifth and sixth centuries. The site preserves a Nabataean temple, a Roman bathhouse, Byzantine churches with mosaic floors, and entire neighbourhoods of stone houses. It is a UNESCO World Heritage Site as part of the Negev Spice Route inscriptions. The reconstructed Nabataean farm nearby demonstrates ancient water harvesting techniques still used in modern experiments.

## 6. Capernaum, Sea of Galilee

Capernaum is a small site with an outsized historical footprint. A Jewish fishing village during the Second Temple period, it appears in the Gospels as Jesus's base during his Galilean ministry. The archaeological record here shows continuous Jewish settlement from around the second century BCE to the seventh century CE. The most striking structure is a large synagogue built in white limestone, probably in the fourth or fifth century CE, sitting directly above the basalt foundations of an earlier synagogue that recent scholarship dates to the first century. Octagonal Byzantine church foundations are visible nearby, built over what tradition identified as the house of the apostle Peter. The columns and carved friezes of the limestone synagogue are among the most photographed ancient stonework in Israel. The site is administered by Franciscan friars and is open to visitors year-round.

## 7. Tel Hazor, Upper Galilee

The largest ancient tel in Israel, Hazor covered roughly 200 acres at its peak during the Late Bronze Age, around the fourteenth and thirteenth centuries BCE, when it was a major Canaanite city-state with a population estimated in the tens of thousands. Egyptian texts and the Mari archive in Syria both mention it as a significant regional power. Extensive excavations have revealed monumental city gates, temples with carved basalt orthostats, and a sophisticated water system. The city was destroyed violently — the burning is visible in the archaeological record — and rebuilt repeatedly. A UNESCO World Heritage Site shared with Megiddo and Beer-sheba. The visitor centre at the site presents the excavation history clearly and is a useful orientation before walking the tel.

## 8. Tel Beer-sheba, Negev

Tel Beer-sheba preserves an Iron Age city planned with a precision that still impresses archaeologists. The streets are laid out on a grid, the buildings follow a tripartite design, and the entire settlement is enclosed by a casemate wall — two parallel walls with rooms between them that could serve as barracks or storage. A remarkable find was a large horned altar that had been dismantled and reused as building material, suggesting religious reform during the Israelite kingdom period. An elaborate water system descends to a large plastered cistern. As with Hazor and Megiddo, it is a UNESCO World Heritage Site. The reconstructed city gate at the entrance gives an immediate sense of the Iron Age urban layout.

## 9. Caesarea Philippi (Banias), Golan Heights

At the foot of Mount Hermon, a powerful spring emerges from a cave that the Greeks dedicated to Pan in the Hellenistic period, calling the place Panias (later softened to Banias). Herod the Great built a marble temple to Augustus here; his son Philip the Tetrarch expanded it into a city. The sanctuary of Pan is cut directly into the cliff face — niches for statues are still visible in the rock alongside Greek dedicatory inscriptions. A large first-century temple platform, an elaborately carved Byzantine gate, and the remains of a Crusader bridge span different historical chapters. The site is set in a nature reserve with the Banias waterfall nearby, making it an unusually pleasant archaeological visit in summer.

## 10. Apollonia (Arsuf), Coastal Plain

Apollonia sits on sea cliffs north of Tel Aviv and holds a compact sequence of occupation from the Persian period through Crusader times. The Persians and later the Hellenistic Seleucids used it as a harbour town. A glassmaking industry flourished here in the Roman and Byzantine periods — raw glass produced at coastal Levantine sites was shipped across the Mediterranean. The Crusaders built a substantial castle on the headland in the twelfth century; its hall, towers, and dry moat survive in good condition. The site is an Israeli national park. The combination of Persian-period remains, Hellenistic urbanism, evidence of ancient industry, and a well-preserved Crusader fortification makes Apollonia one of the more compressed archaeological experiences on the coastal plain.

---

Israel rewards the methodical visitor. The national parks authority maintains most major sites well, and English signage is widespread. Spring and autumn are the most comfortable seasons — summer temperatures on the Jordan Valley sites like Beit She'an can exceed 40°C, while Masada's Snake Path is best climbed before sunrise in summer to avoid the heat. Most sites charge modest admission fees; a combined national parks pass is cost-effective if you plan to visit several.

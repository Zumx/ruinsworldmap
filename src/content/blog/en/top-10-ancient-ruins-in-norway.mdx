Norway's ancient heritage stretches from the rock carvings of the Mesolithic and Bronze Age through the burial mounds and maritime towns of the Viking Age, set against a landscape of fjords, islands, and coastal plateaus that shaped the societies who left these monuments behind. The country is not conventionally associated with the kind of monumental stone ruins found around the Mediterranean, but Norway's archaeology is rich and diverse: Bronze Age rock art panels at Tanum and Alta, Iron Age burial mounds that rival anything in Scandinavia, Viking-period trading centres, and the stone churches that marked the end of the pre-Christian era. Many of these sites are embedded in working landscapes of extraordinary natural beauty, making them as rewarding to visit for the setting as for the archaeology itself.

## 1. Borre Mound Cemetery, Vestfold

The Borre mound cemetery at Borreparken near Horten in Vestfold is the largest group of Viking Age royal burial mounds in Scandinavia, comprising around seven large mounds and a number of smaller ones arranged across a forested headland above the Oslofjord. The mounds were constructed between roughly the 6th and 10th centuries CE and are associated with the Yngling dynasty, predecessors of the Norwegian royal house. One mound was excavated in 1851, yielding a ship burial with spectacular gilded bronze fittings — the 'Borre style' of Norse ornament takes its name from these finds. The material is now in the Viking Ship Museum in Oslo. The surviving mounds, the largest reaching around eight metres in height, create a dramatic prehistoric skyline in a park setting open year-round.

## 2. Kaupang, Vestfold

Kaupang, on the western shore of the Oslofjord near Larvik, was Norway's first documented town, active from roughly 800 to 900 CE. Known in contemporary sources as Sciringesheal (Shirin's Hall), it was described by the traveller Ohthere, whose account was recorded by King Alfred of Wessex. Excavations from the 19th century onward, and a major project in the 1990s–2000s led by Dagfinn Skre, have revealed a densely occupied artisan quarter with evidence of metalworking, amber processing, textile production, and long-distance trade connections to the Rhineland, the British Isles, and the Arab world (via Volga trade routes). The site is not visible as standing structures — occupation debris and postholes constitute the evidence — but a small exhibition at the nearby Larvik Museum explains the finds and significance.

## 3. Avaldsnes, Karmøy

Avaldsnes, on the island of Karmøy off the Rogaland coast, was the seat of Harald Fairhair, traditionally regarded as the first king of Norway, during the late 9th century CE. The 'North Way' — the sheltered coastal sailing route that gave Norway its name — passes directly below the Avaldsnes headland, and control of this bottleneck made the site a seat of considerable power. The standing monument is St Olav's Church (13th century), which incorporates an older church of the early Christian period, but adjacent to it stands the Nordvegen History Centre and the excavated remains of an early medieval great hall complex identified through geophysical survey and selective excavation. A replica hall has been built nearby. The 'Virgin Mary's Needle', a slender standing stone that leans against the church wall, is a local legend with prehistoric origins.

## 4. Tanum Rock Carvings (Bohuslän/border context)

The Rock Carvings of Tanum, inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1994, technically fall within Sweden's Bohuslän province, but the rock art tradition they represent is shared across the broader Scandinavian landscape including Norwegian sites in Østfold. The Bronze Age carvings (roughly 1700–500 BCE) depict ships, humans in ritual postures, animals, weapons, and scenes of ploughing and fighting in a style found along both sides of the modern border. On the Norwegian side, the Østfold carvings at Begby and elsewhere preserve comparable imagery. The tradition as a whole represents the most extensive and best-documented Bronze Age rock art corpus in northern Europe, and understanding the Norwegian examples is impossible without reference to the wider Scandinavian context.

## 5. Alta Rock Art, Finnmark

The Alta rock carvings and paintings, inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1985, are the largest known concentration of prehistoric rock art in northern Europe, with over 6,000 individual figures spread across several locations around the Altafjord in Finnmark. The images were created by hunter-fisher-gatherer peoples between roughly 4200 and 500 BCE, depicting reindeer herds, elk, bears, salmon, boats, and human hunters in compositions of considerable narrative complexity. Some panels show remarkable superimposition over many centuries, providing a visual record of changing human concern. The Hjemmeluft site has a walkway system allowing close viewing without disturbance, and the adjacent museum provides excellent contextual material. Alta is accessible by air from Oslo and makes a natural base for northern lights tourism combined with prehistoric heritage.

## 6. Oseberg Ship Burial Site, Vestfold

The Oseberg burial mound near Tønsberg in Vestfold was excavated in 1904 and yielded the most spectacular Viking Age burial assemblage ever discovered: a complete 9th-century ship nearly 22 metres long, packed with extraordinarily well-preserved wooden artefacts including a cart, sledges, carved bedposts, textile fragments, and the remains of two women. The ship and its contents are now in the Viking Ship Museum in Oslo (currently being relocated to the new Viking Age Museum at Bygdøy). The mound itself at the farm of Lille Oseberg is a low grass-covered rise in agricultural land. While the museum in Oslo provides the primary encounter with the assemblage, visiting the mound site contextualises the scale of enterprise involved in constructing such a burial monument.

## 7. Trondenes Historical Centre and Church, Troms

Trondenes, on the Harstad peninsula in Troms, claims to host the northernmost medieval stone church in the world, built in the 13th–15th centuries CE on the site of an earlier Viking Age estate church. Archaeological investigation around and beneath the existing stone church has revealed extensive evidence of Iron Age and Viking Age settlement, including longhouse foundations, burial mounds, and artefacts indicating that Trondenes was a major chieftain's seat controlling access to northern Norwegian waters. The church interior preserves three carved medieval altarpieces. The adjacent history centre provides narrative context for the Norse occupation of Arctic Norway. The site sits at the edge of the town of Harstad, accessible by road and ferry.

## 8. Borgund Stave Church, Lærdal

Borgund Stave Church in the Lærdal valley is the best-preserved of Norway's roughly 28 surviving stave churches, built around 1150–1200 CE from the end of the Viking Age period when Christianity was reshaping Norwegian society but timber construction techniques remained the same as earlier sacred and secular architecture. The building's structure — vertical stave posts set on a sill beam, with tiered rooflines covered in wooden shingles — represents a distinctly Norwegian architectural form with no exact parallel elsewhere. Carved dragon heads on the roof ridges echo the imagery of the earlier pagan period. The church was in use until 1868 and is now managed as a heritage monument. The Urnes Stave Church in Luster, a UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1979, is the other essential Stave Church destination.

## 9. Hafrsfjord, Stavanger

Hafrsfjord, a bay just west of Stavanger in Rogaland, is traditionally identified as the location of the Battle of Hafrsfjord (around 872 CE), at which Harald Fairhair decisively defeated rival Norwegian chieftains and consolidated his control over a substantial kingdom. The site is marked by three large standing stones — the Swords in Rock monument (1983) — which are a modern installation but reference the Viking tradition of memorial stone-setting. The fjord itself and the surrounding landscape preserve archaeological features including burial mounds and coastal settlement traces associated with the Iron Age and early Viking Age. The broader Stavanger region is rich in prehistoric archaeology, and the Stavanger Museum holds significant regional collections.

## 10. Tune Ship Mound Site, Østfold

The Tune mound in Østfold, excavated in 1867, produced fragments of another of the great Viking Age ship burials, contemporary with Oseberg and Gokstad. Less complete than those two, the Tune ship nevertheless represents the oldest of the three clinker-built vessels and preserves the bottom strakes and keel of a ship dating to roughly 900 CE. The burial mound, on the farm of Rolvsøy near Fredrikstad, is a low earthen rise. The ship fragments and associated finds are housed in the Viking Age Museum in Oslo alongside the Oseberg and Gokstad ships. The Østfold region around Fredrikstad also contains Norway's best-preserved fortified medieval town at Gamlebyen and extensive Bronze Age rock carvings, making it one of the densest heritage landscapes in southeastern Norway.

Norway is well served by international and domestic airports, and the main heritage sites cluster conveniently around the Oslofjord, making day trips from Oslo viable for Borre, Oseberg, and Tune. Alta requires a flight to Finnmark and is best combined with a multi-day trip to Arctic Norway. Stave churches and Viking Age sites along the western fjords are accessible via the express boat network and the Flåm railway corridor. Most sites are open May to September; Alta is also excellent for northern lights viewing in winter. Entrance fees are modest; the Viking Age Museum in Oslo is the essential indoor complement to outdoor site visits.

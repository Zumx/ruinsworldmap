## What Makes a Palace?

The word 'palace' comes from the Palatine Hill in Rome, where the emperors built their residences, and the term has been extended backwards into deep antiquity to describe the great administrative, residential, and ceremonial complexes that served as the physical seats of power in ancient states. Identifying a building as a palace rather than a temple, a large private house, or an administrative centre requires some care; in many ancient societies the distinctions were fluid or non-existent. The ruler was simultaneously priest, administrator, and military commander, and the building that housed these functions combined throne room, audience hall, storeroom, treasury, shrine, and residence in a single complex.

What palaces share across cultures and centuries is scale, spatial hierarchy, and the deliberate use of architecture to express authority. The visitor approaching a palace was meant to feel small. The sequence of courtyards, gateways, and thresholds was designed to make access conditional, to filter the petitioner through escalating degrees of monumentality before reaching the inner sanctum. The materials — fine stone, imported timber, precious metals, vivid pigments — announced that the ruler commanded resources beyond what any individual could accumulate.

## Minoan Palaces: Knossos and the Labyrinth

The Bronze Age palaces of Minoan Crete, built and rebuilt between roughly 2000 and 1450 BC, are among the earliest and most architecturally sophisticated in Europe. Knossos, near modern Heraklion, is the largest: at its peak it covered roughly 22,000 square metres across four or more storeys around a central rectangular court. Sir Arthur Evans, who began excavating in 1900 and continued for decades, reconstructed large sections in reinforced concrete — a decision that has attracted sustained criticism but gives visitors a vivid, if sometimes misleading, impression of the original. The palace contained a throne room with a gypsum throne flanked by painted griffins, elaborate plumbing systems including ceramic drain pipes and a flushing toilet, enormous storage magazines for oil, wine, and grain in great pithoi jars, workshops, shrines, and the 'piano nobile' of state apartments.

Phaistos and Akrotiri Triada on the Mesara plain in southern Crete, and Mallia and Zakros on the northern and eastern coasts, each have their own palace complexes. The unifying feature of all the Minoan palaces is the central court oriented roughly north-south, around which functional zones are arranged. The absence of obvious fortification walls at most Minoan palaces has been interpreted as evidence of Minoan confidence in their maritime dominance — the 'Minoan thalassocracy' of ancient literary tradition.

## Mycenaean Citadels: Tiryns and Pylos

The Mycenaean palace tradition of mainland Greece (roughly 1600–1100 BC) operated on different principles. The citadels at Mycenae and Tiryns in the Argolid are heavily fortified, their massive Cyclopean walls of enormous limestone blocks conveying military power rather than the openness of Minoan design. The palace at Mycenae, built on the summit of its rocky hill within the Lion Gate, centred on a megaron — a large rectangular hall with a central hearth and four columns supporting the roof — which was the throne room and ritual centre of Mycenaean rule. The walls of the megaron were painted with fresco cycles of warriors and chariots.

The palace at Pylos in the western Peloponnese, the 'Palace of Nestor' of the Homeric tradition and excavated from 1939 onwards, is the best preserved of all Mycenaean palaces, having been destroyed by fire around 1180 BC and not subsequently built over. The fire, while catastrophic, had the incidental effect of baking the palace archive: hundreds of Linear B tablets, the palace's administrative records in the earliest form of Greek writing, were hardened rather than destroyed. They reveal a complex bureaucratic state tracking personnel, livestock, land tenure, and military preparations — a palace economy of considerable sophistication.

## Near Eastern Palaces: Mari, Ugarit, and Persepolis

The palaces of the ancient Near East operated at a different scale from the Aegean examples. The Palace of Zimri-Lim at Mari on the Euphrates in Syria, dating to around 1800–1760 BC, covered roughly 2.5 hectares and contained around 260 rooms organised around a series of courtyards. Excavated by André Parrot from 1933 onwards, it yielded an archive of around 20,000 cuneiform tablets documenting the diplomatic, administrative, and economic affairs of an Amorite kingdom at the height of its power. The palace walls were painted with elaborate compositions including an investiture scene of the king receiving symbols of authority from the goddess Ishtar — the 'Investiture Fresco', now in the Louvre.

Persepolis in modern Iran, the ceremonial capital of the Achaemenid Persian Empire, represents the apotheosis of Near Eastern palace architecture. Built under Darius I from around 518 BC and completed and elaborated by his successors Xerxes I and Artaxerxes I, the complex is set on a great artificial terrace — the 'Terrace of Persepolis' — rising from the plain. It comprised the Apadana (audience hall, with a hypostyle hall of 72 columns and capacity for thousands of supplicants), the Gate of All Nations, the Throne Hall (also called the Hall of a Hundred Columns), and multiple palaces for the different Achaemenid kings. The stone reliefs on the staircases of the Apadana, showing delegations from across the empire bearing tribute — Lydians with bowls and bracelets, Nubians with a giraffe and elephant tusks, Indians with donkeys and bags — are the most detailed pictorial record of the multi-ethnic Achaemenid world. Persepolis was burnt by Alexander the Great in 330 BC, an act later interpreted as revenge for the Persian burning of Athens in 480 BC.

## Mesopotamian Palaces and the Palace of Sargon II

The Assyrian imperial palaces of the 1st millennium BC at Nimrud, Nineveh, and Khorsabad are known as much from museum collections as from the surviving sites, since the great British and French excavations of the 19th century removed enormous quantities of relief sculpture. The Palace of Sargon II at Khorsabad (Dur-Sharrukin), built between roughly 717 and 706 BC, covered roughly 10 hectares and was decorated with hundreds of metres of carved limestone reliefs showing military campaigns, ceremonial processions, and mythological scenes. The entrance was flanked by enormous lamassu — human-headed winged bulls — weighing up to 40 tonnes. Sargon II never completed his palace: he was killed in battle in 705 BC, and his son Sennacherib moved the capital to Nineveh, abandoning Khorsabad after less than a year of occupation.

## Visiting the Great Palaces

Several of the most important ancient palaces are fully accessible today. Knossos receives large numbers of visitors and has an excellent site museum at the Heraklion Archaeological Museum. Persepolis, a UNESCO World Heritage Site, is one of the most impressive ancient sites in Iran — the scale of the terrace and the quality of the stone carving, though damaged by Alexander's fire and later plundering, remain extraordinary. Pylos, though less dramatically visible than Knossos, offers the most authentic Mycenaean palace experience, with the megaron's central hearth, porch, and portico surviving to substantial height. Tiryns and Mycenae together can be visited in a single day from the modern city of Nafplio.

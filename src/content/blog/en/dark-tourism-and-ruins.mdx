## The Pull of the Catastrophic

Every year, millions of people travel specifically to places associated with death, disaster, and collective trauma. Pompeii, destroyed by the eruption of Vesuvius in 79 AD, receives around 3.5 million visitors annually — more than almost any other archaeological site in Europe. Auschwitz-Birkenau, the Nazi extermination complex in Poland, receives over two million. Chernobyl's exclusion zone, opened to guided tours in 2011, draws tens of thousands of visitors each year. The Killing Fields of Cambodia, the 9/11 Memorial in New York, the Hiroshima Peace Memorial Park — these are among the most visited commemorative sites in the world.

The academic term 'dark tourism' was coined by John Lennon and Malcolm Foley in a 1996 article and developed into a formal field of study. It describes the practice of visiting sites associated with death, atrocity, tragedy, or the macabre — and it turns out to be not a marginal curiosity but one of the largest and fastest-growing sectors of global tourism. Understanding why requires asking what people are actually looking for when they travel to places of catastrophe.

## Pompeii: The Ur-Case of Catastrophic Tourism

Pompeii holds a special place in any discussion of dark tourism because it was among the first ancient sites to attract visitors specifically because of its manner of destruction, and because the catastrophe that destroyed it is precisely what preserved it. The city of Pompeii was buried under several metres of volcanic ash and pumice on 24 August (or possibly late October) 79 AD; the buildings, streets, furniture, food, graffiti, and — most disturbingly — the shapes of the victims were preserved beneath the debris.

The plaster casts of the victims, made by pouring liquid plaster into the voids left by decomposed bodies in the hardened ash, are the most viscerally affecting objects at the site. They are not sculptures in the artistic sense; they are death masks of entire bodies, capturing postures of terror and asphyxiation preserved for nearly two thousand years. Visitors crowd around the glass cases holding these figures in a way that they do not crowd around the fine frescoes or the forum. The human presence in extremis — the moment of death, made permanent — is the thing that people come to see.

Pompeii has been attracting visitors since the 18th century, when systematic excavation began under the Bourbon kings of Naples. Goethe visited in 1787 and was both horrified and transfixed. The Romantic tradition of ruin-visiting found in Pompeii its perfect subject: a complete city, halted in a specific moment, full of the remains of ordinary life alongside evidence of violent destruction.

## Herculaneum and the Question of Interpretation

Pompeii's neighbour Herculaneum, a smaller, wealthier city that was buried under a different deposit — not ash and pumice but a deep flow of pyroclastic material that carbonised organic matter rather than decaying it — presents similar issues of visitor management and interpretation. The carbonised wooden furniture, doors, and roof beams at Herculaneum are unique survivals; the boat sheds on the ancient beach, where around 300 skeletons were found huddled, give a similarly direct encounter with death.

The interpretation of these skeletal remains — their causes of death (thermal shock from the pyroclastic surge), their demographic profile, the objects found with them — raises questions about the difference between scientific analysis and the display of human remains for public engagement. Herculaneum's site management, since the 2001 establishment of the Herculaneum Conservation Project, has worked toward a more contextualised and respectful presentation, but the fundamental tension between scientific transparency and the dignity of the dead is not easily resolved.

## Abandoned Cities: Productive and Industrial Ruin

Not all ruin tourism involves catastrophic death. Entire categories of abandoned settlements attract visitors for reasons that are partly aesthetic — the melancholy beauty of decay, the reclamation of human space by nature — and partly historical. The abandoned coal-mining town of Kolmanskop in the Namibian desert, where sand dunes have half-filled the German colonial-era buildings, is a popular photographic destination. Pripyat, the Soviet city built to house the workers of the Chernobyl nuclear plant and abandoned after the 1986 disaster, has become one of the most photographed abandoned places in the world.

In the ancient context, the category of 'abandoned city' is large: Ani, the medieval Armenian capital in eastern Turkey, abandoned after a Mongol invasion and an earthquake; Vijayanagara (Hampi) in Karnataka, India, sacked by the Deccan Sultanates in 1565 and never reoccupied; Pagan (Bagan) in Myanmar, with its thousands of Buddhist temples spread across a dry plain. Each attracts visitors partly for its history and partly for the visual experience of scale and abandonment that a functioning city cannot provide.

## The Ethics of Visiting Dark Sites

Dark tourism raises genuine ethical questions that its practitioners, scholars, and site managers continue to debate. The most acute concerns involve the display of human remains and the relationship between visitor experience and memorialisation. Are the plaster casts of Pompeii's victims educational exhibits or objects of voyeuristic fascination? Should human skeletal material be displayed at all, or reburied as some indigenous communities and descendants' groups argue? How do you manage a site like Auschwitz — where the obligation to preserve memory and educate future generations sits alongside the obligation to maintain the dignity of the site as a place of mass murder?

For ancient sites, the ethical dimensions are somewhat different because there are no living descendants whose immediate grief needs to be respected, but the questions of interpretation and respect remain. Battlefield sites, massacre sites, and sites of human sacrifice raise questions about whether commercial tourism is an appropriate mode of engagement with what was, however long ago, a scene of violence and death.

## Ruin Aesthetics and the Making of Melancholy

The Romantic tradition of ruin aesthetics, which emerged in 18th-century Europe, explicitly cultivated the emotional experience of encountering decay and destruction. Capricci — imaginary paintings of ruins, often combining real monuments in fantastical compositions — by artists including Giovanni Battista Piranesi and Hubert Robert created a visual language for ruins that was simultaneously nostalgic, sublime, and melancholic. The 'pleasure of ruins', as Rose Macaulay called it in her 1953 book of that name, was a cultivated aesthetic response rather than a natural one.

This aesthetic framework shapes how many visitors still experience ancient sites. The broken column, the fragment of sculpture, the grass-covered mound where a city once stood — these are powerful images precisely because their incompleteness invites imaginative reconstruction. The ruin is a presence that is also an absence, an invitation to project the past onto the present.

## Managing the Dark Tourist

Site managers at major ruins face a specific challenge: visitor numbers driven partly by the very qualities — catastrophic destruction, human remains, sensational history — that responsible interpretation tries to contextualise. Pompeii introduced a new management plan in the 2010s to improve access, restore deteriorating structures, and control visitor flow. The Great Pompeii Project, funded partly by European Union resources, has worked systematically through the unexcavated third of the site and the deteriorating structures of the excavated two-thirds.

Visitor limits, ticketing windows, and guided-tour requirements have been introduced at various dark sites globally. Whether these measures adequately balance the educational value of mass access with the conservation needs of fragile sites and the dignity due to the dead remains an open and contested question.

The territory of modern North Macedonia occupies a central position in the ancient Balkans, lying at the intersection of the Greek, Roman, and later Byzantine worlds. It formed the heartland of the ancient Macedonian kingdom — the state that produced Alexander III and redrew the map of the known world — and was subsequently reorganised as the Roman province of Macedonia Prima. The region's river valleys, particularly those of the Vardar and the Crna, funnelled trade and armies between the Aegean and the Danube, and the cities that grew along these routes left substantial ruins. From the Hellenistic theatre at Heraclea Lyncestis to the Byzantine episcopal complex at Plaošnik, North Macedonia contains a dense layer of Greco-Roman and early Christian archaeology within a compact, easily navigable country.

## 1. Heraclea Lyncestis, Bitola

Heraclea Lyncestis is the best-preserved ancient city in North Macedonia and one of the outstanding Hellenistic and Roman sites in the entire Balkans. Founded around the mid-4th century BCE by Philip II of Macedon as a military base and named after the hero Heracles, the city was captured by Rome in 168 BCE and subsequently flourished as a municipal centre. The visible remains include a Roman theatre with well-preserved stage building, a colonnaded portico, baths, and two magnificent Late Antique mosaic floors that are considered among the finest in southeastern Europe. The larger mosaic, the Great Basilica floor, depicts birds, fish, and architectural frames in exceptional detail. A small Episcopal basilica and bishop's residence were added in the 4th–5th centuries CE. The site lies on the southern edge of Bitola and is open daily.

## 2. Stobi, Gradsko Municipality

Stobi, at the confluence of the Crna (Erigon) and Vardar (Axios) rivers, was one of the most important cities of the Roman province of Macedonia from the 1st century BCE through the 6th century CE, when it was devastated by the Avar invasions. It served as the capital of the later province of Macedonia Salutaris and was a significant episcopal see. The excavated remains cover a substantial area and include a Roman theatre, a late Roman palatial building, a large residential house (House of Peristeria) with outstanding mosaic floors, the Episcopal Basilica with a baptistery, and the street grid of the lower city. Stobi's ceramic and numismatic finds span several centuries of continuous occupation. The site has an on-site museum and is actively excavated by a joint American-Macedonian team.

## 3. Scupi, Skopje Suburb

Scupi, now within the northwestern suburbs of Skopje near the village of Zlokukani, was the Roman colony Colonia Flavia Scupinorum, established in the 1st century CE on the site of an earlier Dardanian settlement. It served as the chief urban centre of the Dardania region and lies on the road between Naissus (Niš) and Thessalonica. Systematic excavations since the mid-20th century have revealed a theatre, public bath complex, forum, water supply system, and extensive residential areas. The theatre, with a capacity of roughly 5,000, is one of the most significant Roman theatrical structures in the region. Scupi was damaged by earthquake in 518 CE and later incorporated into Byzantine and medieval settlement patterns. Many finds are now in the Museum of Macedonia in Skopje.

## 4. Bargala, Štip Municipality

Bargala is a Late Roman and Early Byzantine fortified city in eastern North Macedonia, most actively occupied between the 4th and 7th centuries CE. The site was identified through survey and excavated from the 1960s onward, revealing a compact urban nucleus with a basilica, episcopal complex, public bath, water cisterns, and a defensive circuit wall with towers. The Episcopal Basilica is one of the best-preserved early Christian ecclesiastical structures in the country, with floor mosaics still in place. Bargala was likely a bishop's seat and served an administrative function for the surrounding agricultural region. The city was abandoned following the Avar-Slav incursions of the late 6th and early 7th centuries. Located near the village of Beli, it is accessible by road from Štip.

## 5. Plaošnik (Ohrid Old Town)

The Plaošnik headland within the UNESCO World Heritage Old Town of Ohrid contains the remains of one of the most significant early medieval ecclesiastical complexes in southeastern Europe. Archaeological excavations beneath and around the 11th-century Church of Saints Clement and Panteleimon have uncovered the foundations of a large Early Christian basilica of the 5th–6th centuries CE — three-aisled, with an atrium and extensive mosaic floors. It was on this site that Saints Clement of Ohrid established one of the first Slavic literary schools in the late 9th century CE, creating the Glagolitic alphabet and contributing to the translation of Christian texts into Old Church Slavonic. The medieval church above has been reconstructed; the archaeological foundations are visible in the crypt. Ohrid itself was a major Byzantine episcopal and cultural centre.

## 6. Lychnidos (Ohrid), Lake Ohrid Shore

Before the medieval city of Ohrid, the settlement of Lychnidos occupied the same promontory above Lake Ohrid and was an important Macedonian and later Roman town on the Via Egnatia. Roman-era remains visible in and around Ohrid include a theatre built into the hillside — partially restored and still used for summer performances — as well as stretches of Roman road and scattered architectural fragments incorporated into later buildings. Excavations in the old city have produced Hellenistic and Roman ceramics, coins, and structural remains beneath the medieval layers. The lake itself, one of the oldest and deepest in Europe, has yielded evidence of prehistoric pile-dwelling settlements on its shores, extending human occupation of the area back several millennia before the Roman period.

## 7. Isar Marvinci, Valandovo Municipality

Isar Marvinci is a hilltop settlement site in the Vardar valley south of Valandovo with occupation phases spanning from the Bronze Age through the early medieval period. The Iron Age and Hellenistic phases are best documented archaeologically, with substantial pottery assemblages and evidence of intensive craft production recovered during excavation. The site's elevated position above the Vardar gorge reflects the defensive priorities of pre-Roman valley communities. A late 4th-century BCE destruction level has been associated with the reorganisation of the region following Macedonian consolidation. The hilltop preserves visible traces of terracing and walling and is accessible by unpaved road from the valley floor.

## 8. Kokino, Kumanovo Municipality

Kokino is a Bronze Age sanctuary and astronomical observation site on a volcanic rocky outcrop at around 1,013 metres elevation near the village of Kokino in northeastern North Macedonia. Dating to roughly the 19th century BCE, the site consists of stone thrones and throne-like niches cut into the rock, along with marker stones aligned to significant solar and lunar events, including sunrise at the winter and summer solstices and the spring and autumn equinoxes. NASA listed Kokino in 2005 as one of the world's oldest astronomical observatories. The interpretation of the site as an elite ritual and calendar-marking location is supported by finds of Bronze Age pottery, animal bones, and metal objects in the crevices of the rock. The site is now formally protected and has a small visitor infrastructure.

## 9. Skupi Ancient Theatre Area, Skopje Region

Beyond the main Scupi site, ongoing urban archaeology in the Skopje region has revealed further Roman-period infrastructure including elements of road networks, bridges over the Vardar, and suburban villa complexes with mosaic floors. The Kale fortress in central Skopje itself, though principally a medieval structure, sits on a site with continuous occupation from at least the Roman period, and excavations in the lower fortress have produced Roman-era finds including inscriptions and architectural elements. The city's archaeology museums — particularly the Macedonia Museum of Natural History and the Museum of Macedonia — provide the primary access point for finds from the broader Skopje-Scupi archaeological zone.

## 10. Demir Kapija Gorge Rock Sanctuary

The dramatic Demir Kapija gorge in the southern Vardar valley, where the river cuts through high limestone cliffs, contains evidence of cult activity from the Hellenistic and Roman periods. Niches carved into the cliff face, votive inscriptions, and relief sculptures of deities including the Thracian Horseman have been found at several points along the gorge. The location — a natural gateway on one of the main Balkan routes — made it a logical site for propitiation of journey gods, and the concentration of carved niches suggests sustained use over several centuries. The gorge is now partly flooded by a reservoir, but accessible cliff-face sections preserve visible cult niches. The village of Demir Kapija, with its wine production, is a comfortable base.

North Macedonia is a compact country and all sites can be reached within a few hours of Skopje or Ohrid. The country has a well-developed road network and no visa requirements for most European and North American visitors. The best season for site visits is April to October; summer heat at valley sites can be intense in July and August, making spring and autumn the most comfortable periods. Heraclea and Stobi have entrance fees; most other sites charge little or nothing.

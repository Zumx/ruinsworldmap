Mexico's pre-Columbian heritage spans nearly three millennia of urban civilisation across extraordinary geographic diversity: the arid highlands where Teotihuacan rose to become the largest city in the western hemisphere; the tropical lowlands of Chiapas and the Yucatán where Maya lords built cities of carved limestone; the cloud-forest ridges of Oaxaca where the Zapotec founded one of Mesoamerica's first urban centres; and the Gulf Coast where the Olmec laid the template for much that followed. The sites below span from around 500 BCE to the early sixteenth century and represent the full range of Mesoamerican architectural achievement — from the monumental pyramids of the Basin of Mexico to the cliff-edge temples of Tulum overlooking the Caribbean. Most are easily accessible and well maintained, making Mexico one of the world's great destinations for ancient archaeology.

## 1. Chichen Itza, Yucatán

Chichen Itza was a major Maya city in the northern Yucatán that reached its greatest power between roughly 900 and 1200 CE, when it became the dominant political centre of the region. The site blends classic Maya architectural traditions with a distinctive 'international' style that some scholars link to influence from central Mexico. El Castillo (Temple of Kukulcan), a stepped pyramid of precise proportions, produces a famous visual effect each equinox: triangular shadows on the balustrade create the illusion of a serpent descending the staircase. The Great Ball Court — the largest in Mesoamerica, 168 metres long — has carved reliefs of players and sacrifice scenes. The Sacred Cenote (natural sinkhole) received offerings including gold, jade, and human remains. A UNESCO World Heritage Site. El Castillo's interior is no longer open to climbing; tickets and tourist crush are significant — arrive at opening time or visit with a night-access ticket.

## 2. Teotihuacan, Basin of Mexico

Teotihuacan was the largest city in pre-Columbian Mesoamerica, with a population estimated between 100,000 and 200,000 at its peak between roughly 200 and 550 CE. Its builders remain uncertain — they preceded the Aztecs by many centuries and left no deciphered written record. The city was planned on a grid aligned to celestial orientations; the Avenue of the Dead runs between the Pyramid of the Moon at its northern end and the Great Compound market at the south. The Pyramid of the Sun, the third-largest pyramid in the world by volume, rises to around 65 metres. Painted murals in the residential compounds at Tetitla and Tepantitla are among the finest surviving examples of Mesoamerican painting. A UNESCO World Heritage Site. About 50 kilometres from Mexico City, reached by bus from Terminal Norte.

## 3. Palenque, Chiapas

Palenque sits in a dramatic position at the foot of the Chiapas highlands where the mountains meet the tropical lowland jungle. It was one of the most artistically refined Maya centres, reaching its height under K'inich Janaab' Pakal (known as Pakal the Great, reigned 615–683 CE) and his successors. The Palace complex — a multi-storey administrative and residential structure with a unique tower — and the Temple of the Inscriptions, which covers Pakal's extraordinary carved sarcophagus and tomb, define the site's core. The tomb, discovered by Alberto Ruz Lhuillier in 1952, was the first royal burial found in a Mesoamerican pyramid. The temples are surrounded by jungle, and the sound of howler monkeys punctuates the visit. A UNESCO World Heritage Site. The carved stucco portraits and inscriptions from Palenque are among the finest achievements of Maya art.

## 4. Tulum, Quintana Roo

Tulum is the only major Maya site built on a cliff edge overlooking the Caribbean Sea, making it the most visually dramatic of all Mexico's ancient ruins. The walled city was occupied primarily in the Postclassic period, between roughly 1200 and 1521 CE, and was still inhabited when Spanish sailors sighted it from the sea in 1518 — one of the first Mesoamerican cities Europeans observed. The Castillo temple, El Dios Descendente (Diving God) temple, and the Temple of the Frescoes, whose interior murals retain blue-green pigment, are the main structures. The site is easily the most visited in Mexico due to its beach setting; the best strategy is to arrive at opening time (8am) before the cruise-ship crowds arrive around mid-morning. A UNESCO World Heritage Site.

## 5. Uxmal, Yucatán

Uxmal was the dominant Maya city of the Puuc Hills region in northern Yucatán, flourishing between roughly 700 and 1000 CE. The Puuc architectural style is distinctive: smooth lower walls in limestone, with the upper facades densely covered in carved geometric mosaic — lattice patterns, stacked Chac rain-god masks, and serpent motifs in finely cut stone. The Pyramid of the Magician (also called the Pyramid of the Dwarf) has a distinctive oval base unique in Mesoamerican architecture. The Governor's Palace, with a 100-metre long mosaic facade, is considered by many archaeologists the finest single building in pre-Columbian Mesoamerica. Uxmal is significantly less crowded than Chichen Itza and is easily combined with Kabah, Sayil, and Labna on a Puuc route day trip. A UNESCO World Heritage Site.

## 6. Monte Albán, Oaxaca

Monte Albán was the Zapotec capital, built on a levelled mountain summit above the Oaxaca Valley from around 500 BCE. At its height between 300 BCE and 700 CE it held perhaps 25,000 people and dominated the surrounding valleys politically and militarily. The main plaza, artificially levelled and surrounded by pyramids and platforms, is one of the earliest planned urban spaces in Mesoamerica. The Building of the Danzantes ('dancers') is covered in carved stone slabs depicting captives or sacrificed individuals — some of the earliest identifiable political-narrative carving in Mesoamerica. Shaft tombs beneath the plazas have yielded extraordinary gold, jade, and ceramic objects. A UNESCO World Heritage Site. The summit position above Oaxaca city means it requires a short taxi or bus ride; the views over the three joining valleys are spectacular.

## 7. Calakmul, Campeche

Calakmul was one of the two superpowers of the Classic Maya world (the other being Tikal in Guatemala), controlling a vast territory in the lowland jungle of the Yucatán Peninsula from roughly 400 to 900 CE. It contains the largest pyramid in the Maya world by volume — Structure II, about 45 metres tall — and over 6,000 recorded structures. More stelae (carved stone monuments) have been found here than at any other Maya site; they record the names and deeds of the 'Snake Kingdom' dynasty in some of the most elaborate royal portraiture in Maya art. Calakmul is in a UNESCO-listed biosphere reserve; the combination of ancient ruins in primary rainforest — with jaguars, tapirs, and parrots regularly observed — makes it one of the most atmospheric sites in Mexico. Access is a three-hour drive from Chetumal or Campeche, the last stretch on an unpaved road.

## 8. Cobá, Quintana Roo

Cobá was a major Maya city that reached its height in the Early Classic period (roughly 250–600 CE) and remained occupied into the Postclassic. It is notable for two features: Nohoch Mul pyramid, at 42 metres the tallest structure in the Yucatán Peninsula (still climbable as of recent years, though policy changes periodically), and the most extensive network of sacbeob (raised stone causeways) in the Maya world — over 50 sacbeob radiate from the city's central plazas, some extending for dozens of kilometres through the jungle. The site is large and relatively unexcavated; most visitors see only a small fraction of the mapped structures. Bicycles can be rented at the entrance to cover the distances between plazas.

## 9. Palenque, Yaxchilan, and Bonampak Area, Chiapas (Usumacinta Region)

The Usumacinta River basin preserves several spectacular Maya sites beyond Palenque. Yaxchilan, accessible only by boat on the Usumacinta, is famous for its elaborately carved stone lintels above doorways, which record the deeds of a dynasty of warrior-kings with some of the finest narrative carving in Mesoamerica. Bonampak, inland in Lacandón jungle, contains the most complete surviving Maya mural cycle: three rooms painted around 790 CE depict a battle, the torture of captives, and a victory celebration in vivid polychrome that transformed understanding of Maya warfare and court ritual. Both sites require overnight trips from Palenque and involve jungle travel; a guide is essential for Bonampak.

## 10. Tenochtitlan (Templo Mayor), Mexico City

Tenochtitlan was the Aztec (Mexica) capital, built on an island in Lake Texcoco from around 1325 CE and grown by 1519 into a city of perhaps 200,000 to 300,000 people — larger than any European city of the time. The Spanish destroyed it in 1521 and built Mexico City on its ruins. The Templo Mayor, the great dual pyramid at the city's ceremonial centre, was rediscovered beneath Mexico City's historic centre in 1978 and has been excavated progressively since. Over 200 ritual caches have been found, containing carved stone sculptures, turquoise mosaics, jaguar skeletons, and the remains of sacrificial offerings. The Templo Mayor Museum adjacent to the excavation is one of the finest archaeological museums in the Americas.

---

Mexico's major sites are well served by the national archaeological service (INAH). Most sites charge modest admission fees; photography is permitted but tripods may require an additional fee. Yucatán and Oaxaca sites are best visited October to April when humidity is lower and tropical rains less frequent. Jungle sites like Calakmul and Yaxchilan are most accessible in the dry season (December to April). Many Yucatán sites can be combined efficiently with beach time on the Riviera Maya.

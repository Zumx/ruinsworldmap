## What a Temple Was — and Was Not

The word 'temple' comes from the Latin templum, which originally denoted a consecrated area of sky observed for augury before it acquired the sense of a sacred building. This etymology hints at something important: across most ancient cultures, a temple was first and foremost a bounded space where the human and divine could meet. What went on inside varied enormously. Greek and Roman temples housed cult statues and temple treasures but were not intended for congregational worship; sacrifices took place at altars outside, in the open air. Egyptian temples were sealed residences for divine statues, entered only by the priesthood. Hindu temples were cosmograms, architecturally embodying the mountain at the centre of the universe. Understanding ancient sacred architecture requires setting aside the association with Sunday services.

## Egyptian Temples: Enclosure of the Divine

Egyptian temple design reached its canonical form in the New Kingdom (roughly 1550–1070 BC) and persisted with remarkably little variation into the Ptolemaic and Roman periods. The standard plan moved from the most public to the most sacred along a single processional axis: a pair of massive tapering pylons forming the entrance gateway, then an open colonnaded courtyard, then a hypostyle hall — its ceiling supported by forests of columns — and finally the innermost sanctuary housing the cult statue, accessible only to senior priests.

Karnak at Luxor accumulated this formula over fifteen centuries, creating a layered architectural biography of the New Kingdom and its successors. Its Hypostyle Hall, covering roughly 5,000 square metres, is the largest surviving columned room from the ancient world. The Temple of Luxor, connected to Karnak by an avenue of sphinxes, was largely the work of Amenhotep III in the 14th century BC, though Ramesses II added the entrance pylon and the colossal seated statues in front of it. One of the original pair of obelisks from that pylon stands today in the Place de la Concorde in Paris, given to France by Egypt in 1833.

Philae, an island temple complex in the Nile near Aswan dedicated primarily to Isis, was built mostly in the Ptolemaic and Roman periods but followed the classical Egyptian template precisely. When the Aswan High Dam threatened to submerge Philae permanently in the 1960s and 1970s, UNESCO coordinated a massive international rescue operation that dismantled the entire temple complex stone by stone and reassembled it on the higher island of Agilkia between 1972 and 1980. The project remains one of the most complex conservation operations ever undertaken.

## Greek Temples: Mathematics Made Sacred

The Greek temple was essentially a house for the deity's statue, raised on a stepped platform (the stylobate) and surrounded by a colonnade. The refinement of the Doric, Ionic and Corinthian orders over the 7th through 4th centuries BC produced some of the most precisely calibrated architecture in the ancient world.

The Parthenon, completed on the Athenian Acropolis in 432 BC under the direction of Pheidias and the architects Iktinos and Kallikrates, is the canonical example. Its apparent simplicity conceals extraordinary optical corrections: the columns are not straight cylinders but swell slightly at the mid-point (entasis) to avoid appearing concave; the stylobate curves upward slightly at the centre; the corner columns are slightly thicker and lean inward. These adjustments, barely measurable individually, combine to make the building appear perfectly regular to the eye. The temple originally housed an immense gold and ivory statue of Athena Parthenos, destroyed in late antiquity; the outer marble frieze depicted the Panathenaic procession in running high relief. Much of what remained of the sculptural programme was removed by Lord Elgin between 1801 and 1812 and is now in the British Museum.

The Temple of Apollo at Corinth, with seven standing Doric columns surviving from the late 6th century BC, is among the oldest standing Greek temple remains on the Greek mainland. Paestum (Poseidonia) in southern Italy preserves three exceptional Doric temples in a state of completeness unmatched in Greece itself: the Temple of Hera I ('Basilica', around 550 BC), the Temple of Hera II ('Neptune', around 460 BC) and the Temple of Ceres (actually Athena, around 500 BC). UNESCO inscribed Paestum as part of the Cilento and Vallo di Diano National Park in 1998.

## Roman Temples: Imperial Synthesis

Roman temple architecture absorbed Greek forms and reworked them. Where Greek temples stood freely in the landscape, Roman temples were typically raised on a high podium with a single frontal staircase, emphasising the facade and creating a sense of vertical drama suited to urban settings. The Corinthian order, favoured for its decorative richness, became standard.

The Pantheon in Rome, rebuilt under Hadrian around AD 125 on the site of an earlier structure, is the best-preserved ancient building in the world, continuously used since antiquity (consecrated as a Christian church in AD 609, which ensured its survival). Its unreinforced concrete dome, with an internal diameter of 43.3 metres, was the largest in the world for nearly 1,300 years. The oculus at its apex — nine metres across — is the building's only source of natural light. The dome's coffering, originally gilded, was removed over centuries; what remains still astonishes.

The Temple of Jupiter at Baalbek in Lebanon's Bekaa Valley, built by the Romans between roughly the 1st century BC and the 3rd century AD, was among the largest temples in the Roman world. Six of its original 54 Corinthian columns, each standing roughly 21 metres high and made from single granite shafts quarried in Aswan and transported across the Mediterranean, still stand. Baalbek is inscribed by UNESCO.

## Hindu and Buddhist Temples in Asia

In South Asian temple architecture, the form of the building is inseparable from its meaning. The Brihadeeswara Temple in Thanjavur, Tamil Nadu, built under the Chola king Rajaraja I and completed around AD 1010, rises to a height of roughly 66 metres — the vimana (tower) above the sanctuary soaring above the flat Cauvery delta landscape. It is the largest temple built in India up to that time, constructed almost entirely from granite without mortar, the stones cut with extraordinary precision. UNESCO inscribed it as part of the Great Living Chola Temples designation in 1987.

Angkor Wat in Cambodia, begun by the Khmer king Suryavarman II in the early 12th century AD and covering roughly 400 hectares including its moat and causeway, synthesises the Hindu temple-mountain concept at an unprecedented scale. The five central towers represent Mount Meru; the moat represents the cosmic ocean. Its nearly 800 metres of bas-relief galleries depict the Mahabharata, the Ramayana and historical scenes of Suryavarman's army. The temple was rededicated to Theravada Buddhism in the 14th or 15th century, a transition that preserved rather than destroyed it.

## Mesoamerican and Andean Temples

Mesoamerican temple pyramids share a stepped-platform form but were typically surmounted by small sanctuaries reached by steep external staircases, making public ritual visible to crowds below. The Great Temple (Templo Mayor) of Tenochtitlan, excavated in downtown Mexico City from 1978 onward after a chance discovery during electrical cable laying, was dedicated jointly to Tlaloc (rain and agriculture) and Huitzilopochtli (war and the sun). Successive Aztec rulers rebuilt over the existing structure at least seven times between the temple's foundation around AD 1325 and the Spanish conquest of 1521, each new skin enclosing the previous one.

At Chavin de Huantar in the Peruvian Andes, occupied primarily between roughly 900 and 200 BC, the Old Temple and subsequent New Temple were built over an elaborate network of underground stone-lined galleries whose acoustic properties — the galleries channel sound in a way that would have created dramatic auditory effects during ritual — appear to have been deliberate. UNESCO inscribed Chavin de Huantar in 1985.

Iraq is the heartland of Mesopotamian civilization — the land between the Tigris and Euphrates rivers where urban life, writing, codified law, and monumental architecture first emerged. Sumerian city-states flourished here from around 3500 BC; the Akkadian Empire, the Third Dynasty of Ur, Babylon, and Assyria all rose to dominate the ancient world from this territory. The sites that remain are among the most historically important on earth, though decades of conflict, looting, and limited archaeological access have taken a severe toll. The UNESCO inscription of Babylon in 2019, and the ongoing recovery of Mosul-area sites after the destruction wrought by ISIS between 2014 and 2017, mark a cautious new chapter in Iraq's efforts to protect and present this extraordinary heritage. ## 1. Babylon, Babil Province

Babylon was the capital of the Babylonian Empire and, at its height under Nebuchadnezzar II in the 6th century BC, one of the largest and most spectacular cities in the ancient world. The Ishtar Gate — faced with brilliant blue-glazed brick decorated with bulls and dragons — and the Processional Way that led through it were among antiquity's most celebrated monuments; most of the original gate was excavated by German archaeologists in the early 20th century and reconstructed in the Pergamon Museum in Berlin. At the site itself, the foundations of palaces, temples, and the Processional Way survive, along with the remains of the outer city walls and the mound of Etemenanki — the ziggurat that may have inspired the biblical Tower of Babel. Saddam Hussein's 1980s reconstruction project added incongruous modern brick over ancient foundations, a controversial decision that complicates both interpretation and conservation. UNESCO World Heritage Site since 2019. Located about 85 kilometres south of Baghdad.

## 2. Ur, Dhi Qar Province

Ur was a Sumerian city-state that rose to dominance in the 3rd millennium BC and gave its name to the Third Dynasty of Ur (2112–2004 BC), a brief period of imperial centralization that is sometimes called the Sumerian Renaissance. The Ziggurat of Ur, built by King Ur-Nammu around 2100 BC and restored by Nebuchadnezzar II some 1,500 years later, is the best-preserved ziggurat in Mesopotamia — its lower two stages still standing to a height of around 30 metres, the mud-brick core protected by a baked-brick outer casing. Leonard Woolley's excavations of the Royal Cemetery at Ur in the 1920s produced extraordinary finds including the Ram in a Thicket, the Standard of Ur, and gold headdresses — most now in the British Museum and the Iraq Museum in Baghdad — along with evidence for mass human sacrifice accompanying royal burials. The site is located near the modern town of Nasiriyah and is accessible by road; security conditions should be checked before travel.

## 3. Hatra, Ninawa Province

Hatra was a fortified city and religious centre of the Parthian period, flourishing between roughly the 1st century BC and 240 AD as a semi-autonomous vassal of the Parthian Empire on the edge of the Mesopotamian steppe. Its massive circular city wall — roughly two kilometres in diameter — and its extraordinary temple complex in the city centre, known as the Great Temenos, contained shrines to a syncretic mixture of Babylonian, Parthian, and Hellenistic deities. The colossal Iwan halls of the temple precinct, their pointed arches and deeply carved stucco work, represent a distinctive Parthian architectural tradition that influenced later Islamic architecture. Hatra famously withstood two Roman sieges (under Trajan and Septimius Severus) before falling to the Sasanian king Shapur I around 240 AD. In 2015 ISIS used bulldozers and explosives to systematically destroy substantial portions of the site, including its sculptural programme. UNESCO World Heritage Site, now on the Danger List.

## 4. Nimrud (Kalhu), Ninawa Province

Nimrud was the Assyrian capital under Ashurnasirpal II (883–859 BC) and his successors. Ashurnasirpal's renovation of the city was celebrated with a ten-day feast for 69,574 guests, recorded in an inscription. The site's Northwest Palace, excavated by Austen Henry Layard in the 1840s, yielded the colossal human-headed winged bull (lamassu) figures now in the British Museum and the Metropolitan Museum of Art. Layard's sensational finds effectively launched the field of Assyriology and transformed popular understanding of the biblical world. The site also contains the ziggurat of Kalhu, temples, and elite residential areas. Like Hatra, Nimrud was a target of deliberate ISIS destruction in 2015; the lamassu figures were sledgehammered in footage that shocked the world. Reconstruction and stabilization efforts have been underway since the site's liberation.

## 5. Nineveh, Ninawa Province

Nineveh was the greatest city of the Assyrian Empire, the capital of Sennacherib and Ashurbanipal in the 7th century BC, when it was reputedly the largest city in the world. Its walls, over 12 kilometres in circuit, contained palaces of extraordinary size and decoration; the Palace of Ashurbanipal housed a library of over 30,000 cuneiform tablets, including the Epic of Gilgamesh — the world's oldest narrative literature. The site sits on the outskirts of modern Mosul, and the Adad Gate and Mashki Gate have been reconstructed from their original materials. ISIS occupation from 2014 to 2016 resulted in the destruction of the Mosul Museum's Assyrian collection and deliberate damage to the site's gates and sculptures. Post-liberation assessment and restoration work by Iraqi and international teams has been extensive. The site is accessible from Mosul.

## 6. Ctesiphon, Baghdad Governorate

Ctesiphon served as the capital of both the Parthian and Sasanian empires and, for several centuries, was one of the largest cities in the world. The Taq Kasra (Arch of Ctesiphon) — the only standing structure of the ancient city — is a colossal brick barrel vault approximately 37 metres high and 26 metres wide, part of the audience hall (iwan) of the Sasanian royal palace. It is the largest single-span brick arch constructed in antiquity and remained the largest vault in the world until modern engineering. The remainder of the city has been destroyed by centuries of flooding, brick robbing, and agricultural expansion. The arch stands about 35 kilometres south of Baghdad near the town of Salman Pak; access and security conditions should be verified locally.

## 7. Uruk (Warka), Muthanna Province

Uruk is arguably the most important archaeological site in Iraq — perhaps the world — for the origins of urbanization. Occupied from around 5000 BC and reaching its greatest influence around 3300–3100 BC, Uruk gave the world its first monumental public architecture, the first writing (proto-cuneiform tablets), and possibly the earliest administrative bureaucracy. The Anu Ziggurat and the White Temple on its summit date to around 3100 BC; the Eanna precinct nearby was the religious centre of the goddess Inanna. Uruk is traditionally identified as the city of Gilgamesh. The site was extensively excavated by German archaeologists from 1912 through 1989, revealing an unmatched stratigraphic sequence of nearly 5,000 years of urban occupation. UNESCO World Heritage Site (as part of the Ahwar of Southern Iraq property). Located near Samawah in the southern desert.

## 8. Assur (Qal'at Sherqat), Saladin Province

Assur was the original capital of the Assyrian Empire and the seat of the national god Assur, whose name the city and the empire shared. Occupied from roughly 2500 BC, it was the Assyrian heartland before successive kings moved the capital to Nimrud, Khorsabad, and finally Nineveh. The site preserves the remains of temples to Assur and Ishtar, an Assyrian royal palace, and the royal tombs where a remarkable assemblage of gold jewellery and objects was found in excavations in the 1980s (some now in the Iraq Museum). Assur was designated a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2003 and simultaneously placed on the Danger List due to a planned dam on the Tigris that would have flooded the site; the dam project was cancelled but other threats remain. Located near the modern town of Shirqat.

## 9. Eridu, Dhi Qar Province

Eridu is arguably the oldest city in the world by Mesopotamian reckoning: the Sumerian King List records it as the first city, the place where kingship descended from heaven. Archaeological excavations have confirmed occupation from around 5400 BC, making it one of the earliest known urban settlements anywhere. The site preserves an extraordinary sequence of 18 superimposed temple levels representing the continuous development of religious architecture over more than 2,000 years before the great Sumerian dynasties. Eridu is part of the Ahwar of Southern Iraq UNESCO inscription. It lies in the southern desert near Nasiriyah, adjacent to the ancient shoreline of the Persian Gulf, which was considerably further north in antiquity.

## 10. Dur-Sharrukin (Khorsabad), Ninawa Province

Khorsabad was the short-lived capital built by the Assyrian king Sargon II between roughly 717 and 706 BC. Sargon constructed an entirely new city here — the name means 'Fort Sargon' — completing it just before his death in battle, after which his son Sennacherib immediately abandoned it for Nineveh. The site was excavated in the 19th century by French consul Paul-Émile Botta, whose discoveries of lamassu and wall reliefs inspired the contemporary British work at Nimrud and Nineveh. The most important finds are now in the Louvre. The site itself preserves mounded remains of the palace citadel and city layout, less visited than Nimrud and Nineveh but historically significant as a monument to Assyrian ambition and the contingency of imperial planning.

Iraq's major archaeological sites are distributed across the country, from the Tigris-Euphrates alluvial plain to the northern foothills. Security conditions have improved significantly in most of the country since the defeat of ISIS in 2017, and the Iraqi government has actively promoted heritage tourism. The southern sites (Ur, Uruk, Eridu) cluster around Nasiriyah, reachable by domestic flight; the northern Assyrian sites (Nimrud, Nineveh, Hatra) are in the Ninawa Province around Mosul. Babylon is the most accessible major site from Baghdad. Guided tours through specialist cultural operators offer the most secure and informed access.

The Swahili Coast — the 3,000-kilometre strip of East African shoreline stretching from the Lamu Archipelago in northern Kenya southward through Tanzania to the Mozambique Channel — was one of the great maritime trading worlds of the medieval period. From at least the 8th century AD and possibly earlier, fishing and farming communities along this coast became integrated into an Indian Ocean exchange system linking East Africa to Persia, the Arabian Peninsula, India, and eventually China. The commodities flowing outward — ivory, gold, iron, enslaved people, tortoiseshell, and mangrove poles — were matched by imports of glazed pottery, glass beads, fine textiles, and porcelain. The Swahili city-states that grew to control this trade were sophisticated, literate, Muslim from the 9th century onward, and architecturally ambitious: they built in coral rag and lime plaster, creating mosques, palaces, and multi-storey courtyard houses whose ruins now stand in forest and mangrove. The archaeology of the Swahili Coast remains one of the most active and productive in sub-Saharan Africa.

## 1. Kilwa Kisiwani, Southern Tanzania

Kilwa Kisiwani — 'Kilwa on the island' — was the dominant commercial city on the Swahili Coast from the 11th through the 15th centuries, controlling the gold trade from the Zimbabwe plateau to the Indian Ocean world. The Arab traveller Ibn Battuta, who visited in 1331, described it as one of the most beautiful cities in the world. The ruins on the island include the Husuni Kubwa palace complex, a remarkable structure with a domed audience hall, a sunken bathing pool, a large warehouse, and extensive domestic quarters; it was built around 1310–1320 by Sultan al-Hasan ibn Sulaiman. The Great Mosque of Kilwa, one of the oldest and largest coral-built mosques in sub-Saharan Africa, has a vaulted nave and a later domed extension added in the 15th century. Kilwa Kisiwani is a UNESCO World Heritage Site. Access is by boat from the town of Kilwa Masoko.

## 2. Gedi (Gede), Northern Kenya

Gedi is a ruined Swahili town in the coastal forest behind Watamu in Kilifi County, abandoned — for reasons still debated — in the late 17th or early 18th century and subsequently swallowed by the Arabuko-Sokoke forest. The ruins include a large Friday mosque, a palace, eight smaller mosques, and dozens of stone-built houses whose walls still stand to several metres in places. Imported Chinese porcelain, Venetian glass beads, and faience from Persia recovered from the excavations indicate active trade through the 15th and 16th centuries. The palace's elaborate carved doorframes and internal room sequence give a vivid sense of elite Swahili domestic architecture. Gedi is a national monument managed by the National Museums of Kenya, with a small on-site museum. It is accessible from Watamu or Malindi.

## 3. Songo Mnara, Southern Tanzania

Songo Mnara is the second island of the Kilwa UNESCO World Heritage Site, a small coral island adjacent to Kilwa that preserves an unusually complete late 14th–15th-century Swahili town. Unlike Kilwa Kisiwani, which was heavily reoccupied in later centuries, Songo Mnara was abandoned relatively cleanly, leaving a town plan of streets, houses, mosques, and tombs in a state of arrested decay that makes it exceptionally valuable for understanding Swahili urbanism. Recent British-led excavations have mapped the entire town using drone survey and ground-penetrating radar and have found evidence that household economics — the management of food, goods, and ritual — were more complex and varied than previously assumed. The island is accessible only by boat from Kilwa Masoko, usually combined with Kilwa Kisiwani.

## 4. Lamu Old Town, Lamu Archipelago, Kenya

Lamu is the oldest continuously inhabited Swahili settlement in East Africa, with a history of occupation stretching back to at least the 12th century. The old town, a UNESCO World Heritage Site, retains its medieval street plan — narrow lanes between tall coral-block houses with elaborately carved wooden doorframes — and a living community that has practised Swahili culture without major disruption for centuries. The Riyadha Mosque draws pilgrims from across East Africa for the Maulidi festival. The Lamu Museum occupies a 19th-century house and holds important collections of Swahili material culture. Lamu Fort, built by the Oman Sultanate in the early 19th century on the site of an earlier structure, dominates the waterfront. Lamu is accessible by short flight from Nairobi or Mombasa; vehicles are banned on the island.

## 5. Shanga, Pate Island, Kenya

Shanga, on the northern shore of Pate Island in the Lamu Archipelago, is one of the earliest known Swahili towns, with occupation beginning in the 8th century AD and reaching its peak in the 9th through 11th centuries before gradual decline. British excavations in the 1980s revealed a sequence of mosque buildings, the earliest a small timber structure of the 8th century replaced by successively larger coral-built Friday mosques through the 10th and 11th centuries — a unique stratigraphic record of early Islamisation on the East African coast. The town also yielded the Shanga hoard, a cache of early Islamic silver coins and glass that provides crucial evidence for the dating of early Swahili commercial activity. The ruins are overgrown and remote. Access requires a boat to Pate Island from Lamu and a long walk or hire of local transport.

## 6. Manda Island Ruins, Lamu Archipelago, Kenya

Manda Island, across the channel from Lamu, preserves the ruins of the medieval Swahili town of Manda, whose occupation overlaps partially with Shanga. British Museum excavations recovered Chinese Tang-dynasty ceramics, early Islamic pottery, and the remains of a substantial coral-block town. Manda Toto, a smaller associated site on a neighbouring islet, has produced the most easterly dated evidence of early Swahili occupation in the archipelago. The ruins are accessible by boat from Lamu and are managed alongside the broader Lamu archaeological zone.

## 7. Jumba la Mtwana, Mtwapa, Kenya

Jumba la Mtwana — 'the large house of the slave-master' in Swahili, a name applied by local people to the mysterious ruined town — is a 14th–15th-century Swahili settlement on the coast north of Mombasa near Mtwapa Creek. The ruins include four mosques, a group of coral-built houses, and a series of tombs with pillar-and-panel decoration characteristic of the Swahili Coast. The town appears to have been abandoned rapidly, possibly in the late 15th century, leaving household goods in situ. A small museum interprets the site; it is managed by the National Museums of Kenya and is accessible by road from Mombasa, about 15 kilometres north.

## 8. Vumba Kuu, Southern Kenya Coast

Vumba Kuu near the Kenya-Tanzania border is a relatively little-known Swahili town site occupied between roughly the 13th and 17th centuries. Its ruins include a mosque, domestic structures, and tombs, and its importance lies partly in its position as a link in the chain of coastal settlements between Mombasa and Kilwa. Survey and limited excavation have demonstrated its participation in the wider Indian Ocean trade system through assemblages of imported ceramics and glass. The site is in a forest setting and is not currently developed for tourism, though it is known to researchers of the Swahili Coast.

## 9. Takwa, Manda Island, Kenya

Takwa is a ruined Swahili town on the northwestern edge of Manda Island, distinct from the Manda town excavated by the British Museum. It was occupied between roughly the 15th and 17th centuries and was probably abandoned around 1700. The ruins include a Friday mosque — the best-preserved of any Swahili mosque in Kenya, with intact qibla wall and mihrab niche — a palace, and dense stone-built houses. The town is surrounded by dense mangrove forest that has preserved the ruins from robbing for building material. It is a national monument accessible by boat from Lamu, usually as a half-day excursion.

## 10. Ras Mkumbuu, Pemba Island, Tanzania

Ras Mkumbuu at the western tip of Pemba Island off the northern Tanzanian coast is one of the oldest known Swahili stone towns, with occupation documented from at least the 8th century AD through the 15th century. The ruins include a large congregational mosque and several residential structures. Pemba was an important centre of clove cultivation in the medieval period and may have supplied aromatics to the wider Indian Ocean world before the advent of Zanzibar's dominance. The site is in a forest reserve at the end of Pemba's western peninsula and requires local boat access. Zanzibar's Stone Town, on the nearby island, offers the most accessible concentrated Swahili architectural heritage in the region and is itself a UNESCO World Heritage Site.

## Planning Your Visit

Lamu and Gedi are the most accessible sites, both reachable from Nairobi and Mombasa with established tourist infrastructure; Lamu requires a short flight. Kilwa Kisiwani and Songo Mnara require reaching the remote Kilwa Masoko on the southern Tanzanian coast — accessible by bus from Dar es Salaam or by small aircraft. Shanga, Manda, and Takwa all require boat access from Lamu. The Swahili Coast is best visited in the dry seasons: January to February and June to September.

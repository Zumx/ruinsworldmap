Sri Lanka — ancient Taprobane to Greeks and Romans, Lanka and Serendib to South Asian and Arab traders — was one of the great centres of Buddhist civilisation in Asia. From around the 3rd century BC, when the teachings of the Buddha arrived according to tradition with the monk Mahinda, son of the Mauryan emperor Ashoka, the island's kings channelled enormous resources into religious architecture. The result is a landscape studded with stupas (dagobas), monasteries, carved rock temples, and elaborate irrigation infrastructure that sustained large populations for over a millennium. The 'Cultural Triangle' of the north-central dry zone — an area containing Anuradhapura, Polonnaruwa, and Sigiriya — holds one of the highest densities of UNESCO-listed sites in Asia. Much of this ancient hydraulic landscape is still operational, with tanks and channels built in the first millennium AD still supplying water.

## 1. Anuradhapura, North Central Province

Anuradhapura served as the capital of Sri Lanka for over a thousand years, from roughly the 4th century BC to the late 10th century AD when it was abandoned following invasions from southern India. The ancient city spreads across many square kilometres and contains an extraordinary assembly of Buddhist monuments. The Ruwanwelisaya dagoba, built by King Dutugamunu in the 2nd century BC, stands over 55 metres tall. The Jetavanaramaya, raised in the 3rd century AD, was one of the tallest brick structures in the ancient world when built. The Thuparamaya, traditionally held to contain relics of the Buddha, may be the oldest surviving stupa in Sri Lanka. The sacred Bodhi tree, grown from a cutting of the original tree under which the Buddha attained enlightenment, has been tended continuously since around 250 BC. The site is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and a living place of worship; dress modestly.

## 2. Polonnaruwa, North Central Province

After Anuradhapura fell, Polonnaruwa became the second great medieval capital of Sri Lanka, reaching its height between the 11th and 13th centuries AD under kings including Parakramabahu I. The ruins spread across a planned city with a royal precinct, council chamber, temples, and the great Parakrama Samudra reservoir — one of the largest ancient reservoirs in Asia. The Gal Vihara is the site's most iconic monument: four Buddha images carved directly from a single granite face, including a 14-metre-long reclining figure and a 7-metre-tall standing figure, both of outstanding sculptural quality. The Vatadage (circular relic house) at the Quadrangle is notable for its delicate stone screen carvings. Polonnaruwa is a UNESCO World Heritage Site with a good on-site museum.

## 3. Sigiriya, Central Province

On an abruptly rising granite monolith in the dry zone, the fortress palace of Sigiriya was built by King Kasyapa I between approximately 477 and 495 AD. The rock rises nearly 200 metres from the surrounding plain; a palace and audience hall occupied the flat summit. The approach to the summit passes a famous gallery of 22 surviving fresco paintings — the 'cloud maidens', painted in a sheltered cavity of the rock face — and a pair of massive lion's paws in brick and plaster, the remnant of a lion-shaped gateway that once framed the final staircase. The gardens at the base of the rock, with their geometric pools and water gardens, are among the oldest surviving landscaped gardens in the world. Sigiriya is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and one of Sri Lanka's most visited monuments; arrive early to avoid the worst of the midday heat.

## 4. Dambulla Cave Temple, Central Province

In the hills near Dambulla, a series of five natural cave chambers has been converted into Buddhist shrines over roughly two millennia. The earliest paintings and sculptures may date to the 1st century BC; the majority of what survives — 153 Buddha statues and over 2,000 square metres of ceiling frescoes — belongs to phases of patronage between the 11th and 18th centuries AD. The caves were used as a refuge by King Vattagamani Abhaya during his exile from Anuradhapura in the 1st century BC; he is traditionally credited with commissioning the first major phase of shrine construction on his return. The Golden Temple of Dambulla is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and an active place of Buddhist pilgrimage. Admission includes the cave temples; the surrounding rock offers views across the plain toward Sigiriya.

## 5. Mihintale, North Central Province

On a group of rocky hills about 12 kilometres northeast of Anuradhapura, Mihintale is traditionally held to be the site where the monk Mahinda first preached Buddhism to King Devanampiya Tissa around 247 BC, making it one of the most sacred sites in Sri Lankan Buddhism. The hill is ascended by a long staircase of over 1,800 granite steps. At the top stand several ancient dagobas including the Ambasthale Dagoba, marking the spot of Mahinda's first sermon, and the Mahaseya Dagoba. The 8th-century monastic hospital — the Mihintale Hospital — is the best-preserved ancient hospital complex in Sri Lanka, with stone medicine troughs and surgical equipment recovered in excavations. The site is freely accessible during daylight hours; shoes must be removed for the upper terraces.

## 6. Yapahuwa, North Western Province

Yapahuwa served briefly as the royal capital of Sri Lanka in the late 13th century AD, during a period when the island's political centre shifted south under pressure from South Indian invaders. The site is dominated by an enormous granite outcrop rising about 100 metres, with a royal palace and shrine on the summit. The most celebrated feature is the ornate decorated stairway and gateway on the western face, combining Sinhalese and South Indian architectural elements in intricately carved stone screens and balustrades. The Tooth Relic of the Buddha — the most sacred object in Sri Lankan Buddhism — was housed here briefly. Yapahuwa receives far fewer visitors than the main Cultural Triangle sites, lending it a peaceful quality. It is situated near Maho on the Colombo-Kandy-Jaffna railway line.

## 7. Ritigala Forest Monastery, North Central Province

Hidden within a nature reserve on a lonely hill rising from the dry zone plain, Ritigala was a forest monastery occupied by the Pamsukulika sect — ascetic monks who eschewed communal monastery life for isolated forest meditation. The ruins, dated roughly to the 1st through 8th centuries AD, include unusual double-platform structures connected by a raised paved pathway, all built from large blocks of granite without mortar. The identity and purpose of the platform structures is debated: they may be meditation walkways, library platforms, or ritual spaces. The dense secondary forest that has reclaimed the site lends it an atmosphere of genuine remoteness unusual in Sri Lanka's otherwise well-managed heritage zone. A forest department permit and guide are required for access.

## 8. Buduruvagala, Uva Province

On a granite outcrop near Wellawaya in southern Sri Lanka, seven large figures carved in high relief into the rock face date to around the 9th century AD, during the Mahayana Buddhist period of Sri Lankan religious culture — a tradition less visible in the country than Theravada Buddhism. The central group includes a large standing Buddha flanked by bodhisattva figures, with a vajra motif and a rare surviving trace of original gilded plaster. The site is smaller and quieter than the major Cultural Triangle monuments; it rewards visitors with an insight into the Mahayana tradition that briefly flourished on the island before the Theravada orthodoxy reasserted itself. Easily visited as a stop on the drive between Ella and Tissamaharama.

## 9. Kantarodai, Northern Province

Near Chunnakam on the Jaffna Peninsula, Kantarodai (also known as Kadurugoda) is a dense cluster of over 60 small dagobas of various sizes crowded together within a compact area, thought to date from around the 2nd century BC through to the 10th century AD. The site may represent a major funerary complex; some scholars interpret the concentration of small stupas as enshrining relics of monks or important laypeople. The Jaffna Peninsula has its own distinctive archaeological record, shaped by Tamil Hindu culture as well as Buddhist practice, and Kantarodai illustrates the Buddhist layer of this multi-religious landscape. The site is managed by the Central Cultural Fund; access is straightforward from Jaffna town.

## 10. Deraniyagala and the Prehistoric Caves of Fa Hien

Fa Hien Cave near Kalutara on the southwestern coast is one of the most significant prehistoric archaeological sites in South Asia. Excavations have recovered human skeletal remains dated to around 37,000 years ago, making them among the earliest anatomically modern humans outside Africa with associated tool kits. The cave yielded evidence of early use of bow-and-arrow technology, personal ornaments of perforated shell, and pigment use — a behavioural modernity comparable to contemporary sites in Africa and Europe. The site provides essential context for understanding the deep prehistory of the island before the first recorded historical civilisations arrived. Access is via a short walk from the main Colombo-Galle road; the cave itself is modest in scale but extraordinary in scientific significance.

## Planning Your Visit

Sri Lanka's main ancient sites cluster in the Cultural Triangle of the north-central dry zone, most conveniently based from Dambulla or Sigiriya. A dedicated archaeological circuit covering Anuradhapura, Polonnaruwa, Sigiriya, Dambulla, and Mihintale requires at minimum four to five days. The dry zone is most comfortably visited from December through March, when rainfall is minimal and temperatures manageable. Entrance to the major UNESCO sites is covered by the Cultural Triangle Round Ticket or sold individually; prices for foreign visitors are significantly higher than for Sri Lankan nationals. Dress code at all Buddhist religious sites requires covered shoulders and knees, and shoes must be removed at temple entrances.

## Beyond the Raid

The word Viking conjures longships and raids, but the Norse peoples of Scandinavia between roughly 750 and 1100 AD were above all traders, settlers, and town-builders whose commercial networks stretched from the Arctic circle to the Caspian Sea. The so-called Viking Age was a period of remarkable Norse expansion that founded towns, established new kingdoms, and left physical traces across an enormous geographic range — from L'Anse aux Meadows at the tip of Newfoundland to the Volga river markets of Russia, from the isle of Gotland in the Baltic to the streets of Dublin and York.

The archaeological record of Norse settlement is far richer than the monastic annals recording Viking raids, and in recent decades excavations at sites across Scandinavia, Britain, Ireland, Normandy, Iceland, and the North Atlantic have transformed our understanding of how these communities actually lived, traded, and died. Ship burials, ring fortresses, trading emporia, runic inscriptions, and harbour deposits collectively paint a picture of a sophisticated and cosmopolitan culture that was deeply integrated into the wider medieval world.

## The Trading Towns: Hedeby, Birka, and Kaupang

The economic engine of the Viking Age was the trading emporium — a specialised site of commercial exchange that concentrated craftspeople, merchants, and goods from wide networks. Three of the most important were Hedeby in present-day northern Germany (near Schleswig), Birka on an island in Lake Mälaren west of Stockholm, and Kaupang in the Vestfold region of Norway.

Hedeby, occupied from roughly 800 to around 1050 AD, was the largest trading centre in northern Europe at its height, with a dense settlement enclosed by a substantial earthen rampart. Excavations have yielded evidence for craft production including metalworking, comb-making from antler, glass bead production, and textile weaving, alongside imported goods from the Rhineland, the British Isles, and the Islamic world. Hedeby was destroyed by fire around 1049 and never rebuilt; the town's successor was Schleswig nearby. The site is now part of the Danevirke and Hedeby UNESCO World Heritage Site.

Birka, occupied from around 750 to 975 AD, was the main Swedish trading centre of the early Viking Age, protected by a hillfort and serving as a node in networks connecting Scandinavia with the Frankish Empire and the rivers of eastern Europe. Its cemetery contains over 1100 excavated graves, including warrior burials with weapons, riding equipment, and imported luxury objects. Birka is a UNESCO World Heritage Site.

Kaupang, in the Oslofjord area of Norway, is the Norwegian equivalent, active between roughly 800 and 900 AD. Excavations have revealed a dense scatter of craft debris and imported goods confirming its role as an organised market site.

## Ring Fortresses: Trelleborg and Its Siblings

Among the most striking physical expressions of organised Norse power are the ring fortresses, a series of precisely circular military enclosures built in Denmark during the reign of Harald Bluetooth (Harald Blåtand) in the late 10th century. The best preserved is Trelleborg in western Zealand, Denmark, which consists of a near-perfect circular earthen rampart with four gateways, subdivided internally into four quadrants each containing a set of large bow-sided longhouses. The fort's geometry — its diameter, the symmetry of its layout, the standardised dimensions of its buildings — implies a centralised authority capable of organising construction at scale.

Other Danish ring fortresses of the same period include Aggersborg (the largest, in northern Jutland), Fyrkat (in central Jutland), and Nonnebakken (beneath modern Odense). The near-identical design of all four, combined with dendrochronological dating that places their construction within a few years of each other around 980 AD, strongly suggests they were part of a coordinated royal building programme. A fifth example, Borgeby in Sweden, is also associated with this tradition.

Trelleborg is open to visitors and has an adjacent museum; reconstruction longhouses help visitors understand the original layout. It is among the best Viking Age military sites accessible in Scandinavia.

## Ship Burials and Burial Mounds

The Norse practice of burying prominent individuals in or with ships produced some of the most spectacular finds in European archaeology. The Oseberg ship, excavated from a burial mound near Tønsberg in Norway in 1904, was a fully equipped Viking vessel dating to around 834 AD, buried with two women and an extraordinary array of grave goods including carved wooden sleighs, a wagon, textiles, kitchen equipment, and decorative animal-head posts. The ship itself is one of the best-preserved examples of Norse shipbuilding in existence and is displayed in the Viking Ship Museum in Oslo alongside the Gokstad and Tune ships.

The burial mounds at Borre, also in Vestfold near Tønsberg, form the largest group of royal burial mounds from the Viking Age in Scandinavia. The Borre National Park contains seven large mounds and several smaller ones, dating from roughly the 6th to 10th centuries. Though the mounds were largely robbed in antiquity, the Borre find of 1852 — a bridle and harness set of extraordinary quality — survives in Oslo and gives its name to the Borre style of decorative art.

Gamla Uppsala in Sweden is another site of major burial mounds: three enormous royal mounds dating to around the 5th and 6th centuries AD, predating the Viking Age proper, sit above a landscape of hundreds of smaller burial mounds and served as a focal site for Norse religion and politics well into the Viking period. The nearby medieval cathedral was built over a pre-Christian temple site.

## Norse Settlement in Britain and Ireland

Norse settlers arrived in Britain and Ireland from the late 8th century onward. In Ireland, they founded the major coastal towns — Dublin, Waterford, Wexford, Cork, and Limerick — that formed the basis of Ireland's urban network. Viking Dublin (Dyflin) occupied the ridge above the Liffey at what is now the Wood Quay area of the city; excavations in the 1970s and early 1980s, despite considerable controversy over development pressure, recovered thousands of artefacts from dense organic deposits and revealed the layout of 10th-century Norse houses, streets, and harbourside activity.

In England, the Norse settled the Danelaw — broadly, England north and east of a line from London to Chester — from the late 9th century. Jorvik (York) was a major Norse city from 866 AD and has produced some of the finest Viking Age urban archaeology in Europe through the Coppergate excavations of the early 1980s. The anaerobic conditions of the waterlogged deposits preserved organic materials including leather shoes and belts, wooden structures, textile fragments, food remains, and evidence for craft production. The Jorvik Viking Centre presents finds and reconstructed environments from these excavations.

## Iceland, Greenland, and North America

The Norse colonisation of the North Atlantic represents one of history's most remarkable feats of open-sea exploration. Iceland was settled by Norse colonists from roughly 870 AD, and the farmstead at Reykjavik, excavated beneath the modern city centre, preserves the oldest Viking Age longhouse in Iceland. Greenland was settled by Norse colonists from Iceland around 985 AD; the Eastern and Western Settlements maintained communities for nearly 500 years before they disappeared in the 15th century, leaving behind the ruins of farmhouses, churches, and a bishop's seat at Gardar (modern Igaliku).

At L'Anse aux Meadows on the northern tip of Newfoundland, Canada, a Norse site was discovered in 1960 by Helge and Anne Stine Ingstad and subsequently excavated, revealing eight turf-walled structures including residential longhouses and a smithy where iron was smelted. The site dates to around 1000 AD and represents the only confirmed Norse site in North America, almost certainly associated with the voyages described in the Vinland sagas. L'Anse aux Meadows is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and is open to visitors during the summer months.

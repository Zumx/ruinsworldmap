Turkmenistan occupies one of the great crossroads of the ancient world. The Karakum desert covers most of the country, but along its margins — particularly in the piedmont zone south of the desert and along the Murghab and Tedjen river deltas — civilisation has flourished for at least five millennia. The region known to the ancient Greeks as Margiana, and to the Persians as Margush, was the location of one of the world's earliest urban cultures: the Bactria-Margiana Archaeological Complex, a Bronze Age civilisation of the 3rd and 2nd millennia BC that erected large mudbrick cities with sophisticated irrigation and produced distinctive art objects that have appeared across a huge territory from the Gulf to Afghanistan. Later, the Parthian Empire had its heartland in what is now northern Turkmenistan, building a capital at Nisa whose ruins survive remarkably well. The Silk Road passed through the country, and the medieval city of Merv was for a time the largest city in the Islamic world. Every period of conquest — Achaemenid Persian, Seleucid, Parthian, Sasanian, Arab, Seljuk, Mongol, Timurid — left strata of monumental architecture on this soil.

## 1. Merv (Ancient Margiana), Mary Province

Merv is among the oldest and most continuously occupied urban sites in Central Asia, with habitation spanning roughly five millennia from the Bronze Age to the Mongol destruction of 1221 AD. The remains lie in a complex of overlapping city plans — Erk Kala (Bronze Age and Achaemenid), Gyaur Kala (Hellenistic through Sasanian), Sultan Kala (Seljuk medieval), and several smaller enclosures — spread across a desert plain near the modern city of Mary. At its Seljuk peak in the 11th and 12th centuries AD, Merv may have been one of the largest cities in the world, and Arabic geographers described it in superlatives. What survives today includes the enormous mudbrick walls of Gyaur Kala enclosing hundreds of hectares, the tomb of Sultan Sanjar (a 12th-century domed mausoleum still standing to its full height and recently restored), Buddhist stupas, Zoroastrian fire temples, and extensive ice houses. Merv is a UNESCO World Heritage Site. Access is from the city of Mary.

## 2. Nisa (Parthian Nisa), Near Ashgabat

Nisa lies on the outskirts of Ashgabat and preserves the remains of two related sites: Old Nisa, a fortified treasury and ceremonial complex of the Parthian Arsacid dynasty from around the 3rd century BC to the 3rd century AD, and New Nisa, a larger residential town. Old Nisa is the more important archaeologically: Italian-Turkmenistani excavations have revealed a square walled enclosure with round towers containing a series of monumental halls, storage chambers, and a wine-making facility. The most celebrated finds are a set of ivory rhytons — drinking horns with elaborate sculptural finials combining Hellenistic and Persian iconography — that represent one of the masterpieces of Parthian art. Nisa is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and is easily visited from Ashgabat.

## 3. Konye-Urgench (Old Urgench), Northern Turkmenistan

Konye-Urgench is the former capital of the Khwarazmian Empire, one of the major powers of the Islamic medieval world, which was catastrophically destroyed by Genghis Khan in 1221 AD. The site preserves a remarkable collection of medieval Islamic monuments: the Kutlug-Timur Minaret (12th–14th century) stands at around 60 metres and is the tallest medieval minaret in Central Asia; mausoleums of Khwarazmian sultans and Sufi saints exhibit some of the most sophisticated tilework and muqarnas vaulting of the pre-Mongol Islamic world. The site continued to function as a pilgrimage destination after the Mongol destruction, and later Timurid-era additions complement the pre-Mongol structures. Konye-Urgench is a UNESCO World Heritage Site located in the far north of Turkmenistan near the Uzbek border.

## 4. Dehistan (Misrian), Western Turkmenistan

Dehistan, known also by the name of its most prominent ruins as Misrian, is a large medieval Islamic city in the western piedmont zone near the Caspian, active from roughly the 8th through the 14th centuries. Its most remarkable survivals are two tall portal towers in fired brick — the remains of congregational mosque minarets or gate towers — that rise dramatically from the flat plain. The town itself, enclosed by mudbrick walls, contains the remains of a central Friday mosque, bathhouses, and dense residential occupation. The ceramic assemblages indicate participation in Caspian Sea and Silk Road trade. Dehistan is off the standard tourist route and requires a long drive from Turkmenbashi or Balkanabat on the Caspian coast.

## 5. Anau (Anow), Near Ashgabat

Anau is a twin-mound site on the edge of Ashgabat's suburbs whose lower layers represent some of the earliest farming settlements in Central Asia, with occupation beginning in the Neolithic period and continuing through the Bronze Age. American excavations by the Pumpelly expedition in 1904 brought Anau to international attention as one of the first Neolithic sites identified in Central Asia. The Bronze Age levels yield pottery, figurines, and evidence for agricultural and pastoral economies typical of the BMAC cultural horizon. A medieval mosque whose portal arch of glazed tilework survived into the 20th century was demolished by a 1948 earthquake. The mounds are visible in the south of Ashgabat near the Iranian border; a small museum provides context.

## 6. Gonur Depe, Mary Province

Gonur Depe is the largest known site of the Bronze Age Bactria-Margiana Archaeological Complex, a city of roughly 25 hectares occupied between approximately 2300 and 1800 BC. Russian-Turkmenistani excavations led by Viktor Sarianidi from the 1970s onward revealed a fortified palace complex with concentric walls, fire temples, a royal necropolis with rich grave goods, and evidence for ritual consumption of a fermented beverage brewed from ephedra, cannabis, and poppy. The material culture — distinctive chlorite vessels, compartmented metal seals, bronze weapons, and lapis lazuli and carnelian jewellery — ties Gonur to a wide network of contemporary Bronze Age societies from the Gulf to the steppes. The site is in the Murghab delta, reachable from Mary with a 4x4.

## 7. Ulug Depe, Ahal Province

Ulug Depe is a large prehistoric tell near Dushak in the Tedjen oasis whose occupation spans from the Chalcolithic through the Iron Age, with particularly significant levels dating to the BMAC Bronze Age. French-Turkmenistani excavations have documented a substantial mudbrick urban site with monumental architecture, extensive craft production, and imported goods indicating long-distance contact. The site bridges the agricultural settlements of the Iranian plateau and the Margiana urban centres and is important for understanding the spread of early Bronze Age urbanism across Central Asia.

## 8. Serakhs (Medieval), Near the Iranian Border

The medieval city of Serakhs on the Tejen River was an important waystation on the Silk Road, contested repeatedly between competing powers and destroyed and rebuilt several times between the Arab conquest and the Mongol period. The surviving monuments include the Mausoleum of Yahya ibn Zeyd, an early Islamic funerary structure of some antiquity, and the remains of a caravanserai and fortifications. The modern border town of Serakhs is bisected by the Turkmenistan-Iran border, and the archaeological zone requires coordination with local authorities.

## 9. Abiverd, Near Kaka, Ahal Province

Abiverd is a medieval Islamic city in the Tedjen piedmont zone, occupied from the early Islamic period through the 13th-century Mongol destruction. French-Turkmenistani survey has mapped the city walls, a central citadel, mosques, bathhouses, and a large cemetery. The site is notable for its position in the chain of oasis cities linking Merv to Nishapur (in present-day Iran) along the Khorasan Road — the eastern branch of the Silk Road. Pottery and coin evidence shows its integration into the commercial and administrative network of the Ghaznavid and Seljuk periods.

## 10. Sanjar's Ice Houses (Yakhdan), Near Merv

The ice houses at Merv — locally called yakhdan — are remarkable structures that made it possible to preserve ice through the summer in one of the hottest deserts in Central Asia. Large conical or vaulted mudbrick buildings shaded a sunken pit into which ice cut in winter from nearby channels was packed and insulated; the ice was then sold through the summer months to the urban population. Several of these structures survive near the Sultan Kala enclosure of Merv and are among the most unusual and best-preserved examples of pre-modern refrigeration technology anywhere. They represent the sophisticated urban logistics that allowed medieval Merv to function as a major city in an extreme climate.

## Planning Your Visit

Ashgabat is the entry point for most visitors. Nisa is a short taxi ride from the city. Merv and Gonur Depe require a flight or long drive to Mary; the drive across the Karakum takes roughly five hours. Konye-Urgench requires a separate excursion to the far north, accessible from Dashoguz. Turkmenistan requires a visa that typically must be obtained in advance through an invitation letter, and independent travel is restricted — most visitors travel with a local guide as required by the authorities.

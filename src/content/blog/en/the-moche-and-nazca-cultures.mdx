## Coastal Peru Before the Inca

Long before the Inca Empire organised the Andean world into a single administrative state, the coastal valleys of Peru were home to a succession of complex societies that built large monuments, sustained dense populations through sophisticated irrigation agriculture, and produced some of the finest ceramic and textile art in the pre-Columbian Americas. Two of these societies — the Moche, who flourished in the northern coastal valleys from roughly AD 100 to 800, and the Nazca of the southern coastal plateau, active from roughly 100 BC to AD 800 — left remains that continue to challenge and fascinate archaeologists.

The Peruvian coast is one of the most extreme environments in South America: a hyperarid desert, almost entirely rainless, running between the cold Pacific and the abrupt rise of the Andes. River valleys cutting across this desert were the only zones of agricultural possibility, fed by snowmelt from the mountains. Control of water — through irrigation canals, reservoirs and the management of river flow — was the foundation of power in coastal Peru, and the elaborate canal systems that fed the Moche valleys are as impressive an engineering achievement as the pyramids they used their agricultural surplus to build.

## The Moche: Pyramid Builders of the North

The Moche (also spelled Mochica) were not a single unified state but a shared cultural tradition expressed across multiple independent valleys. At their most concentrated in the Moche Valley itself, near modern Trujillo on Peru's northern coast, they built two extraordinary adobe brick pyramids: Huaca del Sol and Huaca de la Luna, the largest pre-Columbian structures in South America during their period of construction.

Huaca del Sol (Pyramid of the Sun) once stood roughly 40 metres high and was built from an estimated 140 million adobe bricks — each individually marked by the production unit that made it, a remarkable system of labour accounting. Spanish colonial looters diverted the adjacent Moche River to wash away the pyramid's base and extract the burials they suspected within; as a result, perhaps two-thirds of the original structure is gone, and what remains is a dramatically eroded but still massive mound. Excavations are ongoing.

Huaca de la Luna (Pyramid of the Moon), separated from Huaca del Sol by a plaza that was once a densely occupied urban centre, has fared better. Excavations since the 1990s by archaeologists from the Universidad Nacional de Trujillo have revealed its extraordinary painted polychrome facades: the great mask of Ai-apaec, the Moche's primary deity — a fanged, spider-legged being — executed in vivid reds, yellows and whites and covering large sections of the pyramid's exterior walls. The pyramid was rebuilt multiple times, each new version encasing the previous one; earlier facades are preserved inside the later construction. A modern visitor shelter now protects the excavated area.

## Moche Ceramics and the Iconographic Record

In the absence of a writing system, Moche narrative and religious imagery survives almost entirely in its ceramic tradition — the richest and most technically sophisticated in ancient South America. Moche potters produced stirrup-spout vessels in extraordinary variety: portrait heads so individualised that scholars believe they depict specific named individuals (though this remains debated), narrative scenes painted in fine-line detail showing supernatural battles and ritual sacrifice, and an extensive corpus of erotic ceramics that occupied a significant portion of Moche iconographic output and whose ritual significance is still not fully understood.

The fine-line painting on Moche ceramics encodes a complex cosmology. The 'Presentation Theme' or 'Sacrifice Ceremony', identified and named by scholars in the late 20th century, depicts a sequence of supernatural beings receiving cups of blood from sacrificed captives — a scene that was thought to be purely mythological until excavations at Huaca de la Luna in the 1990s found platforms with evidence of ritual combat, prisoner sacrifice and the deposit of human blood, matching the ceramic imagery with striking precision. Art and reality were not separate categories in Moche religious life.

## Royal Burials at Sipán

In 1987, local looters near the town of Sipán in the Lambayeque Valley began extracting extraordinary gold objects from a large adobe mound. When the Peruvian archaeologist Walter Alva was alerted by police who had seized some of the looted material, he began a legal excavation that would become one of the most significant discoveries in the archaeology of the Americas.

The Huaca Rajada at Sipán contained multiple intact royal burials of the Moche period. The principal burial, the 'Lord of Sipán', was interred around AD 300 in a wooden coffin surrounded by extraordinary gold and silver regalia: a pectoral of gold and turquoise, ear ornaments of gold and inlaid figures, a golden backflap, a sceptre topped with a chamber containing a tiny three-dimensional scene, banners of gilded copper, and the remains of attendants — men, women and a dog — buried simultaneously. The iconography of the burial goods matches Moche ceramic depictions of the 'Presentation Theme' closely enough that the Lord of Sipán appears to have embodied one of the supernatural officiants in life. A dedicated Royal Tombs of Sipán Museum opened in Lambayeque in 2002, holding the finds in outstanding purpose-built facilities.

Subsequent excavations at Sipán revealed additional burials, including an even older interment below the Lord of Sipán (dubbed the 'Old Lord of Sipán') and a Priest burial with distinct regalia. Together they provide the most complete picture available of Moche elite mortuary practice.

## The Nazca: Geometry on the Desert

Several hundred kilometres south of the Moche heartland, in the Nazca and Pampas rivers' drainage on the southern coast of Peru, a different society was producing its most famous monument: not pyramids but drawings. The Nazca Lines are a series of geoglyphs — figures and geometric forms created by removing the reddish-brown surface stones of the desert pavement to expose the lighter yellowish ground beneath — distributed across roughly 450 square kilometres of arid plateau.

The lines include straight lines running for kilometres without deviation, trapezoids and spirals, and about 70 figurative geoglyphs depicting animals, plants and human-like figures: a hummingbird (roughly 96 metres long), a condor (roughly 135 metres), a spider, a monkey with a spiral tail, a dog, an orca, a pair of hands, and what is sometimes described as an astronaut but is far more plausibly a human figure. The straight lines in particular are of extraordinary precision — maintained across rough terrain, ravines and hills over distances far exceeding the line of sight of any single observer on the ground.

The Nazca people were not hiding the lines or making them only for aerial viewers: many features are visible and even walkable at ground level, and the lines were presumably experienced from the edges of hills, in procession, and in ceremony rather than from above. Maria Reiche, a German mathematician who dedicated much of her life from the 1940s onward to measuring and documenting the lines, proposed astronomical alignment theories that have not been systematically confirmed. More recent scholarship has emphasised the connection between the trapezoid features and water: the lines may have guided ritual processions connected to water sources, including subterranean aqueducts called puquios, several of which are still in use.

UNESCO inscribed the Lines and Geoglyphs of Nasca and Pampas de Jumana in 1994. The Mirador, a metal observation tower near the Pan-American Highway, provides views of a few of the animal figures from an elevated position; the most complete view remains aerial, and small aircraft and microlight flights operate from the Nazca airfield.

## Nazca Ceramics and the Cahuachi Ceremonial Centre

Nazca pottery, like Moche pottery, is technically distinguished and iconographically rich, but its aesthetic is very different: polychrome surfaces in up to thirteen distinct colours, geometric and trophy-head motifs, stylised natural forms — hummingbirds, killer whales, humans — rendered in flat areas of intense colour rather than narrative fine-line painting. Trophy heads (decapitated human heads modified for suspension) appear frequently in Nazca art and were found archaeologically at the ceremonial site of Cahuachi.

Cahuachi, on the south bank of the Nazca River roughly 20 kilometres from the modern city of Nazca, was the principal Nazca ceremonial centre: a large complex of adobe mounds, plazas and platform structures covering roughly 24 square kilometres. Excavations by Giuseppe Orefici since the 1980s have established that Cahuachi was primarily a pilgrimage centre rather than a permanent urban settlement — the population was mostly temporary, gathering for ceremonies and then dispersing. The mounds incorporated natural hills modified by adobe construction and were repeatedly rebuilt and enlarged over several centuries.

The Nazca culture is often described alongside the Paracas culture that preceded it on the south coast; many traits — including the treatment of trophy heads and certain ceramic styles — show continuity between them, and the boundary between the two traditions in the archaeological sequence is gradual rather than abrupt.

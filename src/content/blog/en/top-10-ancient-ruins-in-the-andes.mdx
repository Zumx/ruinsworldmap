The Andes mountain chain — the longest continental mountain range on earth — was one of the few places in the world where complex civilisations arose independently, without outside stimulus. From the Pacific coast of what is now Peru, up through the cloud forest and over the high puna grasslands above 4,000 metres, and down again into the Bolivian altiplano and the eastern slopes toward the Amazon basin, successive cultures built cities, temples, roads, and irrigation systems of remarkable ingenuity. The Chavín, Nazca, Moche, Tiwanaku, Wari, Chimú, and finally the Inca each contributed to a cumulative heritage spanning roughly three thousand years of pre-Columbian urbanism. The Inca Empire, the last and largest of these civilisations, achieved a degree of political and logistical organisation that astonished the Spanish conquerors who arrived in 1532 and found a state stretching from Colombia to central Chile, connected by thousands of kilometres of engineered roads.

## 1. Machu Picchu, Peru

Perched on a ridge above the Urubamba canyon at roughly 2,430 metres, Machu Picchu was built by the Inca emperor Pachacuti in the mid-15th century AD as a royal estate and ceremonial centre. Abandoned within a century — probably within a generation of the Spanish conquest — it was never demolished or repurposed, allowing it to survive in remarkable completeness. The approximately 200 structures of the site, assembled using the Inca technique of interlocking polygonal stonework without mortar, include the Temple of the Sun (an elliptical tower oriented to the June solstice sunrise), the Intihuatana solar stone, the Room of the Three Windows, and extensive agricultural terraces. The surrounding landscape of cloud forest and dramatic canyon adds to its visual power. A UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1983; timed entry tickets are required and sell out weeks ahead in peak months.

## 2. Tiwanaku, Bolivia

On the southern shore of Lake Titicaca in Bolivia at an altitude of roughly 3,840 metres, Tiwanaku was the capital of one of the most influential pre-Inca civilisations in South America. From roughly 400 to 1000 AD the city dominated the altiplano and extended its cultural influence across the Andes and coastal Peru through trade and colonisation rather than military conquest. The site's surviving monuments include the Akapana pyramid — a stepped earthen platform faced with stone blocks — the Kalasasaya temple enclosure with its famous Gateway of the Sun, a massive carved stone monolith, and the Pumapunku platform, whose precisely cut interlocking stone blocks display a level of stoneworking virtuosity that continues to attract archaeological attention. Tiwanaku is a UNESCO World Heritage Site, about 70 kilometres west of La Paz by paved road.

## 3. Chan Chan, Peru

On the northern coast of Peru near Trujillo, Chan Chan was the capital of the Chimú Empire from roughly the 9th through 15th centuries AD — the largest pre-Columbian city in South America, covering around 20 square kilometres. The city was organised around ten royal ciudadelas: massive rectangular adobe compounds each built for a different ruler and containing his burial mound, plazas, storehouses, and reservoir. The ciudadela walls, up to nine metres tall, carry intricate geometric and zoomorphic friezes — fish, birds, and sea otters pressed into the wet adobe in repeated patterns. The Inca conquered Chan Chan around 1470 and deported Chimú artisans to Cusco, where their skills influenced late Inca metalwork and textile production. UNESCO listed Chan Chan in 1986 and placed it on the Danger List due to flood and erosion damage.

## 4. Sacsayhuamán, Peru

On the hillside directly above the city of Cusco, Sacsayhuamán is the most dramatic surviving example of Inca military and ceremonial architecture. Built under Pachacuti and his successors in the late 15th century, its three zigzag terrace walls of limestone and andesite blocks — some weighing over 100 tonnes — run for roughly 300 metres. The largest stones were transported from quarries up to 20 kilometres away over mountain terrain. The Spanish took the site in 1536 after a siege led by Manco Inca and subsequently dismantled much of the inner structure for building material, leaving the walls as the primary survival. Sacsayhuamán is included in the UNESCO listing for the City of Cusco and is a 20-minute walk from the main plaza. The Inti Raymi winter solstice festival is celebrated here each June.

## 5. Ollantaytambo, Peru

At the northern gateway of the Sacred Valley, Ollantaytambo was a royal Inca estate and administrative centre built primarily under Pachacuti in the late 15th century. The hilltop Temple of the Sun is distinguished by six enormous monolithic red porphyry blocks fitted together with extraordinary precision, the largest measuring around four metres tall. The terraced agricultural platforms below the temple drop in dramatic cascades to the valley floor. The town at the base retains its original Inca urban plan — a grid of rectangular blocks called canchas, each enclosed by a single outer wall — making it one of the few places in the Andes where Inca urbanism can still be walked. Ollantaytambo also marks the point where Manco Inca defeated a Spanish cavalry charge in 1536. It lies on the train route to Machu Picchu.

## 6. Kuélap, Peru

In the cloudforest of Peru's Amazonas region above the Utcubamba valley, Kuélap was built by the Chachapoya people between roughly the 6th and 15th centuries AD. The site consists of an enormous stone platform — roughly 600 metres long and up to 20 metres of wall height — enclosing around 400 circular stone structures. The Chachapoya, called 'cloud warriors' by the Inca who eventually conquered them, decorated building exteriors with geometric friezes and carved faces. The construction scale rivals anything in the Inca heartland, yet Kuélap was built by a people without the centralised imperial organisation the Inca later achieved. The isolation of the northern Peruvian cloudforest delayed archaeological attention for decades. Access improved with a cable car from Nuevo Tingo in 2017, though it has periodically been suspended; an alternate hiking trail remains.

## 7. Chavín de Huántar, Peru

In a high valley at roughly 3,200 metres in the Ancash region, Chavín de Huántar was the central ceremonial site of the Chavín culture — the first major horizon culture of the Andes, flourishing from roughly 900 to 200 BC. The Old Temple, built around 900 BC and extended in subsequent centuries, is a complex of sunken plazas, platforms, and a network of interior galleries. The galleries house the Lanzón — a 4.5-metre carved stone lance with a fanged deity image — which remains in situ in its original dark shaft. The exterior walls were once studded with carved stone tenon heads representing deity faces. Chavín de Huántar is a UNESCO World Heritage Site. The access road from Huaraz crosses the Kahuish tunnel at over 4,500 metres; alternative trekking routes exist for the adventurous.

## 8. Pumapunku, Bolivia

Adjacent to the main Tiwanaku complex, Pumapunku is a large stone platform whose scattered carved blocks have generated both intense archaeological interest and persistent popular controversy. The platform, built around 500 to 600 AD, consisted of terraced stonework in andesite and red sandstone; a catastrophic collapse — possibly from earthquake or deliberate demolition — scattered the components. What makes Pumapunku remarkable is the precision of the carved stone: H-shaped interlocking blocks, perfectly flat surfaces, and stone-to-stone fitting achieved without mortar or metal tools. The function of the platform — whether a temple, a gateway precinct, or a royal reception space — is debated. The site is administered jointly with Tiwanaku; visitors to one generally see both.

## 9. Pisac, Peru

Above the Sacred Valley town of Pisac, the Inca site occupies a hilltop commanding broad views up and down the valley. The upper precinct contains a group of temples and an intihuatana (solar observatory stone) of considerable quality, while the surrounding slopes carry some of the most extensive terraced agricultural systems in the Inca world — hundreds of individual terraces, each with its own irrigation channel, descending in long sweeps to the valley floor. Around the site lie numerous cliff tombs of the Inca period, most long since looted. Pisac occupies the junction of the Kitamayo and Chongo river valleys, a location of clear strategic and agricultural significance. It is reachable by road from Cusco in about 45 minutes and is usually combined with Ollantaytambo in a day trip.

## 10. Caral, Peru

In the Supe Valley about 200 kilometres north of Lima at the edge of the coastal desert, Caral is one of the oldest known cities in the Americas, with radiocarbon dates for the main construction phases clustering around 2600 to 2000 BC — contemporary with the Middle Kingdom of Egypt and the Indus Valley civilisation at Mohenjo-daro. The site covers roughly 65 hectares and includes six large platform mounds, two sunken circular plazas, residential structures, and storage facilities. Caral belonged to the Norte Chico culture, which appears to have developed urban complexity independently on the Peruvian coast before any other society in the Americas. No evidence of warfare or weapons has been found, suggesting the city's power rested on trade and ritual. A UNESCO World Heritage Site since 2009; a dedicated tour from Lima takes approximately five to six hours.

## Planning Your Visit

The Andean sites span two countries and enormous altitudinal variation, and altitude acclimatisation is a genuine logistical consideration. Cusco (3,400 metres) is the gateway for Peru's Inca heartland; allow two to three days before undertaking strenuous walks. Tiwanaku and Pumapunku near La Paz (3,600 metres) are manageable as day trips once acclimatised to the Bolivian capital. Caral is best visited directly from Lima as a day excursion. The best season for highland Peru and Bolivia is May through October (the dry season); the coast is accessible year-round. Most Peruvian Inca sites require a boleto turístico from Cusco, which covers multiple monuments. Machu Picchu requires a separate timed-entry permit purchased online in advance.

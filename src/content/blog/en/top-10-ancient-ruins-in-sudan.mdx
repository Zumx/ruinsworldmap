Sudan holds one of the most undervisited concentrations of ancient heritage in the world. The middle Nile valley — ancient Nubia — was home to sophisticated African civilisations that rivalled Egypt in ambition and at times eclipsed it in power. The Kingdom of Kush, centred first at Kerma from around 2500 BC, then at Napata near Jebel Barkal from the 9th century BC, and finally at Meroë from around the 4th century BC onward, built temples, pyramids, and cities that drew on Egyptian models while developing distinctly Sudanese artistic and architectural traditions. Kushite kings of the 25th Dynasty actually conquered and ruled Egypt for several decades in the 8th and 7th centuries BC. The Meroitic period, lasting from roughly 300 BC to 350 AD, produced a script that has only been partially deciphered, a testament to how much of this civilisation remains imperfectly understood. Sudan's ancient sites are numerous, largely undisturbed by mass tourism, and set in landscapes of compelling beauty.

## 1. Meroë, River Nile State

On the east bank of the Nile about 200 kilometres north of Khartoum, the ruins of Meroë were the capital of the Kushite Kingdom from roughly the 4th century BC to the 4th century AD. The most striking visual feature is the Royal Cemetery on a ridge east of the city: over 200 steep-sided pyramids, smaller than Egyptian pyramids but with distinctive proportions and elaborate mortuary chapels at their bases, built for rulers and their consorts over several centuries. The pyramid field divides into three groups — south, north, and west cemeteries. The city itself, below the ridge, is less excavated, but the Royal Baths and the Temple of Amun have been partially cleared. Meroë is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and increasingly visited by international travellers despite Sudan's political instability.

## 2. Jebel Barkal, Northern State

A flat-topped sandstone butte rising abruptly from the Nile plain at Karima, Jebel Barkal was considered by both Egyptians and Kushites to be the dwelling of the god Amun. The New Kingdom pharaoh Thutmose III established a temple at its base around 1450 BC; later rulers expanded the complex dramatically. The Kushite kings of Napata, who used proximity to this sacred mountain as a source of legitimacy, built a series of temples and a palace here between roughly the 9th and 4th centuries BC. The Temple of Amun (B500) is the largest and most impressive, retaining columns and reliefs of considerable quality. Several pyramids of Napatan rulers crown a ridge nearby. The site is part of the Island of Meroe UNESCO World Heritage Site. Karima is reached from Khartoum by road (about 360 kilometres) or by Nile steamer.

## 3. Naqa, River Nile State

About 50 kilometres east of the Nile in the Butana steppe east of Meroe, Naqa was a significant Meroitic religious and urban centre active from roughly the 1st century BC to the 4th century AD. The site preserves two outstanding monuments: the Temple of Amun, with a well-preserved pylon decorated with carved reliefs of the ruler smiting enemies, and the Lion Temple of Apedemak — an entirely African lion-headed god — decorated with exceptionally fine carved reliefs depicting King Natakamani and Queen Amanitore in ritual scenes. Adjacent to the Lion Temple stands the Kiosk of Naqa, a Roman-period open-air structure blending Roman and Egyptian architectural elements in a way found nowhere else. Naqa is remote, accessible only by four-wheel drive from the Khartoum-Atbara road, but the combination of monuments here is extraordinary.

## 4. Musawwarat es-Sufra, River Nile State

A few kilometres from Naqa across the Butana plain, Musawwarat es-Sufra is a complex of Meroitic temples and enclosure walls dating primarily from the 3rd century BC through the 1st century AD. The site is dominated by the Great Enclosure — an enormous mudbrick and stone complex of interconnected courts, ramps, and rooms whose precise function remains debated: it may have served as a pilgrimage centre, an elephant-training facility, or a royal retreat. The Temple of Apedemak within the complex is decorated with carved reliefs of elephants, lions, and royal figures, and the walls of the Great Enclosure bear one of the largest collections of ancient graffiti in Africa: hundreds of sketches and inscriptions left by pilgrims and travellers. The Humboldt University Berlin has conducted extensive research here for decades.

## 5. Old Dongola, Northern State

On the west bank of the Nile in the Dongola Reach, Old Dongola (Dunqula al-'Ajuz) was the capital of the Christian Nubian kingdom of Makuria from roughly the 6th through 14th centuries AD. Makuria was one of three Christian Nubian kingdoms that survived the Arab conquest of Egypt in the 7th century and maintained Christianity for several centuries longer than any other African kingdom outside Ethiopia. The ruins include several churches, the remains of the royal palace, and a building known as the Throne Hall, whose brick vault is among the most intact medieval structures in sub-Saharan Africa. A significant 8th-century AD church with painted walls has been excavated. The Polish Archaeological Mission has worked here continuously since the 1960s and produced some of the most important results in Nubian archaeology.

## 6. Kerma, Northern State

Near modern Kerma in the Nile valley north of Dongola, the ancient city of Kerma was the capital of the earliest sub-Saharan African kingdom, the Kingdom of Kerma, from roughly 2500 to 1500 BC. At its height, Kerma controlled the Nile trade between central Africa and Egypt, and its rulers were wealthy enough to challenge Egyptian power. The most visible remains are the Deffufa — two massive mudbrick structures, the Western Deffufa (religious building, over 18 metres tall) and the Eastern Deffufa (funerary chapel) — that have survived partially intact for 4,500 years. Around the Eastern Deffufa lies the royal cemetery with tumulus burials containing sacrificed retainers, horses, and cattle, indicating extraordinary social stratification. The Kerma Museum on site is well presented.

## 7. Nuri Pyramids, Northern State

Across the Nile from Jebel Barkal, the royal cemetery of Nuri contains the pyramids of the Napatan Dynasty rulers who ruled Egypt as the 25th Dynasty in the 8th and 7th centuries BC, as well as their successors into the 4th century BC. The pyramid of Taharqa, the most famous of the 25th Dynasty pharaohs, is the largest at Nuri, with a base roughly 51 metres square. Taharqa ruled a territory stretching from the Nile Delta to what is now central Sudan and was a formidable military and cultural force. Nuri's 20 royal pyramids and over 50 queen's pyramids are less visited than Meroë's cemetery but arguably more historically significant in terms of the dynasties interred here.

## 8. Meroe City and the Royal Baths, River Nile State

The urban area of Meroë, distinct from the pyramid cemeteries on the ridge, contains the excavated remains of the city's core. The Royal Baths are the most visible structure: a substantial pool and building complex of the 1st century AD showing clear Roman stylistic influence — an importing of Roman bathing culture into a Kushite royal context, possibly introduced via trade contacts with Roman Egypt. The Temple of Amun in the city is one of the largest temples in Sudan, with pylon and hypostyle hall. The temple was built and rebuilt over several centuries and preserves inscriptions in the still-partially-undeciphered Meroitic script. Ongoing excavations by the Sudan Archaeological Research Society and other teams continue to expand understanding of the city's plan.

## 9. Sedeinga, Northern State

In the far north of Sudan near the Egyptian border, Sedeinga contains a field of over 35 small pyramids belonging to the late Meroitic period (roughly 3rd and 4th centuries AD), making it one of the most intact provincial pyramid cemeteries in Sudan. The site also preserves the remains of a New Kingdom Egyptian temple of Amenhotep III, built to honour his queen Tiye — one of very few temples dedicated to a living queen in ancient Egypt. The French Archaeological Mission has excavated here extensively and published important findings on late Meroitic burial practice and social organisation. Sedeinga is remote, requiring four-wheel drive from Abri or Soleb; the combination of New Kingdom Egyptian and late Meroitic remains within a small area is unique.

## 10. Meroe Island — The Entire UNESCO World Heritage Site

The UNESCO designation known as the Island of Meroe encompasses a broader zone of the Butana steppe including Meroë, Naqa, and Musawwarat es-Sufra as an integrated landscape. Understanding the three sites together — the capital city and royal cemeteries, the religious complex at Naqa, and the pilgrimage centre at Musawwarat es-Sufra — reveals the spatial logic of the Meroitic state, which organised its sacred geography across the steppe between the Nile and the Atbara river. The connections between sites along ancient roads, marked by way-stations and wells, suggest a landscape of movement and pilgrimage that individual site visits cannot fully convey. A visit linking all three in a single day by four-wheel drive is the most rewarding way to understand this ancient territorial state.

## Planning Your Visit

Visiting Sudan's ancient sites requires realistic planning. Political conditions have been volatile since the 2019 revolution and subsequent conflict; check current Foreign Office and State Department travel advice before booking. When accessible, the standard approach to the northern sites is to fly to Khartoum and then either hire a private vehicle with driver (the most flexible option) or arrange a specialist guided tour from Khartoum. The drive from Khartoum to Meroe is about two and a half hours on a paved road; Naqa and Musawwarat require a four-wheel drive from there. The best season is November through February, when temperatures are manageable. Despite the logistical challenges, those who reach these sites encounter ancient monuments of world significance in an atmosphere of quiet that more accessible destinations cannot offer.

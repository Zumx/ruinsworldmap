The Korean peninsula has been continuously inhabited since the Palaeolithic, but it is the Three Kingdoms period — when Goguryeo, Baekje, and Silla competed for dominance between roughly the 1st century BC and 668 AD — that produced the most visible and architecturally ambitious archaeological record. Silla, whose capital at Gyeongju dominated the southeastern peninsula, left behind one of the most extraordinary concentrations of royal burial tumuli in East Asia, alongside temples, pagodas, and carved Buddhist sanctuaries. The Baekje kingdom, whose successive capitals lay in what is now Chungcheong province, produced different but equally refined architectural traditions. Later, the Joseon dynasty (1392–1897) constructed massive walled fortresses, several of which survive in excellent condition. South Korea has invested substantially in archaeological heritage management, and many of its most significant sites are UNESCO-inscribed and well served by on-site museums and visitor infrastructure.

## 1. Gyeongju Historic Areas

Gyeongju served as the capital of the Silla kingdom from around 57 BC to 935 AD, and the city and its surroundings constitute one of the most concentrated archaeological zones in Asia. The Daereungwon Tumuli Complex in the city centre contains 23 enormous royal burial mounds, including Cheonmachong and Hwangnamdaechong, the latter covering roughly 120 by 80 metres and containing gold crowns, glass vessels of Central Asian origin, and thousands of gold ornaments now in the Gyeongju National Museum. The Anapji Pond (Donggung Palace) is a reconstructed garden complex on the site of a 7th-century Silla royal detached palace. Together, the Gyeongju Historic Areas form a UNESCO World Heritage Site inscribed in 2000. The city is reachable by KTX express train in roughly two hours from Seoul.

## 2. Bulguksa Temple, Gyeongju

Bulguksa, founded in the mid-6th century and substantially rebuilt in 751 AD under the Silla king Gyeongdeok, is one of the masterworks of East Asian Buddhist architecture. The temple complex climbs a series of stone terraces above the Toham mountain foothills, and its two stone bridges — Cheongungyo and Baegungyo — lead to the main sanctuary gate across an artificial terrace whose retaining walls are original 8th-century construction. The Dabotap and Seokgatap pagodas, flanking the main hall, are among the most admired stone structures in Korean history; Seokgatap was found to contain a scroll of the Pure Light Dharani Sutra, one of the world's oldest surviving printed texts. Bulguksa is inscribed as part of the UNESCO 'Gyeongju Historic Areas' property and is visited year-round.

## 3. Seokguram Grotto, Gyeongju

Seokguram, excavated into the granite of Toham Mountain above Bulguksa, is an artificial granite grotto housing a Buddha figure of exceptional sculptural refinement, completed around 774 AD. The central Sakyamuni sits on a lotus pedestal within a domed rotunda, surrounded by carved relief figures of bodhisattvas and guardians. The precision of the construction — the dome is assembled from individually carved stone blocks without mortar — and the quality of the carving place Seokguram among the finest Buddhist sanctuaries in the world. It shares the UNESCO World Heritage inscription with Bulguksa. A glass enclosure protects the grotto from humidity changes; visitors view the interior through the glass. The site is a 15-minute walk uphill from Bulguksa or reachable by taxi from Gyeongju.

## 4. Hwaseong Fortress, Suwon

Hwaseong, the massive walled fortress enclosing Suwon city, was built between 1794 and 1796 under the orders of King Jeongjo of the Joseon dynasty to honour his father and to serve as the administrative centre of a new planned city. The circuit wall stretches roughly 5.7 kilometres around Paldalsan hill and incorporates four main gates, four secret gates, multiple watchtowers, bastions, and a command post. The fortress combined Korean military design principles with ideas absorbed from Chinese and Western military architecture via Jesuit missionary texts. The design was the work of scholar Jeong Yak-yong. Hwaseong was damaged during the 20th century but carefully restored; UNESCO inscribed it as a World Heritage Site in 1997. It is easily reached from Seoul by urban rail.

## 5. Baekje Historic Areas — Gongju and Buyeo

The Baekje kingdom established successive capitals at Ungjin (modern Gongju) from 475 AD and Sabi (modern Buyeo) from 538 AD. Both cities preserve outstanding Baekje remains. In Gongju, the Songsan-ri Tomb Complex contains royal burial mounds including that of King Muryeong (died 523 AD), which was found intact in 1971 and yielded gold crowns, bronze mirrors, and Chinese ceramics documenting Baekje's connections across the East China Sea. In Buyeo, Nakhwaam Rock and the Gungnamji Garden are associated with the final years of the kingdom. The Buyeo National Museum holds the Baekje Incense Burner, a gilt-bronze masterwork of 6th-century craftsmanship. Both sites are part of the UNESCO 'Baekje Historic Areas' World Heritage property inscribed in 2015.

## 6. Gongsanseong Fortress, Gongju

Gongsanseong is a mountain fortress above the Geum river at Gongju that served as the royal citadel of the Baekje kingdom during its Ungjin period, from 475 to 538 AD. The original earthwork ramparts were rebuilt in stone during the Joseon period; the circuit is roughly 2.6 kilometres and winds along the ridge and down to the river bank. Within the enclosure, excavations have uncovered palace building foundations, storage pits, and evidence of military occupation across multiple periods from the Baekje era through to the Joseon. Two Baekje ponds have been excavated and partially restored, along with a governor's building and pavilions. Gongsanseong is part of the UNESCO Baekje Historic Areas inscription. It overlooks the Geum river with views across to the Songsan-ri tombs.

## 7. Jongmyo Shrine, Seoul (Joseon Royal Ancestral Shrine)

Jongmyo, the Confucian royal ancestral shrine of the Joseon dynasty, is the oldest and most authentic of the Confucian royal shrines preserved in East Asia. First built in 1395 immediately after the founding of the dynasty, the main shrine hall Jeongjeon has been expanded over centuries and now stretches roughly 110 metres in a single unbroken colonnaded facade housing the spirit tablets of nineteen Joseon kings and their queens. The auxiliary hall Yeongnyeongjeon houses additional tablets. The architecture is deliberately restrained, its stark horizontal lines contrasting with the elaborate decoration of Buddhist temples. The Jongmyo Jerye, the royal ancestral rite performed here, is still enacted annually with music, dance, and ceremony. UNESCO inscribed both the shrine and the ritual in 1995 and 2001 respectively.

## 8. Haeinsa Temple and the Tripitaka Koreana, South Gyeongsang

Haeinsa temple, founded in 802 AD in the Gayasan mountains, is revered above all as the repository of the Tripitaka Koreana — a complete edition of the Buddhist scriptural canon carved onto roughly 81,340 wooden printing blocks between 1237 and 1248 AD, during the Goryeo dynasty. The printing blocks are stored in four Janggyeong Panjeon depository halls built in the 15th century, whose passive ventilation system and the quality of the clay floors have preserved the blocks in near-perfect condition for over seven centuries. The depository buildings were inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1995; the Tripitaka itself was separately inscribed on the Memory of the World Register. The temple is active and accessible; the depositories are viewable from outside.

## 9. Gochang, Hwasun, and Ganghwa Dolmens

South Korea possesses the largest concentration of dolmen megalithic tombs in the world, with estimates ranging upward of 30,000 examples, representing a substantial fraction of all dolmens globally. The UNESCO World Heritage inscription of 2000 covers three clusters: the Gochang dolmen fields in North Jeolla, where around 1,500 examples are arranged across a gentle hillside; the Hwasun fields in South Jeolla; and the Ganghwa Island examples in Gyeonggi, which include some of the largest table-dolmens known. The dolmens date from around 1000 to 300 BC and mark elite burials of the Korean Bronze Age. The UNESCO sites are well managed with walking paths and interpretive signage. Gochang is the most accessible from major transport routes.

## 10. Namsansseong Fortress and Ancient Silla Hillforts, Gyeongju

Beyond the major tombs and temples of Gyeongju, the surrounding mountains preserve multiple hillforts and beacon-signal stations of the Silla period. Namsangseong on Namsan hill immediately south of Gyeongju is a vast mountain fortress whose dry-stone walls and Buddhist rock carvings are distributed across the entire ridge system. Namsan is often called an 'open-air museum' because over 100 Buddhist sculptures and shrine sites are carved into or placed along its rocky spine, produced over several centuries from roughly the 5th to 9th centuries AD. A full traverse of the ridge takes a full day's hiking; individual approach routes from several valley villages allow shorter visits to specific carved Buddha figures and rock-cut reliefs. The mountain requires no entry fee and no booking.

South Korea's archaeological heritage is generally well maintained and well signed. Gyeongju is the natural hub for Silla-period sites and can be reached by KTX in about two hours from Seoul. The Baekje sites in Gongju and Buyeo require a separate journey, most practically by bus or car from Daejeon. Spring cherry blossom and autumn foliage seasons draw large crowds; visiting in late summer or early winter gives a quieter experience. National museums in Gyeongju, Seoul, and Buyeo provide essential context, and their collections are exceptional.

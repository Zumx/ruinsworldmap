France conceals a remarkably deep archaeological record beneath its wine regions and cathedral towns. Before the Romans arrived in force after Julius Caesar's Gallic Wars of 58–51 BC, the territory that became Gaul was home to Celtic and earlier pre-Celtic cultures that raised the largest concentration of megalithic monuments in Europe. Rome then transformed Gaul into one of its most prosperous provinces, leaving behind aqueducts, theatres, arenas, and forum towns that in some cases survive better than comparable structures in Italy. From the prehistoric standing stones of Brittany to the Roman cities of Provence, France offers a sweep through more than six thousand years of monumental history. ## 1. Pont du Gard, Occitanie

The Pont du Gard is the best-preserved Roman aqueduct bridge in the world and arguably the most spectacular piece of Roman engineering still standing in western Europe. Built around 19 BC under the authority of Marcus Agrippa, it carried water across the Gardon river gorge as part of a 50-kilometre system supplying the Roman colony of Nemausus (modern Nîmes). The three-tiered structure of honey-coloured limestone reaches 48.8 metres at its highest point and spans 275 metres, its massive stones fitted without mortar. The calcite deposits inside the water channel still trace the original hydraulic gradient. A UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1985, the Pont du Gard sits in a scenic natural park about 20 kilometres northeast of Nîmes; entry to the bridge itself is free while the surrounding visitor centre charges admission.

## 2. Arena of Nîmes, Occitanie

The amphitheatre at Nîmes — known locally as Les Arènes — is one of the best-preserved Roman arenas in the world, superior in completeness to the Colosseum in Rome. Built around 70 AD, it seated roughly 24,000 spectators for gladiatorial combat and animal hunts. Its two-storey exterior arcade, with 60 arches per level, stands almost entirely intact, and the interior vaulting and seating terraces remain largely in place. Unlike many Roman monuments converted to other uses, the Nîmes arena still hosts spectacles — bullfights (corridas) and concerts — which has ensured its continuous maintenance. A small museum inside the upper galleries displays surviving mosaics and sculptural fragments. The arena stands in the centre of Nîmes, easily reached by train from Montpellier or Avignon.

## 3. Roman Theatre of Orange, Provence

The Théâtre Antique d'Orange preserves the most complete Roman stage wall — the scaenae frons — surviving anywhere in the world. Built during the reign of Augustus, probably in the late 1st century BC or early 1st century AD, the theatre seated around 10,000 people and its rear wall rises to 37 metres. Louis XIV reportedly called it 'the finest wall in my kingdom.' A colossal statue of Augustus occupies the central niche of the stage wall, a reproduction of the original found during excavations. Orange and its Roman arch (the Triumphal Arch, also Augustan-era) together form a UNESCO World Heritage Site. The theatre is still used for summer opera and concerts; visiting during the Chorégies d'Orange festival in July and August means hearing music in an extraordinary acoustic shell.

## 4. Glanum, Provence

Glanum, set at the foot of the Alpilles range just south of Saint-Rémy-de-Provence, was a major Gallo-Greek and then Roman city whose occupation spans roughly 600 years, from the 2nd century BC through its abandonment after a Germanic raid around 270 AD. The site preserves the foundations of temples, baths, a forum, private houses with mosaic floors, and — uniquely for a Roman provincial town — two almost perfectly preserved monuments standing just outside the ancient gate: a mausoleum (the Mausolée des Jules) and a triumphal arch, both Augustan-era. The mausoleum, rising 18 metres, is perhaps the finest surviving example of Roman funerary architecture outside Italy. Excavations have also revealed an earlier Hellenistic sanctuary built by the local Saluvian Celtic tribe in imitation of Greek sacred architecture. Entry is ticketed; the adjacent Musée des Alpilles adds context.

## 5. Carnac Stones, Brittany

The Carnac alignments constitute the largest concentration of megalithic standing stones in the world. Erected by Neolithic peoples beginning around 4500 BC and continuing across many centuries, the complex encompasses more than 3,000 menhirs arranged in long parallel rows stretching across the landscape near the Morbihan coast. Three main groups — Ménec, Kermario, and Kerlescan — each contain hundreds of stones that grow progressively smaller from west to east, a deliberate gradation whose purpose remains debated. Nearby, the Tumulus Saint-Michel and the Grand Menhir Brisé (a fallen monolith that originally stood around 20 metres high, now broken into four pieces) amplify the sense of a sacred prehistoric landscape. Direct access to the alignments is managed seasonally; off-season visits allow close approach, while summer brings guided circuits from the Carnac-Alignements visitor centre.

## 6. Alésia, Burgundy

Alésia marks the site of Julius Caesar's decisive victory over Vercingetorix and the Arverni tribe in 52 BC, an engagement Caesar himself described at length in his Gallic Wars. The Romans built an extraordinary double circumvallation — an inner wall facing the besieged hilltop town and an outer wall facing potential relief armies — totalling about 40 kilometres of earthworks, towers, and ditches. The site at Alise-Sainte-Reine preserves traces of these fortifications and has yielded extraordinary quantities of weapons, Roman military equipment, and Gaulish artefacts in excavations. The MuséoParc Alésia, opened in 2012, combines an outdoor archaeological site with a modern interpretive museum. A 19th-century bronze statue of Vercingetorix by Aimé Millet crowns the hill and is itself a monument to French Romantic nationalism's relationship with the Gallic past.

## 7. Vaison-la-Romaine, Provence

Vaison-la-Romaine preserves two excavated quarters — the Puymin and the Villasse — of the Roman city of Vasio Vocontiorum, capital of the Vocontii tribe under Rome. Unlike most Roman provincial sites where only foundations remain, Vaison offers complete street plans, intact mosaic floors in private houses, a colonnaded street, a large theatre, and a former portico garden. The House of the Dolphin and the House of the Silver Bust give an unusually complete picture of wealthy urban domestic life in the 1st and 2nd centuries AD. A Roman bridge crossing the Ouvèze river still carries foot traffic today. The living medieval upper town adds a historical layer above the Roman remains. Entry to both excavated quarters is covered by a single ticket available from the Vaison tourist office.

## 8. Château Gaillard, Normandy

Château Gaillard is not ancient in the Roman sense but represents medieval military engineering at its most refined. Built with remarkable speed between 1196 and 1198 by Richard I of England (Richard Coeur de Lion), it stands on a chalk spur above the Seine at Les Andelys and was considered impregnable at the time of its construction. Philip II of France captured it in 1204 after an eight-month siege. The ruins — substantial walls, towers, and the keep — command panoramic views of the Seine valley and are freely accessible. The site completes any French archaeological tour with its demonstration of how building ambition persisted from Rome through the high medieval period.

## 9. Bibracte, Burgundy

Bibracte, on the summit of Mont Beuvray in the Morvan hills, was the oppidum — fortified capital — of the Aedui, one of the most powerful Celtic tribes of Gaul. Caesar himself wintered here after his campaigns and is believed to have written portions of the Gallic Wars on site. The hilltop fortification covered around 135 hectares and contained workshops for metalworking, glassworking, and luxury goods production. Excavations since the 1860s, and intensively since the 1980s, have revealed the internal layout of streets, artisan quarters, and elite residences. The Musée de Bibracte at the site foot provides excellent context for the excavations viewable on the hilltop walk. Bibracte is about 30 kilometres west of Autun.

## 10. The Lascaux Cave Complex, Dordogne

Lascaux IV, the modern facsimile centre adjacent to the original sealed Lascaux cave, gives access to what are considered the finest Palaeolithic painted caves in the world. The original cave, discovered in 1940, contains around 600 painted and engraved animals produced roughly 17,000 years ago during the Magdalenian period. Among the images: a massive aurochs frieze, a swimming stag sequence, and a remarkable scene showing a bird-headed human figure beside a bison — the only narrative scene in Palaeolithic art. The original cave is permanently closed to protect the fragile pigments, but the Lascaux IV replica (opened 2016) uses advanced photogrammetry and digital replication to recreate the experience at life scale. Les Eyzies, the nearby village, is the hub of the Dordogne's extraordinary concentration of prehistoric sites.

France's ancient sites are well served by public transport to regional cities, from which local buses or hire cars reach rural sites. Provence in spring and autumn combines pleasant weather with smaller crowds at Nîmes, Orange, and Glanum. Carnac's alignments are best seen in the low-season months when access is unrestricted. Most sites charge modest entry fees, and the national Centre des Monuments Nationaux pass offers good value across multiple visits. [Open the map](/map) to find more sites across the country.

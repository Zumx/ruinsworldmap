Portugal's position on the far western edge of the Iberian Peninsula placed it at the end of the known world for most of antiquity, and yet successive cultures left monuments of remarkable ambition here. Neolithic farmers raised megalithic tombs called antas across the Alentejo before 3000 BC. Iron Age Celtic tribes built hilltop fortified settlements called castros. Phoenician and Carthaginian traders founded coastal outposts, followed by Romans who stayed for centuries, building cities, roads, temples, and bridges that still define the landscape. The Visigoths and Moors added further layers. Portugal's often-overlooked ancient heritage stretches from the granite uplands of the north to the limestone plains of the south, offering a depth and variety that rewards the traveller willing to venture beyond the Atlantic coast.

## 1. Conímbriga, Coimbra District

The largest and best-excavated Roman town in Portugal, Conímbriga stands about 16 kilometres south of Coimbra. Settled since the late Iron Age, it flourished as a Roman civitas from the 1st through 4th centuries AD. The site is famous above all for its spectacular mosaic floors — the House of the Fountains and the House of Cantaber contain some of the most elaborate in the entire western Roman Empire, with intricate polychrome geometric and figural scenes preserved in situ. The standing remains include city walls, thermae, the forum precinct, and a complex water supply system. Conímbriga was designated a UNESCO World Heritage candidate and has a purpose-built on-site museum. It is accessible by bus from Coimbra and well signposted.

## 2. Roman Temple of Évora, Alentejo

Standing in the centre of Évora, fourteen granite Corinthian columns rising to a complete entablature are among the best-preserved Roman temple remains on the Iberian Peninsula. Built in the 1st or 2nd century AD and dedicated most likely to the imperial cult, the temple survived because its podium was incorporated into the medieval castle and later a slaughterhouse, which protected the columns from dismantlement. The surrounding old town of Évora is itself a UNESCO World Heritage Site, and the temple serves as its visual centrepiece. The Museu Nacional Frei Manuel do Cenáculo next door holds finds from excavations across the Alentejo. Évora is reachable by train from Lisbon in around ninety minutes.

## 3. Citânia de Briteiros, Braga District

On a hillside in the Minho, Citânia de Briteiros is the most celebrated castro — a Celtic hilltop fortified settlement — in Portugal. Occupied from roughly the 4th century BC through to the 2nd or 3rd century AD, when Roman influence gradually transformed or replaced it, the site preserves the stone foundations of over 150 circular and rectangular houses arranged along paved lanes, surrounded by multiple concentric defensive walls. Two roundhouses were reconstructed in the 19th century to give a sense of the original built form. The Museu Martins Sarmento in Guimarães holds the most important finds from the site, including carved stone pedras formosas — decorated slab facades associated with bathing or funerary ritual. The site is open daily with a small admission charge.

## 4. Milreu, Faro District

Near the village of Estói in the Algarve, Milreu is a well-preserved Roman rural complex (villa rustica) that developed from the 1st through 4th centuries AD. Unusual among Roman Portuguese sites for retaining above-ground walls to considerable height, the complex includes a central peristyle house, fish-pond rooms decorated with marine mosaic, and a circular mausoleum-like structure later converted into a Christian church — one of the earliest known in Iberia. The site illustrates the gradual transition from Roman paganism to Christianity in the late antique period. Milreu is managed by the municipality of Faro, has a small museum on site, and is easily combined with a visit to the Estói palace gardens nearby.

## 5. Mértola, Baixo Alentejo

Mértola, on a promontory above the Guadiana river near the Spanish border, has been continuously occupied since at least the Phoenician period in the 1st millennium BC. Under the Romans it was Myrtilis, a significant river port trading copper from the Alentejo mines. The Visigoths made it an episcopal seat, and the Moors subsequently built a substantial alcáçova (fortified palace) whose walls and tower still dominate the skyline. The parish church preserves Almohad-period structural fabric and a mihrab, while the archaeological museum in the Roman house beneath the town hall displays Phoenician, Roman, and Islamic material in stratified sequence. Mértola's entire historic centre is treated as an open-air archaeological museum, making it an unusually coherent site for understanding deep urban continuity.

## 6. Conimbriga Aqueduct and Town Walls, Coimbra District

Distinct from the main excavated area of Conímbriga, the aqueduct and the late-Roman defensive wall that cuts brutally through the earlier domestic quarters deserve separate attention. When the Empire's frontier pressure grew in the late 3rd century AD, the townspeople built a shortened defensive circuit that severed the northern suburbs — a decision still readable in the archaeology. The aqueduct fed the site's elaborate hydraulic system of fountains, pools, and heated baths. Understanding this infrastructure alongside the mosaics reveals how sophisticated Roman urban life in Lusitania actually was, and how quickly it contracted under pressure.

## 7. Ruínas de Troia, Setúbal District

On a sand spit at the mouth of the Sado estuary south of Lisbon, the ruins of Troia represent one of the largest known Roman fish-salting factories (cetariae) in the western Mediterranean. From roughly the 2nd through 5th centuries AD, hundreds of stone tanks here processed garum — the fermented fish sauce that was a staple of Roman cooking across the empire — for export by sea. The complex also contained a necropolis, baths, and residential quarters. The ruins are embedded within a modern resort development but remain accessible to visitors; a small museum interprets the site. The ferry from Setúbal to Troia makes the approach part of the experience.

## 8. Castelo dos Mouros, Sintra

Perched on the granite ridgeline above Sintra, the Castelo dos Mouros is a Moorish fortification dating from the 8th or 9th century AD, built to control the approaches to Lisbon during the period of Islamic governance of the peninsula. The circuit walls and towers climb dramatically across the rocky crest, offering panoramic views from the Atlantic to the Tagus. After the Christian reconquest of Sintra in 1147, the castle fell into gradual decline. It was partly restored in the 19th century under the patronage of Fernando II. The castle forms part of the Cultural Landscape of Sintra, inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1995, and is reachable on foot or by tuk-tuk from Sintra station.

## 9. Alcácer do Sal Roman Forum, Setúbal District

Modern Alcácer do Sal overlies the Roman town of Salacia, a significant Lusitanian and Roman coastal settlement known for salt production and metalwork. Excavations beneath the town's castle hill have uncovered forum structures, inscriptions, and a rich Iron Age level beneath the Roman layers. The castle itself includes Moorish and later medieval fabric. The municipal museum houses finds from excavations across the district. Salacia's importance lay in its salt trade — the same resource that gave the town its name and that financed many of the monuments whose traces survive.

## 10. Antas of the Alentejo, Évora and Portalegre Districts

The Alentejo plateau is scattered with Neolithic megalithic tombs called antas — dolmens consisting of large upright orthostats capped by a massive cover stone, many still retaining their earthen mound. Built between roughly 4000 and 2500 BC, they served as collective burial chambers for agricultural communities. The Anta Grande do Zambujeiro near Évora is one of the largest dolmens in Europe, its chamber stones reaching five metres in height. The Cromeleque dos Almendres, also near Évora, is a substantial stone circle predating Stonehenge. These monuments remind visitors that Portugal's ancient heritage begins long before Rome or even the first Iron Age settlers. Many antas are set in open farmland accessible year-round on foot.

## Planning Your Visit

Portugal's ancient sites are spread across a country roughly the size of Indiana, and distances are manageable by car. The Alentejo, home to Évora, Mértola, and the megalithic monuments, rewards a two- to three-day circuit from Lisbon. Conímbriga and Briteiros sit along the north-south highway between Lisbon and Porto, easily added to that journey. Algarve sites like Milreu are most comfortably visited from October through May, before the summer heat intensifies. Entry to most state-managed sites costs between three and seven euros.

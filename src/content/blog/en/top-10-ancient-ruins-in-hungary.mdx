Hungary sits in the Carpathian Basin, a broad inland plain ringed by mountains that has served throughout history as both a corridor and a crossroads. Celtic tribes settled here in the Iron Age. The Romans made the Danube the frontier of their empire — the Limes — and the province of Pannonia west of the river became one of the most thoroughly Romanised parts of the empire, with cities, amphitheatres, villas, and bath complexes built from Vindobona (Vienna) south to the Save. East of the Danube, the Sarmatian Iazyges and later Hunnic confederations kept Rome at bay. After the Roman withdrawal in the late 4th and 5th centuries, Huns, Avars, and then the Magyar confederation swept through. The Magyar settlement of around 895 AD established the Hungarian homeland, and the conversion to Christianity under Stephen I (crowned 1000 AD) created the medieval kingdom. Every one of these layers left archaeological traces across the Hungarian landscape.

## 1. Aquincum, Budapest

Aquincum was the capital of the Roman province of Pannonia Inferior, established as a legionary fortress in the 1st century AD on the west bank of the Danube at the site of modern Budapest's III. district (Óbuda). At its height the city had a population estimated in the tens of thousands and was furnished with all the amenities of Roman urban life: forum, temples, amphitheatres (two survive, one civilian and one military — the military one, beneath a modern road junction, is the largest amphitheatre north of the Alps), baths, and a complete water supply system. The Aquincum Museum stands within the excavated civilian city and presents finds of exceptional quality, including an organ (hydraulis) reconstructed from surviving bronze parts. The military town is visible in scattered excavations throughout Óbuda.

## 2. Gorsium-Herculia, Tác

Gorsium near the village of Tác, south of Székesfehérvár, is the largest open-air Roman archaeological site in Hungary. Founded in the 1st century AD as a mansio (official inn and administrative post), it grew into a significant city and later became the provincial capital Herculia in the late 3rd century AD under Diocletian's reforms. Excavations have revealed forum complexes, temples, an early Christian basilica, a governor's palace, and extensive residential areas, all open for inspection across a large parkland site. The on-site museum presents over 40,000 registered finds. Gorsium hosts an annual Roman festival with re-enactors, making it one of the most accessible ways to engage with Roman-period archaeology in Hungary.

## 3. Sopron (Scarbantia), Western Hungary

Sopron, the westernmost city in Hungary, preserves unusual quantities of Roman fabric from the town of Scarbantia, which stood on the Amber Road — the ancient trade route connecting the Baltic to the Adriatic. The Roman forum underlies the central square of the old town, and the curved line of the forum can be traced in the modern street plan. Several Roman carved stones are built into medieval walls, and a Roman lapidary is housed in the Fabricius House museum. The surrounding area contains numerous Roman villas and mosaics. Sopron itself is also notable for the Hallstatt-period Iron Age cemetery at Sopron-Várhely (Burgstall), which produced remarkable situla-decorated urns in the 8th–7th centuries BC.

## 4. Savaria (Szombathely), Western Hungary

Szombathely (Roman Savaria) was the capital of the province of Pannonia Prima and the birthplace of St. Martin of Tours, one of the most important saints of the early medieval Western church. The Garden of Ruins (Romkert) in the city centre presents substantial remains of a Roman palace complex and early Christian basilica in situ. The Savaria Museum holds finds from the city and region, including a reconstructed Amber Road milestone. The medieval bishop's palace was built directly over Roman remains, and the old town centre retains the basic Roman grid in its street layout. A Roman-period temple precinct and several further excavated areas are accessible to visitors.

## 5. Pécs (Sopianae), Southern Hungary

Pécs was known in Roman times as Sopianae and became a significant Christian city in the 4th century AD. The UNESCO-listed early Christian mausoleum complex beneath the cathedral and in the surrounding streets is among the finest late Roman Christian archaeology in Europe. The burial chambers — decorated with frescoes of Christian scenes including a remarkable depiction of Daniel in the lions' den — are preserved in an underground visitor centre opened in 2007. Sopianae was the capital of Valeria province in the late Roman reorganisation and remained important through the early medieval period. The Janus Pannonius Museum in Pécs coordinates access to the various mausoleum sites scattered around the cathedral quarter.

## 6. Roman Limes on the Danube

The Danubian Limes in Hungary was a frontier system of watchtowers, fortlets, and forts stretching along the right (west) bank of the Danube from Vindobona (Vienna) to the Drava confluence. The Hungarian section, between Rajka and Dalj, includes over twenty significant Roman military installations. Key accessible sites include the fort at Visegrád (Pone Navata), the watch towers at Dunabogdány, and the fort at Százhalombatta (Matrica). In 2021 the Danubian Limes was inscribed as part of the transnational UNESCO World Heritage Site 'Frontiers of the Roman Empire.' The Danube Bend stretch between Esztergom and Vác is particularly well studied and offers attractive countryside alongside the archaeological sites.

## 7. Esztergom Castle Hill and Early Medieval Remains

Esztergom was the seat of the first Hungarian kings and the primatial see of the Hungarian Catholic Church from the time of Stephen I (reigned 997–1038 AD). The castle hill preserves remains of the royal palace complex including a Romanesque chapel with frescoes that are among the earliest surviving figurative paintings in Hungary. Archaeological investigations on and around the hill have documented the phases of the royal court from the 10th through the 13th centuries. The dominant feature of the modern hill is the vast 19th-century neoclassical basilica, but the older archaeological structures are visible in the castle museum complex and in the treasury. Esztergom is accessible from Budapest by rail along the Danube.

## 8. Fehér-tó Bronze Age Tell, Tiszaug

The Bronze Age tell settlements of the Hungarian plain represent one of Europe's most significant concentrations of prehistoric cultural stratification. Tells — artificial mounds formed by the accumulation of mudbrick debris over generations of occupation — are rare in central Europe but common in Hungary along the rivers Tisza and Körös. The mound at Tiszaug-Kéményteteje and the well-known site at Tiszapolgár represent the Otomani-Füzesabony Bronze Age culture (roughly 2000–1500 BC), when the plain was densely settled by hierarchical agricultural societies exchanging goods with communities across the Carpathian Basin and beyond. The associated material culture — bronze weapons, gold ornaments, wheel-made pottery — is displayed in the Hungarian National Museum in Budapest.

## 9. Letenye Roman Villa, Zala County

Letenye in Zala County near the Croatian border preserves the excavated remains of a substantial Roman villa rustica, a rural estate that formed part of the agricultural infrastructure of Pannonia. The site illustrates how Roman agricultural and domestic culture penetrated the countryside well beyond the major urban centres. A hypocaust heating system, mosaic floors, and bath suite remains are visible on site. Villa sites are distributed throughout western Hungary — several in the Lake Balaton region have also been investigated — and collectively they testify to the thoroughness of Roman colonisation in Pannonia. The Letenye villa is accessible as a day trip from the Lake Balaton region.

## 10. Pannonhalma Archabbey Hill

Pannonhalma Archabbey, founded in 996 AD by Prince Géza and completed under Stephen I, is one of the oldest and most significant monastic establishments in central Europe and a UNESCO World Heritage Site. While the present buildings are mostly medieval and later, archaeological investigations have established the continuity of occupation on the hill from at least the Roman period, and the abbey's foundation places it at the very beginning of the Hungarian Christian state. The Romanesque crypt and basilica elements within the complex represent the earliest surviving ecclesiastical architecture of the Hungarian kingdom. The hilltop setting above the Pannonian plain is magnificent, and the abbey remains an active Benedictine community.

Hungary is compact and well connected by rail and road. Budapest makes an ideal base for Aquincum and the Danube sites; Pécs and Szombathely are the main bases in the south and west. Most open-air sites are accessible April through October. The Hungarian National Museum in Budapest and the Savaria Museum in Szombathely are the primary destinations for Roman provincial material culture.

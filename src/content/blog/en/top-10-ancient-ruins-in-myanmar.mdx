Myanmar's ancient archaeological landscape is dominated by Buddhism and kings, two forces that together produced an extraordinary density of temples, pagodas, monasteries, and royal cities across more than fifteen centuries. The earliest urban civilisation in mainland Southeast Asia emerged here in the Pyu city-states — Sri Ksetra, Beikthano, and Halin — whose walled cities and Buddhist stupas predated the Bagan kingdom by several centuries. When Bagan flourished in the eleventh to thirteenth centuries it produced over 3,000 religious buildings across a 50-kilometre plain that, even after centuries of earthquake damage and partial collapse, remains the most concentrated field of ancient religious architecture in Southeast Asia. Later capitals at Inwa, Sagaing, and Mrauk U carried on distinct regional traditions. What survives is not ruined in the conventional sense — many structures are still maintained as active Buddhist sites — but the scale of what has collapsed, been abandoned, or lies half-excavated makes Myanmar's archaeological record both profound and melancholy.

## 1. Bagan, Mandalay Region

Bagan was the capital of the Pagan Kingdom from its founding as a major polity under Anawrahta in 1044 CE until the Mongol invasions of the late thirteenth century ended its dominance. In its heyday, the plain held over 10,000 religious structures; around 3,500 remain in various states. The building program was funded by royal patronage and required an enormous concentration of labour and wealth; its abrupt end when the capital was abandoned left the temples in a state of gradual but incomplete decay. Key structures include the Ananda Temple (built around 1105 CE, its white-painted exterior and gilded spire visible for kilometres), the massive Dhammayan Gyi temple (the largest in Bagan, its interior never completed), and the older Shwesandaw Pagoda, a step-pyramid form that predates the Anawrahta period. A UNESCO World Heritage Site inscribed in 2019. Hot-air balloon flights at dawn remain the iconic way to grasp the plain's scale.

## 2. Mrauk U, Rakhine State

Mrauk U was the capital of the Arakan Kingdom from around 1430 to 1785 CE, when the Burmese Konbaung dynasty conquered it. In its time it was a significant trading port that hosted Bengali, Portuguese, Dutch, and Indian merchants, and its kings wielded a cosmopolitan cultural influence that produced a distinctive architectural tradition combining Mon-Buddhist temple design with Bengali and Hindu elements. The Shitthaung Temple, built in 1535 CE, has an extraordinary interior gallery covered floor-to-ceiling with carved Buddha images and narrative reliefs — over 80,000 figures by traditional count. The Htukkan Thein ordination hall has a corbelled tunnel interior lined with standing Buddha figures. The archaeological zone around the city contains dozens of further temples, stupas, and palace foundations in a landscape of paddy fields and forested ridges. Access has been severely affected by conflict in Rakhine State since 2017; check current conditions carefully before planning a visit.

## 3. Inwa (Ava), Mandalay Region

Inwa served as the capital of successive Burmese kingdoms for much of the period between 1364 and 1838 CE, with interruptions when rival capitals held power. Its position on an island formed by the Irrawaddy and Myitnge rivers made it naturally defensible. Today the archaeological zone covers a large area of paddy fields and forest reachable by a short ferry from the southern bank. The most striking ruins are the Maha Aungmye Bonzan brick monastery complex (early nineteenth century, badly damaged by the 1838 earthquake that finally ended Inwa's role as capital) and the Nanmyin 'leaning tower', a round brick watchtower tilted by earthquake damage. Horse-cart tours are the traditional way to navigate the widely scattered ruins. The site gives a clear sense of the cycle of capital-building and abandonment that characterised Burmese dynastic history.

## 4. Sri Ksetra (Thayekhittaya), Magway Region

Sri Ksetra is the largest of the three Pyu city-states inscribed as UNESCO World Heritage Sites, covering roughly 1,400 hectares within its outer walls. Occupied from around the second century BCE or earlier until roughly the ninth century CE, it was the capital of a Pyu kingdom that traded with India and China and practised a form of Buddhism (along with other religions) centuries before Bagan. The earthen city walls with their gatehouses, three large brick stupas (the Bawbawgyi stupa is the most complete), palace mounds, and cemeteries are distributed across an agricultural landscape. The stupas are of a cylindrical form ultimately derived from Indian Buddhist architecture but developed into a distinctly Pyu type. A UNESCO World Heritage Site as part of the Pyu Ancient Cities serial inscription (2014). Located near Pyay (Prome) in central Myanmar.

## 5. Mingun, Sagaing Region

Mingun sits on the west bank of the Irrawaddy opposite Sagaing, reachable by river boat from Mandalay. Its defining feature is the Mingun Pahtodawgyi, an enormous unfinished stupa begun in 1790 CE by King Bodawpaya and abandoned — with roughly one third of its intended height completed — when the king died in 1819. What remains is a colossal brick mass, around 50 metres tall and severely cracked by the 1839 earthquake, that gives a vivid impression of the intended scale: had it been finished, it would have been the largest stupa in the world. The adjacent Mingun Bell, cast in 1808, is the largest uncracked hanging bell in the world at around 90 tonnes. The Hsinbyume Pagoda (rebuilt 1874), a whitewashed terraced structure representing Mount Meru, is a complete and strikingly beautiful contrast to the massive ruin beside it.

## 6. Halin Pyu City, Sagaing Region

Halin is one of the three UNESCO-inscribed Pyu city-states, located in the dry zone north of Mandalay. Occupied roughly from the first to the ninth centuries CE, it covers around 600 hectares within its rectangular earthen ramparts. Halin has been less extensively excavated than Sri Ksetra, but the city wall circuit, several brick mound structures, and evidence of glass bead and iron production give a picture of a productive Pyu urban centre linked to Indian Ocean trade networks. The site is in an active agricultural zone; its earthworks are the most visible feature. A UNESCO World Heritage Site.

## 7. Beikthano Pyu City, Magway Region

Beikthano is the oldest of the three UNESCO-inscribed Pyu city-states, with occupation dated from roughly the first century BCE to the fourth century CE. It is smaller than Sri Ksetra or Halin, covering around 870 hectares, and its earlier abandonment means it preserves a more purely Pyu (pre-Bagan) character without later overlay. Excavations have found evidence of Pyu funerary practices, including urn burials, and early Buddhist material. As with the other Pyu sites, the earthen city walls are the dominant visible landscape feature. A UNESCO World Heritage Site.

## 8. Sagaing Hill and Ancient Sagaing, Sagaing Region

Sagaing Hill, across the Irrawaddy from Mandalay, is covered in hundreds of white-painted Buddhist pagodas and monasteries, many built over foundations from the Sagaing Kingdom period (roughly 1315–1364 CE) and subsequent periods when Sagaing served as a monastic centre. The ancient town below the hill preserves traces of palace walls and older stupas from the Sagaing Kingdom, which ruled a short-lived independent state before being absorbed by Inwa. The hill is still an active centre of Buddhist monasticism — thousands of monks and nuns reside in the monasteries — making it simultaneously a living religious site and an archaeological landscape. The panoramic views over the Irrawaddy and the plain are exceptional at dawn.

## 9. Shwebo (Moksobo), Sagaing Region

Shwebo was the founding capital of the Konbaung Dynasty, built by Alaungpaya in 1752–1753 CE as his base for unifying the Burmese kingdoms. The earthen city walls, moat, and gate structures survive around a largely modern town. While Shwebo represents relatively recent history by the standards of the other sites here, its intact fortification earthworks and the brevity of its role as a capital — it was superseded by Inwa, Sagaing, and eventually Mandalay within decades — make it an instructive case study in Konbaung-era city-building patterns.

## 10. Pinle and Nyaungyan, Sagaing Region

Pinle was a short-lived capital of the Restored Toungoo Kingdom in the mid-seventeenth century, and its archaeological remains — brick monasteries, stupas, and palace foundations in a wooded area — are representative of the wave of urban centres built and abandoned across the Irrawaddy basin over five centuries of political fragmentation and consolidation. Sites like Pinle, Nyaungyan, Pinya, and others form a larger system of related royal centres that preceded and followed Inwa as capital; taken together they document the remarkable energy of Burmese dynastic building even as individual centres rose and fell within single generations.

Myanmar's political situation since the 2021 military coup has significantly affected tourism infrastructure and created serious safety concerns; current travel advisories from your government should be consulted before planning any visit. When the country has been accessible, Bagan has been the primary destination for archaeological visitors, reachable by domestic flight from Yangon or Mandalay. Sri Ksetra is a half-day trip from Pyay. Mrauk U, while extraordinary, has required additional logistical planning even in stable periods. The best seasons across all these sites are November to February, when temperatures are lower and the dry season ensures passable roads.

The Netherlands lies almost entirely within what the Romans called Germania Inferior, the lower Rhine frontier province that Rome administered from the late 1st century BC until the great incursions of the 4th and 5th centuries AD. For three and a half centuries the Rhine formed a military and economic artery, lined with legionary fortresses, auxiliary forts, river-patrol stations, and civilian towns that supplied and serviced the army. The soils of the Netherlands preserve this legacy remarkably well: waterlogged conditions along former river channels have conserved organic material — timber piles, leather, textiles, wooden writing tablets — that would have perished almost anywhere else in the Roman world. Beyond the Roman period, the coastal zone preserves prehistoric terp settlements built on artificial mounds above the flood plain, and the mouth of the Rhine was the site of one of the most complex hydraulic landscapes in the ancient world. The country's archaeological heritage is less dramatic in its visible remains than southern European counterparts but is no less significant in what it tells us about life on the empire's northern edge.

## 1. Nijmegen (Ulpia Noviomagus Batavorum), Gelderland

Nijmegen is the oldest city in the Netherlands and its Roman past is substantial. The legionary fortress of Castra Vetera was established nearby in the 1st century BC, and under Domitian a new auxiliary fort and civilian settlement grew on the hill above the Waal. Trajan elevated the settlement to the status of a municipium — Ulpia Noviomagus — named in his honour. The Museum het Valkhof houses the finest collection of Roman material from the region, including military equipment, inscriptions, glass, and remarkable bronze statuettes. Excavations beneath the city centre have revealed forum structures, bathhouses, and an extensive suburban cemeteries. The Valkhof park itself occupies a medieval tower built on Roman foundations. The museum is open year-round and is easily reached in the city centre.

## 2. Forum Hadriani (Voorburg), South Holland

Voorburg preserves the remains of Forum Hadriani, the capital of the Cananefates civitas and one of the few known Roman towns in the coastal zone of the Netherlands. Founded in the early 2nd century AD and elevated to the status of a municipium — possibly by Hadrian himself, which would explain the name — the town covered roughly 30 hectares along the banks of a Rhine distributary. Aerial photography and systematic fieldwork have mapped streets, a forum complex, bathhouses, and a harbour quay. Very little is visible above ground today, but the Archeon open-air museum nearby in Alphen aan den Rijn reconstructs Roman buildings based on regional excavation data and provides excellent context. Most finds are in Leiden's National Museum of Antiquities (Rijksmuseum van Oudheden).

## 3. Heerlen (Coriovallum), Limburg

Heerlen was a road junction town — Coriovallum — where two important Roman roads crossed, bringing it modest but steady commercial traffic throughout the 1st through 4th centuries AD. Its most impressive surviving structure is the Roman bathhouse complex, the Thermenmuseum, which protects the exposed remains of a 2nd-century bathing complex in situ beneath a purpose-built museum roof. Visitors walk above the caldarium, tepidarium, frigidarium, and service corridors on a system of elevated walkways. Mosaics, hypocaust floors, and sections of original wall standing to nearly a metre survive. The museum also displays the sculptural and ceramic finds from decades of urban excavation. Heerlen is in the southeastern tip of the Netherlands near the German and Belgian borders and is accessible by train.

## 4. Domburg, Zeeland

The coastal site of Domburg on the island of Walcheren was a place of ritual significance in both the Iron Age and Roman periods. A votive deposit recovered from the dunes in the 17th and 19th centuries included extraordinary bronze figurines, inscribed altars, and relief sculptures dedicated to the goddess Nehalennia — a local deity associated with seafarers and trade, depicted with a dog, fruit basket, and ship's prow. The deposit likely came from a shrine that served the merchants and mariners who crossed the North Sea between Britain and the Rhine delta. Similar Nehalennia material was recovered from the Oosterschelde estuary at Colijnsplaat. Many of the Domburg objects are in the Rijksmuseum van Oudheden in Leiden. The modern coastal resort of Domburg offers limited on-site interpretation.

## 5. Valkenburg aan de Geul, Limburg

The hillfort and Roman auxiliary fort at Valkenburg preserves a sequence of occupation from the Iron Age through the late Roman period. The Roman fort — one in a chain of installations defending the Rhine-Meuse zone — was excavated systematically in the 20th century, revealing a rare sequence of nine successive rebuilding phases in timber and stone. The waterlogged conditions of the lower levels preserved wooden structural elements, leather equipment, and textual material that have made Valkenburg one of the best-documented auxiliary forts anywhere in the Roman world. The town today is better known for its medieval castle ruins, but the Roman layers underlie the town centre and finds are displayed at the local museum.

## 6. Archeon, Alphen aan den Rijn, South Holland

Archeon is an open-air archaeological park that reconstructs prehistoric, Roman, and early medieval buildings and landscapes based on excavations from across the Netherlands. While not a ruin in the conventional sense, it preserves and presents genuine archaeological knowledge in experiential form: a Neolithic longhouse village, a Bronze Age farm, a 1st-century AD Roman street with shops, bathhouse, and temple, and a Frankish settlement. The Roman section draws directly on research from Forum Hadriani nearby and from Valkenburg. For visitors seeking to understand what Roman-period Dutch sites looked like in use, Archeon provides an irreplaceable complement to the sometimes sparse visible remains elsewhere.

## 7. Castra Herculis (Lobith/Tolkamer), Gelderland

The Roman fort at the site known from ancient sources as Castra Herculis was one of the final Rhine frontier installations before the river reached the sea. Roman texts and inscriptions record its garrison in the 4th century. Systematic field survey and aerial photography have identified the fort outline and associated vicus settlement near the modern Dutch-German border village of Tolkamer. Though nothing stands above ground, the site lies within an interpreted archaeological landscape along the Rhine, and recent collaborative Dutch-German research programmes have mapped the course of the Roman limes in this sector with precision. It is listed as part of the Frontiers of the Roman Empire UNESCO nomination.

## 8. Naaldwijk (Madestein), South Holland

The coastal villa at Madestein near Naaldwijk represents the agricultural hinterland of the Roman Rhine frontier — a rural estate exploiting the fertile coastal strip behind the dune barrier. Excavations have revealed a substantial stone-built villa complex with a bathhouse, outbuildings, and evidence of mixed farming including cattle, horse, and grain cultivation. The villa was active from roughly the 1st to the 4th century AD. It illustrates how civilian life and agricultural production functioned within sight of the military zone. Finds from the excavation are held in regional museums. The site itself is not publicly displayed but the research it generated informs interpretation panels across the South Holland Roman landscape.

## 9. Zwammerdam (Nigrum Pullum), South Holland

Zwammerdam on the Old Rhine was a Roman auxiliary fort and river-harbour station whose excavations in the 1970s produced something remarkable: the wooden hulls of several flat-bottomed river boats preserved in the anaerobic river sediment. These Rhine barges, dating to the 2nd and 3rd centuries AD, are among the best-preserved examples of ancient inland watercraft found anywhere in Europe and have shed detailed light on the logistics of supplying the frontier. The site itself is beneath agricultural land and not publicly accessible, but replicas of the boats and original hull timbers are displayed at the Archeon park and at the Drents Museum in Assen.

## 10. Tiel (Passewaaij), Gelderland

The rural settlements of the Batavian population — the indigenous people who supplied Rome with its most prized auxiliary cavalry and infantry — are represented at the extensively excavated landscape of Passewaaij near Tiel. Decades of rescue excavation ahead of development have revealed a dense pattern of farmsteads, field systems, ditched enclosures, and cemeteries occupied from the late Iron Age through the Roman period and into the early medieval centuries. The continuity of settlement and the combination of indigenous and Roman material culture make Passewaaij one of the key reference sites for understanding how the Batavians lived under and alongside Roman power. The findings are synthesised in academic publications and some material is on display in the Geldersland museum in Arnhem.

## Planning Your Visit

The best starting point for Roman Netherlands is Leiden's Rijksmuseum van Oudheden, which holds the finest collection of material from the entire Rhine delta and displays the Nehalennia altars from Domburg and Colijnsplaat to outstanding effect. Heerlen's Thermenmuseum is the only site where Roman structures are exposed in situ at scale. Nijmegen offers the most substantial urban context. Spring and autumn are ideal for visiting; most museum sites are open year-round.

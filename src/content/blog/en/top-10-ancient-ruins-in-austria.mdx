Austria's ancient history is dominated by two civilisations: the Celts who built hillforts and produced the distinctive La Tène and Hallstatt cultures, and the Romans who colonised the region from the 1st century BC onward, establishing major frontier legionary fortresses and civilian towns along the Danube. The result is a country where world-class Roman town remains sit alongside the prehistoric salt mines that gave one of Europe's defining Iron Age cultures its name. Austrian archaeology punches well above the country's size, and several of its sites have shaped global understanding of European prehistory.

## 1. Carnuntum, Lower Austria

Carnuntum was the capital of the Roman province of Pannonia Superior and one of the largest Roman settlements north of the Alps, with a civilian town and legionary fortress that housed up to 50,000 people at its peak. Major excavated monuments include a triumphal arch known as the Heidentor, a gladiatorial school, the palace district of the civilian town, and large sections of the urban street grid. The Archaeological Park Carnuntum uses innovative '4D' digital reconstruction techniques alongside reconstructed Roman buildings to present the site. The museum town (Römerstadt Carnuntum) includes full-scale reconstructions of Roman townhouses furnished to period. Open April to November.

## 2. Magdalensberg, Carinthia

Magdalensberg is a hillfort and early Roman town on a mountain summit in Carinthia, representing the transition from Celtic oppidum to Roman provincial capital. Occupied from the late 2nd century BC into the 1st century AD, it served as the capital of the kingdom of Noricum before Noricum was incorporated into the Roman Empire. Excavations have revealed a Roman forum, temples, workshops, and residential quarters. The site museum displays finds including bronzes and inscriptions; the summit setting, with sweeping views over the Zollfeld plain, is exceptional.

## 3. Aguntum, Tyrol

Aguntum, near Lienz in eastern Tyrol, was a Roman town granted the status of municipium under the emperor Claudius and flourishing from the 1st to 5th centuries AD. It is notable as the only Roman town in the Tyrol whose ruins are directly accessible to visitors. The excavated area includes sections of the forum, baths, an early Christian basilica, and the town's water and drainage systems. The Museum Aguntum on-site provides interpretation. The location — in the upper Drau Valley surrounded by alpine scenery — is strikingly beautiful.

## 4. Hallstatt, Upper Austria

Hallstatt gave its name to the Hallstatt culture (roughly 800–450 BC), the early Iron Age culture that preceded the La Tène period across central Europe. The village sits on a narrow lake shore in the Salzkammergut mountains and has been inhabited since the Neolithic thanks to its salt deposits. The prehistoric salt mines above the village, worked continuously since the Bronze Age, have preserved organic materials including wooden tools, leather clothing, and human remains to an extraordinary degree. The site is part of the Hallstatt-Dachstein / Salzkammergut UNESCO World Heritage Site.

## 5. Virunum, Carinthia

Virunum was the Roman capital of Noricum, located on the flat Zollfeld plain near modern Maria Saal in Carinthia. The town, founded in the late 1st century AD, replaced Magdalensberg as the provincial capital and grew into a prosperous city with a forum, temples, amphitheatre, and extensive residential quarters. The site is largely unexcavated beneath agricultural land, but aerial photography reveals the full town plan, and sections of the forum and other buildings have been partially investigated. Finds from Virunum are displayed in the Landesmuseum Kärnten in Klagenfurt.

## 6. Wels (Ovilava), Upper Austria

Wels, ancient Ovilava, was a Roman town elevated to the status of colonia under the emperor Hadrian. The modern city of Wels sits largely atop the Roman settlement, limiting excavation, but the Stadtmuseum Wels (City Museum) presents an impressive collection of Roman finds including the famous Wels Venus, a bronze statuette of exceptional quality. Sections of Roman mosaic floors are preserved in situ in the museum, and elements of the ancient street grid survive in the modern layout.

## 7. Brigantium (Bregenz), Vorarlberg

Bregenz was the Roman town of Brigantium, a key harbour and road junction on Lake Constance (Lacus Brigantinus) where the main route connecting Italy with the Rhine frontier crossed the water. The Vorarlberg Museum in Bregenz displays finds from excavations in and around the town including military equipment, ship timbers, and everyday objects. The Roman road network around the lake can be partially traced, and the town's role as a communication node is illustrated by the density of inscriptions found in the area.

## 8. Flavia Solva, Styria

Flavia Solva was a Roman town in Styria, the capital of a local administrative district (civitas), active from the 1st to 4th centuries AD. Excavations have revealed a forum, temple precinct, baths, and a dense residential quarter. The Leibnitz museum holds an important collection of finds including over 700 inscriptions. The site is partially accessible, and the nearby Roman road remains visible in stretches of the countryside.

## 9. Vindobona (Vienna)

Vienna's predecessor, the Roman legionary fortress of Vindobona, lies beneath the first district of the modern city. Established in the 1st century AD, the fortress housed the Legio X Gemina and later the Legio XIV Gemina and was a key element of the Danubian frontier (limes). The Roman remains are largely inaccessible beneath the city, but the Wien Museum and the Museum of Roman Ruins in the Michaelerplatz display finds and expose elements of the Roman fabric. Emperor Marcus Aurelius died at Vindobona in 180 AD.

## 10. Noreia / Gracarca, Carinthia

The location of Noreia, the Celtic capital destroyed by the Cimbri around 113 BC (the defeat that prompted Rome's emergency mobilisation), is debated, but the hillfort of Gracarca above the Klopeiner See in Carinthia is one of the strongest candidates. The site preserves substantial earthworks of a Celtic oppidum, and the landscape of the eastern Carinthian hills is dotted with other Iron Age hillforts attesting to the dense Celtic occupation of the region before the Roman conquest.

Austria's archaeological sites are well-managed and typically open from spring through autumn. Carnuntum and Hallstatt are the most visitor-ready, with full interpretation and on-site museums; Magdalensberg and Aguntum reward visitors willing to seek them out. The Kunsthistorisches Museum in Vienna holds an outstanding collection of Roman and Celtic antiquities from the Austrian provinces.

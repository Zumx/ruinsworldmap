Greece gave the western world its foundational vocabulary of architecture, democracy, philosophy, and drama, and the ruins where that vocabulary was forged are among the most visited and most studied in the world. Yet familiarity can mask genuine depth: the Acropolis of Athens is not simply a scenic backdrop but the physical record of roughly 5,000 years of human occupation; the sanctuary of Delphi was the closest thing the Greek world had to a single religious and political focal point for several centuries. Beyond the canonical sites, the Greek landscape is dense with theatres, sanctuaries, and city-state remains that reward attention. ## 1. Acropolis of Athens, Attica

The Acropolis plateau was fortified in the Mycenaean period around 1200 BC, but the monuments that dominate it today were built in an extraordinary burst of civic ambition under Pericles between roughly 447 and 406 BC. The Parthenon, dedicated to Athena Parthenos, is a Doric temple of Pentelic marble whose proportional refinements — columns that bulge slightly at the centre, a platform that curves gently upward, column shafts that lean imperceptibly inward — were deliberate optical corrections to prevent the building from appearing to sag. The Erechtheion, with its porch of Caryatid figures (originals now in the Acropolis Museum), and the Propylaea gatehouse complete the main ensemble. The New Acropolis Museum at the hill's foot presents the surviving sculpture in superb conditions. The site is a UNESCO World Heritage Site. Visit early in the morning before summer heat and crowds build.

## 2. Delphi, Phocis

Delphi occupied a steep shelf on the southern slopes of Mount Parnassos and served as the seat of the Oracle of Apollo — the most consulted oracle in the Greek world — from roughly the 8th century BC through the late 4th century AD. The Sanctuary of Apollo contains the Temple of Apollo (in its current form rebuilt after 330 BC), the Sacred Way lined with treasury buildings dedicated by Greek city-states, a well-preserved theatre, and high above it, a stadium that hosted the Pythian Games. The adjacent Marmaria precinct preserves the circular Tholos — its three restored columns one of the most photographed structures in Greece — and the Sanctuary of Athena Pronaia. The Delphi Archaeological Museum holds the famous bronze Charioteer (478 BC) and the Naxian Sphinx. UNESCO designation covers the entire site. Delphi is about 180 kilometres northwest of Athens; day trips are possible but an overnight stay allows early access.

## 3. Ancient Olympia, Peloponnese

Olympia in the Alpheios valley was the site of the Ancient Olympic Games, held every four years from traditionally 776 BC until their suppression by Emperor Theodosius I around 393 AD. The sacred precinct known as the Altis contains the ruins of the Temple of Zeus — originally housing the colossal chryselephantine statue of Zeus by Pheidias, one of the Seven Wonders — and the Temple of Hera, the oldest surviving Doric temple at Olympia. The workshop of Pheidias has been identified by excavation, and the palaestra, gymnasium, and stadium are largely preserved. The Archaeological Museum of Olympia houses the pediment sculptures from the Temple of Zeus and the Nike of Paionios. The site is a UNESCO World Heritage Site. It lies near the modern village of Archaia Olympia, accessible by bus from Pyrgos.

## 4. Knossos, Crete

Knossos was the largest Bronze Age palace on Crete and the administrative centre of the Minoan civilization, which flourished from roughly 2000 to 1450 BC. The palace complex, with its labyrinthine floor plan of more than 1,000 rooms, gave rise to the Greek myth of the Minotaur. Excavated and controversially restored by Arthur Evans between 1900 and 1935, Knossos presents reconstructed concrete columns (painted in the characteristic Minoan red and black), wall paintings (replicas of the originals now in Heraklion), and storage magazines lined with massive pithoi. The Minoan Throne Room, with its gypsum throne and flanking griffin frescoes, is one of the most celebrated spaces in European prehistory. The Heraklion Archaeological Museum, a ten-minute drive away, holds the original frescoes, Linear A and B tablets, and the Phaistos Disc. Knossos is a UNESCO World Heritage Site.

## 5. Mycenae, Peloponnese

Mycenae, on a rocky promontory in the Argolid, gave its name to the Bronze Age civilization — Mycenaean — that dominated mainland Greece and much of the Aegean from roughly 1600 to 1100 BC. Heinrich Schliemann excavated here from 1874 onward, finding the Shaft Graves of Grave Circle A and the gold death masks now in the National Archaeological Museum in Athens. The Lion Gate, built around 1250 BC, is the largest surviving sculpture of Bronze Age Greece — two carved lions flanking a column above a threshold still used by visitors today. The Treasury of Atreus (a misnomer introduced by Schliemann; it is actually a tholos tomb) is a corbelled beehive tomb some 14 metres high and 14 metres wide, its 120-tonne lintel stone still in place after 3,300 years. UNESCO-listed. Accessible by bus from Nafplio or Argos.

## 6. Epidaurus, Peloponnese

The Sanctuary of Asclepius at Epidaurus was the most important healing sanctuary of the ancient Greek world, drawing pilgrims from across the Mediterranean to seek cures through ritual incubation and medical treatment from the sanctuary's priests. The complex includes temples, a gymnasium, a hotel (katagogion), and a stadium, but is most famous for its theatre, built around 350 BC and considered the finest ancient theatre for both acoustics and preservation. Its 14,000 seats in 55 rows — the upper 21 added in the 2nd century BC — rise in perfect harmony with the hillside. The acoustics are famously precise: a coin dropped on the circular orchestra can be heard from the top row. UNESCO-listed. The Epidaurus Festival (July–August) stages ancient tragedy and comedy in the original space; advance booking is essential.

## 7. Delos, Cyclades

The small island of Delos in the Cyclades was, according to Greek myth, the birthplace of Apollo and Artemis. By the Archaic period it had become one of the most important Panhellenic sanctuaries, and by the 2nd century BC, under Roman protection, it was the busiest commercial port in the Aegean — a free port handling the slave trade and luxury goods exchange of the Mediterranean world. The island was comprehensively sacked by Mithridates VI of Pontus in 88 BC and again in 69 BC, and was never substantially reoccupied. As a result, the entire ancient city survives unbuilt-upon: the Sanctuary of Apollo with its treasuries, the Avenue of Lions (5 of the original 9 marble lions remain in situ, with originals in the museum), the Theatre Quarter with its mosaic-floored merchants' houses, and the summit of Mount Kynthos with its pan-Mediterranean temple precinct. Delos is uninhabited; day trips depart from Mykonos.

## 8. Ancient Corinth, Peloponnese

Ancient Corinth controlled the isthmus connecting mainland Greece to the Peloponnese — one of the most strategically valuable locations in the ancient world — and was one of the wealthiest Greek cities of the Archaic and Classical periods. The Romans destroyed it in 146 BC and refounded it as a Roman colony in 44 BC; the ruins visible today are therefore a mixture of late Archaic Greek and Roman imperial urban fabric. Seven monolithic columns of the Doric Temple of Apollo (c. 540 BC) are among the oldest standing temple columns in mainland Greece. The excavated agora reveals Roman-era shops, basilicas, and public fountains, including the Peirene fountain house with its plastered reservoir chambers. Acrocorinth, the fortified rocky hill above the ancient city, preserves circuit walls from Greek, Roman, Byzantine, and Ottoman periods stacked in layers. The site is roughly 80 kilometres southwest of Athens by road.

## 9. Ancient Agora of Athens, Attica

The Agora below the Acropolis was the commercial, civic, and philosophical heart of Classical Athens — the place where Socrates walked and debated, where citizens voted, and where markets, law courts, and temple precincts coexisted. The Stoa of Attalos, a two-storey portico donated by the king of Pergamon in the 2nd century BC, was fully reconstructed in the 1950s by the American School of Classical Studies and now houses the Agora Museum. The Temple of Hephaistos (the Hephaisteion), visible from across Athens, is the best-preserved Doric temple in Greece, its entablature and coffered ceiling still largely intact. Excavations have yielded ostraca (pottery sherds used in votes to exile politicians), bronze voting equipment, and thousands of objects illustrating daily Athenian life.

## 10. Ancient Thessaloniki, Macedonia

Thessaloniki preserves a remarkable overlay of Roman, Byzantine, and early Christian monuments within a living city. The Arch of Galerius (c. 298–305 AD), the Rotunda (a massive domed mausoleum later converted to a church and then a mosque), and the Palace of Galerius remain visible in the city centre. The city's Byzantine walls and towers, together with the White Tower on the waterfront, extend the heritage timeline through the medieval period. The Archaeological Museum of Thessaloniki holds the extraordinary gold burial goods from the Macedonian royal tombs at Aigai (Vergina), including the larnax believed to contain the bones of Philip II.

Greece's major sites are served by an extensive tourist infrastructure, with direct buses from Athens to Delphi, Olympia, Epidaurus, and Mycenae. Island sites like Knossos and Delos require ferry connections. The summer heat from July to August is intense; spring (April–May) and autumn (September–October) offer the most comfortable conditions for walking the open-air sites. Most sites charge a combined ticket or have individual entry fees; the Archaeological Sites of Athens combined ticket covers the Acropolis, Agora, and several other central sites.

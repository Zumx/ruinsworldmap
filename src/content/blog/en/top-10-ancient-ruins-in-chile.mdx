Chile's geography — 4,300 kilometres of coastline, the world's driest desert in the north, Patagonian ice fields in the south — shaped the extraordinary variety of cultures that developed along its length. In the Atacama, where almost no rain falls, the preservation of organic material is unparalleled: textiles, mummies, and wooden objects routinely survive thousands of years in the soil. Andean peoples built fortified villages, aligned geoglyphs, and elaborate irrigation systems; further south, the Mapuche defended their territory against Inca expansion and resisted Spanish colonisation for over three centuries. And far to the west, in the middle of the Pacific Ocean, the inhabitants of Rapa Nui created one of the most iconic archaeological landscapes on Earth. Chile's ruins span an enormous chronological and cultural range, from pre-ceramic coastal fisherfolk to Andean states connected to the Inca empire.

## 1. Ahu Tongariki, Easter Island (Rapa Nui)

Ahu Tongariki is the largest ceremonial platform (ahu) on Easter Island, supporting fifteen moai restored to standing position after being toppled and partially buried. The platform stretches roughly 200 metres along the coast near Rano Raraku volcano, and its moai — the tallest stands around 9 metres — face inland as all the island's statues do, overseeing the settlement rather than the sea. The ahu was devastated by a 1960 tsunami and the figures were re-erected in the 1990s with Japanese funding. Rapa Nui National Park, which includes Tongariki, is a UNESCO World Heritage Site. The island is reached by flight from Santiago or Tahiti; a local permit system now regulates visitor numbers to protect the fragile landscape.

## 2. Rano Raraku, Easter Island (Rapa Nui)

Rano Raraku is the volcanic crater quarry where around 95 percent of all moai were carved, and it remains scattered with nearly 400 figures in various states of completion — some buried to their chins, others still attached to the living rock. The quarry was effectively abandoned in the 17th century, leaving a strange assembly of half-finished giants in the hillside. Recent excavations have revealed that the buried statues have full bodies with carved designs on their torsos, fundamentally changing earlier assumptions about their form. The crater lake inside Rano Raraku is one of only three freshwater sources on the island. Visiting requires the Rapa Nui National Park entry fee, and early morning visits avoid the worst of the midday crowds.

## 3. Pukará de Quitor, San Pedro de Atacama

Pukará de Quitor is a fortified pre-Columbian settlement built on a rocky hilltop above the Atacama oasis town of San Pedro de Atacama, constructed by the Atacameño (Lickanantay) people roughly around the 12th century AD. The ruins consist of stone-walled enclosures climbing the hillside, some with well-preserved walls still standing to head height. The pukará served as a defensive stronghold and was the site of one of the final battles before Spanish conquest in 1540. The views over the San Pedro river valley and the surrounding salt flats are dramatic. The site is just 3 kilometres from San Pedro de Atacama town centre and reachable on foot or by bicycle. An entrance fee applies and a site guardian is usually present.

## 4. Geoglyphs of Pintados, Tarapacá Region

The Pintados geoglyphs, in the Tarapacá Region of northern Chile near the town of Pozo Almonte, constitute one of the largest concentrations of geoglyphs in South America, with more than 350 designs covering the slopes of a cerro (hill) in the Atacama. The figures — made by removing dark desert pavement to expose lighter soil beneath — include human figures, animals such as llamas and birds, geometric patterns, and abstract designs created by pre-Columbian Andean cultures over a long period from roughly 900 to 1450 AD. They likely functioned as waymarkers for llama caravans travelling Andean exchange routes. The site is a UNESCO World Heritage component within the Tarapacá geoglyph system. A visitor centre and protective barriers allow close inspection without damaging the designs.

## 5. Tulor, Atacama Desert

Tulor is a partially excavated pre-Columbian village in the Atacama Desert, occupied by the early Atacameño culture from around 800 BC to 400 AD. The circular adobe structures — with walls still preserved in places to a metre or more thanks to the extreme aridity — represent some of the earliest permanent architecture in the Atacama. The village was gradually buried by drifting sand, which both destroyed and preserved it. Excavations have uncovered ceramics, tools, and evidence of agricultural activity in what is now hyperarid terrain. The site is located about 10 kilometres from San Pedro de Atacama and is accessible by arranged tour from the town. A small roofed shelter protects the most significant excavated area.

## 6. Pukará de Lasana, Atacama

Pukará de Lasana occupies a dramatic rocky promontory above the Loa River canyon, around 100 kilometres north of Calama. The Atacameño settlement, dated to roughly the 10th to 15th centuries AD, consists of over 100 stone structures including dwellings, storage rooms, and ceremonial spaces arranged across multiple terraces. The Inca later occupied and modified the site, adding their characteristic architectural elements to the pre-existing layout. The contrast between the dark volcanic stone of the walls and the red canyon walls of the Loa gorge creates a visually striking ruin. A small site museum provides context. The pukará is reachable by paved road and is one of the most accessible fortified sites in the Atacama region.

## 7. Atacameño Mummy Sites (San Pedro de Atacama Museum)

The driest desert in the world has preserved human remains of exceptional quality. The Museo Gustavo Le Paige in San Pedro de Atacama holds one of South America's most important collections of Atacameño material culture, including mummies, textiles, ceramic vessels, and wooden artefacts recovered from the surrounding cemeteries. The collections span several thousand years and illustrate the evolution of Atacameño society from nomadic hunter-gatherers to settled agriculturalists and herders integrated into Andean trade networks. The museum underwent a major renovation in the early 21st century. While the cemetery sites themselves are largely off-limits to visitors, the museum provides the essential context for understanding the region's rich archaeological landscape.

## 8. Cerro Unitas and Gigante de Atacama

The Gigante de Atacama (Giant of the Atacama) on Cerro Unitas near Huara is the largest prehistoric anthropomorphic geoglyph in the world, measuring roughly 86 metres in height. The figure, dating to around 900–1400 AD, depicts a human form with a rectangular headdress and is associated with agricultural and astronomical ceremonies along the Andean exchange corridor. The surrounding hillside carries additional geoglyphs of animals and abstract forms. Access requires a drive into the pampa north of Huara; the site is best viewed from a distance to appreciate the full scale of the figure. No formal infrastructure exists, and visits are typically combined with other geoglyph sites in the Tarapacá region.

## 9. El Molle Culture Sites, Elqui Valley

The Elqui Valley in the Coquimbo Region was home to the El Molle culture from roughly 300 BC to 700 AD, one of the earliest ceramic-using groups in Chile. The El Molle people left behind pit burials, distinctive black-incised pottery, and evidence of intensive agriculture along the valley floor. Several sites in the valley have been excavated, and a regional museum in the town of Vicuña preserves artefacts from the culture. The Diaguitas people succeeded the El Molle in the valley from around 900 AD, producing polychrome ceramics now regarded as among the finest in pre-Columbian Chile. The Elqui Valley is accessible from La Serena and is also renowned for its clear skies and astronomical observatories.

## 10. Incallajta and Inca Road (Qhapaq Ñan) in Northern Chile

The Inca empire extended into northern Chile from around 1430 AD, incorporating Atacameño settlements into its administrative system and leaving physical evidence throughout the region. The Qhapaq Ñan — the great Inca road system — included several segments through northern Chile, and sections of the original stone-paved road survive in the high Andean puna, along with tambo (waystation) ruins. In the Arica region, Inca administrative centres and storage complexes have been identified at several locations. The Chilean section of the Qhapaq Ñan was inscribed as part of the transnational UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2014. The most accessible sections of Inca road in Chile are in the highlands of the Arica y Parinacota Region, reachable from Arica with a four-wheel-drive vehicle.

The best time to visit northern Chile's desert ruins is April through November, when temperatures are more moderate. Easter Island is accessible year-round, though February's Tapati festival brings crowds. Guided tours from San Pedro de Atacama cover most of the Atacama sites efficiently. Bear in mind that some remote sites have no services and require four-wheel-drive access.

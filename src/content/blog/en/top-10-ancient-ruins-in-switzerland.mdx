Switzerland sits astride some of the most important passes and river corridors of western Europe, a fact that gave its valleys disproportionate strategic weight throughout antiquity. The Celts of the La Tène culture — the most refined expression of Iron Age European civilisation, named after a lake site in Switzerland — left dense settlements and spectacular metalwork. Rome conquered the Helvetii after Caesar's campaigns and built a network of well-appointed colonial towns along the Rhine and the major road routes. The Rhine frontier itself was one of the most intensively garrisoned stretches of the Roman world, and Switzerland preserves some of the finest material evidence of limes fortification anywhere. Beneath all of this lies an even older layer: the Bronze Age pile-dwelling settlements of the Swiss lakes, preserved by waterlogged conditions and collectively inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site. The country is relatively small but its archaeological density is remarkable.

## 1. Augusta Raurica, Near Basel

Augusta Raurica, near modern Augst on the Rhine east of Basel, is the best-preserved Roman town north of the Alps and one of the major archaeological sites of western Europe. Founded around 44 BC as a Roman colony, it became a substantial city of perhaps 20,000 inhabitants during the 2nd century AD, laid out on an orthogonal grid with a forum, theatre, temples, amphitheatre, and multiple bath complexes. The amphitheatre, restored in part, seats several thousand and is used today for open-air performances. The site museum houses the Augusta Raurica Silver Treasure, a hoard of 270 silver objects buried around 350 AD and discovered in 1962, which is among the most important collections of late Roman silver known. The in-situ ruins extend over a large area of the Rhine terrace; admission to the site is free, with a charge for the museum.

## 2. Avenches (Aventicum), Canton Vaud

Aventicum was the capital of the Roman province of Gallia Belgica's civitas Helvetiorum and one of the most important cities of Roman Switzerland, with a peak population estimated in the tens of thousands during the 2nd and 3rd centuries AD. The city circuit wall, roughly 5.7 kilometres in circumference with 73 towers, is partially preserved and visible in the fields around modern Avenches. The most striking surviving monument is the isolated theatre tower known as the 'Cigognier' (stork tower), a fragment of a temple precinct podium standing to about eight metres. A well-equipped Roman museum in the town centre displays finds from excavations including a golden bust of Marcus Aurelius discovered in 1939. The amphitheatre's outer wall survives and the arena is used for events. Avenches is accessible by rail from Payerne or Murten.

## 3. Vindonissa (Windisch), Canton Aargau

Vindonissa, at modern Windisch in the Aargau at the confluence of the Reuss and Aare rivers, was the only permanent legionary fortress in Switzerland, housing different legions between roughly 15 AD and the early 2nd century. The XIII Gemina, XXI Rapax, and XI Claudia legions successively occupied the fortress. The site is large — the standard Roman legionary fortress of roughly 20 hectares — and has been excavated over many decades. A civilian amphitheatre survives as earthwork banks immediately outside the modern town. The Vindonissa Museum in Brugg, a short distance from the site, holds the most significant Roman military collection in Switzerland, including complete sets of legionary equipment, medical instruments, and the famous Vindonissa writing tablets. The museum is open year-round.

## 4. Nyon (Colonia Iulia Equestris), Canton Vaud

Nyon on the southwestern shore of Lake Geneva was one of Caesar's earliest colonies in Gaul, founded around 45 BC and established to settle veterans of his cavalry. The town retained its function as a regional administrative centre through the Roman imperial period and has left visible traces in the modern town centre. The Roman columns displayed in the Place du Château are the most photographed element; they formed part of a large basilica or forum portico. The Nyon Roman Museum, housed in a former church, displays the town's Roman finds including pottery, bronzes, and inscriptions. A late Roman lighthouse base survives on the lakeshore. Nyon is easily accessible from Geneva by intercity train in about 25 minutes.

## 5. Martigny (Octodurus / Forum Claudii Vallensium), Canton Valais

Martigny, at the foot of the Great St Bernard Pass, was the capital of the Roman civitas Vallensium and an important staging post for trans-Alpine travel. The town was established as Forum Claudii Vallensium under the emperor Claudius in the 1st century AD and grew into a substantial urban centre with an amphitheatre, temples, bath houses, and an episcopal complex by late antiquity. The amphitheatre, excavated from the 1970s onward, is partially restored and ranks among the best-preserved Roman amphitheatres in the Alpine region. The Foundation Pierre Gianadda has constructed a Roman museum adjacent to a discovered temple precinct; the museum displays locally excavated material including bronze cult objects and carved stone. Martigny is accessible by rail from Lausanne and serves as a base for Great St Bernard Pass excursions.

## 6. Extensive Pile Dwellings, Swiss Lakes

Switzerland's lake shores and wetlands preserve some of the most important prehistoric archaeological deposits in Europe. Pile-dwelling settlements — built on platforms extending over the water — were established on dozens of Swiss lakes from roughly 4300 to 800 BC, spanning the Neolithic and Bronze Age. The anaerobic mud has preserved wood, textiles, foodstuffs, and other organic materials that leave no trace on dry-land sites. Notable lake sites include Zurich-Mozartstrasse, Auvernier on Lake Neuchâtel, and Arbon-Bleiche on Lake Constance. The Laténium museum at Hauterive near Neuchâtel provides the best overview of Swiss prehistoric lake settlement, and finds from many sites are displayed there. Together with pile-dwelling sites in neighbouring countries, the Swiss examples form part of the UNESCO 'Prehistoric Pile Dwellings around the Alps' inscription of 2011.

## 7. La Tène, Lake Neuchâtel

La Tène, a shallow bay at the northeastern tip of Lake Neuchâtel, is the type site for the La Tène culture, the mature expression of European Iron Age civilisation that flourished from roughly the 5th to 1st centuries BC and spread across most of central and western Europe. Dredging and excavation in the late 19th and 20th centuries recovered enormous quantities of iron swords, spearheads, shield bosses, woodwork, and other objects from the lake sediments, apparently deposited deliberately over several centuries — possibly as votive offerings. The finds redefined understanding of Celtic material culture and gave the entire cultural phase its name. The objects are now distributed between the Laténium museum at Hauterive and the Swiss National Museum in Zurich. The La Tène bay site itself is signposted; a modest memorial marks the location.

## 8. Kaiseraugst Treasure Site and Late Roman Fort

Kaiseraugst, adjacent to Augusta Raurica, was the site of the late Roman fort Castrum Rauracense, built in the late 3rd century AD as part of the Rhine frontier defence system. The fort's construction used recycled stone from the earlier civilian city at Augusta Raurica. The famous silver treasure was buried within or near the fort's bathhouse sometime around 350 AD and lay undiscovered until a building excavation in 1962. The hoard includes a large decorated dish — the Achilles Dish — a pepper pot, a horse-and-rider statuette, and numerous silver spoons and bowls, totalling over 25 kilograms of silver. The fort's outline is partially visible in the landscape. The treasure is permanently displayed in the Augusta Raurica Museum a short distance to the west.

## 9. Schleitheim (Iuliomagus), Canton Schaffhausen

Iuliomagus, at modern Schleitheim in Canton Schaffhausen near the Rhine bend, was a Roman small town whose principal monument is a temple precinct excavated and partially restored in the 1970s and 1980s. The temple, a Romano-Celtic type with a square cella and ambulatory, has been partially reconstructed in concrete to show its original outline. Associated finds include votive bronzes, pottery, and coins spanning the 1st to 3rd centuries AD. Schleitheim sits in the region known as the High Rhine zone, a corridor of moderate-sized Roman settlements between the major legionary fortresses. The site is accessible and freely visited; it provides a useful contrast to the more spectacular urban remains at Augusta Raurica and Avenches.

## 10. Munot Fortress, Schaffhausen

The Munot, the circular Renaissance tower that dominates Schaffhausen on the Rhine, was built using local medieval labour under a Dürer-derived design between 1564 and 1589. While it is not ancient in the Roman sense, the site beneath the Munot and the river bend it overlooks was actively used during the Roman period as a river crossing and watchtower position on the Rhine limes. The broader Schaffhausen area contains Roman boat landing sites, a civilian settlement, and milestones from the Rhine road. The Munot itself is freely accessible and gives panoramic views over the Rhine bend and the surrounding landscape of ancient crossings and fortification lines. The All Saints Museum in central Schaffhausen holds Roman and medieval finds from the immediate region.

Switzerland's Roman sites are well maintained and clearly signed, with excellent site museums at Augusta Raurica and Vindonissa in particular. The pile-dwelling museums at Laténium (Neuchâtel) and the Swiss National Museum (Zurich) provide indispensable prehistoric context. Summer is the primary visiting season for outdoor sites, though Augusta Raurica and the major museums are open year-round. Public transport reaches most major sites with moderate walking.

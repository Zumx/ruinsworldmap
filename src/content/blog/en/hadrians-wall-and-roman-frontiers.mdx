## Why Rome Built Walls

The image of a wall defining the Roman Empire's edge is both accurate and misleading. Rome built physical barriers at several frontiers, but for much of the empire's history the frontier was a zone — a band of territory where Roman influence graduated from full administrative control through client kingdoms and allied tribes to acknowledged enemy territory. The walls that Rome built were not the outer edge of this zone but rather control mechanisms within it: ways to channel movement, collect tolls, regulate trade, and project military authority rather than to stop armies.

The decision to build Hadrian's Wall was a strategic choice made in a specific context: a visit by the Emperor Hadrian to Britain in AD 122, probably in response to unrest in the north and a desire to consolidate a stable frontier after decades of aggressive northward campaigning under Trajan. The wall was not a retreat but an administrative statement, defining the empire's northern limit in Britain and providing a basis for systematic control of movement between the highland peoples to the north and the Romanised lowlands to the south.

## Hadrian's Wall: Construction and Garrison

Hadrian's Wall runs 118 kilometres from Wallsend (Segedunum) on the River Tyne in the east to Bowness-on-Solway in the west. Its construction occupied the legions of Britain — primarily Legion II Augusta, Legion VI Victrix, and Legion XX Valeria Victrix — over roughly six years from AD 122. The original design was for a wall 3 metres wide and perhaps 4–5 metres high, but this was revised during construction to a narrower gauge of around 2.4 metres (the 'Narrow Wall'), possibly to speed completion or conserve materials.

Along the wall, at every Roman mile (approximately 1.48 kilometres), stood a milecastle: a small fortlet with a gate through the wall and accommodation for perhaps eight to thirty-two men. Between each pair of milecastles were two turrets, spaced roughly a third of a Roman mile apart, used for observation and signalling. To the south of the wall ran a feature called the Vallum — a flat-bottomed ditch flanked by earthen mounds — which defined the military zone and controlled access from the south as well as the north.

The major forts, sixteen in total, were added to the wall itself during or shortly after construction, their north gates opening directly onto the frontier. The best-preserved is Housesteads (Vercovicium) on the central section, where the outline of the headquarters building, commanding officer's house, granaries, and barrack blocks are clearly legible. The latrine at Housesteads, with its continuous stone bench over a sluiced channel, remains one of the most visited Roman latrines in the world.

The wall's garrison over its 300-year operational life was provided by auxiliary units — soldiers drawn from across the empire, from Spain, Germany, the Balkans, North Africa, and the Near East. Inscriptions from the forts record units such as the First Cohort of Tungrians (from present-day Belgium) at Housesteads and the Second Asturian Cavalry (from northern Spain) at Chesters (Cilurnum). The garrison was not a fixed Roman legionary force but a cosmopolitan frontier community.

## The Antonine Wall: A Second Frontier

Roughly twenty years after Hadrian's Wall was completed, the Emperor Antoninus Pius ordered a new frontier built further north in Scotland, running approximately 63 kilometres from the Firth of Forth to the Firth of Clyde. The Antonine Wall, completed around AD 142, was built from turf on a stone foundation rather than in stone, with a wide ditch to the north and a road — the Military Way — running behind it. Forts were more closely spaced than on Hadrian's Wall, approximately every three kilometres.

The Antonine Wall was occupied for only about twenty years before Roman forces withdrew south to Hadrian's Wall in the early 160s, under circumstances that are not fully understood. The turf construction has largely returned to the landscape, surviving as earthwork ridges rather than standing masonry. Sections at Watling Lodge and Rough Castle — where the elaborate series of pits called lilia (lily pits) to the north of the fort are still visible — give the best impression of the original scale.

## The Rhine-Danube Frontier: The Limes

The longest continuous Roman frontier earthwork was the Upper German-Raetian Limes, running roughly 550 kilometres from Rheinbrohl on the Rhine to Eining on the Danube, demarcating the empire from the peoples of the German interior. At its most complex, the Limes comprised a wooden palisade or stone wall, watchtowers spaced roughly every kilometre, and a road running behind the frontier line connecting forts and smaller fortlets.

The Limes was not a continuous fortification intended to stop armies; its watchtowers were surveillance and signal stations. A force approaching the frontier would have been visible from several towers simultaneously, allowing rapid communication back to the nearest fort. The response — a cavalry patrol, a infantry cohort, a diplomatic negotiation — depended on the nature of the incursion.

The Obergermanisch-Raetischer Limes was inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2005, along with Hadrian's Wall and the Antonine Wall as part of the wider Frontiers of the Roman Empire World Heritage Site. The Saalburg fort near Bad Homburg in Hesse, reconstructed in the early 20th century on its original footprint under Kaiser Wilhelm II, gives the clearest impression of a working auxiliary fort's internal layout, though the reconstruction is more complete — and more optimistic — than the evidence strictly supports.

## The Eastern Frontier: Deserts and Client Kingdoms

The eastern frontier was defined not by a continuous wall but by a network of roads, forts, and client states managing the transition between the Roman world and the Parthian and later Sasanian empires. The Strata Diocletiana, built under Diocletian around AD 300, ran from Sura on the Euphrates south through Palmyra to the Gulf of Aqaba, connecting a chain of forts across the Syrian desert. The purpose was less to stop Parthian armies — no wall could do that across a desert — than to control nomadic movement, protect cultivated land from raiding, and signal Roman administrative presence.

At Dura-Europos on the Euphrates, excavated jointly by Franco-American teams in the 1920s–30s, extraordinary preservation conditions (the site was buried under sand after its destruction by the Sasanians around AD 256–257) produced wall paintings, papyrus documents, parchment, leather armour, and organic materials rarely preserved on Roman sites. The synagogue at Dura-Europos preserves the oldest known cycle of figured biblical paintings, covering the entire interior with narrative scenes from the Hebrew Bible, dating from the 3rd century AD.

## What Survives and How to Visit

Hadrian's Wall is the best-preserved and most accessible Roman frontier monument. The central section between Hexham and Haltwhistle in Northumberland runs along the dramatic ridgeline of the Whin Sill, with the wall surviving to several metres in height at Walltown Crags and Peel Gap. The Hadrian's Wall Path National Trail runs the full 118 kilometres from sea to sea. The fort site at Vindolanda, just south of the wall, has produced exceptional organic preservation — wooden writing tablets recording personal letters, supply requests, and a birthday party invitation, now housed in the British Museum — and active excavations continue annually.

Vietnam's archaeological and historical heritage spans more than three millennia of complex, layered civilisation. The northern plains saw the development of the Dong Son culture in the first millennium BC — famous for its elaborately decorated bronze drums — followed by Chinese domination lasting nearly a thousand years before Vietnamese independence in 938 AD. Simultaneously, in the centre and south, the Cham civilisation built a remarkable string of Hindu and later Buddhist temple complexes over thirteen centuries, leaving the finest collection of Indianised monumental architecture in mainland Southeast Asia. Further south, the Khmer Empire extended into the Mekong delta, and the maritime trade city of Hoi An drew merchants from China, Japan, and Europe through the 17th and 18th centuries. Vietnam's most recent dynasty, the Nguyen, built an imperial capital at Hue in the early 19th century that combined Chinese and Southeast Asian models in a walled enclosure of considerable grandeur. Each of these traditions has left substantial physical remains, making Vietnam one of the most rewarding destinations for archaeological tourism in Southeast Asia.

## 1. My Son, Quang Nam Province

My Son is the most important Cham temple complex in Vietnam, a valley sanctuary in the mountains of central Vietnam that served as the religious capital of successive Cham kingdoms from roughly the 4th to the 13th century AD. At its peak, the valley contained more than seventy brick tower-temples dedicated to Shiva and other Hindu deities, built and modified by generations of Cham kings who styled themselves as divine rulers in the South Asian tradition. The towers are built in fired brick with carved sandstone detailing — kala masks, apsaras, devatas, and narrative relief panels — in a style that drew on Indian iconography while developing a distinctly Cham vocabulary. American bombing during the Vietnam War destroyed many towers; those that survive range from the small but almost intact Group A towers to the large but heavily ruined Group B. My Son is a UNESCO World Heritage Site. Access is from the city of Da Nang or the town of Hoi An.

## 2. Imperial Citadel of Thang Long, Hanoi

The Imperial Citadel of Thang Long in Hanoi was the political centre of Vietnam for nearly a thousand years, from its establishment by the Ly dynasty in 1010 on the site of a Chinese Tang-dynasty fortress through the 18th century. The citadel complex was built, destroyed, rebuilt, and modified by successive Vietnamese dynasties — Ly, Tran, Le, Mac, and Nguyen — and Chinese occupiers, creating a stratigraphy of extraordinary depth. Excavations since 2003 have revealed a sequence of palace buildings, drainage systems, ceramic workshops, and luxury goods spanning ten centuries. The surviving aboveground monuments include the Doan Mon (southern gate), the Hau Lau pavilion, and the Flag Tower — a tall three-staged masonry structure built in 1812 that is one of Hanoi's most recognisable landmarks. The site is a UNESCO World Heritage Site located in central Hanoi.

## 3. Citadel of the Ho Dynasty, Thanh Hoa Province

The Citadel of the Ho Dynasty is the most impressive surviving example of 14th-century Vietnamese military architecture. Built between 1397 and 1400 by Ho Quy Ly — who briefly supplanted the Tran dynasty before Chinese conquest ended his reign — the citadel is constructed from massive blocks of green schist, some weighing 10–20 tonnes, cut and fitted without mortar in a technique that has no close parallel in earlier Vietnamese construction. The four gates, especially the southern gate whose four-story stone tower survives to a considerable height, and the curtain walls enclosing a rectangular precinct of roughly 600 by 900 metres, give the impression of extraordinary permanence and ambition. The citadel is a UNESCO World Heritage Site near Vinh Loc in Thanh Hoa; it receives relatively few visitors despite its outstanding quality.

## 4. Co Loa Citadel, Hanoi Region

Co Loa — 'snail citadel' — is the oldest known citadel in Vietnam, the capital of the Au Lac kingdom under King An Duong Vuong in the 3rd century BC. The site consists of three concentric earthen rampart rings surrounding an inner citadel mound, with moats dug between them fed from a tributary of the Red River. The outermost rampart encloses roughly 600 hectares; the inner citadel mound rises several metres above the plain. Legend, archaeology, and documentary sources agree that this was the first planned fortified capital in Vietnamese history. Recent excavation has found large quantities of Dong Son bronze arrowheads — thousands in a single deposit — that may relate to the citadel's legendary defence. Co Loa is about 20 kilometres north of central Hanoi and is accessible by motorbike or taxi.

## 5. Hoi An Ancient Town, Quang Nam Province

Hoi An was the most important maritime trading port in 17th and 18th-century Southeast Asia, a city where Japanese, Chinese, Dutch, Portuguese, and local Vietnamese merchants conducted business in adjacent quarters of a compact riverside town. The architectural result is unique: a streetscape of merchant houses combining Japanese, Chinese, and Vietnamese forms — deep narrow shophouses with interior courtyards, timber structures with ceramic-tiled roofs, covered bridges, and clan assembly halls — that survived the subsequent centuries of commercial decline largely intact. The Japanese Covered Bridge, probably dating to the late 16th or early 17th century, is the most photographed structure. Hoi An is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and is among the most popular tourist destinations in Vietnam, easily reached from Da Nang.

## 6. Cham Museum, Da Nang, and Cham Sculpture

The Museum of Cham Sculpture in Da Nang is not a ruin but the custodian of the most important collection of Cham art in the world — over 300 original sandstone and terracotta sculptures recovered from Cham sites across central Vietnam from the 4th to the 15th century. The museum building, dating to 1915, was specifically designed by French colonial architects to display the material from their Cham excavations. The Tra Kieu (ancient Simhapura) hall contains altar pedestals and narrative reliefs of extraordinary refinement; the Dong Duong room holds Buddhist Cham sculpture from a 9th-century monastery; the My Son room shows the development of Cham temple decoration across five centuries. Anyone visiting My Son should spend time here before or after.

## 7. Oc Eo, Mekong Delta, An Giang Province

Oc Eo was the principal port city of the Funan kingdom, an early Indianised state that controlled the Mekong delta and maritime trade routes through Southeast Asia from roughly the 1st to the 6th century AD. French archaeologist Louis Malleret conducted large-scale excavations in the 1940s, revealing a grid-plan city with an extensive canal network, a large moat, and buildings constructed on wooden piles above the swampy delta ground. The finds included Roman coins, Indian gold jewellery, Chinese mirrors, and objects from as far as Persia — a remarkable testimony to Oc Eo's role in the early Indian Ocean trading world. The site is in An Giang province in the Mekong delta; limited infrastructure exists for visitors, but the site has national monument status.

## 8. Dong Son Culture Site, Thanh Hoa Province

The eponymous site of Dong Son near Thanh Hoa, excavated by French and Vietnamese archaeologists beginning in 1924, gave its name to the Dong Son culture (roughly 700–100 BC) — the Iron Age civilisation of the Red River delta whose most characteristic product is the ornate cast-bronze drum. These drums, decorated with geometric friezes, processions of animals, and scenes of ritual life, were prestige objects traded across Southeast Asia and are found as far afield as Indonesia and southern China. The village of Dong Son preserves the original excavation area and a small museum. The National History Museum in Hanoi holds the finest collection of Dong Son bronzes, including the celebrated Ngoc Lu drum.

## 9. Po Nagar (Thap Ba), Nha Trang

The Po Nagar temple complex on a hilltop above the Cai River estuary at Nha Trang is a Cham sanctuary dedicated to the goddess Po Nagar (Lady of the Realm), a female deity assimilating both Hindu and Cham indigenous religious traditions. The site has been in continuous religious use for roughly 2,000 years — earlier wooden temples preceded the brick towers visible today. Of the four surviving towers, the main tower dating to around 817 AD is the most impressive, with a tall curved sikara roof and carved doorway jambs depicting deities and devotees. A later Cham-style tower was added in the 10th century. The site is actively used for both Cham religious ceremonies and Vietnamese folk worship and is accessible in the centre of Nha Trang.

## 10. Ancient Capital of Hue, Thua Thien Hue Province

The imperial city of Hue was the capital of the Nguyen dynasty from 1802 to 1945 and the last imperial capital of Vietnam. The Citadel, modelled partly on Beijing's Forbidden City but adapted to Vietnamese spatial concepts, encloses roughly 10 square kilometres with moats, rampart walls, and a Purple Forbidden City at its core. The complex was severely damaged during the 1968 Tet Offensive and again later in the war, but extensive reconstruction and conservation work since the 1990s has restored many structures. The Nguyen royal tombs — seven elaborate complexes in the hills south and west of the city, each a composition of lakes, pavilions, gardens, and burial chambers — are among the most beautifully designed funerary landscapes in Asia. Hue is a UNESCO World Heritage Site.

## Planning Your Visit

Vietnam's UNESCO World Heritage Sites — My Son, Hoi An, Hue, the Imperial Citadel of Thang Long, and the Ho Dynasty Citadel — are all accessible with well-developed tourist infrastructure. Hoi An and My Son are logistically linked; the Ho Dynasty Citadel is an easy side trip if travelling between Hue and Hanoi. Co Loa is a half-day excursion from Hanoi. The best season for central and northern Vietnam is October to April, avoiding the heavy typhoon season. The south is drier from November to April.

Lebanon's coastal strip and its mountain hinterland form one of the oldest continuously inhabited corridors on earth. The Phoenician city-states that dominated Mediterranean trade from roughly 1200 to 300 BCE were based here — Byblos, Sidon, Tyre — and their legacy in writing, glassmaking, purple dye, and shipbuilding shaped the ancient world in ways that persist. Over these Phoenician foundations came the Macedonian conquest, Roman colonisation, Byzantine Christianity, the Umayyad Caliphate, the Crusades, and Ottoman rule. What survives archaeologically reflects that layering: a Roman temple complex that dwarfs anything in Rome itself, a Phoenician harbour city whose stratigraphy descends eight thousand years, and an Umayyad palace city that lasted barely eighty years before earthquake and abandonment preserved it under the Bekaa Valley floor. The five sites that follow are Lebanon's most significant and accessible.

## 1. Baalbek, Bekaa Valley

Baalbek contains the largest and most imposing Roman temple complex in the world. The sanctuary was built on a Phoenician sacred precinct and developed by the Romans from the first century BCE onward. The Temple of Jupiter Heliopolitanus — never fully completed — stood on a platform of stone blocks so large that the three surviving stones in the western retaining wall, each weighing around 800 tonnes, are among the largest dressed stones used in any building in history. Six Corinthian columns of the temple still stand, each over 20 metres tall. Alongside it, the smaller and better-preserved Temple of Bacchus (actually probably dedicated to Mercury), from around the second century CE, has almost its complete colonnade and an ornate doorway with a coffered ceiling arch intact. A UNESCO World Heritage Site. The site is in the Bekaa Valley, reachable from Beirut in roughly 90 minutes by road.

## 2. Byblos (Jbeil), North Lebanon

Byblos claims continuous occupation from around 7000 BCE, making it one of the oldest cities in the world. Neolithic and Chalcolithic levels have been excavated beneath the medieval Crusader castle that dominates the site. Phoenician temples from the third and second millennia BCE, an Egyptian-period obelisk temple, a Bronze Age necropolis with shaft tombs and sarcophagi, Hellenistic and Roman street grids, and a Byzantine-period baptistery all occupy the excavated area. The Crusader castle itself is built entirely of Roman column drums and ashlar blocks recycled from earlier phases. The papyrus trade that passed through Byblos gave the Greek word for book (biblos) and ultimately 'Bible'. The ancient harbour is still in use as a fishing port. A UNESCO World Heritage Site.

## 3. Tyre (Sour), South Lebanon

Tyre was the leading Phoenician maritime city from roughly the tenth to the sixth century BCE, controlling trade networks across the Mediterranean and founding colonies including Carthage. Alexander the Great besieged the island city in 332 BCE, building a causeway to reach it that has since silted into the isthmus that joins Tyre to the mainland today. The Roman-period remains on the isthmus include the most extensive ancient hippodrome still standing anywhere — nearly 500 metres long, with track, spina, and turning posts visible — along with a colonnaded street, an arch, and a necropolis. Beneath the modern city lie Phoenician levels that remain largely unexcavated due to the active urban settlement above them. A UNESCO World Heritage Site. Access is straightforward from Beirut; the site is managed as an archaeological park.

## 4. Anjar, Bekaa Valley

Anjar is unique: an Umayyad palace city built from scratch in the early eighth century CE, probably by Caliph Walid I, and abandoned after the Abbasid revolution of 750 CE largely destroyed the Umayyad dynasty. The city was never rebuilt, which means its original plan survives intact beneath the debris. Two main colonnaded streets cross at a tetrapylon in the Roman tradition, dividing the walled city into four quarters: a large palace with mosaic floors, a small palace, a mosque, baths, and merchant buildings. The columns reuse earlier Roman and Byzantine material. The mix of Roman city planning with Islamic palace architecture in a single short-lived settlement makes Anjar a singular site for understanding the Umayyad synthesis of inherited and new forms. A UNESCO World Heritage Site. Quiet, well signed, and easy to combine with Baalbek.

## 5. Sidon (Saida) Sea Castle, South Lebanon

The Sidon Sea Castle was built by the Crusaders in 1228 CE on a small island just off the Phoenician-period harbour, connected to the shore by a causeway. It is a compact but well-preserved Frankish fortification with two towers, vaulted interior spaces, and clear views of the ancient harbour. The city of Sidon itself overlies the ancient Phoenician settlement, and ongoing urban archaeology in the modern city has produced extraordinary finds including a Bronze Age glass workshop, Phoenician inscriptions, and Persian-period tombs. The National Museum of Sidon (within the Murex Khan) displays some of these finds. While access to the deep Phoenician levels is limited, the Sea Castle is freely accessible by the causeway and represents the visible anchor for understanding Sidon's long maritime history.

## 6. Niha Temples, Bekaa Valley

In the hills above the Litani River gorge, two Roman-period temples built roughly in the first and second centuries CE stand in a state of partial preservation on a terrace overlooking the valley. The larger temple was dedicated to a local deity, Hadaranes, whose cult combined Semitic and Roman elements. The smaller temple has better-preserved carved friezes. Neither site is on the main tourist circuit, but the quality of the carved stonework — eagles, bucrania, vine scrolls — and the dramatic mountain setting reward the detour. The temples are accessible by road from Zahle; they represent the kind of rural sanctuary that served agricultural communities of the Bekaa under Roman rule.

## 7. Faqra Ruins, Mount Lebanon

High on the western slope of Mount Lebanon near the ski resort of Faraya, Faqra holds two Roman temples and a large altar tower dated to the reign of Claudius (41–54 CE) by a dedicatory inscription. One temple preserves four columns and part of its entablature. The altar tower, built in dressed limestone ashlar, stands to several metres and is one of the few precisely dated Roman monuments in Lebanon. The mountain setting — snow-covered in winter, dramatic in all seasons — is unusual for a Roman sanctuary, reflecting the extent to which the Romans accommodated pre-existing upland cult sites. An additional large Phoenician tumulus is visible nearby. The site is freely accessible.

## 8. Beaufort Castle (Qal'at Shaqif), South Lebanon

Built by the Crusaders on a spur above the Litani gorge, Beaufort — the 'beautiful fort' of its Frankish name — was one of the most contested positions in the medieval Levant. It changed hands between Crusader, Ayyubid, and Mamluk lords repeatedly between its construction in the twelfth century and the Mamluk final capture in 1268. The stone keep, curtain walls, rock-cut moat, and cisterns survive in substantial condition. It was used as a military position again in the twentieth century, and some modern concrete reinforcement is visible, but the medieval fabric is largely intact. The views down the Litani gorge and toward northern Israel are extraordinary.

## 9. Qadisha Valley Hermit Caves and Monasteries, North Lebanon

The Qadisha (Holy) Valley in the mountains above Bsharri preserves a monastic landscape used by Maronite Christian communities from at least the fourth century CE. Rock-cut hermit cells, cliff-face chapels, and medieval monasteries including Deir Mar Antonios Qozhaya (which claims to hold one of the oldest printing presses in the Middle East, from the sixteenth century) are set into the cliff walls of a valley of extraordinary scenic drama. The Cedars of God forest at the valley's head contains trees several thousand years old. A UNESCO World Heritage Site. The valley is walkable but the paths are steep and require appropriate footwear.

## 10. Tell el-Burak Phoenician Settlement, South Lebanon

Tell el-Burak is a Phoenician tell site south of Sidon currently under excavation by a Lebanese-German team. Occupation layers from the Iron Age through the Hellenistic period have produced substantial domestic architecture — unusually well-preserved mud-brick walls, storage facilities, and olive-oil production equipment — that illuminate everyday Phoenician life rather than the elite contexts (temples, tombs) that dominate most Phoenician sites. The site is not yet a formal visitor attraction, but for those with a specific interest in Phoenician archaeology it represents the current edge of fieldwork. Publication of results is ongoing in specialist literature.

Lebanon's security situation has fluctuated in recent years; check current travel advisories before planning a visit. When conditions are stable, the country is compact enough to visit Baalbek, Byblos, Tyre, and Anjar within two or three days using Beirut as a base. Most sites charge modest entry fees; Anjar and Baalbek are the most straightforward for independent visits.

The Levant — the eastern Mediterranean littoral stretching from the Taurus Mountains south through Syria, Lebanon, Jordan, and into the southern reaches of the Fertile Crescent — is among the most archaeologically dense regions on the planet. Bronze Age city-states, Phoenician harbour towns, Hellenistic metropolises, Roman provincial capitals, and Nabataean caravan kingdoms all flourished here in succession, each leaving a stratigraphy of stone and ceramic that still astonishes excavators. Routes linking Mesopotamia to Egypt, and Arabia to Anatolia, passed through these valleys and desert margins, meaning that wealth, ideas, and armies converged here for millennia. What survives today ranges from the rose-red rock temples of Petra to the ghostly colonnades of Palmyra, from harbour walls built by Phoenician merchants to Roman theatres that still echo with performances. Few corridors on earth compress such a span of human ambition into so small a geography.

## 1. Petra, Southern Jordan

The capital of the Nabataean kingdom from around the 4th century BC until Roman annexation in 106 AD, Petra was carved into the rose-red sandstone of the Jordanian highlands by a people who grew rich controlling the incense trade between Arabia and the Mediterranean. The Siq, a narrow 1.2-kilometre slot canyon, delivers visitors abruptly to the Treasury (Al-Khazneh), a temple facade nearly 40 metres high whose columns, urns, and eagle relioles were cut directly from living rock. Beyond it lies a city of several thousand more rock-cut tombs, a colonnaded street, a Roman-era theatre seating 4,000, and a great temple with a garden court. Petra is a UNESCO World Heritage Site. Access is from the town of Wadi Musa; the site is large enough to require two days for a thorough visit, and temperatures in summer are punishing — spring or autumn is recommended.

## 2. Palmyra, Central Syria

Rising from the Syrian steppe roughly halfway between Damascus and the Euphrates, Palmyra was a Semitic caravan city that reached its apogee in the 1st through 3rd centuries AD as an autonomous client state within the Roman Empire. At its height under Queen Zenobia, who briefly seceded from Rome in 270 AD, it controlled much of the eastern empire. The remains are extraordinary: the Temple of Bel, consecrated in 32 AD, is among the best-preserved Romano-Syrian temples ever built; the Great Colonnade stretched nearly a kilometre through the heart of the city; the Agora, theatre, funerary towers, and Camp of Diocletian complete a picture of a wealthy cosmopolitan metropolis. Deliberate destruction by the Islamic State in 2015–2016 damaged the Temple of Bel and demolished the Arch of Triumph. Access and safety conditions have remained difficult since 2011.

## 3. Baalbek, Bekaa Valley, Lebanon

No site in the Levant equals Baalbek for sheer architectural audacity. The Romans built their provincial sanctuary here — called Heliopolis, city of the sun — on a massive artificial platform whose podium stones include three blocks each weighing around 800 tonnes, the largest dressed stones incorporated into any ancient structure anywhere. The Temple of Jupiter, begun under Augustus and embellished through the 3rd century AD, was one of the largest temples in the Roman world; six of its 54 columns still stand at more than 20 metres. The adjacent Temple of Bacchus, smaller but almost perfectly preserved, is a masterpiece of Antonine-era decoration. Baalbek has been a UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1984. The site is in Lebanese government-controlled territory in the Bekaa Valley; access is generally straightforward from Beirut, roughly 85 kilometres away.

## 4. Byblos (Jbeil), Lebanon

Byblos has a reasonable claim to being one of the longest continuously inhabited towns on earth, with occupation stretching back to at least the Neolithic and intensive settlement from the Early Bronze Age around 3000 BC. As a Phoenician city it was the principal exporter of cedar wood to Egypt and the source of the Greek word for book — byblos, from the papyrus traded through its harbour. The excavated tell reveals strata from Neolithic pit-dwellers through Bronze Age temples (the Temple of Baalat Gebal dates to around 2700 BC), an Egyptian-era obelisk temple, Persian-period necropoleis, and a Crusader castle built directly over the Roman cardo. The site is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and is easily visited from Beirut in a half-day.

## 5. Jerash (Gerasa), Northern Jordan

Jerash is one of the largest and best-preserved Roman provincial cities outside Italy, its grid of colonnaded streets, two theatres, an oval forum, nymphaeum, and triumphal arches largely intact under a thin skin of turf and soil. Founded in the Hellenistic period, it flourished as a member of the Decapolis confederation and reached peak prosperity in the 1st and 2nd centuries AD under Roman rule. The South Theatre seats roughly 3,000 and still hosts performances during the annual Jerash Festival. The Hadrianic Arch, erected to celebrate the emperor's visit in 129 AD, stands almost complete at the city's southern entrance. Jerash is about 50 kilometres north of Amman and is the most visited archaeological site in Jordan after Petra; admission and facilities are well-organised.

## 6. Jerusalem, the Old City, Israel/Palestine

The layered archaeological record within and beneath the Old City of Jerusalem spans more than three millennia of continuous occupation. The City of David — the original Bronze Age and Iron Age settlement on a ridge south of the later city — preserves remains of 10th–8th century BC structures and water tunnels including Hezekiah's Tunnel, 533 metres long, cut around 700 BC to secure the water supply against Assyrian siege. The Western Wall is the surviving retaining structure of Herod the Great's massive 1st-century BC expansion of the Temple Mount. The Old City's four quarters overlay Byzantine churches, Crusader buildings, Mamluk caravanserais, and Ottoman structures. The entire Old City is a UNESCO World Heritage Site. Access requires attention to opening hours and, at certain periods, security conditions.

## 7. Ugarit (Ras Shamra), Northwestern Syria

Ugarit was the most important Bronze Age port city on the Levantine coast, flourishing from around 1450 to 1185 BC as a cosmopolitan trading hub whose merchants corresponded in Akkadian cuneiform with Egypt, Hatti, Cyprus, and the Aegean. Excavations begun by French archaeologists in 1929 have revealed a palace complex of roughly 90 rooms, a royal archive holding thousands of clay tablets, and the earliest known alphabet — the Ugaritic script of 30 cuneiform signs, the ancestor of Phoenician and ultimately of most modern alphabets. The site lies on the Syrian coast near Latakia. Conditions for access have been severely disrupted since 2011.

## 8. Apamea (Afamia), Northwestern Syria

Apamea was a Seleucid foundation of the early 3rd century BC that became one of the great cities of the ancient Near East; at its height in the Roman period it may have housed 500,000 people. Its cardo maximus, stretching nearly 2 kilometres and lined with spirally fluted columns, is one of the most visually striking colonnaded streets in the entire Roman world. A cathedral complex, agora, and odeon supplement the street. The site near Hama in northwestern Syria has been badly affected by illegal excavation during the recent conflict, with satellite imagery documenting catastrophic looting of its subsurface. Access conditions have been uncertain since 2011.

## 9. Beit She'an (Scythopolis), Northern Israel

Beit She'an stands at the junction of the Jordan Valley and the Jezreel Valley, a strategic position occupied almost continuously from the Chalcolithic period through Byzantine times. Egyptian garrison texts and temples from the New Kingdom period (14th–12th centuries BC) sit atop a tell rising above the lower city. Below, excavations have exposed a large Roman and Byzantine Scythopolis — colonnaded streets, a theatre seating 6,000–7,000, a Roman bath, a Byzantine street with a 6th-century mosaic, and a semicircular plaza. A major earthquake in 749 AD levelled much of the city, preserving its final phase in collapsed but identifiable form. The Israeli national park is well maintained with good visitor infrastructure, and the site is easily reached from the city of Beit She'an.

## 10. Lachish, Southern Levant, Israel

Lachish was the second most important city of the Kingdom of Judah after Jerusalem, a large fortified town that Egyptian and later Assyrian campaigns documented in famous relief carvings. The Lachish reliefs in the British Museum depict Sennacherib's siege and capture of the city in 701 BC in extraordinary detail, showing siege ramps, battering rams, deportees, and the systematic destruction of the city gates. Excavations by the University of Tel Aviv have confirmed and amplified the Assyrian account: a massive destruction layer, a counter-ramp built by the defenders, and a mass burial of over 1,500 individuals. A second destruction by Nebuchadnezzar in 587 BC is equally evident. Tel Lachish National Park is accessible from the road between Kiryat Gat and Beit Shemesh, with modest facilities and an interpretive trail.

## Planning Your Visit

The most accessible sites in this survey are Petra, Jerash, and Beit She'an, all with established tourist infrastructure and convenient transport links. Petra and Jerash are best visited in spring (March to May) or autumn (September to November) when temperatures are moderate. Baalbek and Byblos are day-trip range from Beirut. Several Syrian sites — Palmyra, Ugarit, Apamea — remain difficult or impossible to access safely and their condition on the ground continues to change. Jerusalem's Old City is accessible year-round but benefits from advance planning given the density of significant sites.

The ancient ruins of the United States belong to a diverse set of Indigenous cultures whose histories reach back thousands of years before European contact. The Southwest preserves the most architecturally visible remains: cliff dwellings and multi-storey masonry pueblos built by Ancestral Puebloan peoples between roughly 700 and 1300 AD, abandoned during a century of severe drought and social reorganisation and never reoccupied. Farther east, the mound-building cultures of the Mississippi and Ohio valleys raised earthworks on a scale comparable to anything in the ancient world. These are not ruins of a vanished civilisation in the romantic 19th-century sense — their descendants are the Pueblo peoples of New Mexico and Arizona, the Navajo, Hopi, and many other nations who maintain living connections to these sites. ## 1. Mesa Verde, Colorado

Mesa Verde National Park in southwest Colorado preserves the largest concentration of cliff dwellings in North America, built by Ancestral Puebloan people between roughly 550 and 1300 AD. Cliff Palace, the most famous of the cliff dwellings, contains over 150 rooms and 23 kivas (circular ceremonial chambers) set into an alcove in the canyon wall; it was home to perhaps 100–150 people at its height in the late 13th century. Spruce Tree House and Balcony House are also accessible on ranger-led tours. The mesa tops hold hundreds of surface pueblos representing earlier occupation phases. The Ancestral Puebloans departed around 1300 AD, likely moving south to join communities in New Mexico and Arizona. UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1978; most cliff dwellings require advance-booked ranger tours.

## 2. Chaco Canyon, New Mexico

Chaco Canyon, in the high desert of northwest New Mexico, was the ceremonial and organisational centre of the Ancestral Puebloan world between roughly 850 and 1150 AD — the height of what archaeologists call the Chaco Phenomenon. The canyon holds 12 major 'great houses', multi-storey masonry complexes of exceptional construction quality. Pueblo Bonito, the largest, contained around 650 rooms and 40 kivas, standing four or five storeys in places, and was built over several generations. The great houses were connected by an elaborate road system visible from the air; the roads led to over 150 outlier communities across a region spanning hundreds of kilometres. The canyon also preserves petroglyphs, astronomical markers, and evidence of turquoise and macaw feather trade. UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1987; the site requires a long unpaved road approach.

## 3. Cahokia, Illinois

Cahokia, across the Mississippi from modern St. Louis, was the largest pre-Columbian city north of Mexico, reaching its peak around 1050–1100 AD with a population estimated between 10,000 and 20,000. It was the centre of Mississippian culture, which was characterised by platform mounds, a ranked social hierarchy, and a ceremonial complex focused on a flat-topped earthen pyramid. Monks Mound, the largest pre-Columbian earthwork in North America, covers about 5.6 hectares at its base and rises to roughly 30 metres — it required the movement of an estimated 22 million cubic feet of earth. The city was surrounded by a wooden palisade, and the Grand Plaza to its south was kept clear for public gatherings. Cahokia was abandoned by around 1350 AD. UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1982.

## 4. Bandelier, New Mexico

Bandelier National Monument in the Jemez Mountains of northern New Mexico preserves pueblo ruins and cave dwellings built by Ancestral Puebloan and early Pueblo peoples from around 1150 to 1550 AD. The main concentration in Frijoles Canyon includes Tyuonyi, a circular ground-level pueblo once two storeys high with around 400 rooms enclosing a central plaza, and the Talus House ruins with their alcove chambers — carved or naturally formed cavities in the soft volcanic tuff, with hand- and toe-hold ladders providing access. Cave kiva murals survive in fragments. The canyon's year-round stream made it habitable; the inhabitants cultivated crops on the canyon floor and mesa tops. The National Park Service manages the site; a visitor shuttle from the town of White Rock operates during peak season.

## 5. Montezuma Castle, Arizona

Montezuma Castle National Monument near Camp Verde in the Verde Valley of central Arizona preserves a 20-room, five-storey cliff dwelling built and occupied by Sinagua people between roughly 1100 and 1425 AD. The dwelling is set high in a natural limestone alcove some 27 metres above the valley floor — a position offering both security and shade. Despite the name, the site has no connection to the Aztec emperor Montezuma; it was named by European-American settlers who assumed any impressive Native American ruin must be Aztec. A nearby site, Montezuma Well, is a detached unit of the monument featuring a natural limestone sinkhole fed by a freshwater spring. The Sinagua culture was influenced by both Hohokam and Ancestral Puebloan traditions. The main dwelling is not open for interior access but is clearly visible from a short walking path.

## 6. Hovenweep, Colorado/Utah

Hovenweep National Monument straddles the Colorado-Utah border in the canyon country of the Four Corners region, preserving six clusters of towers and pueblos built by Ancestral Puebloan people primarily between 1200 and 1300 AD. The towers are Hovenweep's most distinctive feature — square, circular, oval, and D-shaped masonry structures set at canyon heads and rimrock edges, some apparently aligned to solstice and equinox sunrise positions. Little Ruin Canyon at the Square Tower Unit contains the largest and best-preserved cluster, with the Castle, the Tower, and several smaller structures. The function of the towers is debated: defensive, ceremonial, astronomical, and storage uses have all been proposed. The monument is remote, reached by unpaved roads; visitation is low relative to the site's quality, making for a quiet, contemplative experience.

## 7. Canyon de Chelly, Arizona

Canyon de Chelly National Monument in the Navajo Nation of northeast Arizona contains cliff dwellings and rock art spanning roughly 5,000 years of occupation, from early Basketmaker II people around 2500 BC through Ancestral Puebloan occupation to the present-day Navajo families who still farm and graze sheep in the canyon floor. The White House Ruin, accessible via the only trail open without a Navajo guide, is a two-part pueblo — an upper alcove structure and a lower ground-level building — occupied primarily around 1060–1275 AD. Spider Rock, an 800-foot sandstone spire at the canyon's junction, is central to Navajo oral tradition. The monument is unique in being jointly administered by the National Park Service and the Navajo Nation; almost all interior canyon access requires a licensed Navajo guide.

## 8. Wupatki, Arizona

Wupatki National Monument north of Flagstaff preserves the ruins of large pueblos built and occupied by a mix of Sinagua, Kayenta Ancestral Puebloan, and Cohonina peoples in the 11th and 12th centuries AD, following the 1064 AD eruption of Sunset Crater Volcano. The volcanic ash paradoxically improved soil moisture retention, drawing agricultural settlers to the area. Wupatki Pueblo, the largest structure in the monument, had around 100 rooms and stood three storeys high, becoming one of the largest communities in the region for a period. Unusual features include a ball court (a form otherwise associated with Hohokam and Mesoamerican traditions) and a blowhole geological feature. The monument contains over 2,700 recorded archaeological sites. It is managed by the National Park Service and is typically combined with a Sunset Crater visit.

## 9. Chaco Culture (Pueblo del Arroyo and Kin Kletso)

Beyond Pueblo Bonito, Chaco Canyon holds several other great houses that deserve individual attention. Pueblo del Arroyo, a large C-shaped structure of around 280 rooms, stood adjacent to Pueblo Bonito and shows evidence of late construction in a distinctive tri-wall form. Kin Kletso, a compact rectilinear great house, was among the last constructed in the canyon, around 1125 AD, and shows architectural influences from the Chaco outlier system. The Pueblo Alto complex on the mesa above Pueblo Bonito sits at the convergence of several of the great roads and preserves middens that have yielded evidence of large-scale feasting events — thousands of ceramic vessels smashed in single episodes, suggesting periodic gatherings of people from across the Chacoan world. The backcountry trails to these sites require a permit.

## 10. Poverty Point, Louisiana

Poverty Point, in the Mississippi alluvial valley of northeast Louisiana, is the earliest monumental earthwork complex included here: built between roughly 1700 and 1100 BC by a hunter-gatherer-fisher culture at a time when agriculture was far from universal in North America. The site consists of six concentric C-shaped earthen ridges, totalling about one kilometre in diameter from one end of the arc to the other, combined with several large platform mounds, the largest (Mound A) standing about 22 metres high and requiring an estimated 238,000 cubic metres of soil. No cultivation of domesticated crops is securely established for the site's builders; the complexity of the monument was produced by a mobile population exchanging goods — jasper, copper, galena, soapstone — across hundreds of kilometres. UNESCO World Heritage Site since 2014.

Ancient sites in the United States are managed across a patchwork of jurisdictions: National Park Service, Bureau of Land Management, tribal governments, and state agencies. Federal law (ARPA, 1979; NAGPRA, 1990) governs archaeological work and the treatment of Native American cultural items. Many Southwestern sites require advance reservations for ranger-led tours, particularly at Mesa Verde and Chaco in summer. Spring and autumn are the most comfortable seasons in the Southwest; summer temperatures at canyon sites can exceed 40°C.

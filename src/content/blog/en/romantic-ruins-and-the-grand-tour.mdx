## The Ruins as a New Kind of Experience

The idea that a ruined building could be beautiful, rather than simply sad or useless, is not timeless. It emerged in Europe during the late 17th and 18th centuries as a specific cultural response to the classical past, to Protestant ambivalence about medieval Catholicism, and to a new philosophical interest in the passage of time, the mortality of civilisations, and the emotions — melancholy, awe, sublimity — that decaying monuments could provoke.

Before this shift, ruins were generally understood as practical problems or simple signs of neglect. The Roman ruins visible in many Italian cities were quarried for building material with little sentiment. Medieval chroniclers who described ancient remains tended to frame them in terms of their practical origin story or their association with biblical or classical narratives. The aesthetic response — the pleasure taken specifically in the ruined condition of a structure, the sense that the broken, overgrown, weathered form was more moving than the complete one would have been — required a particular intellectual and emotional moment to become possible.

That moment arrived in the early 18th century, and it transformed how educated Europeans experienced the past.

## The Grand Tour and Italy

The Grand Tour — the extended journey through France, Italy and sometimes further destinations in Europe undertaken by young British and Northern European men of means, typically between their late teens and mid-twenties — was well established by the early 18th century. Its educational purpose was to complete the formation of a gentleman: exposure to the art, architecture, antiquities and manners of the continent, supplemented by the acquisition of classical languages, taste and useful connections. Italy was its heart.

In Rome, the young tourist encountered ruins on an overwhelming scale. The Colosseum, the Pantheon, the fragments of the Forum, the imperial baths and the triumphal arches were not museum pieces but living presences in the city, incorporated into subsequent structures, used as quarries, lived in by the poor, and grazed by cattle. Giovanni Battista Piranesi's engraving series Le Antichità Romane (1756) and the earlier Vedute di Roma transformed how these ruins were seen across Europe, presenting them at dramatic scale with virtuosic architectural drawing, their grandeur heightened by tiny human figures and deep shadow. Piranesi's Rome was melancholy, vast, and explicitly concerned with the contrast between imperial greatness and present decay.

Tourists also visited Pompeii and Herculaneum, where excavations had been underway since the 1730s and 1740s. The experience of seeing rooms frozen at the moment of the 79 AD eruption — furniture in place, painted walls intact, meals interrupted — was unlike anything available elsewhere. The Bourbon court at Naples controlled access jealously, which added to the site's allure. Goethe, visiting in 1787, recorded in his Italian Journey the impression that no other catastrophe had been as instructive for posterity, though he also noted the haphazard nature of the excavations and the tendency to remove objects to the royal museum without systematic recording.

## The Picturesque and the Sublime

Two aesthetic categories shaped 18th-century responses to ruins: the Picturesque and the Sublime. The Picturesque, theorised by William Gilpin in a series of influential travel books from the 1770s onward, identified a quality of visual interest — roughness, variety, the interplay of vegetation and masonry, the accidental compositions of decay — that made certain landscapes and buildings particularly suitable as subjects for painting. A ruined abbey covered in ivy, its walls broken to irregular profiles against the sky, was Picturesque in a way that the intact building, uniform and predictable, was not. Gilpin's tours of Britain popularised routes to the Wye Valley (with Tintern Abbey), the Lake District and the Scottish Highlands, establishing a vocabulary of appreciation that is still recognisable.

The Sublime, associated primarily with Edmund Burke's 1757 treatise A Philosophical Enquiry into the Origin of our Ideas of the Sublime and Beautiful, described the emotion provoked by vastness, power and the suggestion of danger — a mixture of awe and terror that Burke saw as more intense than ordinary beauty. Ruins lent themselves to the Sublime because they spoke of overwhelming forces (time, conquest, natural disaster) that reduced human constructions to fragments. The vastness of Rome's remains, the uncanny completeness of Pompeii, the sheer scale of Egyptian monuments encountered by travellers on the Egyptian tour — all of these provoked responses that 18th-century writers tried to articulate in terms of this powerful, unsettling aesthetic category.

## Designed Ruins: The Sham Ruin

The taste for ruins was strong enough that where real ruins were unavailable, they could be manufactured. The fashion for sham ruins — deliberately constructed fragments of towers, arches and walls designed to look ancient and half-collapsed — spread through English landscape gardens from the mid-18th century. Painswick Rococo Garden in Gloucestershire, Stourhead in Wiltshire (where a classical temple sits by a lake created in 1743), and Hagley Hall in Worcestershire (where Sanderson Miller built a ruined castle around 1747) are among the many examples.

The sham ruin was entirely honest in its artifice — everyone knew it was not real — but it served the same emotional purpose as a genuine ruin: the creation of a mood, a prompt for reflection, a reference to the transience of human achievement. The landscape garden as a form depended on the visitor moving through a sequence of designed experiences, and the ruin was one of the most emotionally powerful stations in that sequence.

## Romanticism and the Medieval Ruin

As the 18th century progressed into the Romantic period, medieval ruins acquired the same charged aesthetic significance that classical ones had held for the Grand Tour generation. The dissolution of English monasteries under Henry VIII had left a landscape of spectacular Gothic ruins — Fountains, Rievaulx, Whitby, Tintern — and the Romantic poets and painters made these their central subjects. Tintern Abbey as seen by William Wordsworth in 1798 was not an archaeological curiosity but a prompt for meditation on memory, nature, time and the self. The poem 'Lines Composed a Few Miles above Tintern Abbey' is not really about the abbey at all — it uses the ruined building as a trigger for a sustained reflection on the speaker's own changing relationship to the natural world.

J.M.W. Turner painted Gothic ruins prolifically, from the atmospheric watercolours of Tintern and Buildwas to the sweeping oils of Norham Castle on the Tweed. His ruins are saturated with light and atmosphere, the stone dissolving into the surrounding landscape in ways that emphasised transience and the indifference of natural forces to human construction. John Constable's less dramatic approach — Hadleigh Castle (1829), with its crumbled towers above the Thames estuary — produced images of ruins as melancholy witnesses to personal and historical loss.

## The Legacy

The Romantic fascination with ruins left several practical legacies. It stimulated the preservation movement: sites that had been quarried for centuries began to be protected as aesthetic and historical assets. The formation of bodies like the Society of Antiquaries, the founding of county archaeological societies, and eventually the state's assumption of responsibility for protecting ancient monuments — processes that unfolded across the 19th century — owed something to the emotional value that the Romantic generation had demonstrated ruins could carry.

It also left a persistent aesthetic habit that still shapes how ruins are experienced and presented. The ivy, the dramatic sky, the cow grazing in the nave — these are not accidents but the products of a specific tradition of landscape design and visual sensibility that formed in the 18th century and has never entirely dissipated.

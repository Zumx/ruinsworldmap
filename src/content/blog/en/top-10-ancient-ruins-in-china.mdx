China's archaeological record is among the deepest and most complex in the world, spanning more than a million years of human occupation and five thousand years of literate civilization. The scale of Chinese antiquity is such that any list of ten sites must leave out dozens of equally significant places. What distinguishes many of China's most compelling ruins, however, is their variety: neolithic village sites buried under loess, Bronze Age capitals excavated from deep beneath modern cities, Silk Road oasis towns preserved by desert aridity, mountain fortresses, and imperial gardens deliberately destroyed. Each offers a different window onto a civilization of extraordinary continuity and internal diversity. [Open the map](/map) to explore the full range of Chinese archaeological sites.

## 1. Jiaohe, Xinjiang

Jiaohe (Yarkhoto in Uyghur) stands on a plateau eroded by two rivers near Turfan in the Xinjiang region and is one of the best-preserved ancient city ruins in the world. Occupied from the Han dynasty (around 200 BC) through the Yuan dynasty (13th century AD), the city reached its peak as a major administrative and military centre of the Tang dynasty in the 7th–8th centuries AD. The extreme aridity of the Turfan basin has preserved mudbrick buildings to heights of several metres, with streets, residential quarters, a large Buddhist monastery, administrative compounds, and a temple still clearly legible. The city was abandoned following Mongol raids in the 13th century. UNESCO World Heritage Site as part of the Silk Roads nomination.

## 2. Gaochang, Xinjiang

Gaochang (Khocho) was the capital of several successive states — including the Kingdom of Gaochang and later the Uyghur Kingdom of Qocho — from the 2nd century AD to the 14th century. Its substantial mudbrick ruins cover an area of roughly 2 square kilometres on the Turfan Depression floor, surrounded by a triple circuit of rammed-earth walls. The city preserves identifiable palace and administrative precincts, a large Buddhist monastery complex in the outer city, and the remnants of a small sanctuary that still contains fragmentary wall paintings. The Xuanzang Memorial Temple commemorates the Chinese Buddhist pilgrim's visit to Gaochang in 629 AD on his way to India. UNESCO World Heritage Site.

## 3. Yin Xu, Henan Province

Yin Xu, near Anyang in Henan Province, is the site of the last capital of the Shang dynasty, occupied from roughly 1300 to 1046 BC. The discovery of oracle bones here in the late 19th century — animal scapulae and turtle plastrons inscribed with the earliest form of Chinese writing — provided the first material evidence that the Shang dynasty was historical rather than legendary. Excavations begun in 1928 have revealed palace and temple foundations arranged on a north-south axis, royal tombs in the Xibeigang cemetery containing human and animal sacrifices of remarkable scale, and extensive craft-production areas for bronze, bone, and jade. The site museum at Yin Xu displays original finds including bronze ritual vessels and jade figurines. UNESCO World Heritage Site.

## 4. Yuanmingyuan (Old Summer Palace), Beijing

Yuanmingyuan was a vast imperial garden complex northwest of Beijing, built and expanded by the Qing emperors from around 1709 onward to cover roughly 350 hectares. The complex combined Chinese landscaping traditions with European-style Baroque palaces and fountains designed by Jesuit missionaries. In October 1860, during the Second Opium War, British and French forces looted the complex and then burned it over two days — a destruction that has remained a powerful symbol of national humiliation in Chinese historical memory. The ruins of the European section, with their tumbled colonnades and carved stone fragments, have been preserved as a site of patriotic education. A partial reconstruction of some garden areas is open to the public.

## 5. Loulan, Xinjiang

Loulan is a ghost city on the desiccated shore of what was once Lop Nor lake in the Tarim Basin. Occupied as a major Silk Road outpost during the Han dynasty (2nd century BC to 4th century AD), the city was abandoned as the lake retreated and the climate dried. The Swedish explorer Sven Hedin rediscovered the site in 1900, and subsequent expeditions found wooden structures, textiles, and documents in Kharosthi and Chinese that shed extraordinary light on Silk Road commerce and administration. The mummified bodies found in the broader Tarim Basin region, including the famous Tarim mummies with their unexpectedly non-East Asian physical characteristics, have generated significant scholarship on Bronze Age population movements. Access is difficult and requires permits.

## 6. Great Wall at Jiankou, Beijing Municipality

While the Great Wall is a composite structure built and rebuilt across multiple dynasties — the most visited sections near Beijing date primarily to the Ming dynasty (1368–1644 AD) — the unrestored stretch at Jiankou offers the most dramatic ruined experience. The wall here follows the sharp ridge of a mountain above a valley north of Huairou, its unrepaired battlements crumbling where trees have grown through the masonry. Jiankou was built in the Wanli period of the Ming dynasty (late 16th century) and its steep gradients and technical complexity make it one of the most impressive engineering achievements of the wall system. The section was deliberately left unrestored to preserve its atmospheric decay.

## 7. Sanxingdui, Sichuan Province

Sanxingdui, near Guanghan in Sichuan, has yielded one of the most extraordinary and perplexing discoveries in Chinese archaeology. The site was a major city of the ancient Shu culture from roughly 2000 to 1200 BC, and excavations of two ritual pits in 1986 — and a further six pits from 2020 onward — have produced thousands of bronze objects unlike anything previously known from Chinese archaeology: life-size bronze human heads with exaggerated features and gold foil masks, a standing bronze figure nearly 2.6 metres tall, bronze trees adorned with birds, and jade and ivory objects. The Sanxingdui culture shows both connections with and significant independence from the concurrent Shang civilization in the Yellow River valley. The new Sanxingdui Museum, opened in 2023, is outstanding.

## 8. Jiangzhai, Shaanxi Province

Jiangzhai, near Xi'an in Shaanxi Province, is one of the best-excavated Neolithic settlements in China, dating to the Yangshao culture (roughly 4500–3000 BC). The site covers about 55,000 square metres and its excavation in the 1970s and 1980s revealed a complete village plan: a central plaza surrounded by five clusters of residential houses, with communal storage facilities, workshops for pottery and other crafts, and a surrounding ditch that served both drainage and defensive functions. The site demonstrates the organized, planned character of early Chinese agricultural communities and has been a key reference point for understanding the social organization of Neolithic China.

## 9. Liangzhu, Zhejiang Province

Liangzhu, near Hangzhou in Zhejiang Province, was the centre of the Liangzhu culture that flourished from roughly 3300 to 2300 BC — one of the most sophisticated Neolithic cultures in East Asia. The culture is defined by exceptionally fine jade objects, particularly cong (tubular vessels) and bi (disc-shaped objects) carved with intricate designs, and by a complex site hierarchy suggesting a ranked society with specialist craftspeople. Recent excavations have revealed a walled city covering over 300 hectares, with a palace area on an artificial platform, elaborate water-control systems, and a cemetery whose offerings distinguish an elite class with considerable clarity. UNESCO World Heritage Site since 2019.

## 10. Mogao Caves, Gansu Province

Mogao, also known as the Caves of the Thousand Buddhas, is a complex of 492 decorated cave shrines carved into a sandstone cliff at Dunhuang in Gansu Province. Begun in 366 AD and continuously added to through the Yuan dynasty (14th century AD), the caves preserve an unparalleled sequence of Buddhist mural paintings and sculptures spanning nearly a thousand years of Chinese and Central Asian art. Dunhuang was a major staging post on the Silk Road, and the cave paintings reflect a cosmopolitan culture absorbing influences from India, Iran, Central Asia, and China. Cave 17, sealed around 1000 AD, contained the Dunhuang manuscripts — some 40,000 documents in Chinese, Tibetan, Sanskrit, and other languages that are among the most important texts discovered in the 20th century. UNESCO World Heritage Site.

The best time to visit China's desert Silk Road sites is spring (April–May) or autumn (September–October), when temperatures are moderate. Yin Xu and Liangzhu have excellent site museums and are open year-round. Loulan is a restricted military zone; organized expeditions with permits are required.

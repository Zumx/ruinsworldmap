Pakistan contains some of the oldest urban civilisations on earth. The Indus Valley Civilisation, which flourished between roughly 2600 and 1900 BCE, produced cities more populous than contemporary Mesopotamian counterparts, with sophisticated sewage and water management that would not be matched in Europe for millennia. The country's territory also encompassed the Gandharan cultural zone, where Greek, Buddhist, and Persian traditions fused to produce extraordinary sculpture; the ancient trading city of Taxila, which welcomed Alexander the Great and later became a major Buddhist centre; and the vast fortification of Ranikot, which rivals any defensive structure on earth in enclosed area. Pakistan's archaeological wealth is extraordinary and substantially under-visited by international tourists.

## 1. Mohenjo-daro, Sindh

Mohenjo-daro, on the west bank of the Indus River in Sindh, was one of the two largest cities of the Indus Valley Civilisation, flourishing between roughly 2600 and 1900 BCE and covering an area of around 250 hectares. Its name means 'Mound of the Dead Men' in Sindhi. The city was planned on a grid, with streets aligned to cardinal points, brick-built houses with interior bathrooms connected to the world's first known urban sewage system, and a fortified upper citadel containing a great bath, granary, and assembly hall. The city was excavated from the 1920s onward by John Marshall and Ernest Mackay, and the finds — including the famous 'Priest-King' steatite figure, the 'Dancing Girl' bronze, and thousands of inscribed seals — are distributed between Karachi, Lahore, and New Delhi. Mohenjo-daro was inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1980.

## 2. Harappa, Punjab

Harappa, in Punjab's Sahiwal district, gave its name to the Harappan phase of the Indus Valley Civilisation and was the first site of the culture to be systematically excavated, from 1920 onward. Harappa covered roughly 150 hectares at its peak and shows the same grid planning, standardised brick sizes, and sophisticated hydraulic infrastructure as Mohenjo-daro. The site also produced evidence for the Ravi phase of early settlement (around 3300–2800 BCE), predating the mature Harappan city. A significant proportion of the site was destroyed in the 19th century when bricks were quarried for railway ballast, but the surviving mounds and an excellent on-site museum — opened in 1967 and substantially upgraded since — make Harappa one of the most important archaeological parks in South Asia. The site is inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site as part of the serial Mohenjo-daro nomination.

## 3. Taxila, Rawalpindi District

Taxila, near the modern city of Rawalpindi in Punjab, was one of the great ancient cities of the Indian subcontinent, with continuous occupation from roughly the 7th century BCE through the 5th century CE. The city was capital of the Achaemenid province of Gandhara, welcomed Alexander the Great in 326 BCE, flourished under the Mauryan and Indo-Greek kingdoms, and became a major Buddhist centre under the Kushans. Taxila actually encompasses three distinct ancient towns at different locations — Bhir Mound (the oldest), Sirkap (Greco-Bactrian and Indo-Parthian), and Sirsukh (Kushan) — as well as dozens of Buddhist stupas and monasteries in the surrounding valley, including Dharmarajika, Jaulian, and Mohra Muradu. The site museum holds one of the finest Gandharan sculpture collections in the world. Taxila was inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1980.

## 4. Takht-i-Bahi, Khyber Pakhtunkhwa

Takht-i-Bahi, in the Mardan district of Khyber Pakhtunkhwa, is the best-preserved Buddhist monastery complex in Pakistan and one of the finest examples of Gandharan architecture anywhere. Built on a hilltop roughly 150 metres above the surrounding plain — the name means 'Throne of Origins' — the monastery was active from around the 1st century BCE through the 7th century CE, making it one of the longest-occupied monastic sites in Gandhara. The complex comprises a main stupa court, a monastery court with cells arranged around a central quadrangle, assembly halls, and a supplementary stupa court. The architectural masonry, using locally quarried schist, is of high quality. The site yielded numerous Gandharan sculptures when excavated in the late 19th and early 20th centuries. It was inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1980.

## 5. Mehrgarh, Balochistan

Mehrgarh, in the Kachhi plain of Balochistan, is one of the most important Neolithic sites in South Asia, with occupation phases dated from roughly 7000 to 2600 BCE. It provides the earliest known evidence for farming (wheat, barley, cotton) and herding (cattle, sheep, goats) in the region, predating the Indus Valley Civilisation by several millennia and representing the transition from nomadic hunter-gathering to settled agriculture on the subcontinent. Excavations by French and Pakistani teams from 1974 onward have revealed mud-brick storage and residential structures, extensive cemeteries, and a rich material culture including fine pottery, terracotta figurines, and early evidence of bead-making and copper smelting. Mehrgarh is not developed for tourism — the site is remote and lacks visitor infrastructure — but its scientific importance in understanding South Asian prehistory is immense.

## 6. Ranikot Fort, Sindh

Ranikot Fort, in the Kirthar range of Sindh about 90 kilometres north of Sann, is often cited as the world's largest fort by perimeter, with its outer walls enclosing an area of roughly 26 square kilometres and running for around 32 kilometres in total. The origin and date of the fort remain debated; it has been variously attributed to Sassanid Persian, Arab, and Talpur Mir construction, with likely origins in the early Islamic period (8th–9th century CE) and major expansions in the early 19th century. The walls, built from dressed stone, follow the ridgelines of the Kirthar hills in a dramatic landscape of gorges and dry riverbeds. Within the enclosure are the smaller citadels of Miri and Sher Garh and the ruins of settlements. Access requires a long drive on rough tracks from the main Indus highway.

## 7. Mohenjo-daro's Companion Site: Dholavira (context)

Although Dholavira itself is on the Indian side of the border in Gujarat, the Indus Valley Civilisation sites in Sindh and Punjab make more sense when understood as part of a wider urban network. Within Pakistan, the site of Larkana district and the mounds associated with Mohenjo-daro's supporting towns and agricultural hinterland — including Jhukar and Chanhu-daro — provide context for understanding how the mature Harappan urban system was organised. Chanhu-daro, excavated in 1935–36, produced evidence for late Harappan craft workshops and yielded the famous 'inkwell' — a carnelian bead-making toolkit. These secondary sites are rarely visited but fill out the picture of the largest ancient urban civilisation known.

## 8. Lahore Fort (Shahi Qila)

Lahore Fort, on the northern edge of the Walled City of Lahore, stands on a mound with occupation extending to at least the 11th century CE and possibly earlier. The standing fort dates primarily to the Mughal period, with structures from Akbar, Jahangir, and Shah Jahan's reigns, but the underlying mound incorporates earlier Ghaznavid and probably pre-Islamic structural deposits. The famous Sheesh Mahal (Palace of Mirrors), the Naulakha pavilion, and the Alamgiri Gate represent the height of Mughal architecture. The World Heritage inscription covers both the fort and the adjacent Shalimar Gardens. While primarily Mughal rather than ancient, the fort's layered archaeology makes it one of Pakistan's most complex heritage sites and a reference point for the subcontinent's medieval urbanism.

## 9. Kot Diji, Khairpur District

Kot Diji, on a rocky ridge above the eastern plain of Sindh, was occupied from roughly 3300 BCE — before the mature Harappan phase — and provides key evidence for the Early Harappan or 'Kot Dijian' cultural phase that preceded and contributed to the full Indus Valley Civilisation. Excavations in 1955–57 by Fazal Ahmed Khan revealed a stratified sequence with a destruction horizon separating the pre-Harappan from the mature Harappan levels, suggesting a period of disruption in the transition. The site has a well-preserved defensive circuit and is located near the existing 18th-century Kot Diji fort, which makes the area doubly interesting to visit. The finds are in the Karachi National Museum.

## 10. Sirkap (Taxila's Second City), Rawalpindi

Within the broader Taxila World Heritage Site, Sirkap deserves separate attention as an example of a Hellenistic-influenced grid city in South Asia. Laid out in the 2nd century BCE by the Indo-Greek kings following their takeover from the Mauryans, Sirkap was planned on the Hippodamian grid system with a clear main street (roughly 6 metres wide) and parallel side streets, still visible as low earthen ridges across the mound. The excavated main street area reveals a mix of Buddhist stupas, Hindu and Jain shrines, and domestic structures reflecting Sirkap's pluralistic religious character under Indo-Parthian rule. The famous Double-Headed Eagle Stupa from Sirkap, now in the Taxila Museum, is an emblem of the Gandharan cultural synthesis.

Pakistan's major archaeological sites are accessible from Lahore (Harappa, Kot Diji), Rawalpindi/Islamabad (Taxila, Takht-i-Bahi), and Karachi/Sukkur (Mohenjo-daro, Ranikot). Mohenjo-daro has a daily PIA flight from Karachi. The best visiting season is October to March, avoiding the intense summer heat of the Indus plain. Most sites have basic visitor facilities; Taxila and Mohenjo-daro have museums of international standard.

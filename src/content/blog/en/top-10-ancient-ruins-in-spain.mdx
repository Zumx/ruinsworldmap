Spain's ancient heritage is among the richest and most varied in Europe. Iberia attracted settlers and invaders from the east long before Rome arrived: Phoenicians established colonies along the southeastern and southern coasts from around the 9th century BC; Greeks followed with their own trading posts; Carthaginians turned the peninsula into a base for their contest with Rome. When Rome finally consolidated control after the Punic Wars and the brutal Celtiberian campaigns of the 2nd century BC, it inherited an already layered landscape and proceeded to add some of the finest Roman urban and hydraulic engineering outside Italy. Then came the Visigoths, and then the Moors, each leaving their own architectural imprint. Spain's ruins carry all of these strata, and the sheer scale of Roman investment — in cities, roads, aqueducts, and entertainment venues — is visible across the whole peninsula.

## 1. Mérida, Extremadura

The Roman colony of Emerita Augusta, founded in 25 BC for veterans of the Cantabrian Wars, became the capital of the province of Lusitania and one of the most important cities in the western Roman Empire. Modern Mérida preserves an exceptional concentration of Roman monuments: the Roman Theatre, built around 16–15 BC and restored in the Hadrianic period, retains its three-storey scaenae frons facade with marble columns — one of the finest surviving in the world and still used for a summer theatre festival. Adjacent to it stands the Amphitheatre, capable of holding around 15,000 spectators. The National Museum of Roman Art next door is one of the finest specialised archaeology museums in Europe. Also in the city: a circus, a bridge of sixty arches over the Guadiana, and two well-preserved Roman temples. A UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1993.

## 2. Itálica, Andalusia

A few kilometres northwest of Seville, Itálica was founded around 206 BC as one of Rome's first colonies in Hispania, a settlement for veterans of Scipio's campaigns. It is also the birthplace of two emperors: Trajan (born around 53 AD) and Hadrian (born 76 AD). Hadrian enlarged the city significantly, adding a planned new quarter with wide streets, impressive townhouses with mosaic floors, and a vast amphitheatre with an arena floor capable of staging spectacular shows. The amphitheatre, one of the largest in the Roman world with capacity estimated at around 25,000, survives as a dramatic shell. The excavated Hadrianic city shows remarkably intact street grid and mosaic floors. Itálica is managed by the Junta de Andalucía; it is reachable by bus from Seville in about 30 minutes.

## 3. Aqueduct of Segovia, Castile and León

The granite aqueduct that strides across the centre of Segovia is the best-preserved Roman aqueduct in the Iberian Peninsula and one of the most intact in the world. Built probably during the 1st century AD (reign of Domitian or Trajan), it carried water from the Sierra de Guadarrama some 15 kilometres to the city. The surviving section, nearly 800 metres long and standing up to 28 metres at its highest point, is composed of roughly 25,000 granite blocks assembled without mortar. Though the Roman city of Segovia has otherwise largely vanished beneath the medieval and modern city, the aqueduct itself remains structurally sound and in its original position. It is part of the Old Town of Segovia UNESCO World Heritage Site and is freely visible at all times.

## 4. Tarragona, Catalonia

Roman Tarraco was the most important city in the Iberian Peninsula during the early Imperial period, serving as the capital of Hispania Citerior (later Hispania Tarraconensis) and the base of Augustus during his campaigns in northern Iberia. The surviving Roman monuments include an amphitheatre dramatically sited above the sea, a circus whose vaulted substructures survive beneath the old town, the Concilium Provinciae (a provincial forum complex), sections of the city wall incorporating large Republican-period polygonal stones, and a suburban tower tomb. The Archaeological Ensemble of Tarraco is a UNESCO World Heritage Site. The Museu Nacional Arqueològic de Tarragona holds major collections including mosaics, sculpture, and epigraphy. The city is easily reached by train from Barcelona in around one hour.

## 5. Medina Azahara, Andalusia

About eight kilometres west of Córdoba, Medina Azahara was a palatine city — a new capital — built by the Caliph Abd al-Rahman III from around 936 AD as a symbol of Umayyad power and legitimacy in al-Andalus. Covering over 112 hectares on three terraced levels of a hillside, the complex included the caliph's palace, administrative halls, a mosque, gardens, barracks, and workshops. The city was sacked and largely destroyed in civil wars around 1010 AD, barely seventy years after its founding. Systematic excavation began in the early 20th century and continues. The Salón Rico (Rich Hall), with its extraordinarily detailed marble and stucco decoration, has been reconstructed from thousands of fragments and stands as one of the masterpieces of Umayyad art. The site museum opened in 2009. A UNESCO World Heritage Site since 2018.

## 6. Numantia, Castile and León

On a hilltop above the Duero near Soria, Numantia was the Celtiberian city that resisted Rome's legions for over twenty years. When the Roman general Scipio Aemilianus finally took it in 133 BC after an eighteen-month siege, the surviving inhabitants chose mass death over surrender rather than be taken as slaves. The defeat of Numantia became a byword in the Roman world for fierce resistance, and for later Spanish nationalism, a founding myth of resilience. The excavated site shows two phases: the Celtiberian city, with circular and rectangular stone houses, and the Roman siege camps of Scipio that surrounded it. The Museo Numantino in Soria holds the finds, including distinctive polychrome Celtiberian pottery. The hilltop is open daily; the views across the Castilian plateau alone justify the detour.

## 7. Empúries, Catalonia

On the Costa Brava north of Barcelona, Empúries is unique in Spain as an archaeological site showing a Greek city and a Roman city side by side, separated by only a few hundred metres. The Greek colony of Emporion — a name meaning 'trading place' — was established around 575 BC by Phocaean Greeks from Massalia (Marseille). It became a significant port, and the first Roman forces to land in Iberia did so here in 218 BC at the start of the Second Punic War. The Greek sector preserves the agora, sanctuaries, and private houses with mosaic floors. The adjacent Roman town shows a regular street grid, forum, and townhouses. The Museu d'Arqueologia de Catalunya-Empúries is on site. The beach below the ruins is open during summer; the archaeological zone is managed year-round.

## 8. Los Millares, Andalusia

Near Gádor in Almería province, Los Millares is one of the most important Chalcolithic (Copper Age) sites in western Europe, occupied from roughly 3200 to 2200 BC. The settlement was protected by three successive lines of defensive walls with bastions — an extraordinarily sophisticated defensive architecture for its period. Outside the walls lay a necropolis of over 80 passage tombs. The community was probably sustained by metallurgy, exploiting the copper deposits of the Sierra de los Filabres. Los Millares shows that complex, stratified societies with long-distance trade networks existed in Iberia long before any Mediterranean influence. The site is managed by the Junta de Andalucía; access requires a prior appointment with the Museo de Almería.

## 9. Baelo Claudia, Andalusia

On the Atlantic coast near Tarifa, Baelo Claudia was a Roman fish-salting and garum town that flourished from the 2nd century BC through the early centuries AD. Its ruins, spread across a coastal plain backed by low hills, include a forum with temples to Jupiter, Juno, and Minerva, a theatre, a basilica, thermae, and a row of large garum vats (cetariae) near the shore. An earthquake in the 2nd century AD caused significant structural damage from which parts of the town never recovered. The site is exceptionally well preserved partly because it was never densely built over in later periods. A modern visitor centre interprets the garum industry — crucial to understanding Roman Mediterranean food culture. Access from Bolonia village is easy; the adjacent beach is one of the finest on the Atlantic coast.

## 10. Lugo Roman Walls, Galicia

The Roman walls of Lugo (ancient Lucus Augusti) are the only fully intact Roman city circuit in the world — the complete perimeter of roughly 2,100 metres survives to its full height of between eight and twelve metres, with 85 semicircular towers and five of the original gates. Built in the late 3rd century AD using local slate and granite, the walls were maintained and adapted through medieval and modern times, which paradoxically ensured their preservation. A walkway runs along the top of the wall for its entire length, offering a unique promenade. UNESCO listed the walls in 2000. The city itself retains some Roman street alignments and a partially excavated thermal bath. Lugo is in inland Galicia; the nearest large airport is Santiago de Compostela, about 100 kilometres to the southwest.

## Planning Your Visit

Spain's ancient sites are spread across a large country, and regional clustering helps organise a visit. Andalusia rewards a circuit taking in Mérida (technically Extremadura), Itálica, Baelo Claudia, Medina Azahara, and Los Millares. Catalonia offers Tarragona and Empúries on a single coastal trip. Segovia and Numantia lie on a Castilian plateau circuit reachable from Madrid. Most state-managed sites charge modest admission. The best visiting season is spring (March to May) and autumn (September to November); midsummer heat in Andalusia can be fierce.

No country in Europe holds a denser concentration of ancient ruins than Italy. The peninsula was the stage for civilisations that shaped the Western world: Etruscans who built sophisticated necropolises while Rome was still a cluster of hilltop villages; Greek colonists who raised temples in Campania and Sicily that rival anything on the Greek mainland; and the Romans themselves, whose engineering transformed every province they governed. What survived into the modern era — through earthquake, volcanic burial, medieval spoliation, and centuries of farming — remains astonishing. The ruins described below span roughly a thousand years of building, from the third century BCE to the second century CE, and range from a city frozen by volcanic ash to a palace so vast it was subdivided into a town.

## 1. Colosseum, Rome

The Flavian Amphitheatre, begun under Vespasian around 70 CE and completed under Titus in 80 CE, held between 50,000 and 80,000 spectators for gladiatorial combat, animal hunts, and public executions. The exterior elevation of travertine limestone, tufa, and brick stands to its full original height on the northern arc — a rare survival in Rome's stone-hungry medieval city. The hypogeum, the underground network of corridors and cages beneath the arena floor, is now open to visitors and makes the logistics of Roman spectacle immediately legible. A UNESCO World Heritage Site as part of Rome's historic centre. Pre-booked timed entry is essential; queues without booking can be several hours.

## 2. Roman Forum (Foro Romano), Rome

The Forum was the commercial, religious, and political centre of Republican and Imperial Rome. Today the Sacred Way still runs through it, passing the Temple of Saturn (eight Ionic columns still standing), the Arch of Septimius Severus, the Rostrum where orators addressed crowds, and the House of the Vestal Virgins. The Basilica of Maxentius, completed by Constantine in the early fourth century, gives a sense of how vast Roman civic architecture was — its coffered barrel vaults, though only the north aisle survives, inspired Renaissance architects including Bramante. The Palatine Hill above holds the remains of imperial palaces whose scale gave the English word 'palace' its origin. Combined ticket with the Colosseum is standard.

## 3. Pompeii, Campania

When Vesuvius erupted in 79 CE it buried Pompeii under several metres of volcanic debris, preserving a Roman town in mid-life. Streets, taverns, bakeries with millstones still in place, a forum, two theatres, an amphitheatre, and elaborate houses with wall paintings survive at a level of detail found nowhere else in the ancient world. The plaster casts of victims — formed by pouring plaster into the voids left by decomposed bodies in the ash — remain among the most affecting objects in archaeology. Pompeii is a UNESCO World Heritage Site. The Great Pompeii Project has stabilised many structures over the past decade, and excavations continue in previously unexcavated blocks. The site is large enough to require a full day; morning arrival before the tour groups is strongly advised.

## 4. Herculaneum, Campania

Herculaneum was a smaller, wealthier town than Pompeii, buried by the same eruption but under a flow of volcanic mud that hardened into tuff. The result is exceptional preservation: wooden furniture, carbonised scrolls, upper storeys of buildings, and even food still in serving dishes. The urban layout — insulae, a forum, bathhouses, elegant mosaic floors — is extraordinarily legible from the viewing terraces above the excavation site, which sits well below the modern town of Ercolano. Recent excavation in the ancient beachfront area uncovered hundreds of skeletons of people who died sheltering from the pyroclastic surge. A UNESCO World Heritage Site shared with Pompeii. Smaller crowds than Pompeii make it a more intimate experience.

## 5. Paestum, Campania

Paestum was a Greek colony known as Poseidonia, founded by settlers from Sybaris around 600 BCE. Three Doric temples survive here in a state that surpasses almost anything on mainland Greece: the Temple of Hera (sometimes called the Basilica), the Temple of Neptune, and the Temple of Ceres, all built between roughly 550 and 450 BCE. The site's relative isolation — the malarial marshes that emptied the city in late antiquity kept medieval builders away — accounts for their survival. The museum at the site houses the unique Tomb of the Diver paintings, Greek funerary frescoes from around 470 BCE that are the only complete example of Greek figurative painting in existence. A UNESCO World Heritage Site.

## 6. Ostia Antica, Lazio

Ostia was the port of Rome, a city of around 50,000 people at its height in the second and third centuries CE. Its ruins preserve the street grid, multi-storey apartment blocks (insulae), a theatre, a forum with a capitolium, bathhouses, a synagogue, and dozens of mithraea — underground shrines of the mystery cult of Mithras, popular among merchants and soldiers. Unlike Pompeii, Ostia was gradually abandoned rather than buried, so later occupation has removed most smaller finds, but the urban fabric is more varied and instructive for understanding how a real Roman working town was organised. Accessible by train from Rome in under an hour, it is far less crowded than the sites within the city.

## 7. Villa Adriana (Hadrian's Villa), Tivoli

Hadrian built his imperial estate at Tivoli between roughly 118 and 138 CE as an architectural autobiography: different sections referenced places he had visited and ruled across the empire, from Egypt's Canopus canal to the Stoa Poikile in Athens. The estate covered roughly 120 hectares and included at least thirty major buildings — baths, libraries, a theatre, dining pavilions, a private island retreat accessed by a drawbridge, and an elaborate system of underground service passages. What survives is fragmentary but enormous: enough standing masonry and reflective pools to make the estate feel inhabited rather than abandoned. A UNESCO World Heritage Site. Combine with the Este gardens nearby for a full day in Tivoli.

## 8. Valley of the Temples (Valle dei Templi), Agrigento, Sicily

Akragas — Roman Agrigentum, modern Agrigento — was founded by Greek colonists from Gela around 580 BCE and became one of the wealthiest cities in the Greek world. Its building programme produced a series of Doric temples along the ridge above the valley, seven of which survive in varying states. The Temple of Concordia, built around 430 BCE and converted into a Christian church in the sixth century, is one of the best-preserved Greek temples anywhere — its outer colonnade intact, its cella walls largely standing. The Temple of Hera retains its colonnade, the Temple of Heracles is partially re-erected, and the massive unfinished Temple of Zeus, had it been completed, would have been the largest Doric temple in history. A UNESCO World Heritage Site. The site is walkable and especially beautiful at dusk when floodlit.

## 9. Roman Theatres and City at Verona, Veneto

While most visitors to Verona know the Arena — a first-century CE amphitheatre in near-perfect condition that still hosts opera performances for tens of thousands — the Roman theatre on the far bank of the Adige is less visited and almost as impressive. Built in the Augustan period, it is carved into the hillside below the Castel San Pietro and preserves its semicircular seating cavea and stage area. The small museum above the theatre houses stone inscriptions and sculptures. Verona's Roman street grid is still legible in the old town, and the triumphal arch of the Gavi (reconstructed on a nearby site) reflects the prosperity of the early empire. Collectively Verona's historic centre is a UNESCO World Heritage Site.

## 10. Tarquinia and the Etruscan Necropolises, Lazio

Before Rome dominated Italy, the Etruscans built sophisticated city-states on the volcanic plateaus of central Italy. Tarquinia's Necropolis of Monterozzi holds hundreds of painted tomb chambers cut into the tufa rock, dated from around the seventh to the second century BCE. The images inside — banqueting scenes, dancers, athletes, mythological figures — are among the finest surviving examples of ancient Italian painting. The Etruscan National Museum in the town holds carved stone sarcophagi, bronze objects, and the famous Winged Horses terracotta relief from a temple pediment. A UNESCO World Heritage Site. The nearby site of Cerveteri (ancient Caere) preserves a different Etruscan necropolis of massive tumuli — earth-covered stone chambers — and the two sites together give a full picture of Etruscan funerary culture.

---

Italy's archaeological sites vary considerably in access and conservation. Rome's major monuments require advance booking, especially in summer. Southern sites like Paestum and Agrigento are best visited in spring or October when temperatures are moderate and crowds are thinner. The national parks website (parchi.beniculturali.it) manages most state-owned sites and sells combined tickets.

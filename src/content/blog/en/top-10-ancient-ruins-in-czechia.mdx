Czechia occupies the heart of central Europe, and the archaeological layers beneath its fields and forests record a correspondingly central role in the continent's prehistory and early history. Celtic Boii gave the region its Roman name, Boiohaemum — Bohemia. Germanic tribes followed. The Slavic migrations of the 6th and 7th centuries AD brought the ancestors of the Czechs and Moravians. By the 9th century, the Great Moravian Empire had produced a literate, architecturally ambitious society that built stone churches and maintained connections with Byzantium. Bohemian dukes and later kings built hillforts that evolved into royal residences. The density of hillfort sites — over 1,000 documented in Bohemia and Moravia alone — reflects the competitive, fragmented politics of the early medieval centuries. Today, fieldwork by Czech archaeologists and international partnerships continues to reveal the complexity of this overlooked corner of European prehistory.

## 1. Mikulčice, South Moravia

Mikulčice, near the Slovak border in South Moravia, was one of the principal centres of the Great Moravian Empire in the 9th century AD. Archaeological excavations since the 1950s have uncovered the remains of a fortified palace complex, twelve stone churches (an exceptional number for this period in central Europe), and thousands of graves containing weapons, jewellery, and Byzantine-influenced metalwork. The site demonstrates the ambition of the Moravian elite to build in stone and adopt Christian ecclesiastical architecture during the reign of princes like Rastislav and later under Cyril and Methodius's missionary influence. The site museum at Mikulčice-Valy is open seasonally, and a landscape interpretation trail connects the major excavated areas across the island settlement in the Morava floodplain.

## 2. Pohansko near Břeclav

Pohansko, on the Dyje River south of Břeclav, is the best-preserved Great Moravian hillfort in Moravia, with its earthwork ramparts still clearly visible as a rectangle enclosing a large interior. Excavations have revealed a large aisled hall interpreted as a palace building, a church with associated cemetery, and numerous structures demonstrating craft production including ironworking and non-ferrous metallurgy. The hillfort appears to have been a high-status administrative centre rather than a purely defensive site. The surrounding woodland preserves the rampart profile unusually well. Pohansko is part of the South Moravian archaeological landscape associated with the Great Moravian Empire and is managed by the Moravian Museum in Brno, which holds the excavated finds.

## 3. Závist, Central Bohemia

Závist, south of Prague on a prominent ridge above the Vltava River, is the largest Celtic oppidum (fortified settlement) in Bohemia, with an enclosed area of roughly 170 hectares. Occupied primarily from around the 4th to 1st centuries BC by the Celtic Boii, Závist was a major political and economic centre in the La Tène period. The most remarkable feature is a monumental rectangular enclosure on the summit, interpreted as a sanctuary, with evidence of high-status metalwork and imports from the Mediterranean world. The earthwork ramparts — constructed in the murus gallicus technique, with internal timber lacing — survive as impressive earthen banks around the hilltop. The site is accessible on foot from the village of Šárka and is marked with an information trail.

## 4. Bibracte-type Oppida: Stradonice and Staré Hradisko

Stradonice in central Bohemia and Staré Hradisko in Moravia are two further significant Celtic oppida that have yielded exceptional quantities of La Tène metalwork, glass, and coins. Stradonice was excavated in the 19th century under difficult conditions, but the volume of material it produced — hundreds of gold coins, glass bracelets, and imported Roman amphorae — demonstrated its role as a major centre of craft production and long-distance trade. Staré Hradisko near Prostějov has been more systematically investigated and shows the characteristic features of late La Tène urban organisation, including defined craft quarters and a grid-like street system. Material from both sites is displayed in the National Museum in Prague and the Moravian Museum in Brno.

## 5. Libušín Hillfort, Central Bohemia

Libušín, west of Kladno in central Bohemia, is an early medieval hillfort associated in Czech legendary tradition with the mythical princess Libuše, who according to the chronicler Cosmas founded Prague. The archaeological reality is that Libušín was a significant Bohemian hillfort of the 9th and 10th centuries, part of the network of strongholds through which the Přemyslid dynasty consolidated control over Bohemia. Excavations have revealed traces of stone and timber buildings inside the rampart, and the hillfort's position on a prominent ridge above the Vltava basin made it a key strategic point in early medieval Bohemia. The site is accessible to walkers and commands fine views across central Bohemia.

## 6. Prague Castle (Hradčany) — Early Medieval Phase

Prague Castle occupies the site of the original Přemyslid hillfort established in the second half of the 9th century, making it one of the oldest continuously occupied political centres in central Europe. The earliest structures beneath the present castle buildings include the rotunda of St. Vitus (founded around 925 AD), remains of which can be visited in the castle's underground circuit. The Romanesque palace built under Soběslav I in the 12th century also survives beneath later Gothic and Baroque accretions. The castle's archaeological undercroft, open to visitors, reveals the stratigraphic history of the site from the earliest Slavic occupation through the medieval development of the Bohemian capital.

## 7. Stará Boleslav, Central Bohemia

Stará Boleslav (Old Boleslav), east of Prague on the Elbe, is the site of the assassination of Prince Václav (Good King Wenceslas) in 935 AD by his brother Boleslav I — the event that launched Václav's transformation into the patron saint of Bohemia. The site preserves a Romanesque basilica built over the probable location of the martyrdom, and archaeological investigations in and around the town have revealed traces of the early Přemyslid stronghold. The adjacent collegiate church contains the venerated image of the Palladium of Bohemia. Stará Boleslav remains an active pilgrimage site and is easily reached from Prague.

## 8. Říp Hill, Central Bohemia

Říp, a solitary basalt hill rising from the plains of central Bohemia northwest of Roudnice nad Labem, holds a mythological significance in Czech tradition as the place from which the legendary ancestor Čech first surveyed the land that would bear his name. The Romanesque rotunda of St. George on the summit, built around 1126 AD, is one of the finest surviving Romanesque buildings in Bohemia. Archaeological surveys on and around the hill have identified evidence of prehistoric and early medieval activity consistent with the hill's long-standing symbolic importance. The hill is accessible via a well-marked path from the village of Říp and is a popular destination for Czechs with an interest in national history.

## 9. Kouřim, Central Bohemia

Kouřim in central Bohemia preserves one of the most significant early medieval hillfort complexes in Bohemia, with evidence of occupation from the late Bronze Age through the early Přemyslid period. The site was an important early Bohemian political centre before being superseded by Prague. Excavations have revealed traces of a church, cemetery, and fortification elements. The open-air Museum of the Bohemian Village at nearby Kouřim presents reconstructed traditional architecture in an attractive landscape setting, though this is distinct from the archaeological hillfort site itself. The two can be combined in a single visit to the Kouřim area.

## 10. Dolní Věstonice and Předmostí, South Moravia

Dolní Věstonice and the nearby site of Předmostí in the Moravian corridor represent the most important concentration of Upper Palaeolithic sites in central Europe, occupied by Gravettian hunter-gatherers roughly 27,000–30,000 years ago. Dolní Věstonice is internationally known as the find site of the Venus of Dolní Věstonice, the oldest known ceramic figurine in the world, and as the location of the earliest evidence of ceramic firing anywhere. The site also produced the famous triple burial of three young individuals. Předmostí yielded thousands of mammoth bones and evidence of large-scale hunting. Both sites are in agricultural fields and have little visible surface archaeology, but the Dolní Věstonice Museum presents the finds and the Anthropos pavilion in Brno provides comprehensive interpretation.

Czechia's archaeological sites are well served by road and by the country's extensive rail network. Most sites in Bohemia are within two hours of Prague; Moravian sites are best accessed from Brno. Summer is the main season for open-air site museums. The National Museum in Prague and the Moravian Museum in Brno hold the major collections.

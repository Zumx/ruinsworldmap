Algeria holds some of the best-preserved Roman towns in the world. The region the Romans called Africa Proconsularis and Numidia was one of the wealthiest and most urbanised parts of the empire, and when Roman power receded, many of its cities were simply abandoned rather than rebuilt and overbuilt by later medieval settlements. The result is an extraordinary array of largely intact Roman street grids, forums, temples, baths, amphitheatres, and triumphal arches rising from the North African landscape, accompanied by pre-Roman Berber monuments and prehistoric rock art of global significance.

## 1. Timgad, Batna Province

Timgad (Roman Thamugadi) was founded as a veteran colony by the emperor Trajan in 100 AD and is perhaps the most perfectly preserved example of a Roman grid-plan city anywhere in the world. The original colonial grid — covering roughly 12 hectares — is still readable in its entirety, with paved cardo and decumanus streets, a forum with the Temple of Jupiter, a substantial theatre, numerous public fountains, a public library, and over a dozen bath complexes. Timgad was a UNESCO World Heritage Site from 1982 and is open year-round with an associated museum.

## 2. Djémila (Cuicul), Sétif Province

Djémila, the ancient Cuicul, was a Roman city founded in the early 2nd century AD on a triangular mountain ridge, and its surviving remains are among the most spectacularly sited in the Roman world. A forum complex, a Capitoline temple, theatre, baths of Caracalla, triumphal arch, and a remarkable 4th-century Christian quarter including a cathedral, baptistery, and bishop's residence are all well preserved. The arrangement of buildings across the ridge, with their relationship to the dramatic landscape, makes Djémila visually unforgettable. It is a UNESCO World Heritage Site.

## 3. Tipasa, Tipaza Province

Tipasa was a Punic trading post that became a Roman town under the emperor Claudius in the 1st century AD, known in the ancient world for its martyr church and later as a Byzantine administrative centre. The ruins spread along a coastal promontory and include the Great Basilica, several other churches, a temple, baths, a theatre, and an amphitheatre, all set among trees above the Mediterranean. Albert Camus wrote movingly about childhood visits here. Tipasa is a UNESCO World Heritage Site.

## 4. Hippo Regius (Annaba), Annaba Province

Hippo Regius was a major Numidian and then Roman city, famous in the ancient Christian world as the see of Augustine of Hippo, the philosopher and theologian who served as its bishop from 395 to 430 AD. The excavated ruins include a forum, the basilica of peace, baths, mosaic floors, and the remains of Augustine's cathedral. A later Christian basilica and a museum with local finds occupy the site. Augustine died here during the Vandal siege of 430 AD.

## 5. Tiddis, Constantine Province

Tiddis was a small Roman town perched on a steep hillside above the Rhummel River gorge north of Constantine, active roughly from the 1st to 4th centuries AD. Despite its modest size, Tiddis is remarkably well preserved precisely because it was too difficult to quarry for building stone after abandonment. Its streets, temples, a mithraeum, necropolis, and water cisterns are all visible. The setting — the town climbs a near-vertical hillside — is unusual even by the standards of Roman provincial urbanism.

## 6. M'Zab Valley, Ghardaïa Province

The M'Zab Valley is a UNESCO World Heritage Site representing a different kind of ancient heritage: a 10th-century Islamic urban tradition preserved in five ksour (fortified towns) including Ghardaïa, Beni Isguen, Melika, Bou Noura, and El Atteuf. Built by the Mozabite Berbers on a pentagonal plan with a mosque at the summit of each settlement, the ksour represent a distinctive urban philosophy adapted to the desert environment. While medieval rather than ancient, they preserve a living architectural tradition that has barely changed over a millennium.

## 7. Cherchell (Caesarea), Tipaza Province

Cherchell was the ancient Caesarea Mauretaniae, the capital of the client kingdom of Mauretania under the Numidian prince Juba II (installed by Augustus around 25 BC) and his wife Cleopatra Selene (daughter of Mark Antony and Cleopatra VII). Juba made Caesarea a cultured Hellenistic city, and the town museum holds remarkable sculpture including fine portrait busts. The visible remains include a theatre, baths, and sections of the harbour, while the museum houses extensive Roman and Hellenistic collections.

## 8. Lambaesis, Batna Province

Lambaesis was the permanent headquarters of the Third Augustan Legion from around 129 AD and is the best-preserved Roman legionary fortress in North Africa. The praetorium (headquarters building), a large courtyard structure with substantial standing walls, survives to considerable height. The camp layout, a triumphal arch bearing Hadrian's address to the troops (the so-called adlocutio inscription), and the surrounding civilian settlement (canabae) all contribute to the site's significance.

## 9. Tassili n'Ajjer, Illizi Province

Tassili n'Ajjer is a vast plateau in the central Sahara containing one of the world's most important concentrations of prehistoric rock art: thousands of engravings and paintings documenting the Sahara's transformation from a well-watered savanna to a desert over the past ten thousand years. The images depict cattle, horses, chariots, human figures, and extinct fauna in a sequence spanning from roughly 10,000 BC to the beginning of the historical period. The site is a UNESCO World Heritage Site, and access requires organised tours from Djanet. The challenging logistics are well rewarded.

## 10. Calama (Guelma), Guelma Province

Calama was a Numidian and then Roman city, the birthplace of the early Christian writer Apuleius (author of the Golden Ass). The site preserves a theatre with partially standing walls, an amphitheatre, a forum, and a triumphal arch in the city centre. The theatre has been restored for use and is one of the few in Algeria where the architectural shell remains largely intact. A small archaeological museum displays finds from the site and the surrounding region.

Algeria's Roman sites are generally accessible and in good condition, with Timgad and Djémila fully open and staffed year-round. The best seasons are spring and autumn; Timgad and Djémila can be combined in a single day trip from Batna. The Tassili n'Ajjer requires an organised expedition from Djanet or Tamanrasset.

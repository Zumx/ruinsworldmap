Slovakia occupies the northern arc of the Carpathian Basin, a zone that has been crossed, settled, and contested by an unusual succession of cultures. Celts built oppida on its river confluences; Rome pushed its Danube frontier through what is now southwestern Slovakia and built auxiliary forts and watchtowers along the river's northern bank; the Avars dominated the plains in the 7th and 8th centuries; and the emergent Great Moravian Empire of the 9th century left behind fortified centres that are only now being fully understood through lidar survey and systematic excavation. Medieval Slovakia was the highland core of the Kingdom of Hungary, and the dramatic castle ruins that crown its ridges often conceal far older structures beneath the medieval stonework. The sites listed here range from the Roman Danube limes to early medieval hillforts, offering a history that is dense, contested, and endlessly rewarding.

## 1. Devín Castle, Near Bratislava

Devín sits at the confluence of the Morava and Danube rivers, a position so strategically obvious that it was occupied in the late Bronze Age, fortified by the Celts in the 3rd and 2nd centuries BC, and then used as a Roman watchtower post. The rock itself rises steeply above both rivers. The castle visible today is largely medieval, built and expanded between the 9th and 17th centuries, but beneath the medieval walls lie the remains of a Great Moravian palatial complex from the 9th century AD — one of the most significant Slavic-period structures known from Central Europe. The Austrians destroyed the castle in 1809; the dramatic shell that survives is now a national cultural monument. The site museum displays finds from all periods; the castle is reachable from Bratislava by boat or bus.

## 2. Spiš Castle, Eastern Slovakia

Spiš Castle — Spišský hrad — dominates the Hornád valley from a volcanic travertine ridge and ranks among the largest castle complexes by area in Central Europe, covering roughly four hectares of enclosed space. The earliest structures date to the 12th century, but archaeological investigation has demonstrated Bronze Age fortification on the ridge, with finds from around 1200 BC. The castle was a seat of the Spiš County administration and later owned by the Zápolya and Thurzó families. Devastated by fire in 1780 and abandoned, it has stood as a romantic ruin ever since. Together with the adjacent ecclesiastical town of Spišské Podhradie and Spišská Kapitula, it forms a UNESCO World Heritage Site inscribed in 1993. Accessible year-round, with a cable car option in season.

## 3. Gerulata, Rusovce (Bratislava)

Gerulata was a Roman auxiliary fort on the Danube limes — the northern frontier of the empire — situated in what is now the Rusovce district of Bratislava. Built during the 1st century AD and occupied through the 4th century, the fort housed cavalry and infantry units guarding a Danube crossing point. Systematic excavation has uncovered the fort's street plan, headquarters building, bath house, and a substantial extra-mural civilian settlement. The Gerulata site museum, operated by the City Museum of Bratislava, displays pottery, inscriptions, weapons, and personal objects recovered from the site. Stone-built structures are partially preserved in situ. Gerulata forms part of the broader Danube Limes UNESCO World Heritage inscription, which entered the tentative list in stages across multiple countries.

## 4. Bojná, Topoltschany District

Bojná is an early medieval archaeological complex of exceptional importance discovered through metal-detector survey and subsequent excavation from the early 2000s onward. The site consists of a hilltop fortified enclosure dating to the Great Moravian period, roughly the 9th century AD, associated with rich finds of Christian metalwork — bronze crosses, silver mounts, and processional fittings — suggesting it served as an elite religious and administrative centre. The so-called Bojná hoards, three separate caches of liturgical objects, are now in the Slovak National Museum. The hilltop earthworks remain visible in the forest. Bojná has substantially revised scholarly understanding of Christianisation and political organisation in Great Moravia. Access is by forest track from the village of Bojná.

## 5. Bíňa (Biňa), Southern Slovakia

Bíňa is a multiperiod site in southern Slovakia that has yielded material from the late Bronze Age through the early medieval period. Its most significant phase is an early medieval earthwork enclosure with associated church remains dating to roughly the 10th and 11th centuries, linked to the Hungarian conquest and settlement of the Carpathian Basin. A Romanesque church still stands in the village, incorporating stonework from an earlier basilica. Burial grounds excavated here have produced weapons, horse harnesses, and jewellery consistent with early Hungarian aristocratic burials. The flat agricultural landscape preserves earthwork traces detectable by aerial photography, and the site is studied as a key reference point for the transition from Great Moravian to early Hungarian settlement patterns in the region.

## 6. Mikulčice-Kopčany, Záhorie Region

Although Mikulčice lies across the border in the Czech Republic, the associated site of Kopčany is entirely on Slovak territory and preserves the oldest standing building in Slovakia — the pre-Romanesque church of St Margaret of Antioch, dated to the 9th century and identified as part of the same Great Moravian princely complex centred on Mikulčice. The church's fabric contains dressed stone coursing consistent with Carolingian-influenced construction. The Záhorie lowland around Kopčany contains multiple barrow mounds and settlement traces from the Moravian period. The church is accessible and services are occasionally held; the earthworks of the adjoining settlement are visible as low ridges across the floodplain meadows bordering the Morava river.

## 7. Trenčín Roman Inscription

The Roman military inscription carved into the sandstone cliff at Trenčín castle is the northernmost known Roman inscription in Central Europe. It records that the II Legion Adiutrix, part of a force of around 855 soldiers, wintered near the Laugaricio camp — identified with the Trenčín locality — during the Marcomannic Wars of the late 2nd century AD, specifically in the reign of Marcus Aurelius or Commodus, around 179 AD. The inscription is preserved on the cliff face beneath the medieval castle walls. It marks the furthest permanent Roman military presence north of the Danube recorded in any written source from the region. The castle above it is medieval but has its own significant history; the inscription is viewable from the castle courtyard.

## 8. Pobedim Hillfort, Váh Valley

Pobedim is a fortified Great Moravian settlement on a promontory above the Váh river, occupied principally during the 9th century AD. Excavations have uncovered the remains of wooden palisade walls, a smithing quarter with iron-smelting furnaces and slag deposits, residential buildings, and an assemblage of agricultural tools, weapons, and personal ornaments. The metalworking evidence at Pobedim is particularly significant for understanding craft specialisation in Moravian-period settlements. The site was partially destroyed by later ploughing but the bank-and-ditch fortification traces are still legible. Finds are held at the Slovak National Museum in Bratislava and at the regional museum in Piešťany. The promontory is accessible by foot from the village.

## 9. Ducové, Romantic Morava Region

Ducové, near Piešťany, preserves the remains of a Great Moravian fortified nobleman's residence — a so-called dvorec — dating to the 9th century AD. The site consists of a rectangular enclosure with a stone-founded residential building and a small rotunda church, the Church of St Margaret, partially preserved to wall-height. Rotunda churches are a characteristic architectural form of the Great Moravian period, and Ducové's example is among the better-preserved examples in Slovakia. The site is interpreted as the estate centre of a regional elite family operating under the Moravian princes. Excavations in the 1960s and 1970s defined the site's extent and chronology. The ruins are signposted from the village and freely accessible.

## 10. Bratislava (Posonium) Roman Canabae

The area beneath and around Bratislava's old town conceals substantial Roman deposits associated with the civilian settlement that grew beside the military installations of the Danube frontier. Roman Posonium or the associated canabae produced finds of pottery, coins, funerary monuments, and building materials spanning the 1st to 4th centuries AD. The most impressive surviving Roman element is a reconstructed stretch of the limes watchtower system visible at the Devín locality. Urban excavations ahead of construction projects regularly expose Roman-period levels. The Slovak National Museum on the Danube embankment displays the richest assemblage of Roman finds from the region, including carved tombstones, bronze figurines, and military equipment.

Slovakia's ancient and early medieval sites are often off the main tourist routes, which means shorter queues, lower entry fees, and a more immediate encounter with the archaeology. Spring and early autumn offer the most comfortable walking conditions. Castle sites including Spiš require some uphill walking; flat-terrain sites like Gerulata are stroller-accessible. The Slovak National Museum in Bratislava serves as an essential first stop for contextualising the sites.

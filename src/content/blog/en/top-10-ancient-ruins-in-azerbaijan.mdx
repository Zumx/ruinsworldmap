Azerbaijan occupies the meeting point of the Caucasus, the Caspian, and the Iranian plateau — a position that made it a corridor for migrations, trade, and conquests from prehistoric times onward. The country's ancient heritage ranges from Palaeolithic rock engravings to medieval Silk Road cities, with Zoroastrian fire temples, early Islamic monuments, and Albanian Christian churches filling the centuries between. Much of this heritage remains little known outside the region, making Azerbaijan an archaeologically rewarding destination for those willing to look beyond its oil-boom modernity.

## 1. Gobustan Rock Art and Mud Volcanoes, Gobustan District

Gobustan is Azerbaijan's most internationally recognised archaeological site and a UNESCO World Heritage Site. The rocky hillside southwest of Baku preserves around 6000 petroglyphs carved over a span of roughly 40,000 years, from the Upper Palaeolithic through the medieval period. The images include boats, bulls, deer, human figures, and hunting scenes, collectively documenting the long human habitation of the Caspian coastline. A Roman military inscription from the 1st century AD (recording the presence of a Roman unit from the Legio XII Fulminata) is among the most surprising finds in the Caucasus. The associated museum provides excellent interpretation.

## 2. Shirvanshah's Palace Complex, Baku

The Palace of the Shirvanshahs in the walled old city (Icheri Sheher) of Baku is a complex of royal buildings, a mosque, a mausoleum, and associated structures built primarily in the 15th century AD by the Shirvan dynasty. The walled old city, which also includes the cylindrical Maiden Tower of disputed purpose and date, constitutes a UNESCO World Heritage Site. The palace ensemble reflects the sophisticated courtly culture of one of the Caucasus's most durable medieval kingdoms.

## 3. Ateshgah Fire Temple, Surakhani

The Ateshgah (House of Fire) near Surakhani, on the Absheron Peninsula east of Baku, was a place of Zoroastrian and later Hindu pilgrimage, established at a site where natural gas seeps from the ground to produce eternal flames — the phenomenon that gave Azerbaijan its medieval name ('Land of Fire'). The surviving complex, built primarily in the 17th and 18th centuries AD by Indian merchants and pilgrims, includes a central flame altar enclosed by a caravanserai-style courtyard of pilgrim cells. The natural flames that inspired the site are now fed by pipes, but the atmosphere of the complex remains evocative.

## 4. Old City of Sheki (Khan's Palace), Shekhi-Zaqatala Region

The Khan's Palace in Sheki, built in the 18th century by the local khan as a summer residence, is famous for the extraordinary stained-glass windows (shebeke) and elaborate painted interior walls that cover virtually every surface with intricate geometric and floral patterns. While dating to the 18th century, the palace represents the pinnacle of a regional artistic tradition with deep historical roots. Sheki's old city, including its caravanserai, is a UNESCO World Heritage Site.

## 5. Shamakhi and the Yeddi Gumbez Mausoleum, Shamakhi District

Shamakhi (ancient Shamakhy) was the capital of the Shirvan state and an important Silk Road city for over a millennium. The city suffered repeated destruction from earthquakes and invasions, limiting what survives of its medieval fabric, but the Yeddi Gumbez (Seven Domes) mausoleum complex of the 18th-century Shirvan khans is well preserved. The Juma Mosque, rebuilt many times, stands on the site of a structure dating to the early Islamic period. The surrounding region contains numerous lesser-known medieval and earlier monuments.

## 6. Gabala (Ancient Qabala), Gabala District

Gabala (ancient Qabala or Kabalaka) was the first capital of Caucasian Albania, the ancient state that occupied what is now northern Azerbaijan and southern Dagestan. The site, consisting of several mounds at the confluence of rivers in the Caucasus foothills, was occupied from roughly the 4th century BC through the medieval period. Excavations have revealed Hellenistic, Roman, and early medieval layers with imported goods reflecting the city's position on trade routes between the Caucasus and the Caspian. A site museum displays finds from the ongoing excavations.

## 7. Mingachevir Archaeological Site, Mingachevir City

Mingachevir on the Kura River has produced one of the richest archaeological sequences in Azerbaijan, with continuous occupation from the Chalcolithic period (roughly 4000 BC) through the early Islamic period. Excavations during the Soviet era (particularly in the 1940s during dam construction) revealed Bronze and Iron Age burials with rich grave goods, Caucasian Albanian buildings and inscriptions, and early medieval Christian and Zoroastrian structures. The local museum holds the results of these excavations.

## 8. Momine Khatun Mausoleum, Nakhchivan

The Momine Khatun Mausoleum in Nakhchivan city, built in 1186 AD by the architect Ajami Nakhchivani for the wife of the Eldiguzid atabeg, is one of the masterpieces of medieval Islamic architecture in the Caucasus. The ten-sided tower, rising around 25 metres, is clad in brick with an intricate geometric and epigraphic carved decoration of extraordinary refinement. Ajami Nakhchivani is regarded as the founder of the Azerbaijani architectural school, and a second mausoleum by him, the Yusuf ibn Kuseyir, stands nearby.

## 9. Alinja Castle, Nakhchivan

Alinja Castle, set on a dramatic basalt crag above the Alinja River valley in Nakhchivan, is one of the most spectacularly positioned medieval fortifications in the Caucasus. The castle, occupied from roughly the 10th to 15th centuries AD, withstood a 14-year siege by the Mongols in the late 13th and early 14th centuries. Remains of towers, walls, cisterns, and internal buildings are scattered across the steep summit. The climb to the castle is demanding but rewarded by sweeping views over the Nakhchivan plain.

## 10. Boyuk Dash (Great Stone) Petroglyphs, Gobustan

Separate from the main Gobustan visitor complex, the Boyuk Dash (Great Stone) area within the Gobustan Reserve contains some of the finest individual petroglyph panels, including large carved boats with rows of oarsmen that have been interpreted by some researchers as Bronze Age seafaring vessels. Thor Heyerdahl, the Norwegian explorer, visited Gobustan in 1994 and argued that these images supported theories of ancient trans-oceanic contact — a connection not accepted by mainstream archaeology, but the petroglyphs themselves are undeniably impressive and among the best-preserved in the reserve.

Azerbaijan's ancient sites are generally accessible and open to visitors, with the Gobustan UNESCO site having excellent facilities and the Baku old city (Icheri Sheher) walkable and well signposted. Travel to sites outside Baku typically requires a car or organised tour. Spring and autumn are the best seasons; summer is hot on the Absheron Peninsula.

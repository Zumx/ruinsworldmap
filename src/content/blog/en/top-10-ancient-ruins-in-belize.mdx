Belize occupies a strip of the eastern Maya lowlands between Mexico and Guatemala, and its dense tropical jungle conceals an extraordinary number of ancient Maya sites. The Maya settled the region well before 1000 BC, and by the Classic period (roughly 250–900 AD) the territory that is now Belize supported a mosaic of competing polities, each centered on a royal court with its pyramid temples, plazas, ball courts, and elaborate stelae recording the deeds of kings in carved glyphs. Though Belize is a small country, it contains more than nine hundred documented archaeological sites, of which only a handful have been systematically excavated and opened to visitors. The combination of impressive ruins and intact rainforest makes Belize one of the most atmospheric destinations for Maya archaeology in the region. [Open the map](/map) to locate all known sites.

## 1. Caracol, Cayo District

Caracol was the most powerful Maya city ever to have stood on what is now Belizean territory, and at its Classic period peak — roughly 550–900 AD — it may have housed more than 100,000 people, making it larger than any modern Belizean city. Caracol defeated the great city of Tikal in 562 AD, a triumph recorded on a stone altar. The site's central pyramid, Caana ('Sky Palace'), rises to around 43 metres and remains the tallest man-made structure in Belize. Caracol's elaborate causeway system connected its major plazas across a landscape shaped by intensive terraced agriculture. Deep in the Chiquibul Forest Reserve, access requires a four-wheel-drive vehicle or light aircraft.

## 2. Xunantunich, Cayo District

Xunantunich stands on a natural limestone ridge overlooking the Mopan River near the Guatemalan border, and its principal pyramid, El Castillo, rises to about 40 metres. Built and elaborated between roughly 600 and 900 AD, the site was an important regional capital during the Late Classic period. El Castillo's western facade preserves a large stucco frieze depicting astronomical and dynastic imagery — one of the finest examples of Classic Maya architectural decoration in Belize. A hand-cranked ferry crosses the river to reach the site, which is the country's second most visited Maya ruin. Views from the summit take in jungle rolling south into Guatemala.

## 3. Lamanai, Orange Walk District

Lamanai, on the western shore of the New River Lagoon, is unusual among Maya sites for having been occupied more or less continuously from around 1500 BC into the post-conquest colonial period. Spanish missionaries built a church here in the 16th century, and the ruins of that church — a roofless stone structure with a Maya mask embedded in its walls — still stand beside the ancient plazas. The site's High Temple (N10-43) dates in its early phases to roughly 100 BC and stands about 33 metres tall; a later pyramid, the Mask Temple, displays two colossal stucco masks of a ruler. Lamanai is most easily reached by boat along the New River from Orange Walk Town.

## 4. Altun Ha, Belize District

Altun Ha, located only about 50 kilometres north of Belize City, is the most accessible of Belize's major Maya sites and the most recognizable — its Temple of the Masonry Altars (B-4) appears on Belizean currency and the label of Belikin beer. The site was a prosperous trading centre between roughly 200 BC and 900 AD, benefiting from its position near the coast. The most remarkable find from Altun Ha is a large jade head representing the Sun God Kinich Ahau, the largest Maya jade carving ever found, now held in the Belize Bank vault. The two main plazas are well-maintained and give a clear sense of a Classic Maya civic centre.

## 5. Cahal Pech, Cayo District

Cahal Pech ('Place of Ticks' in modern Yucatec Maya) sits on a hill overlooking San Ignacio town and the Belize River valley, and it is among the longest-occupied Maya sites in Belize, with evidence of settlement dating back to at least 1200 BC. The site was home to an elite residential and ceremonial complex during the Preclassic and Classic periods, and its compact core of seven plazas, thirty-four structures, and two ball courts preserves a sense of the daily spaces of Maya royal life. The on-site museum displays ceramics and jade finds from excavations. Easy to visit on foot from San Ignacio.

## 6. Cerros, Corozal District

Cerros, on a small peninsula extending into Corozal Bay in northern Belize, was a significant ceremonial centre during the Late Preclassic period, roughly 350 BC to 250 AD — earlier than most of Belize's better-known sites. Its main pyramid, Structure 4B, preserves large stucco masks representing Maya cosmological concepts, their original paint still faintly preserved. Cerros appears to have been deliberately abandoned and ritually sealed around 250 AD, its buildings filled in and capped. The site is accessible by boat from Corozal Town and offers mangrove-fringed coastal scenery alongside its archaeology.

## 7. Lubaantun, Toledo District

Lubaantun ('Place of Fallen Stones' in Yucatec Maya) was occupied primarily between roughly 700 and 900 AD and is architecturally distinctive: its buildings were constructed without mortar, using precisely fitted limestone blocks that have survived remarkably well. The site served as a regional administrative centre for the Toledo region and may have organized the production and distribution of cacao. Lubaantun is associated — dubiously — with the story of the 'Crystal Skull' allegedly found here in 1924 by Anna Mitchell-Hedges, now understood to be a 19th-century European fabrication. The site itself, accessible from Punta Gorda, is genuine and impressive.

## 8. Nim Li Punit, Toledo District

Nim Li Punit ('Big Hat' in Q'eqchi' Maya, a reference to a headdress carved on a stela) is a Late Classic site in the Toledo foothills occupied from roughly 250 to 900 AD. The site has yielded the second-largest collection of stelae in Belize — twenty-six monuments, eight of them carved — including Stela 15, one of the tallest Maya stelae ever found. The stelae at Nim Li Punit are notable for the unusually large headdresses worn by the depicted rulers and for the quality of the glyphic inscriptions. A small but well-organized visitor centre displays several stelae and explains the site's dynastic history.

## 9. Xibun (Kendal), Stann Creek District

The site known as Kendal, near the Sittee River in the Stann Creek Valley, preserves remains of a small Classic Maya centre whose investigation has contributed to understanding the regional settlement patterns of the Belizean interior. Several mounds with visible stonework can be explored on private land with permission from the landowner. Less developed than Belize's major sites, it offers the experience of discovering Maya architecture in a working farming landscape — the kind of setting in which most Belizean ruins still quietly exist.

## 10. El Pilar, Cayo District

El Pilar straddles the Belize-Guatemala border and preserves a large Classic Maya city whose central precinct covers over 50 hectares. Unusually, El Pilar has been managed from the outset as a forest garden site, with archaeological excavation deliberately limited in favour of maintaining the jungle cover. The approach advocated by archaeologist Anabel Ford is to demonstrate that Maya agriculture and forest management can coexist with conservation. A system of trails leads through the site connecting plazas and structures, and interpretive signage explains both the archaeology and the agro-forestry principles. The setting, with ceiba trees and ramon canopy growing over unexcavated mounds, gives a sense of how most Maya ruins in the region actually look.

Most of Belize's major sites charge modest admission fees and are open daily. The dry season from late November to May offers the easiest road access; during the wet season many interior tracks become impassable without high-clearance four-wheel-drive vehicles. Caracol requires a military escort on certain days, but this is organized straightforwardly at the western border town of San Ignacio.

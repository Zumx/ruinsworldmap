## Why Castles Become Ruins

A castle in good condition is a functioning building. A castle in ruins is history made visible — but the process by which a fortress becomes a ruin is rarely simple or accidental. Most European castles fell into decay through a combination of deliberate demolition, quarrying for building materials, political obsolescence, and economic abandonment rather than dramatic siege or conquest. Understanding why specific castles stand as ruins today reveals as much about the centuries after the Middle Ages as about the medieval period itself.

The most direct route to ruin was slighting — the deliberate partial demolition of a castle rendered tactically or politically inconvenient. During the English Civil War of the 1640s, Parliament ordered the slighting of royalist strongholds after the fighting ended, to prevent their future use. Corfe Castle in Dorset, one of England's most dramatic ruins, was systematically demolished in 1646 following a celebrated siege in which Lady Mary Bankes held it against Parliamentary forces for nearly three years. The ruins were not the result of the siege itself but of the miners and engineers who came afterward, blowing apart the keep and towers with gunpowder until the walls slid down the hill in the distinctive tumbled angles that define the site today.

## Quarrying and Spoliation

Across Europe, the most common fate of a medieval castle after it lost strategic or residential value was to become a quarry. Stone was expensive, cutting and transport labour-intensive; a ruined castle offered a ready supply of pre-cut dressed stone requiring only transport. The Roman wall of London was largely dismantled in the medieval period to build new buildings. Medieval castles, in turn, were quarried for post-medieval construction, with the process often accelerating after the Dissolution of religious houses in the 1530s created both a model and a legal precedent for appropriating institutional property.

Kenilworth Castle in Warwickshire illustrates the layered process of decay. Built in stone in the 12th century, significantly expanded by John of Gaunt in the 1370s, and further developed for Robert Dudley's entertainments of Elizabeth I in 1575, it was partially slighted after the Civil War. But the main agent of its present ruinous state was systematic quarrying over the following century: the red sandstone was reused in nearby buildings throughout the 18th century. What survives is a magnificently photogenic shell — Gaunt's great hall, the Norman keep, Dudley's buildings — but it survives because those sections were too intact or too ornate to demolish efficiently.

## The Romantic Ruin and the Grand Tour

The transformation of ruined castles from embarrassing evidence of military defeat or simple inconvenience into objects of aesthetic admiration happened rapidly in the 18th century. The Picturesque movement — developed by theorists including William Gilpin and Uvedale Price in Britain — found in ruined architecture the essential qualities of irregular beauty, melancholy association, and the pleasing contrast between human ambition and natural decay. A castle ruin occupied by owls and draped in ivy was not a failure; it was a meditation on time.

This aesthetic found its most elaborate expression in the built fake ruin, the so-called 'sham ruin' or 'folly'. At Hagley Hall in Worcestershire, Sanderson Miller designed a ruined castle tower in 1747 for Lord Lyttelton — a structure built new to look ancient, complete with a keep fragment and deliberately tumbled masonry. Dozens of similar structures were created across British country-house estates. The fake ruin acknowledged that the real thing could not always be conveniently located within view of the drawing-room window.

Meanwhile, travellers on the Grand Tour encountered the ruined Colosseum in Rome, the broken temples of Paestum, and the shattered fortresses of the Rhine valley, and brought back drawings, watercolours, and the mental habit of reading landscape through the lens of decay. The castle ruin became a European cultural touchstone: a symbol of the transience of power that served equally as a backdrop for Gothic novels, Romantic poetry, and the emerging science of medieval history.

## Scotland and the Border Castles

Scotland and the English-Scottish border contain a higher density of ruined medieval fortifications than almost anywhere else in Europe, and the reasons are political as much as climatic. The border counties suffered repeated military campaigning from the 13th through the 17th centuries — Scottish independence wars, Edward I's campaigns, the Battle of Flodden (1513), and the chronic raiding culture of the Border Reivers. Many castles were burned, abandoned, and left unrepaired because the financial resources for rebuilding were not available in a landscape already depopulated by warfare.

Dunnottar Castle on the Kincardineshire coast, perched on a sea stack 50 metres above the North Sea, was effectively abandoned after 1715 when the Earls Marischal lost their estates following the Jacobite rising. The castle was partly dismantled and the stone used for local building. What remained was left to the sea winds. Today it is one of the most photographed castles in Scotland, its dramatic setting having protected it from the quarrying that stripped less picturesque sites. The Crown Jewels of Scotland were smuggled out of Dunnottar under the noses of a Commonwealth siege in 1652 — the last military event in its history.

Eilean Donan on Loch Duich was a complete ruin by 1719, having been bombarded by a Royal Navy frigate whose crew were assisting in the suppression of a Jacobite-Spanish landing. It was rebuilt between 1912 and 1932, a restoration of such thoroughness that the result is essentially a 20th-century castle in medieval form, with little original fabric surviving. Its popularity as one of Scotland's most visited castles depends almost entirely on its landscape setting and the romantic associations of its ruinous history rather than authentic medieval fabric.

## German Castles: The Rhine and Beyond

The Rhine valley between Bingen and Koblenz concentrates more ruined medieval castles per kilometre than any other river valley in Europe. Burg Rheinfels, Burg Sooneck, Burg Gutenfels, and dozens more cling to hillsides above the river. Most were built between the 11th and 14th centuries by lords who taxed river traffic — the Rhine was a major trade artery, and a fortified height above a crossing point was a money-making asset. When Rhine tolls were abolished or trade routes shifted, the strategic and economic rationale for maintaining large fortresses evaporated.

The finishing blow for most came during the wars of the late 17th century. Louis XIV's armies systematically destroyed Rhine fortresses during the Palatinate campaigns of 1689–1693, and many were never rebuilt. Napoleon's armies continued the process in the 1790s. The resulting ruins became the defining imagery of German Romanticism: Caspar David Friedrich's paintings of Gothic ruins in misty landscapes, Joseph Turner's watercolours of Rhine castles above the river, and the early 19th-century collectors of German folk traditions who saw in the ruined castle a symbol of national history under threat.

## How to Read a Castle Ruin

Reading a ruined castle requires separating what stands from what has been removed. The visible masonry represents only the portion that was too solid, too remote, or too aesthetically interesting to quarry. Projecting from the surviving walls — how thick the walls are, where the garderobe chutes empty, where putlog holes for scaffolding survive, where the line of a demolished floor joist is marked by a row of corbels — allows the plan of the original building to be reconstructed in the mind.

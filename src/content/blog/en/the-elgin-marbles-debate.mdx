## The Sculptures and Their Building

The Parthenon was completed on the Athenian Acropolis in 432 BC, built under the supervision of Pheidias during the administration of Pericles and designed by the architects Iktinos and Kallikrates. It was a temple to Athena Parthenos — Athena the Virgin — and its sculptural programme was the most ambitious decoration ever applied to a Greek building. The exterior comprised ninety-two metopes carved in high relief depicting battles between Lapiths and centaurs (on the south), Greeks and Amazons (on the west), gods and giants (on the east), and scenes from the Trojan War (on the north). Above the colonnades inside the outer peristyle ran a continuous frieze roughly 160 metres long in low relief depicting a procession, widely interpreted as the Panathenaic festival held every four years in Athena's honour. Above the entrance porches at east and west, pediment groups depicted mythological scenes in fully three-dimensional free-standing figures.

The sculpture was painted. The figures we see today — creamy white marble, worn to varying degrees — would originally have had coloured flesh, hair, clothing and attributes. The frieze and metopes were at a height where colour would have aided legibility in the bright Attic sunlight. That painted surface is entirely gone.

The building underwent a series of traumatic transformations over its long history. Converted to a Christian church in late antiquity, then to a mosque after the Ottoman conquest of Athens in 1458, it survived largely intact until 1687, when a Venetian artillery shell struck a powder magazine the Ottomans had stored inside and the building exploded. The central portion of the cella was destroyed; metopes and frieze blocks fell to the ground; the roof collapsed. The ruin that stood afterward was, by European standards, still remarkably preserved — but the Parthenon as a building was finished.

## Lord Elgin and the Removal

Thomas Bruce, 7th Earl of Elgin, was appointed British Ambassador to the Ottoman Empire in 1799. During his tenure in Constantinople (Istanbul) he conceived the idea of having casts made of the Parthenon's sculptures to enrich British architecture and arts education, and obtained a document — the precise legal status of which has been contested ever since — from the Ottoman authorities. The Italian text of the document, a firman, has been interpreted either as a permit allowing Elgin to remove objects from the Acropolis or merely as permission to draw and cast; the Ottoman original in Turkish has never been found.

Between 1801 and 1812 Elgin's agents, under the direction of the Italian artist Giovanni Battista Lusieri, removed roughly half the surviving frieze blocks (56 out of an original total of 115 surviving sections), fifteen of the ninety-two metopes (primarily from the south), and seventeen pedimental figures, along with a caryatid from the neighbouring Erechtheion and various architectural elements. In some cases, surviving sawn marks on the Parthenon's structure show where blocks were cut free. In others, pieces already fallen to the ground were collected.

Elgin shipped the sculptures to Britain, intending to use them to decorate his Scottish house. When personal and financial difficulties ended that plan, he sold the collection to the British Museum in 1816 for £35,000 — roughly half what he claimed the removal had cost him. A Select Committee of the House of Commons had examined the legal and ethical questions beforehand; some members expressed discomfort, but the purchase was approved.

## What Survives and Where

Today the Parthenon sculptures are divided principally between two institutions. The British Museum in London holds approximately 50% of the surviving original decorated surfaces, displayed in the purpose-built Duveen Gallery. The Acropolis Museum in Athens, opened in 2009 at the foot of the Acropolis rock, holds the remainder of the surviving material from Athens: over 170 frieze blocks, 15 metopes, and fragments from the pediments, along with the five remaining caryatids of the Erechtheion. Smaller pieces are scattered across museums in Paris, Rome, Vienna, Würzburg, Copenhagen, Munich and Palermo.

The Acropolis Museum was partly designed as a deliberate counter-argument: it provides a large, well-lit gallery at the same incline as the original frieze, with the Athens pieces displayed in sequence alongside plaster casts of the London pieces shown in grey to mark their absence. The visual effect of the gaps is a curatorial argument for reunification.

## The Legal and Ethical Arguments

The British Museum's position rests on several foundations. The sculptures were legally purchased by the British government; the museum is prohibited by the British Museum Act 1963 from permanently transferring objects from its collection; the sculptures are better preserved in London than they would have been in 19th-century Athens; and universal museums, which represent all of humanity's heritage, serve a different purpose from national museums. The museum also points to the atmospheric damage the remaining Parthenon sculptures in Athens have suffered — acid rain and pollution from the mid-20th century eroded the surface of pieces that remained on the building, which are now inside the Acropolis Museum but carry visible damage.

Greece's position is that the removal was illegal under Ottoman law as well as any reasonable ethical standard; that the purpose-built Acropolis Museum demonstrates that Athens has adequate facilities for reunited care of the sculptures; that the firman granted no right of permanent removal; and that the sculptures form an indivisible whole whose fragmentary condition in two countries deprives visitors in both of the full work. Greece has formally requested the return of the sculptures through UNESCO's Intergovernmental Committee for Promoting the Return of Cultural Property since 1984.

The legal question is genuinely complex. The 1970 UNESCO Convention that governs most repatriation cases is not retroactive; it applies to transfers after 1970. Earlier removals — including Elgin's — must be evaluated under the law and norms of their time and under broader ethical principles rather than treaty provisions. The British Museum Act prohibition has been repeatedly cited as an obstacle, but it has been amended before for humanitarian reasons (to allow the return of human remains to aboriginal communities), and nothing in the law prevents Parliament from changing it again.

## Recent Developments

The debate has intensified in recent years. In 2021, the Italian government returned three fragments in the possession of the Palermo museum to Greece, the fragments having been formally recognised as part of the Parthenon frieze. In 2023, the Vatican Museums returned three small fragments of their own to Greece. The Sicyon fragments, formerly in the Museo Salinas in Palermo, are now on loan to the Acropolis Museum.

The British Museum under its director Hartwig Fischer had engaged in quiet negotiations with the Greek government from around 2022 onward, with reports of a possible loan arrangement — the sculptures remaining British Museum property but being displayed in Athens. These talks became public and controversial; the museum's trustees remained divided. No agreement had been reached by 2026, and the incoming Greek government reiterated the demand for permanent return rather than a loan, which Greece's culture ministry characterised as treating Athens as a branch of the British Museum.

## Why the Debate Matters Beyond the Parthenon

The Parthenon sculptures are the most famous case in the wider debate over the repatriation of cultural property removed during the colonial era. Similar disputes involve the Benin Bronzes (taken from the Kingdom of Benin in Nigeria during a British punitive expedition in 1897 and now distributed among around 160 institutions worldwide, with Germany and several US institutions having returned pieces); the Rosetta Stone; the Koh-i-Noor diamond; and many other objects.

The arguments rehearsed over the Parthenon sculptures — legal ownership versus ethical claim, universal museum versus national custodianship, the meaning of cultural heritage versus the practical conditions of physical preservation — recur in almost every such case. The resolution of any one of them, particularly one as prominent as the Parthenon, would inevitably reshape the terms of all the others.

Thailand's ancient ruins reflect a remarkable succession of cultures that shaped mainland Southeast Asia over more than two millennia. Before the Tai-speaking peoples who give the country its modern identity arrived from the north in significant numbers around the 13th century AD, the region was home to the Mon-Khmer civilisation of Dvaravati, the Khmer Empire's western expansion into what is now northeastern and central Thailand, and several smaller Indianised kingdoms. The Tai kingdoms of Sukhothai and Ayutthaya then built upon these earlier foundations, drawing on Khmer, Mon, Sinhalese Buddhist, and Chinese influences to create distinctive architectural traditions of their own. The result is a layered landscape: Khmer prang towers in the northeast, Mon chedi in the central plains, and the great ruined cities of the Ayutthaya period that for several centuries were among the largest and wealthiest in Asia.

## 1. Ayutthaya, Ayutthaya Province

Founded in 1351, Ayutthaya served as the capital of the Ayutthaya Kingdom for over four centuries. At its height in the 17th century it was one of the largest cities in the world, with a population perhaps exceeding a million, a cosmopolitan trading port visited by Dutch, French, Portuguese, Japanese, Chinese, and Persian merchants. In 1767, Burmese forces sacked and burned the city, leaving the ruins we see today: the stumps of hundreds of chedis, prang towers, and temple complexes distributed across an island formed by the junction of three rivers. Wat Mahathat, Wat Phra Si Sanphet, and Wat Ratchaburana are among the most striking. The iconic Buddha heads entwined in banyan tree roots at Wat Mahathat are among the most photographed images in Thailand. The Ayutthaya Historical Park is a UNESCO World Heritage Site; entry to individual temples carries a small admission charge.

## 2. Sukhothai Historical Park, Sukhothai Province

Sukhothai — 'dawn of happiness' — was the first major Tai kingdom to emerge on the central plains, flourishing from the mid-13th through the mid-15th centuries AD. The historical park surrounding the old city contains over 190 recorded monuments. The Ramkhamhaeng National Museum and the central zone around Wat Mahathat preserve the lotus-bud chedi style that is characteristic of Sukhothai architecture: elegant tapering towers above a base of stucco-covered laterite and brick. The 14th-century Walking Buddha image type, distinctive for its fluid, flame-like posture, was developed here and influenced Buddhist art throughout Southeast Asia. The park covers the central walled city; outer temples including Wat Si Chum — with its enormous seated Buddha visible through a narrow doorway — require a bicycle or tuk-tuk. A UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1991.

## 3. Si Satchanalai, Sukhothai Province

About 55 kilometres north of Sukhothai, Si Satchanalai was the second city of the Sukhothai Kingdom, administered by a viceroy and linked to the capital by road. The historical park here preserves some of the best Sukhothai-period architecture in a setting less visited than Sukhothai itself. Wat Chang Lom — a chedi surrounded by standing elephant sculptures at its base — is an early example of this form, which the Sukhothai borrowed from Sri Lanka. Wat Chedi Jet Thaew, with seven rows of chedi, is a royal funerary complex of considerable scale. Nearby, the kilns of Ko Noi produced the distinctive Sangkhalok celadon ware that was exported across Southeast Asia in the 14th and 15th centuries; kiln remains are visible and a kilns study centre operates in the park.

## 4. Phimai, Nakhon Ratchasima Province

In the Khorat Plateau of northeastern Thailand, Phimai (ancient Vimayapura) was a major Khmer city connected by a royal road directly to Angkor. The main sanctuary, Prasat Hin Phimai, was built primarily between the late 11th and early 12th centuries AD in white sandstone — a deliberate choice that distinguished it from the pink sandstone more common at Angkor. The temple complex follows the standard Khmer layout of concentric enclosures with a central prang, but the iconography mixes Hindu and Buddhist symbolism in a way that reflects Phimai's position during the transition between Shaivite and Mahayana Buddhist royal patronage. A processional avenue flanked by boundary stones leads from the south gate toward the ancient town. The Fine Arts Department manages the site; a good site museum holds sculpture and architectural elements.

## 5. Phanom Rung, Buriram Province

On the rim of an extinct volcano in Buriram province, Phanom Rung is the finest Khmer monument on Thai territory. Built primarily between the 10th and 13th centuries AD, it was a Shaivite sanctuary dedicated to Shiva — its position on a volcano was cosmologically significant, evoking Mount Meru, the home of the gods. The main approach is a long ceremonial promenade flanked by naga balustrades, leading up through a series of galleries and gopuras to the central prang. The masonry is of exceptional quality, and several carved lintels — including a famous Narai Banthomsindhu lintel depicting Vishnu reclining on the cosmic serpent — are among the finest Khmer bas-reliefs outside Cambodia. During the April festival, the sun rises through all fifteen of the sanctuary's doorways in alignment — a deliberate astronomical design.

## 6. Kamphaeng Phet Historical Park, Kamphaeng Phet Province

Another component of the Sukhothai World Heritage Site, Kamphaeng Phet was a northern garrison city of the Sukhothai Kingdom, guarding the routes from the north. The historical park preserves the remains of Wat Phra Kaeo and Wat Phra That within the old city walls, as well as a remarkable forest temple zone to the north (the Aranyik zone) where large Buddha images — many now just torsos and bases amid the trees — give the area an atmosphere quite different from the more manicured central zone. The city walls themselves, with their brick-and-laterite construction and flanking moat, are among the most intact examples of Sukhothai-period urban fortification. The provincial museum is among the better regional archaeology museums in northern Thailand.

## 7. Wat Phra That Haripunchai, Lamphun Province

In the old Mon town of Lamphun, about 25 kilometres south of Chiang Mai, this active temple complex preserves the chedi (stupa) of the Haripunchai Kingdom, a Mon Buddhist state that flourished in the valley of the Ping river from roughly the 8th through 13th centuries AD. The gilded chedi in its present form dates primarily from the 15th century but stands on older foundations and enshrines important relics. The Haripunchai Museum next door holds Mon-period bronzes, ceramics, and architectural elements that document this often-overlooked precursor to the northern Tai kingdoms. The town of Lamphun retains its rectangular moat plan from the Mon period; walking the circumference gives a sense of the original urban scale.

## 8. Prasat Mueang Tam, Buriram Province

In the lowlands below Phanom Rung, Prasat Mueang Tam is a smaller Khmer sanctuary of the early 11th century AD that is remarkable for the quality of its setting: four large L-shaped ponds with naga balustrades surround the central sanctuary, creating a visual effect of extraordinary refinement when the waters are still. The four gopuras and five towers are built in a pink and grey sandstone, and the carved lintels depicting Khmer deities are largely intact. Mueang Tam sees a fraction of Phanom Rung's visitors despite being only about eight kilometres away, and the relative quiet allows for unhurried appreciation of the hydraulic and spatial design that characterises the best Khmer provincial architecture.

## 9. Chiang Saen, Chiang Rai Province

At the confluence of the Mekong and the Ruak rivers in the far north of Thailand — the heart of the Golden Triangle — Chiang Saen was an early Tai Lanna Kingdom town founded traditionally in the 13th century AD. The walled town, whose brick ramparts partly survive, contains the ruins of multiple temples including Wat Phra That Chom Kitti, whose chedi crowns the hill above the old town, and Wat Chedi Luang within the walls. The National Museum here is excellent, with collections of Lanna-period bronzes, ceramics, and architectural ornament. The Mekong frontage gives the ruins an additional geographic drama: the far bank is Laos, and the river has defined the northern boundary of successive Thai states for centuries.

## 10. Muang Boran (Kadsan), Nakhon Pathom Province

In the low-lying central plains west of Bangkok, the ancient Mon city known today as Nakhon Pathom contains what is thought to be one of the earliest Buddhist monuments in Southeast Asia: the Phra Pathom Chedi, a stupa built on a site associated with the Dvaravati Kingdom (roughly 6th to 11th centuries AD). The original Dvaravati-period chedi was encased and enlarged by Rama IV in the 19th century, creating the enormous structure visible today, but the museum within the compound holds original Dvaravati stone dhamma wheels, boundary stones, and Buddha images of a distinctive rounded style that had pan-Southeast Asian influence. Nakhon Pathom is about an hour from Bangkok by train and is the most accessible window into the pre-Khmer, pre-Tai world of the central plains.

## Planning Your Visit

Thailand's ancient sites are spread from the Mekong in the far north to the Gulf of Thailand in the south. The northeastern Khmer sites — Phimai, Phanom Rung, and Mueang Tam — are best covered by car from Nakhon Ratchasima (Korat) over two days; a dedicated hire car is far preferable to public transport. Sukhothai, Si Satchanalai, and Kamphaeng Phet form a natural northern circuit reachable from Phitsanulok. Ayutthaya is an easy day trip from Bangkok by train. The dry season from November through February is the most comfortable time to visit; the northeast is very hot from March through May. Most historical parks charge a modest admission.

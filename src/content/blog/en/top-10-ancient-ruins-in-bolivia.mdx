Bolivia's archaeological heritage spans a remarkable range of altitudes, environments, and cultures — from the high Andean plateau where the Tiwanaku civilization raised some of the most sophisticated stone monuments of the pre-Columbian Americas, to the lowland jungle regions of the Llanos de Mojos where recent aerial survey has revealed a network of causeways, canals, and earthworks extending across hundreds of kilometres. The country sits at the crossroads of Andean and Amazonian cultural worlds, and its sites reflect that complexity. Many remain poorly studied and difficult to access, but those that have been excavated yield evidence of engineering, astronomical knowledge, and long-distance trade networks that challenge simple narratives of Andean prehistory. ## 1. Tiwanaku, Altiplano

Tiwanaku, on the southern shore of Lake Titicaca at an altitude of roughly 3,850 metres, was the capital of one of the most important pre-Columbian civilizations in South America. The Tiwanaku state flourished from around 300 to 1100 AD, at its height controlling a territory extending across much of the southern Andes and sustaining its population through an innovative raised-field agriculture system that reclaimed lakeside swampland. The site's most famous structures — the Akapana pyramid, the Kalasasaya platform with its standing stone tenon heads, the Puma Punku complex with its precisely cut andesite blocks, and the Gateway of the Sun with its carved frontal figure often interpreted as a deity — were built using stone transported from quarries up to 90 kilometres away. The precision of the stonework at Puma Punku, with H-shaped interlocking blocks cut to tolerances of millimetres, remains a subject of serious archaeological inquiry. UNESCO World Heritage Site.

## 2. El Fuerte de Samaipata, Santa Cruz Department

El Fuerte de Samaipata, in the foothills of the Cordillera Oriental east of Santa Cruz, preserves the largest known carved rock in the world — a sandstone outcrop roughly 250 metres long and 60 metres wide whose surface has been covered with carved channels, niches, seats, feline figures, and geometric patterns. The site was used by pre-Inca cultures from perhaps the 1st millennium AD, then adapted and expanded by the Inca empire in the 15th century, which added administrative structures and incorporated the rock into its ceremonial landscape. The Inca town built around the rock served as an eastern frontier post of Tawantinsuyu. Spanish colonial remains are also present. The site is a UNESCO World Heritage Site accessible from the pleasant town of Samaipata.

## 3. Iskanwaya, La Paz Department

Iskanwaya, in the remote Larecaja Province north of La Paz, is one of the best-preserved ruins of the Mollo culture, a pre-Inca agricultural society that flourished in the Bolivian Yungas valleys from roughly 1000 to 1400 AD before being absorbed into the Inca empire. The site consists of several hundred stone buildings — residential structures, warehouses, terraces, and a temple complex — arranged on steep valley terraces above the Llica River. The dry climate and relative inaccessibility have preserved standing walls to unusual heights, with some structures retaining their original masonry up to two storeys. Reaching Iskanwaya requires a long drive on unpaved roads and a walk of several hours, but the isolation contributes to its extraordinary atmosphere.

## 4. Pumapunku, Altiplano

While administratively part of the Tiwanaku complex, Pumapunku deserves separate consideration for the sheer technical accomplishment of its stonework. The platform mound was built in several phases, with the final construction involving the transport and precise cutting of large andesite blocks — some weighing 100 tonnes or more — from a quarry at Copacabana Peninsula, across the lake. The H-shaped blocks that once interlocked to form walls or doorways show evidence of drilling, grinding, and cutting techniques not yet fully explained. The site was partly dismantled in colonial times, with its stones used for the construction of the nearby church at Tiwanaku village, but enough remains to convey the ambition of the original construction.

## 5. Incallajta, Cochabamba Department

Incallajta, in the Cochabamba department, is considered the largest known Inca archaeological site in Bolivia. Built during the reign of the Inca Huayna Capac in the late 15th or early 16th century, the complex served as a military and administrative centre on the frontier between the Inca empire and the unsubdued Chiriguano people to the east. The site covers roughly 20 hectares and includes a large kallanka (great hall) approximately 78 metres long — one of the largest Inca buildings known anywhere — along with temples, storehouses, a usnu platform, and a plaza. The site is relatively well preserved and lies about four hours by road from the city of Cochabamba.

## 6. Inca Llajta de Inkallajta, Cochabamba Department

Not to be confused with the Inca administrative complex of Incallajta, this smaller site in the Cochabamba valleys preserves remains of local pre-Inca occupation that was later overlain by Inca structures. The stratified deposits allow archaeologists to trace the transition from local polities to imperial Inca control in this transitional ecological zone between the sierra and the tropical lowlands. The site has been the subject of ongoing excavation programs by Bolivian and foreign universities.

## 7. Loma Salvatierra, Beni Department

Loma Salvatierra, in the Beni lowlands of northeastern Bolivia, is one of the best-studied examples of the Llanos de Mojos mound culture. The Mojos people, active from roughly 500 to 1400 AD, raised artificial earthen mounds above the seasonally flooded savannah and connected them with a vast system of causeways and canals extending across the landscape. Loma Salvatierra has been excavated by German and Bolivian teams and has yielded evidence of intensive agriculture, craft production, and ceremonial activity. The mounds of the Beni remain poorly understood as a whole, but recent lidar surveys suggest an urban landscape far larger than previously imagined.

## 8. Khonkho Wankané, Altiplano

Khonkho Wankané, on the southern altiplano south of Lake Titicaca, was an important ceremonial centre of the Tiwanaku formative period, occupied from roughly 100 BC to 500 AD. The site preserves two large sunken courts with stone tenon heads still in situ on some walls, a precursor to the better-known architectural vocabulary later perfected at Tiwanaku itself. The ongoing excavations here have been particularly important for understanding the development of Tiwanaku iconography and the nature of the early ritual landscape around Lake Titicaca.

## 9. Chullpas of Sillustani Area (Bolivian Side)

The Bolivian altiplano south and east of Lake Titicaca contains numerous chullpas — cylindrical or rectangular above-ground burial towers — associated with the Colla people and later with Aymara-speaking populations who inhabited the region before and during Inca domination. Many are built of finely cut stone in the late pre-Inca style. While the most famous chullpas are at Sillustani on the Peruvian side of the lake, towers of comparable quality are found at several locations in the Bolivian altiplano, rising from the windswept puna with dramatic views over the lake.

## 10. Samaipata Lower Town, Santa Cruz Department

Separate from the carved rock of El Fuerte, the lower town of Samaipata preserves urban remains of the Inca administrative settlement that served the sanctuary site. Street layouts, storage structures, and residential foundations have been mapped in the area around the modern village of Samaipata, and the site museum in the village provides detailed context for both the pre-Inca and Inca phases of occupation. The museum's collection of ceramics from the site is particularly strong and well-displayed.

Bolivia's major archaeological sites are generally open year-round, but weather varies dramatically by altitude and region. Tiwanaku and El Fuerte de Samaipata are most comfortable during the dry season from May to October; lowland sites in the Beni are best visited in the dry months as well, when roads and airstrips are accessible. Tiwanaku is roughly a 90-minute drive from La Paz and has a site museum of good quality. Admission fees are modest.

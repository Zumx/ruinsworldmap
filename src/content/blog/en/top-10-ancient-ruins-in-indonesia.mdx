Indonesia's ancient ruins reflect the profound influence of Indian civilization on Southeast Asia between roughly the 4th and 15th centuries AD. Hindu and Buddhist kingdoms that flourished across Java, Sumatra, and Bali left behind temple complexes of extraordinary scale and craftsmanship — some of the largest religious monuments ever built. These kingdoms absorbed Sanskrit learning, Indian cosmological architecture, and Brahminic court culture while adapting them to local traditions, producing a syncretic sacred art that is distinctively Javanese or Sumatran rather than simply derivative. The 9th century alone saw the construction of two world-class monument complexes — Borobudur and Prambanan — within roughly 50 kilometres of each other in central Java, a concentration of ambition without parallel in the ancient world outside Egypt. ## 1. Borobudur, Central Java

Borobudur is the largest Buddhist monument in the world and one of the greatest architectural achievements of any civilization. Built around 800 AD during the Sailendra dynasty on a natural hill above the Kedu Plain, it takes the form of a stepped mandala: nine platforms of decreasing size rising to a central stupa, the entire structure clad in volcanic andesite stone. The lower galleries are lined with roughly 2,500 narrative relief panels depicting the life of the Buddha, Jataka tales, and scenes of everyday Javanese life, totalling around five kilometres of carved panels. The upper circular terraces hold 72 latticed stupas each containing a Buddha figure, and the summit stupa, solid, represents Nirvana. Borobudur fell into disuse after the rise of Islam in Java around the 15th century and was effectively buried under volcanic ash and jungle growth until its rediscovery by Thomas Raffles in 1814. A UNESCO World Heritage Site, it underwent a major restoration project from 1975 to 1982. Best visited at dawn to experience the site before tour groups arrive.

## 2. Prambanan, Central Java and Yogyakarta

Prambanan, built around 850 AD during the Sanjaya dynasty, is the largest Hindu temple complex in Southeast Asia and one of the tallest temple structures of the ancient world. The three central shrines — dedicated to Brahma, Vishnu, and Shiva — rise to around 47 metres each, their surfaces covered with bas-relief panels narrating the Ramayana. The original complex contained 240 structures in a concentric layout; dozens of smaller temples are preserved as varying degrees of ruin or restoration. Prambanan was badly damaged by a major earthquake in 2006 and reconstruction continues. The reliefs of the Ramayana ballet on the main temple's outer gallery are considered among the finest narrative sculpture in Asia. UNESCO World Heritage Site. Prambanan is about 17 kilometres east of Yogyakarta; a combined ticket with Borobudur is available.

## 3. Trowulan, East Java

Trowulan, near Mojokerto in East Java, was the capital of the Majapahit Empire, the most powerful Hindu-Buddhist kingdom in Indonesian history, which at its peak in the 14th century controlled much of the archipelago and extended political influence across Southeast Asia. The Majapahit historian Mpu Prapanca described the capital in his 1365 poem Nagarakertagama as a grand city of brick-built palaces, temples, and markets. The excavated and partly restored remains at Trowulan include the Bajang Ratu gate (a tall ornamental gateway of characteristic red-brick Majapahit construction), bathing pools, temple complexes, and a massive reservoir. The Trowulan National Museum holds an exceptional collection of Majapahit terracotta, ceramics, and bronze objects. The site is spread across a wide area; hiring a local guide or taking a tour from Surabaya is advisable.

## 4. Ratu Boko, Central Java

Ratu Boko sits on a plateau about three kilometres south of Prambanan and combines the remains of a palace complex with temple structures in a way that is unusual in Javanese archaeology — most sites being either predominantly religious or administrative, not both. Built around the 8th and 9th centuries AD during a period of interplay between the Buddhist Sailendra and Hindu Sanjaya dynasties, the complex includes an impressive gateway, bathing pools, an audience hall, and several shrine structures. The exact function and historical owners of Ratu Boko remain debated; some scholars associate it with the Sailendra prince Balaputra, others with later rulers. Sunset from the plateau — looking back toward the towers of Prambanan silhouetted against the profile of Merapi volcano — is one of the most celebrated views in Java. Entry is separate from Prambanan.

## 5. Muaro Jambi, Jambi Province, Sumatra

Muaro Jambi is the most extensive Buddhist temple complex in Southeast Asia, spread across roughly 12 kilometres along the Batanghari river in Jambi province. Built by the Srivijaya and later Melayu kingdoms between roughly the 7th and 13th centuries AD, the complex contains the remains of dozens of brick-built temples (candi), monasteries, ponds, and earthwork enclosures. Srivijaya was the dominant maritime trading empire of the western archipelago for several centuries, controlling the Malacca Strait and deriving enormous wealth from the spice and incense trades; Muaro Jambi served as one of its most important religious centres. The complex is a UNESCO Tentative World Heritage Site. It is accessible from Jambi city by boat or road; its forested setting and relative lack of tourist infrastructure give it a genuinely exploratory quality.

## 6. Singosari Temples, East Java

The Singosari kingdom (1222–1292 AD) preceded the Majapahit empire in East Java and left a series of distinctive temples, most notably the Candi Singosari near Malang, that show a transition in Javanese temple aesthetics toward the increasingly elaborate and vertically dramatic style associated with East Javanese architecture. The temple is notable for its superb guardian statues (dvarapalas), two massive figures flanking the entrance stairway. The Kidal and Jago temples nearby are associated with Singosari royal funerary rites and contain exceptional relief sculpture; Jago's panels depicting the Tantric Buddhist Mahakarmavibhanga narrative are among the most complex in Javanese art.

## 7. Candi Sukuh, Central Java

Candi Sukuh, set on the western slope of Mount Lawu at an elevation of around 1,186 metres, is one of the most enigmatic temples in Java. Built in the early 15th century — very late in the Hindu-Javanese tradition, and close to the period when Islam was beginning to supersede Hinduism on the island — it has a stepped truncated pyramid form that is strikingly different from the typical candi profile and has suggested to some archaeologists a connection to pre-Hindu megalithic traditions. The relief sculptures include explicit depictions of genitalia and birth scenes that have given the site a reputation as a 'fertility temple,' though the iconographic programme is more complex than this label suggests. The mountain setting, with views across the Solo valley, is spectacular. Accessible from Solo (Surakarta) by road.

## 8. Candi Sewu, Central Java

Candi Sewu — 'Thousand Temples' — is the second-largest Buddhist temple complex in Java after Borobudur, built slightly earlier than Prambanan, around 780–792 AD, during the Sailendra dynasty. The complex originally contained 249 structures in a mandala arrangement centred on the main temple, which is dedicated to the Bodhisattva Manjushri. The site was heavily damaged by earthquakes and restoration is ongoing. Candi Sewu adjoins the Prambanan complex and is included in the same site ticket, but it receives far fewer visitors, allowing more contemplative exploration of its restored and partially restored shrines.

## 9. Dieng Plateau Temples, Central Java

The Dieng Plateau, at around 2,000 metres above sea level in the Wonosobo highlands, contains the oldest surviving Hindu temple architecture in Java, dating to roughly the 7th and 8th centuries AD. Of the original eight named temple groups, five small temples known by the names of characters from the Mahabharata — Arjuna, Srikandi, Puntadewa, Sembadra, and Semar — have been restored. Their relatively plain exterior decoration represents an early phase of Javanese Hindu architecture before the elaborate sculptural programmes of the Prambanan period developed. The plateau's volcanic landscape, including coloured crater lakes, fumaroles, and early morning mist, makes the visit as much about the environment as the archaeology. Accessible from Wonosobo.

## 10. Bali's Pura Besakih and Ancient Balinese Sites

Bali preserves a living Hindu tradition maintained without interruption since the Majapahit period, and its temple architecture — though generally maintained and rebuilt rather than ruinous in the conventional sense — embodies the same Javano-Balinese artistic tradition as the east Javanese temples. Pura Besakih, the 'mother temple' on the slopes of Mount Agung, is the largest and most sacred complex on the island, comprising over 80 individual temples in a complex that has been a place of worship since at least the 11th century. The archaeological sites of Goa Gajah (the Elephant Cave, with a 9th-century carved cave entrance), Pura Kehen (Bangli), and the stone sarcophagi of the Pejeng area complete the picture of pre-modern Balinese material culture.

Indonesia's main archaeological corridor runs from Yogyakarta (for Borobudur, Prambanan, Ratu Boko, and surrounding temples) to East Java (for Trowulan, Singosari, and the Malang area temples). Yogyakarta is served by frequent flights from Jakarta and Bali; the Dieng Plateau requires an extra day from Yogyakarta. Muaro Jambi in Sumatra requires a separate trip to Jambi, accessible by flight from Jakarta. The dry season (May through October) is generally most comfortable for outdoor site visits in Java.

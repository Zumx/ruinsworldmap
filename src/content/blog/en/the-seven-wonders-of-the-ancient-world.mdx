## The Idea of a List

The concept of a canonical list of the world's greatest monuments is itself a classical invention. Greek and Roman writers compiled various catalogues of remarkable structures and places, and the list of seven wonders as it is usually cited today — the Great Pyramid of Giza, the Hanging Gardens of Babylon, the Statue of Zeus at Olympia, the Temple of Artemis at Ephesus, the Mausoleum at Halicarnassus, the Colossus of Rhodes, and the Lighthouse of Alexandria — was standardised in roughly the 2nd century BC by writers including the mathematician Philon of Byzantium. The number seven was considered by the Greeks to represent completeness and perfection.

All seven were structures of the Mediterranean and Near Eastern world, reflecting the geographical horizons of the Hellenistic period. They were not random selections: each was notable for a specific quality of architectural ambition, artistic achievement, or engineering innovation that made it remarkable even to contemporaries living in an age of ambitious builders. Of the seven, only one survives: the Great Pyramid at Giza. The rest are known through ancient descriptions, coins and reliefs, and in some cases archaeological investigation of their sites.

## The Great Pyramid of Giza

The oldest of the seven wonders and the only one still standing, the Great Pyramid was built for the pharaoh Khufu (Cheops) of the 4th Dynasty, around 2560 BC. It stands roughly 138 metres tall today — slightly reduced from its original height of about 146 metres due to the loss of its outer casing stones — and contains an estimated 2.3 million stone blocks weighing between 2.5 and 15 tonnes each. The precision of its construction — the base is level to within 2.1 centimetres, and oriented to true north to within a fraction of a degree — continues to generate engineering speculation and scholarly study.

The pyramid sits on the Giza plateau alongside two other major pyramids (for the pharaohs Khafre and Menkaure), the Great Sphinx, subsidiary queens' pyramids, boat pits, and the remains of mortuary temples and a causeway leading to the valley temple on the desert edge. The site is a UNESCO World Heritage property. Tourism pressure is intense; the pyramids attract millions of visitors annually, and access to the interior chambers requires separate ticketing and is physically demanding.

## The Hanging Gardens of Babylon

The most mysterious of the seven wonders, the Hanging Gardens are the only one for which no secure archaeological evidence has been found at the traditionally assigned location. Classical sources describe them as terraced gardens watered by a mechanical system, built in Babylon — in present-day Iraq, about 85 kilometres south of Baghdad — supposedly by the Babylonian king Nebuchadnezzar II in the 6th century BC, as a gift for his wife who missed the green hills of her homeland.

Archaeological excavations at Babylon, conducted extensively by German teams in the late 19th and early 20th centuries and continued by Iraqi archaeologists, have not identified a structure matching the ancient descriptions. Some scholars have proposed that the gardens were located not at Babylon but at Nineveh, where the Assyrian king Sennacherib did build elaborate irrigated pleasure gardens, and that the attribution to Babylon is a geographical error introduced by later writers. The question remains unresolved.

## The Statue of Zeus at Olympia

The cult statue of Zeus at Olympia, made by the Athenian sculptor Pheidias around 435 BC, was housed in the Temple of Zeus at the sanctuary of Olympia in the northwestern Peloponnese — the site that gave its name to the Olympic Games. Ancient sources describe the chryselephantine statue (gold and ivory on a wooden armature) as approximately 12 metres tall, seated on a throne, holding a small figure of Nike in one hand and a sceptre in the other. The Roman writer Strabo said that if Zeus stood up, he would lift off the temple roof.

The Temple of Zeus itself, begun around 470 BC and completed around 456, was one of the finest Doric temples in the Greek world. Substantial ruins survive at Olympia, including drums of fallen columns, architectural sculpture from the pediments (held in the Olympia Archaeological Museum), and the foundations of Pheidias's workshop, where tools and materials of chryselephantine construction were found during excavation. The statue was probably removed to Constantinople in late antiquity, where it perished in a fire.

## The Temple of Artemis at Ephesus

The Temple of Artemis at Ephesus, on the western coast of what is now Turkey, was one of the largest buildings in the ancient world and was destroyed and rebuilt multiple times during antiquity. The temple best known to classical antiquity — the one on the canonical list — was built around 550 BC with the patronage of the Lydian king Croesus, destroyed by arson in 356 BC (on the night, according to a story probably invented for irony, that Alexander the Great was born), and rebuilt on an even larger scale. This version stood until it was sacked by the Goths in 262 AD.

Almost nothing remains above ground at the site, which lies within the modern Turkish city of Selçuk near Ephesus. A single reconstructed column stands in the marshy field that covers the ancient platform, allowing visitors to understand the scale and location of the structure. The Ephesus Archaeological Museum holds sculptures recovered from the site. The wider site of Ephesus itself, with its extraordinary state of preservation, is one of Turkey's most visited ancient sites.

## The Mausoleum at Halicarnassus

The Mausoleum was the tomb of Mausolus, the Persian-appointed satrap of Caria, built after his death in 353 BC in the capital of his territory, present-day Bodrum on the Turkish Aegean coast. His wife (and sister) Artemisia II, who ruled after him, commissioned the structure; its design combined Greek, Egyptian, and Anatolian architectural elements in a way that reflected Caria's position at the intersection of multiple cultural influences. The building was so celebrated that 'mausoleum' became the generic term for any large ornate tomb.

The structure was progressively dismantled during the medieval period, with stones reused in the Knights Hospitaller castle that still dominates the Bodrum waterfront. Sculptures and architectural fragments were recovered during excavations in the 1850s by Charles Newton and are now in the British Museum; Danish excavations from the 1960s onward have further clarified the structure's plan and the platform on which it stood. The Bodrum Museum of Underwater Archaeology incorporates some material from the site, and a small archaeological park marks the location.

## The Colossus of Rhodes

The bronze statue of the sun god Helios erected at the harbour of Rhodes around 280 BC — after the city successfully resisted a siege by Demetrius Poliorcetes and used the abandoned siege equipment as material for the statue — was probably the largest bronze statue in antiquity, standing roughly 30 metres tall. The popular image of it straddling the harbour entrance, with ships passing between its legs, is a medieval invention; ancient sources do not describe its location precisely, and modern scholarship suggests it stood near (but not astride) the harbour mouth.

The Colossus was toppled by an earthquake around 226 BC, after standing for only about 54 years. Its ruins lay on the ground for centuries, reportedly visible and visited, until they were eventually sold as scrap metal. Nothing survives. The site of Rhodes is now one of the best-preserved medieval walled cities in the Mediterranean, with the ancient harbour at its heart.

## The Lighthouse of Alexandria

The Lighthouse of Alexandria, built on the island of Pharos at the entrance to Alexandria's harbour during the reign of Ptolemy II Philadelphus (early 3rd century BC), was for many centuries the tallest human-made structure in the world after the Great Pyramid. Ancient sources describe it as standing between 100 and 140 metres tall, built in three stages (square, octagonal, and circular), topped by a fire whose light was reflected and amplified by polished metal mirrors. It functioned as a working lighthouse for perhaps a thousand years before being damaged by earthquakes in the medieval period.

The remains of the Lighthouse's lower courses, identified by underwater archaeology, lie in the eastern harbour of Alexandria, now occupied by the 15th-century Citadel of Qaitbay. Some of the citadel's stones are ancient blocks recovered from the lighthouse and its precincts. The bay itself is an active archaeological zone; underwater surveys since the 1990s have mapped hundreds of stone blocks on the seabed.

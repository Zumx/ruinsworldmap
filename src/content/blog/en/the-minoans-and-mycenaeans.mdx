## The Aegean Before Greece

The civilisations we call Minoan and Mycenaean are the direct predecessors of the Greek world familiar from Homer and Herodotus, yet they inhabited a Bronze Age world that was in many respects alien to classical antiquity. They wrote in scripts that Greek could not read until the mid-20th century; they built palaces on a scale that later Greeks regarded with bewilderment; and they participated in a network of long-distance exchange reaching Egypt, the Levant, Mesopotamia and central Europe. Understanding them requires setting aside the inherited classical framework and approaching the evidence — archaeological, textual, artistic — on its own terms.

The names themselves are modern inventions. 'Minoan' was coined by Arthur Evans, the British archaeologist who excavated Knossos from 1900 onward, after the legendary King Minos of Crete. 'Mycenaean' derives from Mycenae in the Argolid, the most prominent Bronze Age citadel on the mainland, excavated by Heinrich Schliemann from 1876. The actual names these peoples used for themselves, if they had collective ethnic identities in the modern sense, are unknown.

## Minoan Crete: The Palace Civilisation

The Minoan civilisation flourished on Crete from roughly 3000 BC, with its peak palatial period running from about 1900 to 1450 BC. Several large palaces — Knossos, Phaistos, Malia, Zakros, and the more recently excavated Galatas — served as administrative, economic, religious and redistributive centres for their surrounding regions. These were not palaces in the medieval European sense of fortified royal residences; the Minoan palaces had no defensive walls, a striking absence suggesting either unusual political stability or a reliance on naval power that made land-based fortification unnecessary.

Knossos near Heraklion in northern Crete is the largest and best-known. Covering roughly 20,000 square metres and containing several hundred rooms on multiple floors, it was occupied from the Neolithic period onward and reached its greatest extent in the Neopalatial period (roughly 1700–1450 BC). The palace's multistorey construction, its complex of storerooms containing enormous storage jars (pithoi) for oil, wine and grain, its light wells and drainage system, and its extraordinary frescoes depicting bull-leaping ceremonies, marine life, processions and ritual scenes together convey a society of considerable sophistication. Evans's extensive reconstruction — applying concrete to preserve and stabilise the ruins — has been both invaluable for preservation and controversial for its speculative visual interpretations, but the site remains essential.

Phaistos in the Mesara plain of southern Crete, excavated by Italian archaeologists from 1900 onward, presents the palace architecture in a less restored and arguably more honest state. The famous Phaistos Disc, found there in 1908, is a baked clay disc inscribed on both sides in a spiral arrangement with stamped signs in an undeciphered script unlike either of Crete's two known Bronze Age writing systems.

Minoan writing appears in two forms: the older and completely undeciphered Linear A, used for administrative and religious texts; and Linear B, which is Minoan script adapted to write an early form of Greek, used by Mycenaean administrators and the subject of Michael Ventris's decipherment in 1952.

## The Eruption of Thera

The volcanic island of Thera (modern Santorini) lies roughly 100 kilometres north of Crete. Around the middle of the second millennium BC — the precise date is contested, with estimates ranging from roughly 1628 BC (based on ice-core data) to around 1525 BC (based on conventional Egyptian archaeological chronology) — Thera's volcanic caldera erupted in one of the largest eruptions of the Holocene. The eruption destroyed the Minoan settlement of Akrotiri, burying it under volcanic ash and pumice and preserving it in extraordinary condition.

Akrotiri, excavated from 1967 onward by Spyridon Marinatos and subsequent teams, reveals a prosperous Bronze Age town with multi-storey buildings, sophisticated drainage and plumbing, indoor staircases, and some of the finest frescoes from the Bronze Age world — including the celebrated Spring Fresco with its swallows among red lilies, the Boxing Children, and the miniature Ship Fresco depicting a coastal journey between towns. The volcanic ash preserved organic materials, plaster wall surfaces and architectural detail that have long since eroded elsewhere.

The Thera eruption almost certainly disrupted Minoan civilisation on Crete, though the precise relationship between the eruption and the subsequent decline of the Minoan palatial system around 1450 BC remains debated. A tsunami, atmospheric effects and trade disruption were all possible consequences.

## Mycenaean Greece: Warrior Palaces

The Mycenaean civilisation of mainland Greece flourished from roughly 1600 to 1200 BC, reaching its height in the Late Bronze Age between about 1400 and 1200 BC. It grew partly under Minoan cultural influence — Mycenaean elites adopted Minoan artistic styles, administrative techniques including Linear B, and trade connections — but developed a distinctly different character: militaristic, hierarchical, focused on the accumulation and display of weapons and precious metals.

Mycenae in the Argolid was the most prominent centre, its hilltop citadel enclosed by massive Cyclopean walls of limestone blocks, some weighing many tonnes, built around 1350–1200 BC. The Lion Gate, the main entrance of the citadel, is the only surviving monumental relief sculpture from Bronze Age mainland Europe: two lions (or possibly griffins — the heads are lost) flanking a central column above the lintel, set in a relieving triangle that distributes the weight of the superstructure. Inside the walls lie Grave Circle A, excavated by Schliemann, and the palace itself, of which the megaron — a large columned hall with a central circular hearth — is the central feature.

The Treasury of Atreus (also called the Tomb of Agamemnon), a tholos tomb outside the citadel walls built around 1250 BC, is the most impressive surviving example of Mycenaean corbel-vaulted architecture. Its corbelled entrance passage (dromos) is nearly 36 metres long; the tomb chamber itself has a beehive-shaped vault roughly 14 metres high and 14 metres in diameter, built without mortar from precisely fitted limestone blocks. A smaller side chamber was cut from the living rock. The tomb was already robbed in antiquity, but its architectural achievement is self-evident. UNESCO inscribed the Archaeological Sites of Mycenae and Tiryns in 1999.

Tiryns, a lower citadel on a limestone outcrop in the Argolid plain, has even more massive walls than Mycenae in places — their thickness reaching up to 7 metres — and contains galleries corbelled in a style reminiscent of the Treasury of Atreus, used for storage or as sally ports. The palace megaron at Tiryns was decorated with frescoes depicting bull-leaping and hunting, clearly showing Minoan artistic influence.

Pylos on the southwestern Peloponnese, excavated from 1939 onward by Carl Blegen, preserved its Linear B archive in a dramatic final form: hundreds of clay tablets recording palace administration — inventories of bronze, records of land tenure, requisitions for rowers and watchmen — that had been baked hard in the fire that destroyed the palace around 1180 BC. Without that fire, the tablets would simply have dissolved back into clay. The archive is the largest single collection of Linear B tablets from a single site.

## Linear B and What It Tells Us

The decipherment of Linear B by the British architect Michael Ventris in 1952, confirmed by subsequent scholarship, revealed that the language was an archaic form of Greek — the earliest known Greek texts, predating Homer by about five centuries. The tablets are exclusively administrative: they record palace transactions, not literature or history. But they tell us a great deal about palace organisation: the names of landholders, the quantities of wool, bronze and grain in palace storerooms, the identities of craftsmen and workers, the recording of offerings to gods whose names (Poseidon, Dionysus, Zeus, Hera, Ares) are recognisable from later Greek religion.

The Mycenaean world ended abruptly around 1200 BC as part of the Bronze Age Collapse. The palaces were burned, Linear B disappeared along with the administrative class that used it, long-distance trade collapsed, and population declined sharply. The 'Greek Dark Ages' that followed lasted roughly three centuries before the city-state world of classical Greece began to emerge.

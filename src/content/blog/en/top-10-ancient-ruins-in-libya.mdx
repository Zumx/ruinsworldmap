Libya's Mediterranean coast contains some of the most spectacular Roman ruins in the world, yet they remain among the least visited of any UNESCO World Heritage Sites simply because of geography and, in recent decades, political instability. The ancient Greek and Phoenician settlements that preceded Rome established three great cities — Oea (Tripoli), Sabratha, and Leptis Magna in Tripolitania; Cyrene and its associated towns in Cyrenaica to the east — on a strip of fertile coast between the Sahara and the sea. What Rome built on these foundations is extraordinary: Leptis Magna grew into one of the empire's most lavishly endowed cities under its native son the emperor Septimius Severus, while Cyrene, a Greek city since the seventh century BCE, acquired a Roman imperial overlay that has preserved Greek temples, a theatre, and a sanctuary of Apollo in a state rivalling sites on the Greek mainland.

## 1. Leptis Magna, Tripolitania

Leptis Magna is the crown jewel of North African Roman archaeology. A Phoenician trading post from roughly the seventh century BCE, it became a major Roman city and produced the emperor Septimius Severus (reigned 193–211 CE), who lavished resources on his hometown. His additions — a new forum, a four-way triumphal arch, a covered market (macellum), and a vast Severan basilica — transformed an already substantial city into one of the Roman world's showpieces. The earlier Roman fabric includes a theatre built in the reign of Augustus, an amphitheatre, circus, Hadrianic baths of palace-like scale, and the Arch of Trajan. The harbour still has its lighthouse base and quayside. Because Leptis was abandoned in antiquity rather than rebuilt over, the ruins are exceptionally extensive and largely unbuilt-over — entire city blocks of colonnaded streets are still standing. A UNESCO World Heritage Site. Currently access requires joining an organised tour with a Libyan tour operator.

## 2. Sabratha, Tripolitania

Sabratha was another Phoenician foundation, positioned on the coast west of Tripoli. Its Roman theatre, built in the late second century CE and extensively restored by Italian archaeologists in the 1920s, is one of the three finest surviving Roman theatre facades in the world — three storeys of carved marble columns and figured friezes facing the stage with the Mediterranean visible behind. The rest of the city includes a forum, a capitolium, a Basilica of Justinian rebuilt over the Roman forum in the Byzantine period, multiple baths, and a Phoenician-period sacred precinct. The site is accessible — it sits close to the modern coastal road — and easier to visit independently than Leptis Magna, though the current security environment still makes joining an organised tour advisable. A UNESCO World Heritage Site.

## 3. Cyrene, Cyrenaica (Jabal al-Akhdar)

Cyrene was the oldest and most important Greek city in North Africa, founded by colonists from Thera (modern Santorini) around 631 BCE. It remained essentially a Greek city with Greek institutions — a Doric temple of Apollo founded in the seventh century BCE, a theatre, a gymnasium, and the famous Sanctuary of Apollo with its spring — even after its incorporation into the Roman empire. Roman additions include a forum and a massive capitolium. The extensive necropolis cut into the cliffs below the plateau is one of the largest in North Africa, with thousands of rock-cut tombs spanning the archaic, classical, Hellenistic, and Roman periods. The museum at the site held important sculptures before civil conflict disrupted its operation. A UNESCO World Heritage Site.

## 4. Ptolemais, Cyrenaica

Ptolemais was founded in the Ptolemaic period — probably in the third century BCE — as the port city for the Cyrenaican interior. Under the Romans it became the capital of the province of Libya Superior. Its ruins include a colonnaded street, a palace of columns, a large cistern system, two small odeons, and a villa with unusually well-preserved floor mosaics. The site lies partly under the modern town of Tolmeita but enough is exposed to give a sense of its Hellenistic-Roman urban character. Less visited than Cyrene or Leptis Magna, it is an important supplement for understanding the full range of ancient urbanism along the Cyrenaican coast.

## 5. Apollonia (Marsa Susa), Cyrenaica

Apollonia served as the port of Cyrene, linked to the mother city by a road across the plateau. The ancient harbour — partially submerged due to coastal subsidence since antiquity — preserves quay walls, warehouses, and towers visible at low tide. On the land side, three Byzantine churches with mosaic floors, a palace complex interpreted as the seat of the Byzantine governor, a theatre, and substantial stretches of city walls survive above ground. The combination of partly underwater harbour archaeology and well-preserved Byzantine civic architecture makes Apollonia distinct from the other Cyrenaican sites. The town is small, the setting on the bay is beautiful, and the site museum is housed in a former governor's palace.

## 6. Tocra (Taucheira), Cyrenaica

Taucheira was one of the five cities of the ancient Pentapolis of Cyrenaica. Its Greek foundations in the fifth century BCE were followed by Hellenistic, Roman, and Byzantine phases. City walls, traces of a harbour, and scattered architectural remains survive above ground, though the site has received less systematic excavation than the larger Cyrenaican centres. The Byzantine-period structures include parts of a basilica. Tocra is primarily of interest to specialists and those following the Cyrenaican circuit comprehensively, but it fills in the settlement pattern of the ancient Pentapolis.

## 7. Garama (Germa), Fezzan

Deep in the Saharan interior of the Fezzan region, Garama was the capital of the Garamantian civilisation, a Saharan culture that flourished from roughly the first millennium BCE through to around the seventh century CE. The Garamantes built an elaborate underground irrigation system — foggaras, horizontal tunnels tapping fossil water — that sustained agriculture across an otherwise inhospitable landscape. The capital site at Germa preserves ruins of buildings, cemeteries with above-ground tombstones, and the landscape of the ancient foggaras whose traces are visible on the surface. The nearby Museum of the Fezzan in Germa houses finds from the region. Access requires a significant overland journey into the Sahara, making this a site for adventurous and well-prepared travellers; it is not part of the standard Libyan archaeological circuit.

## 8. Ghirza, Tripolitania Hinterland

Ghirza is a Roman-period farm and funerary complex in the pre-desert zone south of Tripoli, dating from roughly the third to fifth centuries CE. It is one of the best-preserved examples of the rich agricultural estates that the Roman empire supported in what is now semi-arid or arid land, a landscape much more productive under Roman land management. The standing mausolea — tower tombs with carved friezes depicting farming and hunting scenes — are among the finest examples of late Roman funerary art in Africa. The carved reliefs are remarkably detailed and show ploughing, camel caravans, and hunting with dogs. Access requires a four-wheel-drive vehicle and is best arranged with a specialist guide. The site is largely unprotected and fragile.

## 9. Slonta Sanctuary, Cyrenaica

Slonta is a rock-cut sanctuary in the mountains behind Cyrene, a site of pre-Greek and Libyan religious practice that continued into the Roman period. The sanctuary consists of carved rock faces with animals — lions, horses, reptiles — and votive niches cut into the stone. The carved figures have an archaic quality distinct from Greek or Roman art and represent one of the few surviving examples of the indigenous Libyan artistic tradition. The site is little visited and poorly documented compared to the major Greek and Roman sites of the region, but it provides a counterpoint to the Hellenic and Roman layers that dominate Cyrenaican archaeology.

## 10. Ras al-Hammam (Ancient Charax), Tripolitania Coast

Charax was a small coastal settlement east of Tripoli that has yielded significant Punic-period (Phoenician Carthaginian) and Roman remains in excavations. The site is less developed for visitors than Leptis Magna or Sabratha but represents the intermediate scale of coastal settlement that characterised the ancient Tripolitanian shore — not great cities but trading stations and agricultural estates that linked the Mediterranean network to the Saharan caravan routes inland.

---

Libya's remarkable ancient sites have been largely inaccessible to independent travellers for over a decade due to the political situation following 2011. The situation remains fluid; check current Foreign Office or State Department travel advisories before planning any visit. When travel is feasible, tours are operated by specialist companies through Tripoli or Benghazi. Leptis Magna and Sabratha are the primary reasons to make the journey, and the condition of UNESCO monitoring for these World Heritage Sites is an ongoing international concern.

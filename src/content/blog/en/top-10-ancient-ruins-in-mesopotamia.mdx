Mesopotamia — the land between the Tigris and Euphrates rivers, corresponding broadly to modern Iraq — is where urban civilisation began. The first cities, the first writing, the first codified law, the first recorded literature: all emerged in this alluvial plain between roughly 3500 and 2000 BCE. The Sumerians built Uruk into the world's first true city; the Akkadian, Babylonian, and Assyrian empires rose and fell in succession across the following two millennia; and Alexander the Great died in Babylon in 323 BCE attempting to make it the capital of his empire. What survives today is largely in the form of mud-brick tells — mounds of accumulated ancient settlement — because the dominant building material here was unfired brick, not stone. Where stone was available or imported, it was carved with an artistry that influenced every subsequent tradition in Western Asia.

## 1. Babylon, Central Iraq

Babylon was the greatest city of the ancient Near East across much of the second and first millennia BCE. Under Hammurabi (reigned roughly 1792–1750 BCE) it became the centre of a law-giving empire; under Nebuchadnezzar II (reigned 605–562 BCE) it was rebuilt as perhaps the largest city on earth, with the Ishtar Gate — faced in glazed blue tiles with relief figures of bulls and dragons — the Processional Way, a massive ziggurat (the probable inspiration for the Tower of Babel story), and palace complexes of extraordinary scale. The Ishtar Gate itself was removed by German archaeologists in the early twentieth century and is now in the Pergamon Museum in Berlin; a partial reconstruction stands at the site. Babylon was a UNESCO World Heritage Site inscription finally achieved in 2019. Access requires travel to the site south of Hillah in central Iraq.

## 2. Ur, Southern Iraq

Ur was one of the great Sumerian city-states, at its height around 2100–2000 BCE when the Third Dynasty of Ur controlled much of Mesopotamia under kings including Ur-Nammu and Shulgi. The most visible survival is the Ziggurat of Ur-Nammu, a massive three-tiered mud-brick structure built around 2100 BCE and partially restored. Its lower two stages stand to most of their original height, making it the best-preserved ziggurat in Mesopotamia. The Royal Cemetery, excavated by Leonard Woolley in the 1920s, produced 'death pits' where attendants were buried with their rulers, along with extraordinary gold and lapis lazuli objects now in the British Museum and the National Museum of Iraq. The site is near the US Tallil air base at Nasiriyah and has been accessible to visitors in recent years.

## 3. Uruk (Warka), Southern Iraq

Uruk was the world's first city, reaching a population of perhaps 40,000 to 80,000 people by around 3100 BCE — larger than any other settlement on earth at the time. The invention of writing, probably around 3400–3200 BCE, occurred here as an accounting tool for managing the city's resources. Uruk's two temple precincts — the Anu ziggurat precinct and the Eanna precinct — were the religious centre of the city; the Eanna produced some of the earliest monumental architecture known, including columned halls whose brick columns were faced with coloured clay cone mosaics. The site is an enormous mound complex; the German Archaeological Institute has conducted excavations here intermittently for over a century. Surface remains are modest to the non-specialist eye, but the scale of the mound system conveys the city's ancient mass. Access is from Samawah in southern Iraq.

## 4. Nineveh, Northern Iraq (near Mosul)

Nineveh was the final capital of the Assyrian empire, reaching its greatest extent under Sennacherib (reigned 705–681 BCE) and Ashurbanipal (reigned 669–627 BCE). Sennacherib called it 'the city without rival' and built a palace of 80 rooms decorated with carved stone reliefs — the 'Southwest Palace' whose lion-hunt reliefs are among the masterpieces of ancient art, now largely in the British Museum. The city walls enclosed an area of around 750 hectares with fifteen gates; two gates have been reconstructed on the original foundations. Nineveh was damaged by Islamic State in 2014–2016, with the reconstructed gates and the Mosul Museum both attacked. Recovery and stabilisation work is ongoing. The site is within the modern city of Mosul, now under Iraqi government control.

## 5. Hatra, Northern Iraq

Hatra was the capital of a semi-independent Arab kingdom within the Parthian sphere, flourishing from roughly the first century BCE to the third century CE when it was destroyed by the Sassanid Persian king Shapur I around 241 CE. The city's remarkable survival stems from its circular plan — an inner and outer circuit of walls with towers — and particularly its great sanctuary complex at the centre: a series of iwans (arched reception halls) facing a central courtyard, whose carved decoration blends Parthian, Hellenistic, Roman, and Mesopotamian artistic elements in an eclectic synthesis unique to the Hatrene cultural zone. The sculptures found at Hatra, with their frontally posed figures in elaborate clothing, defined a style that influenced later Palmyrene and Byzantine art. A UNESCO World Heritage Site severely damaged by Islamic State in 2015; recovery work is in progress.

## 6. Ctesiphon (Taq Kasra), Central Iraq

Ctesiphon was the Parthian and then Sassanid Persian capital on the eastern bank of the Tigris, south of modern Baghdad. Its most famous survival is the Taq Kasra, the great arch of the Sassanid royal palace built probably in the third or fourth century CE — the largest single-span brick arch built in antiquity, its inner face soaring to around 35 metres. The rest of the palace has largely collapsed or been robbed for brick, but the arch and its flanking wings remain an overwhelming presence in the flat Mesopotamian plain. The structure is fragile and partially damaged; conservation has been a recurrent concern. It is accessible as a day trip from Baghdad.

## 7. Nimrud (Kalhu), Northern Iraq

Nimrud was the Assyrian capital under Ashurnasirpal II and Shalmaneser III in the ninth century BCE. Ashurnasirpal built a palace whose entrance was flanked by colossal winged human-headed lions and bulls (lamassu), and whose interior walls were lined with carved stone reliefs depicting military campaigns and royal hunts. These reliefs, mostly removed to museums in the nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, are among the defining works of Assyrian art. The site's treasuries, excavated by British and Iraqi teams, yielded gold jewellery and carved ivories of extraordinary quality. The site was severely looted and the museum bulldozed by Islamic State in 2015. Reconstruction and stabilisation efforts have begun under UNESCO's coordination. The site is south of Mosul.

## 8. Assur (Qal'at Sherqat), Northern Iraq

Assur was the original capital and sacred city of the Assyrian empire, inhabited from at least the third millennium BCE and serving as the religious heart of Assyria even after political capitals moved elsewhere. The remains of multiple temples to Assur (the state god), Ishtar, and other deities, a ziggurat, and royal tombs with extraordinarily rich furnishings occupy a promontory above the Tigris. German excavations in the early twentieth century established the basic sequence; Iraqi excavations since the 1970s have added to the picture. A UNESCO World Heritage Site inscribed in 2003, simultaneously placed on the List of World Heritage in Danger due to the upstream Makhoul dam project. The site is in Saladin Governorate in northern Iraq.

## 9. Eridu (Abu Shahrain), Southern Iraq

Eridu is the oldest city in the world in the Sumerian tradition, believed to be the first city founded by the gods. Archaeological evidence confirms it as one of the earliest settled sites in southern Mesopotamia, with occupation from around 5400 BCE. Eighteen successive levels of a temple building have been excavated on the main mound, growing progressively more elaborate over millennia — a stratigraphic sequence of religious architecture unmatched in continuity anywhere in the ancient world. The small ziggurat of the Third Dynasty of Ur period is the most visible surviving structure. Eridu is in a remote desert area near Nasiriyah; access is difficult without a guide and appropriate vehicle.

## 10. Kish (Tell Ingharra and Tell Uhaimir), Central Iraq

Kish was one of the leading Sumerian city-states of the Early Dynastic period (roughly 2900–2350 BCE) and is described in the Sumerian King List as the first city to receive kingship after the Flood. The twin mounds of Tell Ingharra and Tell Uhaimir cover the ancient city's different precincts. The plano-convex brick palace excavated at Tell Ingharra in the 1920s–30s is one of the earliest monumental secular buildings documented in Mesopotamia. Kish is southeast of Babylon in the Babil Governorate. Like most Mesopotamian sites beyond the main visitor circuit, it is best visited as part of an organised tour focused on southern Iraqi archaeology.

---

Visiting Mesopotamian sites requires joining an organised tour through a licensed Iraqi tour operator; independent travel is not advisable in most areas due to security considerations, and some sites require military or police escort. The security situation varies by governorate and changes; specialist tour companies that focus on Iraq archaeology provide the most up-to-date access information. The Iraq Museum in Baghdad, which has been substantially restored after the 2003 looting, displays the finest portable objects from these sites — the gold of Ur, the carved ivories of Nimrud, the cuneiform tablets of Nineveh — and should be a first stop for context.

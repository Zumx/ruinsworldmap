Jordan's territory sits at the crossroads of the ancient world — the land bridge between Africa and Asia, the eastern limit of Greek and Roman expansion, a zone where Nabataean, Byzantine, and early Islamic cultures overlapped and sometimes merged. The result is an archaeological record of unusual range: Neolithic villages among the earliest known, Bronze Age cities mentioned in Egyptian texts, a rock-cut Nabataean capital that became one of the most famous sites on earth, and a string of Roman Decapolis cities whose theatre facades still stand twenty metres high. The country's relatively small size means that most of these sites are within a few hours of Amman, and the national infrastructure for visiting them is well developed. What follows are ten sites that best represent this diversity.

## 1. Petra, Ma'an Governorate

The Nabataean capital, carved and built among the rose-red sandstone mountains of southern Jordan, was at its height between roughly the first century BCE and the first century CE, when the Nabataean kingdom controlled the trade in aromatics between Arabia and the Mediterranean. The approach through the Siq — a kilometre-long natural gorge with walls rising to 80 metres — opens to the Treasury (Al-Khazneh), a tomb facade cut directly from the cliff face around the first century CE. Beyond it, several kilometres of royal tombs, a colonnaded Roman street, a Byzantine church with mosaic floors, a large theatre, high-place sacrificial altars, and a monastery (Ad-Deir) accessible via 800 cut stone steps fill a landscape of extraordinary geological drama. A UNESCO World Heritage Site. The Jordan Pass includes entry and is worth buying before arrival.

## 2. Jerash (Gerasa), Jerash Governorate

Jerash is among the best-preserved Roman provincial cities in the world. Founded as a Hellenistic settlement and refounded as a Roman colonia, it reached its peak between the first and third centuries CE. The oval Forum, two theatres (one still used for performances), a hippodrome, two colonnaded streets intersecting at a tetrapylon, multiple temples including the Temple of Artemis with its famously responsive columns, and a triumphant arch built to receive Hadrian in 129 CE all survive at a scale that makes the city's prosperity and ambition legible. Excavation and restoration are ongoing; the site is large enough to require most of a day. The Jerash Festival of Culture and Arts brings performances into the ancient theatre each summer.

## 3. Umm Qais (Gadara), Northern Jordan

Ancient Gadara occupied a ridge above the Yarmouk River gorge with views extending to the Sea of Galilee, the Golan Heights, and on clear days to Mount Hermon. A member of the Decapolis league of Greek cities, it was particularly known in antiquity for its poets and philosophers. The ruins include a colonnaded street of black basalt, two theatres, a nymphaeum, and extensive tombs cut into the hillside. The western theatre has been partially restored. A small Ottoman village built directly over the ancient city in the nineteenth century — itself now abandoned — adds an unusual layer to the site's archaeology. The panoramic position and the black basalt stonework give Umm Qais a distinctive atmosphere quite unlike Jerash.

## 4. Ajloun Castle (Qal'at ar-Rabad), Ajloun

Built in 1184–1185 CE by one of Saladin's generals, Izz al-Din Usama, Ajloun Castle was a Muslim military response to the Crusader fortifications that had established a chain of castles across the Levant. It commands the hills north of the Ajloun forest, overlooking three wadis and the routes between the Jordan Valley and Syria. The castle was expanded several times in the thirteenth century and served as an administrative centre under Mamluk rule. It is a well-preserved example of Ayyubid military architecture: round corner towers, a dry moat, drawbridge slots, arrow slits, and a gatehouse sequence designed to funnel attackers into killing zones. The forested setting and the views over olive groves and wadis are part of the experience.

## 5. Qasr Amra, Zarqa Governorate

Qasr Amra is the most important surviving example of early Islamic secular architecture. Built in the early eighth century CE, probably as a bathhouse and hunting lodge for an Umayyad prince (possibly the caliph Walid I), it is a modest complex of three barrel-vaulted halls and a private bathhouse. What makes it extraordinary is the interior: the walls and ceiling are covered in fresco paintings depicting hunting scenes, bathing women, musical entertainments, craftsmen, and — most famously — six kings of the known world (including the Byzantine emperor and the Sassanid shah) paying tribute to the Umayyad ruler. The astronomical ceiling of the caldarium is one of the earliest known representations of the zodiac in a spherical projection. A UNESCO World Heritage Site. Entry is part of the Desert Castles loop from Amman.

## 6. Umm ar-Rasas, Madaba Governorate

Umm ar-Rasas was a Roman garrison camp (Kastron Mefaa) that developed into a substantial Byzantine and early Islamic town. What distinguishes it is a remarkable collection of mosaic floors: the Church of Saint Stephen has a near-complete mosaic pavement depicting cities of the Nile Delta, the Holy Land, and Jordan in medallions with Greek labels — a kind of map in tesserae comparable to the better-known Madara map nearby. A tall isolated tower of unknown function (possibly used by stylite monks) stands in the plain outside the main ruins. A UNESCO World Heritage Site. The site is off the main tourist circuit and consequently quiet; access requires a car from Madaba or Amman.

## 7. Karak Castle (Kerak), Karak Governorate

Karak Castle is one of the great Crusader fortifications of the Levant. Built by Pagan the Butler, lord of Transjordan, around 1140–1142 CE on a ridge that drops steeply to the valleys below, it commanded the King's Highway trade route. It saw sieges by Saladin in 1183 and 1184 before finally falling in 1188. The Mamluk sultan Baybars subsequently strengthened it in the thirteenth century, adding the lower courtyard. The castle's internal corridors, Crusader hall, dungeon chambers, and the contrast between Crusader ashlar construction and Mamluk tower additions are clearly visible. The attached museum displays inscriptions and architectural finds from the site and the surrounding region.

## 8. Machaerus (Mukawir), Madaba Governorate

Machaerus was built as a hilltop fortress by the Hasmonean king Alexander Jannaeus in the late second century BCE and later rebuilt and expanded by Herod the Great. It commands dramatic views across the Dead Sea to the hills of Judaea. The site appears in Josephus and — according to later tradition — is where John the Baptist was imprisoned and executed. The Herodian palace complex on the summit has been partially uncovered in recent Italian-led excavations that have identified a triclinium (dining hall), a throne room, and associated courtyard. Most of the superstructure is gone, but the promontory setting — a cone of brown rock above sweeping desert valleys — and the outline of the fortifications make for a compelling visit, particularly at sunset.

## 9. Pella (Tabaqat Fahl), Irbid Governorate

Pella is one of the longest-occupied sites in Jordan, with continuous habitation traceable from the Chalcolithic period through to the Islamic period — roughly 6,000 years. Bronze Age Canaanite temples, an Egyptian presence in the late Bronze Age, a Greek Hellenistic city with a colonnaded street, and Byzantine churches are all present in the stratigraphy. The site is well studied by Australian archaeological teams over many decades. The remains on the surface are less dramatic than Jerash or Petra — column stumps, a nymphaeum basin, terrace walls — but the sweep of the Jordan Valley visible from the tell, and the documented depth of human presence here, make it an archaeologically important stop.

## 10. Wadi Rum Rock Inscriptions and Nabataean Remains, Aqaba Governorate

Wadi Rum is known primarily as a desert landscape, but the sandstone cliffs throughout the valley are inscribed with Thamudic, Nabataean, and early Arabic texts and rock drawings dating from roughly the fourth century BCE through the early centuries CE. Nabataean caravans passed through here and left dedications at natural springs and water collection points. The inscriptions record names, prayers, and routes; some panels are dense with layered texts from different centuries. The Nabataean temple at Qasr Ghul and the spring enclosures show the infrastructure that supported caravan traffic across this desert. The landscape is a UNESCO World Heritage Site as a cultural and natural serial property. Access is by 4WD with local Bedouin guides who know the rock-art locations.

---

Jordan's major sites are well served by the Jordan Pass, which covers entry to Petra and most national archaeological sites alongside the visa fee and is the most economical option for most visitors. Hiring a car gives access to sites off the main bus routes such as Umm ar-Rasas and Machaerus. Spring (March to May) and autumn (September to November) are ideal seasons: the heat is manageable and the light on sandstone is extraordinary in late afternoon.

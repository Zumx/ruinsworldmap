Nigeria is one of the most archaeologically rich countries in Africa, yet its ancient sites remain far less visited than their Egyptian or East African counterparts. The evidence of deep human occupation ranges from the Nok terracotta tradition of the first millennium BCE, among the earliest figurative sculpture traditions in sub-Saharan Africa, to the massive earthwork systems of the Benin Kingdom and the Sungbo's Eredo complex, which rank among the largest pre-modern engineering projects on the continent. The forest belt of the south, the savanna and Sahel of the north, and the Nok plateau each produced distinct cultural sequences, and Nigerian archaeology continues to overturn earlier assumptions about African state formation and metallurgy. These ten sites introduce the country's remarkable heritage.

## 1. Sungbo's Eredo, Ogun State

Sungbo's Eredo is a network of earthen ramparts and ditches enclosing roughly 500 square kilometres of forest south of Ijebu Ode in Ogun State. The outer boundary wall reaches heights of around 20 metres with a corresponding ditch, making it one of the largest single earthwork constructions in pre-colonial Africa — larger in enclosed area than the Great Wall of China's defended zones. Radiocarbon dating places its construction primarily between the 10th and 13th centuries CE, associated with the Ijebu Yoruba state. The complex is traditionally attributed to Bilikisu Sungbo, a wealthy Yoruba queen, though her identification with Bilqis of Sheba by some local traditions is a later conflation. The site is largely forested and undeveloped, presenting an extraordinary landscape of overgrown ramparts accessible on foot or motorcycle from nearby villages.

## 2. Benin City Walls, Edo State

The earthworks of the Benin Kingdom in present-day Edo State formed a system of hundreds of inter-connected ditches and ramparts surrounding Benin City and its surrounding towns, covering an estimated 6,500 kilometres of boundary in total. Built primarily from roughly the 13th century onwards and expanded through the 16th century during the kingdom's imperial period, these earthworks served administrative and military functions, defining territories and controlling movement rather than simply walling a city. The main city wall of Benin itself, known as the Iya, once stood over 17 metres high. Though most of the outer earthwork system has been destroyed by urban expansion, sections of the main city wall and inner enclosures survive within and around Benin City. The Benin National Museum holds the context for understanding the earthworks alongside the famous Benin Bronzes.

## 3. Igbo-Ukwu, Anambra State

Igbo-Ukwu, in Anambra State in southeastern Nigeria, yielded one of the most spectacular archaeological discoveries in West Africa when, in 1938 and later excavations in 1959–1960, Thurstan Shaw uncovered a shrine repository, a burial chamber, and a disposal pit containing thousands of bronze, copper, ivory, and pottery objects. The bronze castings — including elaborate ceremonial vessels, a fly-whisk handle, and a pendant — were made using the lost-wax technique to a standard of technical virtuosity that astonished the archaeological world. Radiocarbon dating places the assemblage to around the 9th century CE, predating the better-known Benin bronzes by several centuries. Igbo-Ukwu provides evidence for an early high-status political centre in Igboland with connections to trans-Saharan trade in copper. The objects are now held in the National Museum, Lagos, and the Jos Museum.

## 4. Nok Culture Sites, Kaduna/Plateau States

The Nok culture, named after the village in Kaduna State where the first terracotta figurine fragments were recovered in 1928, produced a tradition of fired clay sculpture lasting from roughly 1500 BCE to 200 CE — among the earliest known in sub-Saharan Africa. The figures are characterised by stylised triangular eyes, elaborate hairstyles, and detailed depictions of jewellery, suggesting a society with differentiated social roles and significant artistic traditions. The culture also provides early evidence of iron smelting in West Africa, from around 500 BCE. Nok sites are spread across the Jos Plateau and the Kaduna River valley; the main deposits are largely below ground and not accessible as open-air monuments, but the Jos Museum holds an outstanding collection of figurines and provides the primary point of access for understanding the culture.

## 5. Sukur Cultural Landscape, Adamawa State

The Sukur Cultural Landscape, inscribed as Nigeria's only UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1999, occupies the Mandara Mountains in northeastern Nigeria near the Cameroon border. It is a palimpsest of hillside terracing, house ruins, forge sites, sacred groves, and the hilltop palace of the Hidi (chief), representing the Sukur Chiefdom's landscape from roughly the 16th to the 19th century CE. The site is remarkable for its integrity — the palace walls, the paved processional way leading to the summit, and the terraced fields are all unusually well preserved — and for the continuity between the archaeological landscape and the living Sukur community, some of whom still inhabit the lower terraces. The iron-smelting tradition that once defined Sukur's regional importance has now ceased, but the forge structures remain.

## 6. Old Oyo National Park, Oyo State

The ruins of Old Oyo (Oyo Ile or Katunga), the capital of the Oyo Empire from roughly the 14th century until its destruction and abandonment around 1836, lie within Old Oyo National Park in Oyo State. The site preserves earthen ramparts, palace foundations, and the outlines of the Gbongan (royal market) and residential compounds across a substantial area. At its height in the 17th and 18th centuries, the Oyo Empire was the dominant power in the western Sudan, with extensive trade networks and a cavalry-based military machine. The city walls enclosed an area of roughly 105 square kilometres. Today the site is overgrown and under-excavated, but aerial survey and limited ground investigation have confirmed its urban scale. Access is via the national park, and wildlife including roan antelope and buffalo shares the landscape.

## 7. Kano City Walls, Kano State

The mud-brick walls surrounding Kano's old city represent one of the most visible surviving examples of Hausa urban fortification. The original enclosure is attributed to the early Hausa period, with the major construction phase associated with the reign of Sarkin Kano Mohamman Rumfa in the 15th century CE. The walls at their greatest extent ran for roughly 21 kilometres, reaching heights of up to 12 metres with bastions and multiple gates. Significant sections of the wall, including the Kofar Mata and Kofar Nasarawa gates, survive within the modern city. The Gidan Makama museum in the old city and the Emir's Palace — the latter incorporating structures of considerable antiquity — provide further heritage context. Kano's surviving walls and historic core are among the best-preserved examples of Hausa urbanism.

## 8. Ife (Ile-Ife) Sites, Osun State

Ile-Ife, regarded in Yoruba tradition as the place of creation and the spiritual capital of the Yoruba world, has yielded some of the most remarkable naturalistic sculpture in African art history. Excavations in the 20th century, particularly at Ita Yemoo and Wunmonije Compound, recovered brass and terracotta heads of extraordinary technical quality, dated to roughly the 12th to 15th centuries CE. The brass castings show the same lost-wax technique that produced the Benin Bronzes, and there are strong stylistic and technical connections between the two traditions. The Ile-Ife Museum (Ife Museum of Natural History) holds the core collection. The city also contains ancient pavement sites — areas covered with potsherd pavements — that have been excavated and are partially visible within the urban fabric.

## 9. Daima Tell, Lake Chad Basin

The Daima tell — an ancient occupation mound — on the edge of the former Lake Chad in northeastern Nigeria was excavated by Graham Connah in the 1960s and revealed a stratified sequence of occupation from roughly 600 BCE to the 16th century CE, providing a rare long-term record of settlement in the Lake Chad basin. The lower levels produced evidence for a pre-iron-age herding and fishing economy that predates the development of the Kanem-Borno state; the upper levels showed iron use, cattle herding, and evidence of interaction with the early Kanem polity. The physical tell is a low mound in a flat, often flooded landscape and is not a visitor destination in any formal sense, but it is one of the key reference points for understanding state formation in the West African Sahel.

## 10. Birnin Gazargamo, Yobe State

Birnin Gazargamo, in Yobe State near the Niger border, was the capital of the Kanem-Borno Empire from roughly the 15th century until it was sacked by the Fulani warrior Usman dan Fodio's forces in 1808. At its height in the 16th and 17th centuries it was one of the largest cities in sub-Saharan Africa, reportedly housing tens of thousands of inhabitants and serving as a major node on trans-Saharan trade routes. The ruins today consist of extensive mud-brick walls, building foundations, and earthworks spread across a flat plain, with a mosque whose walls reach several metres in height still standing in the site's interior. The site is rarely visited by archaeologists and essentially unknown to international tourism, but it preserves the physical remains of one of the most durable political formations in African history.

Nigeria's ancient sites are spread across a huge and geographically diverse country with variable infrastructure. Lagos and Abuja are the main international gateways; most northern and middle-belt sites require domestic flights or long road journeys. The dry season (November to March) offers the most comfortable travel conditions and driest roads, particularly for the remoter sites in Yobe and Adamawa states. Security conditions in the northeast must be assessed before travel; Sungbo's Eredo, Igbo-Ukwu, and the southern sites present fewer logistical challenges.

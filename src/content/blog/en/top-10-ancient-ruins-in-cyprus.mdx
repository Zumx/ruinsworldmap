Cyprus occupies a pivotal position in the eastern Mediterranean, close enough to the coasts of Anatolia, the Levant, and Egypt to have absorbed cultural influences from all of them while developing its own distinctive civilization. The island was producing copper — its Latin name cuprum derives from the island's name — from at least 2500 BC, and this mineral wealth attracted successive waves of settlement, trade, and conquest: Mycenaean Greeks in the late Bronze Age, Phoenicians and Assyrians in the Iron Age, Persians, Ptolemaic Egyptians, Romans, Byzantines, Crusaders, Venetians, and Ottomans. Each left traces, and Cyprus holds an unusual density of major archaeological sites for an island of its size. [Open the map](/map) to explore the island's remarkable layered history.

## 1. Kourion, Limassol District

Kourion was a major city-kingdom of ancient Cyprus, probably founded by Mycenaean Greeks during the late Bronze Age colonization of the island (around 1200 BC) and remaining important through the early Byzantine period. The site's most dramatic features are Roman: an impressive theatre rebuilt in the 2nd century AD and restored to use in the 20th century, a House of Eustolios with exceptional late Roman mosaic floors, baths, and a basilica. The theatre sits on the edge of a cliff with panoramic views over the sea. A separate building, the House of the Gladiators, preserves mosaics depicting gladiatorial combat. Kourion is one of Cyprus's most visited and rewarding archaeological sites.

## 2. Salamis, Near Famagusta (Northern Cyprus)

Salamis was the dominant city-kingdom of ancient Cyprus from the early Iron Age through the late Roman period and is one of the most extensively excavated sites on the island. According to tradition, the city was founded by the hero Teucer after the Trojan War. The excavated remains include a large gymnasium with a palaestra whose colonnades have been partially re-erected, a theatre, baths, and the remains of a forum and agora. A Byzantine basilica of the 4th century AD, one of the largest in Cyprus, stands in the northern part of the site. The associated royal necropolis contains Bronze Age and Iron Age burial mounds of impressive scale, some yielding rich finds now in the Cyprus Museum. The site is in the northern part of Cyprus, accessible from Famagusta.

## 3. Paphos Archaeological Park, Paphos District

The Paphos Archaeological Park preserves one of the finest collections of Roman mosaic pavements in the eastern Mediterranean. The mosaics, found in a series of luxurious private houses of the 2nd–4th centuries AD, depict scenes from Greek mythology — the loves of Dionysus, Theseus and the Minotaur, Leda and the swan — with a technical virtuosity and narrative sophistication that places them among the masterpieces of ancient Roman art. The park also contains the Odeon, a small semicircular theatre, the agora, the ruins of the castle of the 40 Columns (Saranta Kolones), and the Tombs of the Kings necropolis. UNESCO World Heritage Site.

## 4. Choirokoitia, Limassol District

Choirokoitia is one of the most important Neolithic sites in the Near East, occupied from roughly 7000 to 4000 BC. The settlement consists of circular mud-brick and stone houses (tholoi) built on a river bank, surrounded by a substantial stone defensive wall. The inhabitants practiced an economy of hunting, herding, and limited agriculture and buried their dead beneath the floors of their houses — a practice common to many Neolithic communities in the region. What is remarkable about Choirokoitia is its excellent state of preservation and its chronological position: Cyprus was apparently settled for the first time during this Neolithic period, and the Choirokoitia culture has no clear direct predecessor on the island. UNESCO World Heritage Site; a reconstruction of several houses is open to visitors.

## 5. Palaipafos (Kouklia), Paphos District

Palaipafos ('Old Paphos'), at the village of Kouklia, was the site of the great sanctuary of Aphrodite, the most important religious centre in Cyprus throughout antiquity. According to myth, Aphrodite was born from sea foam at a beach nearby (the rock of Aphrodite, Petra tou Romiou, is still pointed out). The sanctuary, built during the Bronze Age and continuously rebuilt, was a place of pilgrimage from across the Greek and later Roman world. The deity was represented not by an anthropomorphic statue but by a conical black stone of uncertain origin. The ruins of the sanctuary precinct, the late Bronze Age walls, and a medieval manor house built over earlier remains are all visible at the site.

## 6. Ancient Amathus, Limassol District

Amathus, on the coast east of Limassol, was one of the ancient city-kingdoms of Cyprus and notable for having maintained strong Phoenician and indigenous Eteocypriot cultural traditions alongside Hellenistic influences. The site covers a substantial hill above the coast and preserves the acropolis sanctuary, urban remains, an agora, and an extraordinary pair of colossal stone pithoi (storage vessels) — the largest ancient storage jars known from Cyprus — that were transported to the Louvre in the 19th century. The necropolis outside the city produced rich finds from the Bronze Age through the Roman period. The site is walkable from the coastal road.

## 7. Tenta, Near Kalavasos

Tenta is a Neolithic site near Kalavasos in the Vasilikos valley, occupied from roughly 7000 to 5500 BC — broadly contemporaneous with Choirokoitia and representing a similar round-house settlement of the Khirokitia culture. The site has been protected under a modern shelter that allows visitors to look down on the excavated remains of circular structures, storage pits, and hearths. The Vasilikos valley also contains the later Bronze Age settlement of Kalavasos-Ayios Dhimitrios, which has yielded evidence of large-scale olive oil production and storage, suggesting an organized palatial economy in the Late Cypriot Bronze Age.

## 8. Kyrenia Castle and Shipwreck, Northern Cyprus

Kyrenia (Girne) Castle, overlooking the harbour of the northern coastal town, has Roman, Byzantine, Crusader, Venetian, and Ottoman phases of construction — a layered military structure reflecting the island's successive rulers. The castle's most remarkable exhibit is the Kyrenia Ship, a Greek merchant vessel that sank around 300 BC and was recovered almost intact from the harbour floor in the 1960s. The ship's hull, preserved by sand and silt, was meticulously conserved and is now displayed in the castle along with its original cargo of amphorae, millstones, and almonds. It is one of the oldest ancient ships to survive anywhere.

## 9. Idalion, Near Dali

Idalion was an ancient city-kingdom of Cyprus, occupied from the Bronze Age through the Hellenistic period, and famous in the ancient world as the site where the Phoenician city of Kition attempted to establish dominance in the 5th century BC — an event recorded on a bronze tablet (the Idalion Tablet) that is the longest surviving inscription in the Cypriot syllabic script. The site preserves an acropolis with remains of temple structures, an ashlar building associated with the city's last Cypriot ruler, and an extensive lower city. Excavations by the University of Georgia have been particularly important in recent decades.

## 10. Enkomi, Near Famagusta (Northern Cyprus)

Enkomi was the principal Bronze Age city of Cyprus during the Late Cypriot period (roughly 1650–1050 BC) and was probably the island's most important urban centre during the height of the Mediterranean Bronze Age trading system. The site has yielded exceptional finds: the Horned God bronze statuette, one of the masterpieces of Cypriot bronze-casting, the Ingot God standing on a bronze ingot, and enormous quantities of Mycenaean, Levantine, and Egyptian imports that testify to Enkomi's role as a hub in the international trade network. The site's grid-planned street layout is one of the earliest examples of urban planning in the Mediterranean.

Cyprus's major archaeological sites in the southern Republic are generally open daily from April to October, with reduced hours in winter. The Paphos Archaeological Park and Kourion are best visited in the morning before afternoon heat in summer. Sites in northern Cyprus require crossing through border points from the south; this is straightforward with a Cypriot or EU passport. Entrance fees are modest.

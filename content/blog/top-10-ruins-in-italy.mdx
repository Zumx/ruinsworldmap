Italy is arguably the greatest ruins country on Earth — Roman, Greek, and
Etruscan layered across the peninsula. These ten are the essentials.

## 1. The Roman Forum & Palatine, Rome

The political heart of the ancient world — temples, basilicas, and the hill
where emperors lived.

## 2. The Colosseum, Rome

The most recognised ruin on the planet, a feat of engineering and a sobering
arena.

## 3. Pompeii, Campania

An entire Roman city stopped in 79 AD — streets, shops, frescoes, and people
frozen in volcanic ash.

## 4. Herculaneum, Campania

Smaller, deeper, and even better preserved than Pompeii, with carbonised wood
and upper floors intact.

## 5. Valley of the Temples, Agrigento, Sicily

Greek temples so complete they rival anything in Greece itself.

## 6. Paestum, Campania

Three of the best-preserved Doric temples in the world, on the mainland south
of Naples.

## 7. Villa Adriana, Tivoli

Hadrian's vast pleasure-palace complex — an emperor's architectural
imagination in ruin.

## 8. Ostia Antica, near Rome

Rome's port city, remarkably complete and far quieter than Pompeii.

## 9. Selinunte, Sicily

A colossal Greek archaeological park of toppled and re-raised temples by the
sea.

## 10. Segesta, Sicily

A lone, near-perfect Doric temple and a hilltop theatre in empty countryside.

## How Italian ruin visits work

The major sites use timed tickets that sell out (the Colosseum especially —
book well ahead; a combined Forum/Palatine ticket is standard). Sicilian
archaeological parks are vast and exposed; allow half a day each. Smaller sites
(Ostia, Paestum, Segesta) reward you with space and quiet.

## Plan around the regions and heat

Rome, the Bay of Naples (Pompeii/Herculaneum/Paestum), and Sicily are three
separate trips. Visit at opening or late afternoon — midday on a shadeless
Roman site in summer is punishing. Spring and autumn are ideal.

## Etiquette and conservation

Stay on paths, never climb walls, columns, or seating, do not touch frescoes or
mosaics, and take nothing — removing even a "loose" fragment is illegal. These
sites are fragile and finite; treat them as the open-air museums they are.
